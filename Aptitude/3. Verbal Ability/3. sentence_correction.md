## 1. WHAT IS SENTENCE CORRECTION?

**Sentence Correction** questions present a sentence (or part of it) that may contain one or more grammatical errors. Your task is to identify the error and choose the corrected version.

---

### Question Formats:

**Format 1 — Find the Error (Underlined Parts)**
```
The committee have (A) decided to postpone (B) the meeting until (C) further notice (D). No error (E).
Answer: A — "committee" is singular → "has"
```

**Format 2 — Choose the Correct Sentence**
```
(A) Neither of the students have submitted their assignment.
(B) Neither of the students has submitted his assignment.
(C) Neither of the students has submitted their assignments.
(D) Neither of the students have submitted his assignment.
Answer: B
```

**Format 3 — Fill in the Blank with the Correct Form**
```
She _______ in this company since 2015.
(A) works   (B) worked   (C) has been working   (D) had worked
Answer: C
```

**Format 4 — Improve the Underlined Part**
```
"Running quickly, the bus was caught by him."
Correct: "Running quickly, he caught the bus."
```

---

### What Skills Are Being Tested?

```
SENTENCE CORRECTION
        │
        ├── Grammar Rules (Subject-Verb, Tenses, Pronouns...)
        ├── Sentence Structure (Modifiers, Parallelism...)
        ├── Word Choice (Prepositions, Confusing Words...)
        └── Logical Clarity (Comparisons, Conditionals...)
```

## 3. CONCEPT 1 — SUBJECT-VERB AGREEMENT

> **The Rule:** A verb must agree with its subject in **number** (singular/plural) and **person** (1st/2nd/3rd).

---

### Basic Rule

```
Singular Subject  →  Singular Verb (adds -s/-es)
Plural Subject    →  Plural Verb   (no -s/-es)

The dog runs.        (singular)
The dogs run.        (plural)
She writes.          (singular)
They write.          (plural)
```

---

### Rule 1 — Ignore Phrases Between Subject and Verb

The words between the subject and verb do NOT change the verb's number. Mentally cross them out.

```
Structure: Subject + [interrupting phrase] + Verb

WRONG:  The quality of the products are poor.
RIGHT:  The quality [of the products] IS poor.
        ↑ subject = "quality" (singular) → needs "is"

WRONG:  The captain, along with his crew, were rescued.
RIGHT:  The captain [along with his crew] WAS rescued.
        ↑ subject = "captain" (singular)
```

**Common interrupting phrases to ignore:**
```
along with / together with / as well as / in addition to /
accompanied by / with / besides / except / rather than /
including / no less than / like / unlike
```

> ⚡ **Trick:** Cover the interrupting phrase with your hand. Only look at the real subject.

---

### Rule 2 — Either/Neither (Singular)

```
Either / Neither + singular noun → SINGULAR verb

WRONG:  Neither of the boys are coming.
RIGHT:  Neither of the boys IS coming.

WRONG:  Either of the options are acceptable.
RIGHT:  Either of the options IS acceptable.
```

---

### Rule 3 — Either...Or / Neither...Nor (Proximity Rule)

When two subjects are joined by **either...or / neither...nor / not only...but also**, the verb agrees with the **subject closer to it** (the one just before the verb).

```
Rule: [Subject 1] or/nor [Subject 2] → verb agrees with Subject 2

WRONG:  Neither the students nor the teacher are right.
RIGHT:  Neither the students nor the teacher IS right.
        ↑ "teacher" is closer → singular → "is"

WRONG:  Either the manager or the employees is responsible.
RIGHT:  Either the manager or the employees ARE responsible.
        ↑ "employees" is closer → plural → "are"
```

---

### Rule 4 — Collective Nouns

Collective nouns (team, committee, jury, family, class, government, army) are treated as **singular** in standard exam English.

```
WRONG:  The jury have reached a verdict.
RIGHT:  The jury HAS reached a verdict.

WRONG:  The committee are meeting tomorrow.
RIGHT:  The committee IS meeting tomorrow.

Common collective nouns:
team / jury / committee / board / class / family / government /
council / crowd / army / faculty / staff / crew / panel / audience
```

> **Exception:** If members of the group are acting individually, plural is sometimes used in British English. In exams, treat all collective nouns as singular.

---

### Rule 5 — Indefinite Pronouns

```
ALWAYS SINGULAR (takes singular verb):
each / every / either / neither / one / anyone / someone /
everyone / no one / nobody / anybody / somebody / everybody /
nothing / anything / something / everything

Each of the students HAS a book.
Everyone IS welcome.
Nobody WANTS to go.

ALWAYS PLURAL (takes plural verb):
both / few / many / several / others

Both of them ARE ready.
Few students WERE present.
Many books HAVE been sold.

CAN BE SINGULAR OR PLURAL (depends on the noun after "of"):
some / any / all / most / none / half / more / the rest

Some of the water IS gone.       ← water (uncountable) → singular
Some of the students ARE gone.   ← students (countable plural) → plural
```

---

### Rule 6 — "The Number" vs "A Number"

```
"The number of" + plural noun → SINGULAR verb
"A number of" + plural noun   → PLURAL verb

The number of students IS increasing.   (= the count, singular)
A number of students ARE absent.        (= several students, plural)
```

> ⚡ **Memory trick:** **"The"** = specific and singular. **"A"** = many, so plural.

---

### Rule 7 — Titles, Names of Countries, Books, Distances, Amounts

Even if they look plural, they take a **singular** verb when referring to one entity.

```
"The United States IS a powerful nation."   (one country)
"Ten kilometres IS a long distance to walk." (one measurement)
"Five hundred rupees WAS the price."         (one amount)
"Romeo and Juliet IS a tragedy by Shakespeare." (one title)
```

---

### Rule 8 — "There is / There are"

The verb agrees with the subject that follows it.

```
There IS a book on the table.     (a book = singular)
There ARE three books on the table. (three books = plural)
There IS a cat and two dogs.      (nearest subject = "cat" = singular — formal rule)
```

---

### Rule 9 — Arithmetic Operations

```
Addition / Multiplication → SINGULAR verb
Subtraction / Division    → SINGULAR verb

Two and two IS four.
Three times three IS nine.
Ten minus four IS six.
Twelve divided by four IS three.
```

---

### Rule 10 — "Every" and "Each" Always Singular

```
Every boy and girl WAS present.
Each student and teacher HAS to sign.
Every + noun (even multiple) → singular verb
```

## 4. CONCEPT 2 — TENSES

> Tenses are the most heavily tested grammar concept. Master the 12 tenses systematically.

---

### The 12 Tenses at a Glance

```
PRESENT:
  Simple Present       → She writes every day.
  Present Continuous   → She is writing now.
  Present Perfect      → She has written the letter.
  Present Perfect Cont → She has been writing for an hour.

PAST:
  Simple Past          → She wrote yesterday.
  Past Continuous      → She was writing when I called.
  Past Perfect         → She had written before he arrived.
  Past Perfect Cont    → She had been writing for an hour before he came.

FUTURE:
  Simple Future        → She will write tomorrow.
  Future Continuous    → She will be writing at 9 PM.
  Future Perfect       → She will have written by tomorrow.
  Future Perfect Cont  → She will have been writing for two hours by noon.
```

---

### TENSE 1 — Simple Present

**Usage:**
- Habits and routines
- Universal truths / scientific facts
- Permanent situations

```
Signal words: always, usually, often, sometimes, rarely, never, every day/week/year

She drinks coffee every morning.
The Earth revolves around the Sun.   ← universal truth (always present tense)
Water boils at 100°C.               ← scientific fact
```

> ⚡ **Exam Trick:** Scientific facts and universal truths are ALWAYS in Simple Present, even in reported speech or historical context.
```
Newton proved that gravity PULLS objects downward. (NOT "pulled")
```

---

### TENSE 2 — Present Continuous

**Usage:**
- Action happening RIGHT NOW
- Temporary actions (not permanent)
- Planned future actions

```
Structure: is/am/are + V-ing

She is studying at the moment.
I am living in Mumbai for now. (temporary)
We are meeting the client tomorrow. (planned)
```

**NOT used with stative verbs:**
```
Stative verbs (no continuous form):
know / believe / think (opinion) / understand / remember / forget /
want / need / prefer / love / hate / like / see / hear / smell /
taste / seem / appear / belong / own / contain / consist / have (possession)

WRONG:  I am knowing the answer.
RIGHT:  I know the answer.

WRONG:  She is having a car.
RIGHT:  She has a car.

EXCEPTION: "Having" is allowed in the continuous form when it means "experiencing":
She is having a good time. ✓ (experiencing, not possessing)
```

---

### TENSE 3 — Present Perfect

**Usage:**
- Action completed at an unspecified time in the past (with relevance to present)
- Actions that started in the past and continue to the present (with "since/for")
- Life experiences

```
Structure: has/have + past participle (V3)

Signal words: just, already, yet, ever, never, recently, lately,
              since [point in time], for [duration], so far, till now,
              up to now, in the past, over the years

She has just left.
I have never eaten sushi.
He has worked here since 2015.
They have been married for ten years.
```

**Common Errors:**
```
WRONG:  She has went to the market.
RIGHT:  She has GONE to the market.       (V3 of go = gone, not went)

WRONG:  I have seen him yesterday.
RIGHT:  I SAW him yesterday.              (specific past time → simple past)

WRONG:  She is living here since 2010.
RIGHT:  She HAS BEEN LIVING here since 2010. (since = present perfect)
```

> ⚡ **Key Rule:** "Since" and "for" (with duration) → ALWAYS use Present Perfect or Present Perfect Continuous. NEVER use Simple Past with "since."

---

### TENSE 4 — Present Perfect Continuous

**Usage:**
- An action that started in the past and is still continuing (emphasis on duration)

```
Structure: has/have + been + V-ing

She has been studying for three hours.
They have been waiting since morning.
```

**Perfect vs Perfect Continuous:**
```
Present Perfect:            Has the action been completed?
Present Perfect Continuous: How long has it been going on?

She has written two reports. (completed — focus on result)
She has been writing since morning. (ongoing — focus on duration)
```

---

### TENSE 5 — Simple Past

**Usage:**
- Completed action at a specific time in the past

```
Signal words: yesterday, last week/month/year, ago, in [year],
              on [date], then, when (in the past)

She left yesterday.
He called me an hour ago.
They built this bridge in 1985.
```

**Most common error — confusing with Present Perfect:**
```
WRONG:  I have seen him yesterday.   (yesterday = specific time → simple past)
RIGHT:  I SAW him yesterday.

WRONG:  She went to Paris and she has returned yesterday.
RIGHT:  She went to Paris and she RETURNED yesterday.
```

---

### TENSE 6 — Past Continuous

**Usage:**
- Action in progress at a specific moment in the past
- Background action interrupted by another (simple past)

```
Structure: was/were + V-ing

She was sleeping when the phone rang.
     ↑ ongoing background          ↑ sudden interruption (simple past)

At 8 PM last night, I was studying.
```

---

### TENSE 7 — Past Perfect

**Usage:**
- The action that happened FIRST in the past (before another past action)

```
Structure: had + past participle (V3)

Signal: before, after, when, by the time, already, no sooner...than, hardly...when

She had left before he arrived.
      ↑ happened FIRST    ↑ happened SECOND

By the time we reached, the train had already departed.
```

**The Sequence Rule:**
```
Earlier action → Past Perfect (had + V3)
Later action   → Simple Past

WRONG:  When I reached the station, the train already left.
RIGHT:  When I reached the station, the train had already LEFT.
```

**No sooner...than / Hardly...when / Scarcely...when:**
```
Structure: No sooner + had + subject + V3 + than + subject + simple past

WRONG:  No sooner did he arrive than she had left.
RIGHT:  No sooner HAD he arrived THAN she left.

WRONG:  Hardly did he sit when the lights went out.
RIGHT:  Hardly HAD he sat WHEN the lights went out.
```

---

### TENSE 8 — Simple Future

```
Structure: will + V1 / shall + V1 (formal/1st person)
           am/is/are going to + V1

She will call you tomorrow.
I am going to visit Delhi next week.
```

**Will vs Shall:**
```
"Shall" with I/We for offers, suggestions, promises (formal):
Shall I help you?   (offer)
We shall overcome. (determination)

"Will" for all persons for future actions:
She will arrive at 5.
They will finish by tomorrow.
```

---

### TENSE 9 — Future Perfect

**Usage:**
- An action that will be completed BEFORE a specific future time

```
Structure: will have + past participle (V3)

Signal: by [future time], before [future time]

By 5 PM, she will have finished the report.
Before you arrive, I will have cooked dinner.
```

---

### Quick Tense Signal Word Table

| Signal Word/Phrase | Tense |
|---|---|
| yesterday, ago, last..., in [year] | Simple Past |
| just, already, yet, recently, ever, never | Present Perfect |
| since, for (with duration) | Present Perfect / Pres. Perf. Cont. |
| when (past + past) | Past Perfect (earlier) + Simple Past (later) |
| by the time (past) | Past Perfect |
| by [future time] | Future Perfect |
| before (past event) | Past Perfect |
| always, usually, often, every... | Simple Present |
| now, at the moment, currently, look!, listen! | Present Continuous |
| tomorrow, next..., soon | Simple Future |

## 5. CONCEPT 3 — PRONOUNS

> Pronouns must agree with their antecedents in **number**, **gender**, and **case**.

---

### Rule 1 — Pronoun Case (Subject vs Object)

```
SUBJECT pronouns (used before verb):  I, he, she, we, they, who
OBJECT pronouns  (used after verb/preposition): me, him, her, us, them, whom

WRONG:  Between you and I, it's a mistake.
RIGHT:  Between you and ME. (after preposition "between" → object case)

WRONG:  She gave the book to he and I.
RIGHT:  She gave the book to HIM and ME. (after preposition "to")

WRONG:  Him and me went to the store.
RIGHT:  HE and I went to the store. (subject of verb "went")
```

> ⚡ **Trick:** Remove the other pronoun and read alone.
> "Between I" sounds wrong → "Between me" is correct.
> "Me went to the store" sounds wrong → "I went" is correct.

---

### Rule 2 — Pronoun-Antecedent Agreement

The pronoun must match its noun in number and gender.

```
WRONG:  Every student must bring their book.
RIGHT:  Every student must bring HIS/HER book.
        (every = singular → his/her, not their)

WRONG:  The company lost their profits.
RIGHT:  The company lost ITS profits.
        (company = singular entity → its)

WRONG:  Each of the girls must submit her assignments on time.
        (Actually CORRECT — "each" = singular → "her" ✓)
```

---

### Rule 3 — Who vs Whom

```
WHO   = subject pronoun (like "he/she/they")
WHOM  = object pronoun  (like "him/her/them")

Test: Replace with he/him
If "he" fits → use WHO
If "him" fits → use WHOM

"Who/Whom called you?"
→ He called = WHO ✓

"To who/whom did you speak?"
→ You spoke to him = WHOM ✓

WRONG:  Who did you give the letter to?
RIGHT:  WHOM did you give the letter to?
        (you gave it to him → object → whom)
```

---

### Rule 4 — Reflexive Pronouns

Reflexive pronouns (myself, yourself, himself, herself, itself, ourselves, yourselves, themselves) are used when the subject and object are the same.

```
WRONG:  She hurt her.          (different person implied)
RIGHT:  She hurt HERSELF.      (same person)

WRONG:  Please contact myself for queries.
RIGHT:  Please contact ME for queries. (no reflexive needed — different subject)

WRONG:  The manager himself are attending.
RIGHT:  The manager himself IS attending.
```

---

### Rule 5 — Relative Pronouns

```
WHO/WHOM/WHOSE   → refers to PEOPLE
WHICH            → refers to THINGS / ANIMALS
THAT             → refers to PEOPLE or THINGS (in restrictive clauses)
WHAT             → "the thing that" (never used after a noun)

WRONG:  The man which I met was kind.
RIGHT:  The man WHO I met was kind.

WRONG:  The book who he wrote is famous.
RIGHT:  The book WHICH/THAT he wrote is famous.

WRONG:  This is what the book that I need.
RIGHT:  This is the book THAT I need.
        OR: This is WHAT I need. (no noun before "what")
```

---

### Rule 6 — Pronoun Shift (Consistency)

Do not shift from one pronoun to another without reason.

```
WRONG:  If one works hard, you will succeed.
RIGHT:  If one works hard, ONE will succeed.
        OR: If you work hard, YOU will succeed.

WRONG:  When a student studies well, they should also rest.
RIGHT:  When a student studies well, HE/SHE should also rest.
        (student = singular → he/she)
```

## 6. CONCEPT 4 — ARTICLES (A / AN / THE)

> Articles are among the most frequently tested areas in competitive exams.

---

### A vs AN — The Sound Rule

```
"A"  before CONSONANT SOUNDS
"AN" before VOWEL SOUNDS

It's the SOUND, not the spelling that matters!

A university (yoo-ni-versity → starts with "y" sound → A)
An umbrella   (um-brella → starts with vowel sound → AN)
An hour       (our → "h" is silent → vowel sound → AN)
A historical  (his-tor-i-cal → "h" is pronounced → A)
An honest man (on-est → "h" is silent → AN)
An MBA        (em-bee-ay → starts with vowel sound "em" → AN)
A B.Tech      (bee-tek → starts with consonant sound "b" → A)
```

---

### When to Use "THE"

**Use THE when:**

```
1. Referring to something already mentioned or known:
   I saw a dog. THE dog was barking loudly.

2. When there is only one of something (unique):
   The sun / the moon / the Earth / the sky / the ocean
   The President of India / the Prime Minister

3. With superlatives:
   She is THE best student in the class.

4. With ordinal numbers:
   THE first chapter / THE second attempt

5. With names of:
   - Rivers: the Ganga, the Amazon, the Nile
   - Mountain ranges: the Himalayas, the Alps
   - Oceans/Seas: the Pacific, the Arabian Sea
   - Deserts: the Sahara, the Thar
   - Island groups: the Andamans, the Philippines
   - Newspapers: the Times of India, the Hindu
   - Historical documents: the Constitution, the Magna Carta

6. With "same":
   We stayed at THE SAME hotel.

7. With "of" phrases identifying something unique:
   The capital of France / the height of the building
```

---

### When NOT to Use "THE"

```
NO ARTICLE before:
1. Names of people: Gandhi (not "the Gandhi")
2. Names of countries (usually): India, France, China
   Exception: the USA, the UK, the Netherlands, the UAE
3. Names of individual mountains: Mount Everest (not "the Mount Everest")
4. Names of languages: English, Hindi (not "the English")
5. Names of sports: cricket, football
6. Names of meals: breakfast, lunch, dinner
7. Names of subjects: Mathematics, History
8. Days and months: Monday, January
9. Diseases (usually): diabetes, cancer
   Exception: the flu, the measles
```

---

### Common Article Errors in Exams

```
WRONG:  She is best student in the class.
RIGHT:  She is THE best student.  (superlative → needs "the")

WRONG:  The honesty is the best policy.
RIGHT:  Honesty is the best policy.  (abstract noun, general → no article)

WRONG:  He is the doctor by profession.
RIGHT:  He is A doctor by profession.  (one among many doctors)

WRONG:  She has a unique idea.
RIGHT:  She has A unique idea.  (unique = "yoo-neek" → consonant sound → A, not AN)

WRONG:  I saw a elephant.
RIGHT:  I saw AN elephant. (vowel sound)

WRONG:  He is in hospital for treatment.   (British English — correct)
        He is in the hospital.             (American English — also correct)
```

## 7. CONCEPT 5 — PREPOSITIONS

> Prepositions show relationships of time, place, direction, manner, and cause. Most errors involve wrong preposition choice.

---

### Prepositions of Time

```
AT  → specific times, clock times, holidays
      at 5 o'clock / at noon / at midnight / at Christmas / at night

ON  → days, dates, specific days
      on Monday / on 15th August / on my birthday / on weekends

IN  → months, years, seasons, centuries, parts of day (except night)
      in January / in 2023 / in summer / in the morning / in the 20th century

BY  → deadline (not later than)
      Submit by Friday. / Complete it by 5 PM.

SINCE → point in time (with perfect tenses)
        She has been here since Monday.

FOR  → duration (with perfect tenses)
       She has been here for three days.

WITHIN → inside a period of time
         Reply within 24 hours.

DURING → throughout a period
         He slept during the lecture.
```

---

### Prepositions of Place

```
AT  → specific point/location
      at the corner / at the bus stop / at home / at school / at work

ON  → surface
      on the table / on the floor / on the wall / on the bus / on the train

IN  → enclosed space
      in the box / in the room / in the car / in the building / in India

ABOVE → higher than, not touching
        The plane flew above the clouds.

OVER → directly above / covering / more than
       She put a blanket over the child.

BELOW → lower than
        The temperature is below zero.

UNDER → directly below / less than
        The cat is under the table.

BETWEEN → two things
          The book is between the two boxes.

AMONG → three or more things
        She was among the best students.
```

---

### Commonly Confused Prepositions in Exams

```
MARRIED TO (not "with"):
  WRONG:  She is married with a doctor.
  RIGHT:  She is married TO a doctor.

DIFFERENT FROM (not "than" or "to" in formal English):
  WRONG:  This is different than that.
  RIGHT:  This is different FROM that.

INFERIOR/SUPERIOR TO (not "than"):
  WRONG:  This product is superior than that one.
  RIGHT:  This product is superior TO that one.
  Other: Junior to / Senior to / Prior to / Anterior to

REASON FOR (not "of"):
  WRONG:  The reason of his failure is laziness.
RIGHT:  The reason FOR his failure is laziness.

AGREE WITH (person) / AGREE TO (proposal) / AGREE ON (subject):
  I agree WITH you.
  She agreed TO the proposal.
  They agreed ON the terms.

SUFFER FROM (not "with"):
  WRONG:  He is suffering with fever.
  RIGHT:  He is suffering FROM fever.

DEAL WITH (not "in" when handling):
  She deals WITH difficult clients. (handles)
  He deals IN antiques. (trades)

COMPLY WITH (not "to"):
  WRONG:  You must comply to the rules.
  RIGHT:  You must comply WITH the rules.

CONGRATULATE ON (not "for"):
  WRONG:  I congratulate you for your success.
  RIGHT:  I congratulate you ON your success.

BLAME FOR (not "on" in this structure):
  She blamed him FOR the accident.
  She put the blame ON him.

COMPARE TO (showing similarity) / COMPARE WITH (examining differences):
  Life is often compared TO a journey. (similarity)
  Compare this model WITH that one. (examine differences)
```

## 8. CONCEPT 6 — MODIFIERS (MISPLACED & DANGLING)

> A modifier is a word, phrase, or clause that describes something. It must be placed CLOSE to what it modifies. Modifier errors are extremely common in exams.

---

### Misplaced Modifier

A modifier is placed near the WRONG word, creating a silly or unclear meaning.

```
WRONG:  She almost drove her children to school every day.
        (almost modifies "drove" → she almost drove but didn't?)
RIGHT:  She drove her children to school almost every day.
        (almost modifies "every day" — she nearly did it every day)

WRONG:  I only eat vegetables on Mondays.
        (only modifies "eat" → weird meaning)
RIGHT:  I eat only vegetables on Mondays.
        (only modifies "vegetables" → the intended meaning)

WRONG:  He saw a dog walking down the road.
        (Who is walking — he or the dog? Ambiguous)
RIGHT:  Walking down the road, he saw a dog.
        OR: He saw a dog that was walking down the road.
```

---

### Dangling Modifier

A dangling modifier has NO clear subject to modify in the sentence. The implied subject of the modifier does not match the actual subject of the main clause.

```
WRONG:  Running to catch the bus, my bag fell.
        (Who is running? "My bag" cannot run — dangling modifier)
RIGHT:  Running to catch the bus, I dropped my bag.
        OR: While I was running to catch the bus, my bag fell.

WRONG:  Having studied all night, the exam was easy for him.
        (Who studied? "The exam" didn't study — dangling modifier)
RIGHT:  Having studied all night, HE found the exam easy.

WRONG:  Walking through the park, the flowers were beautiful.
        (Flowers don't walk — dangling modifier)
RIGHT:  Walking through the park, SHE found the flowers beautiful.

WRONG:  To improve your score, practice is essential.
        ("To improve" implies a person, but "practice" is the subject)
RIGHT:  To improve your score, YOU must practice.
```

> ⚡ **The Golden Rule for Modifiers:**
> The **subject of the modifier** must be the **subject of the main clause**.
> If the modifier starts with V-ing or "To + V", the main clause's subject must be the one doing that action.

---

### Common Misplaced Words

```
ONLY, JUST, EVEN, ALMOST, NEARLY, MERELY, SIMPLY, EXACTLY

These words must be placed IMMEDIATELY before the word they modify.

"I only told her" (I didn't do anything else — just told)
"I told only her" (I told no one else — just her)
"I told her only" (slightly awkward but similar to above)
```

## 9. CONCEPT 7 — PARALLELISM

> **Parallelism** means that items in a list or paired structures must use the **same grammatical form**.

---

### Basic Parallelism Rule

```
If you start with a NOUN → all items must be nouns
If you start with V-ing → all items must be V-ing
If you start with "to + V" → all items must be "to + V"

WRONG:  She likes reading, to swim, and dance.
RIGHT:  She likes reading, swimming, and dancing.
        OR: She likes to read, to swim, and to dance.

WRONG:  The job requires patience, skill, and being dedicated.
RIGHT:  The job requires patience, skill, and dedication.  (all nouns)
```

---

### Parallelism with Conjunctions

```
BOTH...AND         → parallel items
EITHER...OR        → parallel items
NEITHER...NOR      → parallel items
NOT ONLY...BUT ALSO → parallel items
AS WELL AS         → parallel items

WRONG:  She is both intelligent and has creativity.
RIGHT:  She is both intelligent and creative.
        (intelligent = adjective → creative = adjective)

WRONG:  He not only sings but also is playing guitar.
RIGHT:  He not only sings but also plays guitar.
        (sings = simple present → plays = simple present)

WRONG:  I like neither going to movies nor do I like shopping.
RIGHT:  I like neither going to movies nor shopping.
```

---

### Parallelism with Comparisons

```
WRONG:  Swimming is more tiring than to run.
RIGHT:  Swimming is more tiring than running.
        (swimming = V-ing → running = V-ing)

WRONG:  To err is human; forgiving is divine.
RIGHT:  To err is human; to forgive is divine.
        (to err = to + V → to forgive = to + V)
```

---

### Parallelism with Lists

```
WRONG:  The new employee is punctual, has good communication skills,
        and she works hard.
RIGHT:  The new employee is punctual, communicative, and hardworking.
        (all adjectives)
        OR: The new employee comes on time, communicates well, and works hard.
        (all verb phrases)
```

## 10. CONCEPT 8 — COMPARISONS

> Comparison errors involve comparing two things incorrectly or illogically.

---

### Rule 1 — Comparative vs Superlative

```
TWO things   → COMPARATIVE (more/less/-er)
THREE+ things → SUPERLATIVE (most/least/-est)

WRONG:  He is the tallest of the two brothers.
RIGHT:  He is the TALLER of the two brothers.

WRONG:  She is more intelligent of all the students.
RIGHT:  She is the MOST intelligent of all the students.
```

---

### Rule 2 — "Than any other" / "Than all other"

When comparing a member WITH its own group, use "any other" or "all other":

```
WRONG:  Mumbai is larger than any city in India.
        (This implies Mumbai is not in India — illogical)
RIGHT:  Mumbai is larger than any OTHER city in India.
        OR: Mumbai is larger than all OTHER cities in India.
```

---

### Rule 3 — Comparing Like with Like

You must compare the same type of thing with the same type of thing.

```
WRONG:  The population of China is more than India.
        (Comparing "population" with "India" — illogical)
RIGHT:  The population of China is more than THAT OF India.
        OR: China's population is greater than India's.

WRONG:  The price of gold is higher than silver.
        (Comparing "price of gold" with "silver" — illogical)
RIGHT:  The price of gold is higher than THAT OF silver.
        OR: Gold is more expensive than silver.

WRONG:  His handwriting is better than his brother.
RIGHT:  His handwriting is better than HIS BROTHER'S.
        (comparing handwriting to handwriting, not to brother)
```

---

### Rule 4 — Double Comparatives (Wrong)

```
WRONG:  She is more smarter than him.
RIGHT:  She is smarter than him.
        OR: She is more intelligent than him.

WRONG:  This is the most highest building.
RIGHT:  This is the highest building.

WRONG:  He runs more faster.
RIGHT:  He runs faster.
```

---

### Rule 5 — "As...as" Comparisons

```
Structure: as + adjective/adverb + as

WRONG:  She is as tall or taller than him.
RIGHT:  She is as tall as or taller than him.

WRONG:  He is not as smarter as her.
RIGHT:  He is not as SMART as her. (no comparative with "as...as")

WRONG:  The sooner, it is better.
RIGHT:  The sooner, the better. (double "the" structure)
```

---

## 11. CONCEPT 9 — CONJUNCTIONS

> Conjunctions join words, phrases, or clauses. Common errors involve using the wrong conjunction or wrong pair.

---

### Coordinating Conjunctions (FANBOYS)

```
For / And / Nor / But / Or / Yet / So

These join two EQUAL elements (two independent clauses, two nouns, etc.)

She is tired, BUT she continues working.
He studied hard, SO he passed the exam.
```

---

### Correlative Conjunctions (Always in Pairs)

```
BOTH...AND          (not "both...as well as")
EITHER...OR         (not "either...nor")
NEITHER...NOR       (not "neither...or")
NOT ONLY...BUT ALSO (not "not only...but")
WHETHER...OR        (not "whether...or not...or")
HARDLY...WHEN       (not "hardly...than")
NO SOONER...THAN    (not "no sooner...when")
SCARCELY...WHEN     (not "scarcely...than")
AS...AS             (not "as...so" in positive comparison)

WRONG:  Neither she or he was present.
RIGHT:  Neither she NOR he was present.

WRONG:  Not only she sings but she also dances.
RIGHT:  Not only does she SING but she also DANCES.
        OR: She not only sings but also dances. (parallel)

WRONG:  No sooner did he leave when she arrived.
RIGHT:  No sooner did he leave THAN she arrived.

WRONG:  Hardly had he sat when the alarm rang.
RIGHT:  Hardly had he sat WHEN the alarm rang.
```

---

### Subordinating Conjunctions

```
BECAUSE vs SINCE vs AS:
  All mean "for the reason that" but:
  Because (strongest causal link) / Since (also means "from that time") / As (weakest)

  WRONG:  Being that she was tired, she went to bed.
  RIGHT:  Because she was tired, she went to bed.

ALTHOUGH vs DESPITE vs IN SPITE OF:
  Although + clause (Subject + Verb)
  Despite / In spite of + noun/pronoun/V-ing (NOT a clause)

  WRONG:  Despite she was tired, she worked on.
  RIGHT:  Although she was tired, she worked on.
  RIGHT:  Despite her tiredness, she worked on.
  RIGHT:  Despite being tired, she worked on.

UNLESS vs IF NOT:
  Unless = if...not (condition)
  WRONG:  Unless you don't hurry, you'll miss the train.
  RIGHT:  Unless you hurry, you'll miss the train.
          (Unless already contains the negative — don't add "not")

WHILE vs WHEN:
  While = during a period / simultaneous actions (continuous)
  When  = at a specific point in time

  WRONG:  While I was reading, she entered and I stood up.
  RIGHT:  WHILE I was reading, she entered. WHEN she entered, I stood up.
```

## 12. CONCEPT 10 — CONDITIONAL SENTENCES (IF-CLAUSES)

> Conditionals express situations and their consequences. There are 4 types.

---

### Type 0 — Zero Conditional (Always True / Universal)

```
Structure: If + Simple Present + Simple Present

If you heat water to 100°C, it boils.
If it rains, the ground gets wet.
(Always true facts / scientific truths)
```

---

### Type 1 — First Conditional (Real / Possible Future)

```
Structure: If + Simple Present + will + V1

If she studies hard, she will pass.
If it rains, we will cancel the picnic.
(Realistic possibility in the future)

WRONG:  If she will study hard, she will pass.
RIGHT:  If she STUDIES hard, she will pass.
        (NEVER use "will" in the if-clause of Type 1)
```

---

### Type 2 — Second Conditional (Unreal / Hypothetical Present)

```
Structure: If + Simple Past + would + V1

If I were rich, I would travel the world.
If she knew the answer, she would tell us.
(Not real — imaginary / hypothetical situation)

KEY RULE: Use "WERE" (not "was") for all persons in Type 2:
WRONG:  If I was you, I would apologize.
RIGHT:  If I WERE you, I would apologize.
WRONG:  If he was here, he would help.
RIGHT:  If he WERE here, he would help.

WRONG:  If I were rich, I will travel.
RIGHT:  If I were rich, I WOULD travel.
```

---

### Type 3 — Third Conditional (Unreal Past / Regret)

```
Structure: If + Past Perfect (had + V3) + would have + V3

If she had studied harder, she would have passed.
If he had arrived on time, he would have caught the train.
(Imaginary — referring to something that did NOT happen in the past)

WRONG:  If she would have tried, she would have succeeded.
RIGHT:  If she HAD TRIED, she would have succeeded.
        (NEVER use "would have" in the if-clause)

WRONG:  If he had studied, he would passed.
RIGHT:  If he had studied, he would HAVE PASSED.
```

---

### Mixed Conditionals

```
Past condition → Present result:
If she had saved money, she would be rich now.
(if-clause = Type 3, main clause = Type 2)

Present condition → Past result:
If he were more careful, he would not have made that mistake.
```

---

### Quick Conditional Summary Table

| Type | If-Clause | Main Clause | Use |
|------|-----------|------------|-----|
| 0 | Simple Present | Simple Present | Always true |
| 1 | Simple Present | will + V1 | Real future |
| 2 | Simple Past / were | would + V1 | Hypothetical present |
| 3 | Past Perfect | would have + V3 | Unreal past |

## 13. CONCEPT 11 — ACTIVE AND PASSIVE VOICE

> In Active Voice, the subject performs the action. In Passive Voice, the subject receives the action.

---

### The Core Formula

```
ACTIVE:  Subject + Verb + Object
PASSIVE: Object (now subject) + be + past participle (V3) + by + Agent

Active:  She writes the letter.
Passive: The letter IS WRITTEN by her.

Active:  He has finished the work.
Passive: The work HAS BEEN FINISHED by him.
```

---

### Passive Voice Across Tenses

```
Simple Present:     is/am/are + V3
                    The book IS READ by him.

Present Continuous: is/am/are + being + V3
                    The book IS BEING READ by him.

Simple Past:        was/were + V3
                    The book WAS READ by him.

Past Continuous:    was/were + being + V3
                    The book WAS BEING READ by him.

Present Perfect:    has/have + been + V3
                    The book HAS BEEN READ by him.

Past Perfect:       had + been + V3
                    The book HAD BEEN READ by him.

Simple Future:      will + be + V3
                    The book WILL BE READ by him.

Future Perfect:     will + have + been + V3
                    The book WILL HAVE BEEN READ by him.

Modal:              modal + be + V3
                    The book SHOULD BE READ.
                    The book CAN BE READ.
```

---

### Common Passive Voice Errors

```
WRONG:  The work was completed by them last night.
        (Correct — no error here)

WRONG:  She was married with a rich man.
RIGHT:  She was married TO a rich man. (preposition error, not voice error)

WRONG:  The letter was wrote by him.
RIGHT:  The letter was WRITTEN by him. (V3 of write = written, not wrote)

WRONG:  The house is being build.
RIGHT:  The house is being BUILT. (V3 = built)

WRONG:  He is known to visited this place.
RIGHT:  He is known to HAVE VISITED this place.
```

## 14. CONCEPT 12 — DIRECT AND INDIRECT SPEECH

> Indirect (Reported) Speech requires changes in tense, pronouns, and time expressions.

---

### Tense Changes in Reported Speech

```
DIRECT SPEECH          →    INDIRECT SPEECH
Simple Present         →    Simple Past
Present Continuous     →    Past Continuous
Present Perfect        →    Past Perfect
Simple Past            →    Past Perfect
Past Perfect           →    Past Perfect (no change)
Will                   →    Would
Can                    →    Could
May                    →    Might
Shall                  →    Would
Must / Have to         →    Had to
```

---

### Time Expression Changes

```
DIRECT              →    INDIRECT
now                 →    then
today               →    that day
yesterday           →    the previous day / the day before
tomorrow            →    the next day / the following day
last week           →    the previous week / the week before
next week           →    the following week
here                →    there
this                →    that
these               →    those
ago                 →    before / previously
```

---

### Pronoun Changes

```
Pronouns change based on who is speaking and who is being addressed.

Direct:   He said, "I am tired."
Indirect: He said THAT HE was tired.
(I → he, because the speaker is "he")

Direct:   She said to me, "You are right."
Indirect: She told ME that I was right.
(You → I, because "me" becomes the subject)
```

---

### Reporting Verb Changes for Questions

```
Yes/No questions → "whether" or "if"
Direct:   She asked, "Are you coming?"
Indirect: She asked WHETHER/IF I was coming.

Wh-questions → keep the wh-word
Direct:   He asked, "Where do you live?"
Indirect: He asked WHERE I lived.

WRONG:  She asked me where did I live.
RIGHT:  She asked me where I LIVED. (no inversion in indirect questions)

WRONG:  He asked whether did she want tea.
RIGHT:  He asked WHETHER she wanted tea.
```

---

### Reporting Commands and Requests

```
Commands: tell/order/instruct + object + to + V1
Direct:   He said to her, "Close the door."
Indirect: He TOLD her TO CLOSE the door.

Requests: ask/request + object + to + V1
Direct:   She said to him, "Please help me."
Indirect: She ASKED him TO HELP her.

Negative commands: tell/order + object + not to + V1
Direct:   He said, "Don't make noise."
Indirect: He TOLD them NOT TO MAKE noise.
```

## 15. CONCEPT 13 — COUNTABLE VS UNCOUNTABLE NOUNS

---

### The Key Distinction

```
COUNTABLE: Can be counted individually (one, two, three...)
           book → books / student → students / idea → ideas

UNCOUNTABLE: Cannot be counted (no plural form)
             water / information / advice / luggage / furniture /
             equipment / news / knowledge / traffic / weather /
             work / money / research / evidence / homework / bread /
             hair (as a mass) / music / love / peace / time (abstract)
```

---

### Many vs Much / Few vs Little

```
MANY  → countable plural nouns     (many books, many students)
MUCH  → uncountable nouns          (much water, much information)

FEW   → countable nouns (negative) (few friends = not many friends)
LITTLE → uncountable nouns (negative) (little water = not much water)

A FEW  → countable (positive)    (a few books = some books)
A LITTLE → uncountable (positive) (a little water = some water)

WRONG:  There is less students in the class today.
RIGHT:  There are FEWER students in the class today.
        (students = countable → fewer, not less)

WRONG:  There is less water in the tank.
RIGHT:  There is LESS water in the tank. ✓ (uncountable → less)

WRONG:  She gave me many advices.
RIGHT:  She gave me MUCH advice. (advice = uncountable, no plural)
        OR: She gave me a lot of advice.

WRONG:  I have less books than him.
RIGHT:  I have FEWER books than him. (books = countable → fewer)
```

---

### Number vs Amount / Quantity

```
NUMBER  → countable (a large number of students)
AMOUNT  → uncountable (a large amount of water)

WRONG:  A large amount of people attended.
RIGHT:  A large NUMBER of people attended.
        (people = countable → number)
```

---

## 16. CONCEPT 14 — ADJECTIVES AND ADVERBS

---

### Adjective vs Adverb

```
ADJECTIVE: modifies a NOUN or PRONOUN
ADVERB:    modifies a VERB, ADJECTIVE, or another ADVERB

WRONG:  She sings beautiful.
RIGHT:  She sings BEAUTIFULLY. (modifies verb "sings" → adverb)

WRONG:  He runs very fastly.
RIGHT:  He runs very FAST. ("fast" is already an adverb — no -ly needed)
Other flat adverbs: hard, late, early, high, deep, long, near, far

WRONG:  She looked at him angry.
RIGHT:  She looked at him ANGRILY. (modifies verb "looked")

EXCEPTION — Linking verbs take adjectives (not adverbs):
She looks BEAUTIFUL. (not "beautifully" — "looks" is a linking verb here)
He seems TIRED.
The food tastes GOOD.
She feels BAD. (not "badly" unless she has no sense of touch — joke)
The milk smells SOUR.
```

---

### Comparative and Superlative Forms

```
One-syllable adjectives:   add -er / -est
  tall → taller → tallest
  fast → faster → fastest

Two-syllable adjectives ending in -y: change y to i + er/est
  happy → happier → happiest
  easy → easier → easiest

Most two-syllable+ adjectives: more / most
  beautiful → more beautiful → most beautiful
  important → more important → most important

IRREGULAR forms (must memorize):
  good → better → best
  bad  → worse  → worst
  much/many → more → most
  little → less → least
  far  → farther/further → farthest/furthest
  old  → older/elder → oldest/eldest
```

## 17. CONCEPT 15 — COMMON CONFUSING WORDS

---

### Most Tested Confusing Word Pairs

```
AFFECT vs EFFECT:
  Affect (verb)  = to influence something
  Effect (noun)  = the result / outcome
  The rain affected the match. / The rain had a serious effect.
  Effect (verb, rare) = to bring about: "to effect a change"

LIE vs LAY:
  Lie (intransitive) = to recline   → lay, lain
    I lie down. / He lay down yesterday. / She has lain here all day.
  Lay (transitive)   = to place     → laid, laid
    Lay the book on the table. / She laid the book down.
  WRONG:  He was laying on the bed.
  RIGHT:  He was LYING on the bed.

RISE vs RAISE:
  Rise (intransitive) = to go up   → rose, risen
    The sun rises. / The sun rose. / Prices have risen.
  Raise (transitive)  = to lift up → raised, raised
    He raised his hand. / They raised the price.
  WRONG:  He raised up from his seat.
  RIGHT:  He ROSE from his seat.

SIT vs SET:
  Sit (intransitive) = to be seated → sat, sat
  Set (transitive)   = to place     → set, set
  WRONG:  Set down and relax.
  RIGHT:  SIT down and relax.

FEWER vs LESS: (see Concept 13)

FARTHER vs FURTHER:
  Farther = physical distance: He ran farther than I did.
  Further = additional/figurative: Let us discuss this further.

IMPLY vs INFER:
  Imply  = to suggest (speaker/writer implies)
  Infer  = to conclude (listener/reader infers)
  She implied that she was unhappy.
  I inferred from her tone that she was unhappy.

ENSURE vs INSURE vs ASSURE:
  Ensure  = to make certain
  Insure  = to take out insurance
  Assure  = to tell someone confidently (reassure)

ACCEPT vs EXCEPT:
  Accept = to receive / agree
  Except = excluding / apart from
  
PRINCIPAL vs PRINCIPLE:
  Principal = main / head of school (noun & adjective)
  Principle = a rule or belief (noun only)

STATIONARY vs STATIONERY:
  Stationary = not moving (adjective)
  Stationery = writing materials (noun)

COMPLEMENT vs COMPLIMENT:
  Complement = something that completes / goes well with
  Compliment = a remark expressing praise

COMPRISE vs COMPOSE:
  Comprise  = to consist of (the whole comprises the parts)
  Compose   = to make up (the parts compose the whole)
  WRONG:  India is comprised of 28 states.
  RIGHT:  India COMPRISES 28 states.
  RIGHT:  28 states COMPOSE India.

EMIGRATE vs IMMIGRATE:
  Emigrate = to leave a country
  Immigrate = to enter a country
```

## 18. MASTER TRICKS & SHORTCUTS

---

### 🚀 TRICK 1 — The Subject-Finder (Cross Out Phrases)

```
When checking subject-verb agreement, physically cross out all phrases
between commas and "along with / as well as / together with / including."

"The manager, along with his assistants, [are/is] responsible."
→ Cross out: [along with his assistants]
→ Subject = "The manager" (singular) → IS ✓
```

---

### 🚀 TRICK 2 — The Article Sound Test

```
Say the word that follows A/AN out loud.
If it starts with a VOWEL SOUND → AN
If it starts with a CONSONANT SOUND → A

Tricky words:
AN hour (silent H → vowel sound)
A uniform (yoo → consonant sound)
AN MBA (em-bee-ay → vowel sound)
A European (yoo-ro-pean → consonant sound)
AN honest (silent H → vowel sound)
A university (yoo-ni → consonant sound)
```

---

### 🚀 TRICK 3 — The Conditional Quick Check

```
If-clause:       NEVER use "will" or "would"
Main clause:     NEVER use "if"

Type 1: If + present → will + V1
Type 2: If + past/were → would + V1
Type 3: If + had + V3 → would have + V3

See "will" in the if-clause? → ERROR. Replace with simple present.
See "would have" in the if-clause? → ERROR. Replace with "had + V3."
```

---

### 🚀 TRICK 4 — The Modifier Check (Who Is Doing It?)

```
When a sentence starts with a V-ing phrase or "To + V" phrase:
The SUBJECT of the main clause must be the one doing that action.

"Running fast, the bus was missed."
Ask: Who is running? The bus? NO → Dangling modifier → ERROR.
Fix: "Running fast, HE missed the bus."

Step 1: Find the opening action phrase.
Step 2: Ask WHO is doing that action.
Step 3: That person/thing must be the subject of the main clause.
```

---

### 🚀 TRICK 5 — The Parallelism Check

```
In a list or paired structure, convert everything to the same form:
All nouns? All V-ing? All "to + V"? All adjectives?

"She likes reading, to swim, and dances." → 3 different forms → ERROR
Fix: "She likes reading, swimming, and dancing." → all V-ing ✓

Check FANBOYS (for, and, nor, but, or, yet, so) and correlative pairs:
Both... AND / Either... OR / Neither... NOR
What form appears before the conjunction? → Same form must appear after.
```

---

### 🚀 TRICK 6 — The Pronoun Case Trick (Remove One)

```
For "X and I" vs "X and me" constructions:
Remove the other person and read the sentence alone.

"Between you and (I/me)" → "Between I" or "Between me"?
→ "Between me" sounds right → me ✓

"(He/Him) and I went to the store."
→ "Him went" or "He went"? → "He went" ✓ → He and I ✓

"She gave it to (he/him) and (I/me)."
→ "She gave it to he"? No → "She gave it to him and me" ✓
```

---

### 🚀 TRICK 7 — The Who/Whom Substitution

```
Replace who/whom with he/him (or she/her):
If "he" fits → use WHO
If "him" fits → use WHOM

"(Who/Whom) did they select?"
→ They selected him → WHOM ✓

"(Who/Whom) is calling?"
→ He is calling → WHO ✓

"She is the one (who/whom) I trust."
→ I trust her → WHOM ✓
```

---

### 🚀 TRICK 8 — The Fewer/Less, Many/Much Quick Rule

```
Can you COUNT it?  → FEWER / MANY / NUMBER
Can you MEASURE it? → LESS / MUCH / AMOUNT

Students, books, chairs = countable → fewer students, many books
Water, time, information = uncountable → less water, much information

Memory trick: LESS is for MESS (uncountable things make a mess — water, sugar, flour)
```

---

### 🚀 TRICK 9 — The "Unless" Trap

```
"Unless" = "if...not"
So adding "not" after "unless" creates a double negative = WRONG

WRONG:  Unless you don't study, you will fail.
RIGHT:  Unless you STUDY, you will fail.
        (Unless already contains the negative)

Any time you see "unless...not" → It is an error. Remove "not."
```

---

### 🚀 TRICK 10 — The "No Sooner / Hardly / Scarcely" Formula

```
No sooner + HAD + subject + V3 + THAN + subject + simple past
Hardly/Scarcely + HAD + subject + V3 + WHEN + subject + simple past

These structures use INVERTED (question-like) word order.

WRONG:  No sooner he arrived than she left.
RIGHT:  No sooner HAD HE ARRIVED than she left.

WRONG:  Hardly she sat when the phone rang.
RIGHT:  Hardly HAD SHE SAT when the phone rang.
```

---

### 🚀 TRICK 11 — The Stative Verb Check

```
These verbs are NEVER used in continuous (-ing) form:
know / believe / understand / want / need / prefer / love / hate /
like / see / hear / smell / taste / seem / appear / own / belong / contain

WRONG:  I am knowing the answer.
RIGHT:  I know the answer.

Any time you see these verbs in -ing form → likely an error.
Exception: "Think" in continuous = currently thinking/considering:
"She is thinking about the problem." ✓ (process, not opinion)
```

---

### 🚀 TRICK 12 — The Comparison Illogic Detector

```
Step 1: Identify what is being compared.
Step 2: Ask: Are they the SAME TYPE of thing?

"The salary of a doctor is more than a teacher."
→ Comparing "salary" with "a teacher" → NOT the same type → ERROR
→ Fix: "...more than THAT OF a teacher."
       (now comparing salary to salary)

Red flag words: than / as...as / compared to
After these, ask: same type as the first thing? If not → error.
```

---

### 🚀 TRICK 13 — The "Despite / Although" Rule

```
Despite / In spite of + NOUN / PRONOUN / V-ing  (NOT + subject + verb)
Although / Even though + SUBJECT + VERB          (full clause)

"Despite she was tired" → ERROR (clause after despite)
Fix: "Despite her tiredness" OR "Although she was tired"

"Although being tired" → ERROR (no subject after although)
Fix: "Although she was tired" OR "Despite being tired"
```

---

### 🚀 TRICK 14 — Reported Speech Tense Shift

```
Quick way to shift tenses:
Present → Past (is → was, goes → went)
Past → Past Perfect (went → had gone)
Will → Would / Can → Could / May → Might

And NEVER use "will" or inverted question order in reported speech:
WRONG:  She asked where did he go.
RIGHT:  She asked where HE WENT. (no inversion, past tense)
```

---

### 🚀 TRICK 15 — The "The Number / A Number" Memory Trick

```
"THE number" → Think of a specific number → ONE → SINGULAR → IS
"A number" → Think of many → PLURAL → ARE

The number of errors IS increasing.
A number of errors ARE present.
```

## 19. COMMON MISTAKE ALERTS

---

### ⚠️ MISTAKE 1 — Wrong Verb After Collective Nouns

```
WRONG:  The team are playing well.
RIGHT:  The team IS playing well.
Rule:   committee / jury / team / family / board → always singular in exams.
```

---

### ⚠️ MISTAKE 2 — Using Simple Past with "Since"

```
WRONG:  She lived here since 2010.
RIGHT:  She HAS LIVED (or has been living) here since 2010.
Rule:   "Since" always requires Present Perfect or Present Perfect Continuous.
```

---

### ⚠️ MISTAKE 3 — Using "Will" in the If-Clause

```
WRONG:  If she will come, I will tell her.
RIGHT:  If she COMES, I will tell her.
Rule:   NEVER use "will" in the if-clause of a conditional sentence.
```

---

### ⚠️ MISTAKE 4 — Dangling Modifier (Forgetting the Real Subject)

```
WRONG:  Being a student, the rules must be followed.
RIGHT:  Being a student, YOU must follow the rules.
Rule:   The subject performing the opening action must be the main clause's subject.
```

---

### ⚠️ MISTAKE 5 — "Were" Instead of "Was" in Hypothetical Sentences

```
WRONG:  If I was the president, I would change the laws.
RIGHT:  If I WERE the president, I would change the laws.
Rule:   In Type 2 conditionals and wishes, use "were" for ALL persons.
        "I wish I WERE taller." / "I wish she WERE here."
```

---

### ⚠️ MISTAKE 6 — "Advices / Informations / Furnitures"

```
WRONG:  She gave me some useful advices.
RIGHT:  She gave me some useful ADVICE. (uncountable, no plural)
Other uncountable nouns with no plural:
information / furniture / luggage / equipment / news / knowledge /
research / traffic / weather / progress / scenery / machinery
```

---

### ⚠️ MISTAKE 7 — "Less" with Countable Nouns

```
WRONG:  There are less students today.
RIGHT:  There are FEWER students today.
Rule:   Countable = fewer. Uncountable = less.
```

---

### ⚠️ MISTAKE 8 — Preposition After "Superior / Inferior / Junior / Senior"

```
WRONG:  This product is superior than that.
RIGHT:  This product is superior TO that.
Rule:   Superior / Inferior / Junior / Senior / Prior → always followed by "to," never "than."
```

---

### ⚠️ MISTAKE 9 — Breaking Parallel Structure

```
WRONG:  She enjoys cooking, reading, and to paint.
RIGHT:  She enjoys cooking, reading, and PAINTING.
Rule:   All items in a list must use the same grammatical form.
```

---

### ⚠️ MISTAKE 10 — "Married With" Instead of "Married To"

```
WRONG:  She is married with a doctor.
RIGHT:  She is married TO a doctor.
        OR: She got married TO a doctor.
Rule:   "Married" always takes the preposition "to."
```

## 21. ANSWERS & EXPLANATIONS

### SET A — SUBJECT-VERB AGREEMENT

**A1. Answer: (A) is**
"The quality" is the subject (singular). "Of these mangoes" is an interrupting prepositional phrase — ignore it. Singular subject → singular verb → "is."

**A2. Answer: (A) has**
"Neither of the boys/candidates" always takes a singular verb. "Neither" is an indefinite pronoun that is always singular. → "has."

**A3. Answer: (A) has**
"The committee" is a collective noun. Collective nouns take singular verbs in standard exam English. → "has decided."

**A4. Answer: (A) were**
"A number of students" = several students (plural in meaning). "A number of" + plural noun → plural verb. → "were absent." (Compare: "The number of students WAS high.")

**A5. Answer: (A) was**
"Each of the players" — "each" is always singular. → "was given."

**A6. Answer: (A) is**
"Five thousand rupees" refers to a single sum/amount of money → singular verb. → "is a large sum."

**A7. Answer: (A) is**
"The United States" is the name of one country — treated as a singular entity despite the plural-looking name. → "is one of..."

**A8. Answer: (A) are**
With "either...or / neither...nor," the verb agrees with the subject closer to it (Proximity Rule). "teachers" (plural) is closer → "are responsible."

**A9. Answer: (A) was**
"News" is an uncountable noun that always takes a singular verb. → "was shocking."

**A10. Answer: (A) was**
"Not only...but also" — verb agrees with the subject closer to it (Proximity Rule). "the teacher" (singular) is closer → "was surprised."

---

### SET B — TENSES

**A11. Answer: (C) has been working**
"For the past twelve years" indicates an action that started in the past and continues now → Present Perfect Continuous. "Worked" (simple past) would imply it ended. "Had worked" is past perfect (wrong context).

**A12. Answer: (B) had died**
Two past actions: ambulance arrived (simple past = later) and patient died (happened FIRST). Earlier action → Past Perfect. → "had died."

**A13. Answer: (C) boils**
Scientific fact / universal truth → always Simple Present. Never changes tense regardless of surrounding context. → "boils."

**A14. Answer: (C) had**
"Hardly...when" structure: Hardly + HAD + subject + V3 + WHEN + simple past. → "Hardly HAD she reached home when the storm broke out."

**A15. Answer: (C) was reading**
"When I met her" = a specific moment in the past. The reading was in progress at that moment (background action interrupted) → Past Continuous. → "was reading."

**A16. Answer: (C) saw**
"Yesterday" is a specific past time reference → always Simple Present. → "I saw him yesterday." (Present Perfect "have seen" cannot be used with specific past time words.)

**A17. Answer: (B) will have left**
"By the time we reach" = before a future time → the train's leaving will be completed before that future moment → Future Perfect. → "will have left."

**A18. Answer: (C) had the team won**
"No sooner" structure: No sooner + HAD + subject + V3 + THAN + simple past. → "No sooner HAD the team WON than the crowd began to celebrate."

**A19. Answer: (C) pulls**
Universal / scientific truth stated by Newton → Simple Present (even in reported speech). → "gravity pulls."

**A20. Answer: (C) had been working**
"Before she finally solved" = a past moment. She was working continuously before that → Past Perfect Continuous. → "had been working for three hours before she solved."

### SET C — PRONOUNS & MODIFIERS

**A21. Answer: (A) me**
"To" is a preposition → requires object case. "between/to + pronoun" → object pronoun. → "to both the director and ME." (Test: "The award was given to I" → wrong → "me" ✓)

**A22. Answer: (A) his or her**
"Every student" = singular (every always takes singular). → "his or her project." "Their" is plural and doesn't agree with singular "every student" in formal grammar.

**A23. Answer: (A) I dropped my umbrella**
"Running to catch the last bus" is a dangling modifier. Who was running? Not the umbrella. The subject of the main clause must be the runner. → "Running to catch the last bus, I dropped my umbrella."

**A24. Answer: (A) As she walked along the beach, the sunset appeared strikingly beautiful.**
"Walking along the beach" is a dangling modifier — the sunset cannot walk. The person walking must be the subject. → Option A correctly introduces "she" as the subject.

**A25. Answer: (A) he**
"him and the sales team" is the subject of "would present" → needs subject pronoun. → "He and the sales team would present." (Test: "Him would present" → wrong.)

**A26. Answer: (A) whomever**
"The committee had designated [whom?] as the spokesperson" → the pronoun is the object of "designated" → object case → "whomever." (Test: replace with him/her → "the committee designated him" → "whomever" ✓)

**A27. Answer: (B) He drives to work only on Fridays**

### SET D — ARTICLES, PREPOSITIONS & CONJUNCTIONS

The correct preposition with "suffer" is "from." → "suffering FROM a severe headache." (Suffer with = wrong. Suffer from = correct.)

**A30. Answer: (A) from**
"Different" takes the preposition "from" in standard/formal English. → "different FROM last year's."

**A31. Answer: (A) for**
"Reason FOR" is correct. → "the reason FOR his dismissal." ("Reason of" is incorrect in standard English.)

**A32. Answer: (A) Although**
"Despite" must be followed by a noun/pronoun/V-ing, NOT a full clause (subject + verb). Since "she was exhausted" is a full clause → use "Although." → "Although she was exhausted..."

**A33. Answer: (A) Unless you hurry**
"Unless" = "if...not." Adding "don't" after "unless" creates a double negative. → "Unless you hurry" (removing "don't").

**A34. Answer: (A) on**
"Congratulate" takes the preposition "on." → "congratulated him ON his remarkable performance."

**A35. Answer: (A) nor**
"Neither...nor" is the correct correlative pair. "Neither...or" is incorrect. → "Neither she NOR he was present."

---

### SET E — PARALLELISM & COMPARISONS

**A36. Answer: (A) her punctuality**
The list uses noun phrases: "her honesty," "her dedication." The third item must also be a noun phrase → "her punctuality." "Being punctual" (V-ing) breaks parallel structure.

**A37. Answer: (A) spends**
"Not only...but also" requires parallel structure. "Not only works" (simple present) → "but also spends" (simple present). "Is spending" (continuous) breaks parallelism.

**A38. Answer: (A) that of India**
You must compare like with like. "Population of China" must be compared with "population of India," not with "India" itself. → "larger than THAT OF India." ("that" = the population)

**A39. Answer: (A) tallest**
"Most tallest" is a double superlative — incorrect. Use either "tallest" (suffix form) OR "most tall" (though "tallest" is standard). → "the TALLEST player."

**A40. Answer: (A) any other**
Mumbai is a city IN Maharashtra. Saying "larger than any city" would include Mumbai itself → illogical. → "larger than any OTHER city in Maharashtra."

---

### SET F — CONDITIONALS & REPORTED SPEECH

**A41. Answer: (A) studies**
Type 1 Conditional (real/possible future): If + Simple Present → will + V1. NEVER use "will" in the if-clause. → "If she STUDIES harder, she will pass."

**A42. Answer: (A) were**
Type 2 Conditional (hypothetical present): If + were (for ALL persons). → "If I WERE the Prime Minister..." Using "was" in hypothetical sentences is an error in formal English.

**A43. Answer: (A) had informed**
Type 3 Conditional (unreal past): If + Past Perfect (had + V3) → would have + V3. NEVER use "would have" in the if-clause. → "If they HAD INFORMED me earlier, I would have made arrangements."

**A44. Answer: (A) I lived**
In reported questions, use normal statement word order (no inversion) and shift tense to past. → "where I LIVED" (not "where did I live" — no auxiliary "did" in reported speech).

**A45. Answer: (A) would**
In reported speech, "will" changes to "would." The reporting verb "told" (past) triggers the tense shift. → "he WOULD call me."

---

### SET G — MIXED / ADVANCED

**A46. Answer: (A)**
"Opportunities" is countable (you can count opportunities) → use "fewer," not "less." → "fewer opportunities." The rest of the sentence is correct.

**A47. Answer: (A)**
"The manager" is the subject. "Together with his entire team" is an interrupting phrase (ignore it for verb agreement). Singular subject → singular verb → "is attending."

**A48. Answer: (A)**
Dangling modifier: "Having completed the assignment" implies students completed it, but the main clause makes "the teacher" the subject. Fix: the subject must be the students. → "Having completed the assignment, the students informed the teacher."

**A49. Answer: (A)**
Two errors: (1) "more smarter" = double comparative (wrong). (2) "any girl" should be "any other girl" (Mumbai-in-Maharashtra logic — she is a girl in her class). → "smarter than any other girl in her class."

**A50. Answer: (A)**
"No sooner" structure requires: No sooner + HAD + subject + V3 + THAN + simple past. "did...enter" is wrong here; "had...entered" is correct. Also "when" should be "than." → "No sooner HAD the CEO ENTERED...THAN all the employees stood up."

**A51. Answer: (A)**
For yes/no questions in reported speech, use "whether" or "if" (not "that"). Statement word order (no inversion): "whether they had completed" (not "had they completed"). → "whether they had completed their homework on time."

**A52. Answer: (A)**
"An hour ago" is a specific past time reference → Simple Past must be used, not Present Perfect. Also, "has went" is wrong (V3 of go = gone, not went). → "She WENT to the market an hour ago."

**A53. Answer: (A)**
Subject = "pollution levels" (plural) → plural verb "have increased." (Note: "data" is technically plural, so "show" is correct. The error is "has increased" — levels are plural → "have increased.") → "pollution levels in the city HAVE increased."

**A54. Answer: (B)**
"Inspite" is one word error — should be "In spite of." Also, "In spite of working overtime every day" requires a person (not "the project") as the subject. The project doesn't work overtime. → "In spite of working overtime every day, the TEAM did not complete the project on schedule." Option A also works. Option B is the most precise correction.

**A55. Answer: (A)**
"Between" is a preposition → requires object pronoun. → "Between you and ME." "I" (subject pronoun) cannot follow a preposition.

**A56. Answer: (B)**
"Looking" here is a stative/linking verb describing appearance → takes an adjective, not an adverb. → "looking very BEAUTIFUL." However, since it's a continuous form used for appearance (not action), option B (beautiful) is correct. Option A changes the tense unnecessarily.

**A57. Answer: (A)**
"Know" is a stative verb — it CANNOT be used in the continuous (-ing) form. → "I KNOW the answer." Never "I am knowing."

**A58. Answer: (A)**
"Senior" takes the preposition "to," not "than." → "senior TO me." Never "senior than."

**A59. Answer: (A)**
"The number of" (not "a number of") → singular verb. "The number of road accidents" → singular → "HAS increased." (Compare: "A number of accidents HAVE occurred.")

**A60. Answer: (A)**
In reported speech, "will" changes to "would." "She told me (past)" → the future reference shifts: "will graduate" → "would graduate." → "she WOULD graduate the following year."

## 🏆 TOP 15 EXAM TIPS

1. **Cross out interrupting phrases first** — "along with / together with / as well as" do not affect the verb. Find the real subject, then decide singular or plural.

2. **"Since" always demands Present Perfect** — Any sentence with "since" + simple past is wrong. Change it to has/have been or has/have + V3.

3. **Never write "will" in the if-clause** — Type 1: "If she comes" (not "if she will come"). See "will" in the if-clause → always an error.

4. **Type 2 conditionals always use "were"** — "If I were," "If she were," "If he were" — never "was" in hypothetical conditionals.

5. **Opening V-ing phrase → check the subject** — "Running fast, the bus left" is wrong because buses don't run. The doer of the action must be the main clause subject.

6. **A/AN = sound, not spelling** — "AN MBA," "A university," "AN hour," "A European." Always say the word aloud.

7. **"Fewer" for countable, "Less" for uncountable** — Students, books, cars = fewer. Water, time, money = less.

8. **Stative verbs cannot be continuous** — "I am knowing" is always wrong. "Know/want/understand/believe" have no -ing form.

9. **Parallelism check: before and after the conjunction must match** — Both...AND, Either...OR, Not only...BUT ALSO → same grammatical form on both sides.

10. **For comparison: add "other" when comparing within a group** — "She is taller than any other girl" (not "any girl" — she is a girl herself).

11. **"Superior/Inferior/Senior/Junior" always take "to"** — Never "superior than." This is among the most tested preposition rules.

12. **"Unless" already contains "not"** — "Unless you don't hurry" is a double negative error. Always "Unless you hurry."

13. **"No sooner...than" and "Hardly...when" need inversion** — "No sooner HAD he arrived THAN" — not "did arrive," not "when."

14. **Reported speech: no inversion in questions** — "She asked where did I live" is wrong. → "She asked where I lived."

15. **The number of → singular; A number of → plural** — "The number of errors IS increasing." "A number of errors ARE present."

Train yourself to scan each sentence category by category: first check subject-verb agreement, then tense, then pronoun, then modifier, then parallelism. With consistent practice, you will spot errors in under 20 seconds. Trust the rules — they never change. You've got this! 💪*
