## 1. WHAT IS READING COMPREHENSION?

**Reading Comprehension (RC)** tests your ability to:
- **Understand** what a passage says (explicitly)
- **Interpret** what the author implies (implicitly)
- **Analyze** the author's tone, purpose, and structure
- **Apply** the passage's ideas to new situations

> **Golden Rule:** Every answer to every RC question is **either directly stated or logically implied** by the passage. Your personal opinions and outside knowledge are NEVER the answer.

### Why RC Appears in Exams

RC is tested in almost every competitive exam:

| Exam | RC Weight |
|------|-----------|
| CAT / XAT / GMAT | Very High (30–40% of Verbal) |
| SSC CGL / CHSL | Medium (2–3 passages) |
| IBPS PO / Clerk | High (3–5 passages) |
| UPSC CSAT | Medium-High |
| GRE / TOEFL | Very High |
| Campus Placements | High |

## 2. TYPES OF RC PASSAGES

Understanding the passage type helps you mentally prepare for what kind of questions to expect.

---

### 2.1 Descriptive Passages

**What it is:** Describes a topic, phenomenon, place, or concept in detail.

**Example Topics:** Climate change, history of an invention, geographical features.

**Questions expected:** Main idea, supporting details, vocabulary in context.

**Reading approach:** Look for the topic sentence of each paragraph.

---

### 2.2 Argumentative / Persuasive Passages

**What it is:** The author argues for or against a position, trying to persuade the reader.

**Example Topics:** Should social media be regulated? Is nuclear energy safe?

**Questions expected:** Author's tone, assumption, strengthen/weaken, inference.

**Reading approach:** Identify the author's claim and the evidence used to support it.

---

### 2.3 Narrative Passages

**What it is:** Story-like passages, often from literature or personal essays.

**Example Topics:** A childhood memory, a fictional excerpt, a biographical account.

**Questions expected:** Tone, inference about characters, vocabulary, theme.

**Reading approach:** Focus on mood, emotions, and what happens.

---

### 2.4 Scientific / Technical Passages

**What it is:** Dense, information-heavy passages about science, technology, economics.

**Example Topics:** Gene editing, quantum computing, monetary policy.

**Questions expected:** Detail-based, vocabulary, inference from data.

**Reading approach:** Don't get lost in jargon. Focus on the overall idea of each paragraph.

---

### 2.5 Historical / Social Passages

**What it is:** Discusses events, social movements, historical figures.

**Example Topics:** Industrial revolution, women's rights movement, colonialism.

**Questions expected:** Author's perspective, specific facts, tone.

**Reading approach:** Note cause-effect relationships and the author's stance.

## 3. TYPES OF QUESTIONS ASKED

This is the **most important section** to memorize. Every RC question falls into one of these categories.

---

```
RC QUESTION TYPES
      │
      ├── DIRECT (Answer is in the passage)
      │         ├── Main Idea
      │         ├── Specific Detail / Fact
      │         ├── Title / Heading
      │         └── Vocabulary in Context
      │
      └── INDIRECT (Requires reasoning)
                ├── Inference
                ├── Author's Tone / Attitude
                ├── Author's Purpose / Intent
                ├── Assumption
                ├── Strengthen / Weaken
                └── Paragraph Function / Structure
```

---

| Question Type | Key Trigger Words | Strategy |
|---|---|---|
| Main Idea | "primarily concerned with", "central theme", "mainly about" | Find the common thread across all paragraphs |
| Specific Detail | "according to the passage", "the author states", "mentioned in" | Go back and locate the specific line |
| Inference | "implies", "suggests", "can be inferred", "most likely" | What MUST be true based on the passage? |
| Tone/Attitude | "author's tone", "attitude towards", "author's feeling" | Look for positive/negative/neutral language |
| Vocabulary | "as used in the passage", "in context, X means" | Read the surrounding sentences for context clues |
| Title/Heading | "best title", "appropriate heading" | Must reflect the ENTIRE passage, not just one part |
| Author's Purpose | "the author wrote this to", "primary purpose is" | Why did the author write this? To inform? Persuade? |
| Assumption | "assumes", "underlying assumption" | What UNSTATED belief must be true for the argument to work? |
| Strengthen | "which would strengthen the argument" | Find what supports the author's main claim |
| Weaken | "which would weaken / undermine" | Find what challenges the author's main claim |

## 4. CORE CONCEPTS YOU MUST KNOW

---

### 4.1 Main Idea / Central Theme

> **Definition:** The ONE overarching point that the entire passage communicates. Every paragraph somehow connects to this single idea.

**How to find it:**
- Read the first paragraph carefully — it often introduces the main idea.
- Read the last paragraph — it often restates or concludes the main idea.
- Ask yourself: "What is the author talking about throughout the WHOLE passage?"

**What to avoid:**
- Don't pick an idea mentioned in only ONE paragraph (that's a supporting detail).
- Don't pick something too broad (the whole topic area, not the specific point made).
- Don't pick something too narrow (a single example or fact).

---

#### Example Passage (Short):

> *"For decades, scientists believed that stress was purely harmful. Recent research, however, challenges this view. Studies show that moderate stress can sharpen focus, improve memory retention, and even boost immune function. The key distinction lies between acute stress — short, manageable bursts — and chronic stress, which remains harmful. Understanding this difference could reshape how we approach mental health and productivity."*

**Question:** What is the central theme of this passage?

**Options:**
- (A) Chronic stress is dangerous to health
- (B) Scientists have always misunderstood stress
- (C) Moderate stress can have positive effects, challenging traditional views
- (D) Mental health and productivity are linked

**Analysis:**
- (A) — Too narrow, only one part of the passage
- (B) — Distortion; "always" is too extreme
- (C) — Covers the entire passage: the shift in understanding about stress ✓
- (D) — Mentioned only at the very end

**Answer: C**

---

### 4.2 Supporting Details

> **Definition:** Specific facts, examples, statistics, or explanations that the author uses to back up the main idea.

**How to answer:**
- These are "go-back" questions. Don't rely on memory — locate the exact line.
- The question will often include a keyword. Search for that keyword in the passage.
- The answer is very close to that keyword.

**Common traps:**
- "True but not stated" — something may be a fact in the real world but not mentioned in the passage.
- "Stated but distorted" — the passage says X but the option says Y (a twisted version).

---

#### Example:

> *"The Amazon rainforest produces approximately 20% of the world's oxygen. It is home to over 10% of all species on Earth. Despite its importance, deforestation has destroyed nearly 20% of the Amazon over the last 50 years."*

**Question:** According to the passage, what percentage of Earth's species live in the Amazon?

- (A) 20%
- (B) 50%
- (C) 10%
- (D) Not mentioned

**Answer: C** — Directly stated in the second sentence.

---

### 4.3 Inference Questions

> **Definition:** Inference questions ask for what is IMPLIED but not directly stated. The correct answer MUST logically follow from the passage — it cannot be a wild guess or personal opinion.

**The Inference Rule:** The answer must be TRUE based ONLY on information in the passage. It is not stated directly, but it is the only conclusion that logically follows.

**How to approach:**
1. Identify which part of the passage the question refers to.
2. Ask: "If everything in this passage is true, what ELSE must be true?"
3. Eliminate options that are too extreme, too broad, or outside the passage's scope.

**Trap options to watch for:**
- **Too extreme:** uses words like "always," "never," "all," "none," "only" — be very suspicious.
- **True in real life but not supported by passage.**
- **Contradicts the passage.**
- **Barely related to the relevant section.**

---

#### Example Passage:

> *"In cities where cycling infrastructure has been improved — including dedicated lanes and secure parking — cycling rates have increased by up to 40%. Meanwhile, car usage in those areas has declined proportionally. Cities without such infrastructure have seen no significant change in commuting habits."*

**Question:** What can be inferred from the passage?

- (A) All cities should ban cars
- (B) Infrastructure improvements are the sole cause of behaviour change
- (C) The availability of cycling facilities influences commuting choices
- (D) Cycling is always healthier than driving

**Analysis:**
- (A) — Extreme; the passage never suggests banning cars
- (B) — "Sole cause" is too absolute; the passage shows correlation, not absolute causation
- (C) — This logically follows: where infrastructure improved, cycling increased ✓
- (D) — Health benefits not mentioned in this passage

**Answer: C**

### 4.4 Tone & Attitude

> **Definition:** Tone is the emotional quality or attitude of the author's writing. It is how the author FEELS about the subject.

**How to identify tone:**
- Look for adjectives, adverbs, and verbs that carry emotional weight.
- Is the language positive, negative, or neutral?
- Is the author certain, doubtful, enthusiastic, critical, sarcastic?

---

#### Tone Spectrum (from most negative to most positive):

```
NEGATIVE ◄────────────────────────────────────────────────────► POSITIVE

Scathing  Indignant  Critical  Skeptical  Neutral  Appreciative  Enthusiastic  Reverential
Contemptuous  Cynical  Pessimistic  Ambivalent  Objective  Optimistic  Admiring  Celebratory
```

---

#### Example:

> *"The government's latest economic report, riddled with jargon and devoid of actionable solutions, once again demonstrates the bureaucratic tendency to produce voluminous documentation that achieves precisely nothing."*

**Question:** The author's tone is best described as:
- (A) Appreciative
- (B) Neutral and objective
- (C) Cynical and critical
- (D) Optimistic

**Answer: C** — Words like "riddled," "devoid," "achieves precisely nothing" signal sharp criticism and cynicism.

---

#### Tone Words to Remember (Grouped):

**Critical/Negative tones:**
- Scathing, scornful, contemptuous, indignant, cynical, sarcastic, pessimistic, disparaging

**Neutral/Balanced tones:**
- Objective, analytical, impartial, detached, matter-of-fact, clinical, expository

**Positive tones:**
- Appreciative, admiring, enthusiastic, reverent, celebratory, optimistic, laudatory

**Special tones:**
- Nostalgic (longing for the past)
- Didactic (intending to teach)
- Satirical (using humour to criticize)
- Melancholic (sad, sorrowful)
- Ambivalent (mixed feelings)
- Ironic (saying the opposite of what is meant)

---

### 4.5 Vocabulary in Context

> **Definition:** These questions ask for the meaning of a word or phrase AS IT IS USED in the passage — not its dictionary definition.

**Why "in context" matters:** Many words have multiple meanings. The passage determines which meaning is correct.

**How to answer:**
1. Find the word in the passage.
2. Read the FULL sentence it appears in, plus the sentences before and after.
3. Replace the word with each answer option and see which one makes the most sense.
4. The correct answer will NOT change the meaning of the sentence.

---

#### Example:

> *"The scientist's claims were met with considerable scepticism. Even her peers, who had always held her work in high regard, found the new findings hard to **digest**."*

**Question:** The word "digest" as used in the passage most nearly means:

- (A) Eat and absorb food
- (B) Understand and accept
- (C) Summarize
- (D) Dismiss outright

**Answer: B** — In context, "digest" means to mentally process and accept new information. Option (A) is the literal meaning but wrong in context. (D) is contradicted by "hard to" — they aren't dismissing, they're struggling to accept.

---

### 4.6 Title / Heading Questions

> **Definition:** "What would be the best title for this passage?" or "Which heading suits this passage best?"

**Rules for the best title:**
1. It must cover the **entire** passage — not just one paragraph.
2. It should not be too vague ("An Important Topic") or too specific ("Statistics about the Amazon").
3. It should capture the **main argument or theme**.
4. Avoid titles with extreme words ("The Best Solution," "The Only Way").

---

#### Example:

Imagine a passage that discusses: how social media was initially praised, then how its negative effects emerged, and finally how researchers are now trying to redesign platforms for healthier use.

**Best title would be:**
- ✗ "Social Media: The Root of All Evil" — too extreme
- ✗ "How Facebook Was Founded" — too specific, not in the passage
- ✗ "Technology in Modern Life" — too vague
- ✓ "From Promise to Problem: The Evolving Understanding of Social Media" — covers the arc of the whole passage

---

### 4.7 Author's Purpose & Intent

> **Definition:** Why did the author write this passage? What is the primary goal?

**Common author purposes:**

| Purpose | Signal Words/Phrases |
|---|---|
| **To inform** | Presents facts, data, neutral explanations |
| **To persuade** | Uses argument, takes a side, calls for action |
| **To describe** | Vivid language, sensory details |
| **To narrate** | Tells a story, uses chronological order |
| **To criticize** | Points out flaws, uses negative language |
| **To compare** | Uses "whereas," "unlike," "on the other hand" |
| **To explain** | Breaks down a process or concept |

---

#### Example:

> *"Given the mounting evidence of habitat destruction, governments must immediately enact stronger wildlife protection laws. Delay is not merely irresponsible — it is catastrophic."*

**Author's purpose:** To persuade the reader that stronger wildlife protection laws are urgently needed.

---

### 4.8 Assumption Questions

> **Definition:** An assumption is something the author TAKES FOR GRANTED without stating it. It is an unstated premise that the argument depends on.

**The Negation Test (Most Powerful Trick for Assumptions):**

> Negate (make false) each option. The option that, when negated, DESTROYS the argument = the assumption.

---

#### Example:

**Argument:** "Since all our employees completed the new training programme, customer satisfaction will improve."

**Assumption options:**
- (A) The training programme is expensive.
- (B) The training programme improves employee performance.
- (C) Customers have complained in the past.

**Apply Negation Test:**
- Negate A: "The training is not expensive." → Does this destroy the argument? No. A is not the assumption.
- Negate B: "The training does NOT improve employee performance." → Does this destroy the argument? YES — if the training doesn't improve performance, there's no reason to expect better customer satisfaction. B is the assumption ✓
- Negate C: "Customers have NOT complained." → Irrelevant to the argument's logic.

**Answer: B**

---

### 4.9 Strengthen / Weaken Questions

> **Definition:**
> - **Strengthen:** Which option makes the argument MORE convincing?
> - **Weaken:** Which option makes the argument LESS convincing / undermines it?

**How to approach:**
1. Identify the argument's conclusion and its supporting evidence.
2. For STRENGTHEN: Find the option that fills a gap, adds evidence, or removes a doubt.
3. For WEAKEN: Find the option that introduces doubt, contradicts evidence, or shows an alternative explanation.

---

#### Example:

**Argument:** "Students who study for more than 2 hours daily score higher in exams. Therefore, more study time causes better scores."

**Which weakens this?**
- (A) Some students study for 3 hours and still fail.
- (B) Students who score high are naturally more motivated and would study more regardless.
- (C) Exams are becoming easier over time.
- (D) Teachers assign more homework to high achievers.

**Answer: B** — This introduces an alternative explanation (natural motivation, not study hours, causes both high scores and more studying). This breaks the causal link the argument claims.

**Which strengthens this?**
- A controlled experiment where identical students were randomly assigned 2+ hours vs. fewer hours, and the former group scored higher.

---

### 4.10 Paragraph Structure & Function

> **Definition:** These questions ask: "What is the function of paragraph X?" or "Why does the author include the example in paragraph 2?"

**Common paragraph functions:**

| Function | What it does |
|---|---|
| **Introduction** | Introduces the topic, sets up the discussion |
| **Counter-argument** | Presents the opposing view (usually before refuting it) |
| **Refutation** | Challenges and disproves the counter-argument |
| **Illustration** | Provides an example to support a point |
| **Elaboration** | Expands on an idea introduced earlier |
| **Conclusion** | Summarizes and wraps up the argument |
| **Concession** | Acknowledges a point from the other side while maintaining the main argument |

## 5. HOW TO READ A PASSAGE — STEP-BY-STEP STRATEGY

---

### The PQRST Method (Best for Long Passages)

```
P — Preview     Skim the first and last sentence of each paragraph (30 seconds)
Q — Question    Read the questions BEFORE the passage (know what to look for)
R — Read        Read the passage actively with purpose
S — Summarize   After each paragraph, mentally note the main point in one line
T — Test        Answer questions, returning to passage to verify
```

---

### Step-by-Step Process:

**Step 1 — Scan the Questions First (30 seconds)**
- Understand what types of questions are asked.
- Note specific keywords that you should look for while reading.
- Are there vocabulary questions? Note which words.

**Step 2 — Active Reading with Purpose**
- Read the FIRST sentence of every paragraph carefully (topic sentence).
- Read the LAST sentence of every paragraph carefully (conclusion/transition).
- Underline or mentally flag: the main argument, any contrast words ("however," "but," "yet"), any strong opinions.

**Step 3 — Identify the Passage's Structure**
Ask yourself after reading:
- What is the main point?
- What is the author's tone?
- Is there an argument being made? What is it?
- Are there counter-arguments? How are they handled?

**Step 4 — Answer Direct Questions First**
- Fact-based, detail, and vocabulary questions are quick wins.
- Use keywords from the question to navigate to the right part of the passage.

**Step 5 — Answer Indirect Questions Last**
- Inference, tone, assumption, strengthen/weaken require more thought.
- Always eliminate clearly wrong options first (usually 2 can be cut quickly).

**Step 6 — Verify**
- For any answer you're unsure about, go back to the passage.
- Never answer purely from memory or general knowledge.

---

### Time Management (Exam Settings)

| Passage Length | Recommended Time |
|---|---|
| Short (150–250 words, 3–4 questions) | 5–7 minutes |
| Medium (300–450 words, 5–6 questions) | 8–10 minutes |
| Long (500–700 words, 7–8 questions) | 12–15 minutes |

> **Rule:** Spend 40% of time reading, 60% of time on questions. Never spend more than 1.5 minutes stuck on one question — skip and return.

## 6. MASTER TRICKS & SHORTCUTS

---

### 🚀 TRICK 1 — The Keyword Navigation Method

Every specific-detail question contains a **keyword** (a name, number, unique word). Scan the passage for that keyword, read the two sentences around it — your answer is right there.

```
Question: "According to the passage, in which year did..."
Action: Scan for the year/number mentioned → read 2 sentences around it → answer found.
```

---

### 🚀 TRICK 2 — Eliminate "Extreme" Options for Inference

Inference questions that contain **always, never, all, none, only, completely, entirely, impossible** are almost always WRONG. The passage rarely supports such absolute statements.

```
"This implies that scientists NEVER..." → Almost certainly wrong.
"This implies that scientists often..." → More likely correct.
```

---

### 🚀 TRICK 3 — The Negation Test for Assumptions

Negate each option. The one whose negation destroys the argument = the assumption.

```
Argument: "X causes Y."
Negation of option: "X does NOT cause Y."
If this kills the argument → It's the assumption. ✓
```

---

### 🚀 TRICK 4 — Tone: Look at Adjectives and Verbs

For tone questions, ignore the facts and look at the EMOTIONAL words.

```
"The policy brilliantly addresses..." → Admiring/Positive tone
"The policy barely scratches the surface..." → Critical/Negative tone
"The policy aims to address..." → Neutral/Objective tone
```

---

### 🚀 TRICK 5 — Main Idea Test (Umbrella Concept)

The main idea must be like an **umbrella** — it covers everything under it. If an option covers only part of the passage, it's a detail, not the main idea.

```
Passage: 3 paragraphs on pollution's causes, effects, solutions.
"Pollution's causes" → Too narrow (only 1 paragraph).
"Pollution: A Comprehensive Challenge" → Covers all 3 paragraphs. ✓
```

---

### 🚀 TRICK 6 — Vocabulary: Substitution Test

Replace the word in the passage with each answer option. The correct one will make the sentence read naturally without changing its meaning.

```
Original: "The new law was met with fierce opposition."
If "fierce" = answer question:
Substitute A: "The new law was met with intense opposition." → Natural ✓
Substitute B: "The new law was met with unusual opposition." → Changes meaning ✗
```

---

### 🚀 TRICK 7 — Purpose Questions: First and Last Paragraphs

The author's purpose is almost always revealed in the **first paragraph** (what the author sets out to do) and **confirmed in the last paragraph** (how they conclude).

---

### 🚀 TRICK 8 — Strengthen/Weaken: Attack the LINK, Not the Facts

The argument's weak point is usually the **link between the evidence and the conclusion**. To weaken, break that link. To strengthen, reinforce it.

```
Evidence: More study time → Better scores
Conclusion: Study time CAUSES better scores
Weaken: Show another factor explains both (motivation)
Strengthen: Show a controlled experiment proving the causal link
```

---

### 🚀 TRICK 9 — "Except" and "NOT" Questions — Flip Your Thinking

When the question asks "which of the following is NOT mentioned in the passage?" — the correct answer is the one that is FALSE or ABSENT from the passage.

```
Strategy: Verify each option against the passage.
Three options will be found in the passage → Eliminate those.
The one that ISN'T there → That's your answer.
```

---

### 🚀 TRICK 10 — Paragraph Function: Ask "Why Is This Here?"

When asked about a paragraph's function, ask: "Why did the author include this? What job does it do?"

```
If a paragraph presents a view the author then argues against → Counter-argument
If a paragraph gives an example to prove a point → Illustration
If a paragraph admits a limitation while maintaining the main claim → Concession
```

---

### 🚀 TRICK 11 — "According to the Passage" vs. "It Can Be Inferred"

**"According to the passage"** = The answer is DIRECTLY STATED. Find the exact line.

**"It can be inferred"** = The answer is NOT directly stated. Reason from what IS stated.

Never confuse these two. Mixing them up is the #1 RC mistake.

---

### 🚀 TRICK 12 — The "Best" Qualifier

When options say "best describes," "most likely," "primarily" — this means no option is perfect. You are choosing the BEST among imperfect options. Don't reject an option just because it's incomplete; compare all options and choose the most accurate one.

---

### 🚀 TRICK 13 — Beware "True in Real Life" Traps

An option may be a fact that you know to be true from your general knowledge but is NOT mentioned or implied in the passage. In RC, that option is WRONG. The only source of truth is the passage itself.

```
Passage: About deforestation in Brazil.
Option: "Trees produce oxygen." → True in real life but if NOT in the passage → Wrong answer.
```

---

### 🚀 TRICK 14 — Transition Words Are Your GPS

Transition words signal the direction of the argument. Learn to read them like road signs.

```
"However / But / Yet" → Author is about to contradict or shift
"Therefore / Thus / Hence" → Conclusion coming
"Moreover / Furthermore / Additionally" → Adding more support
"Although / Even though" → Contrast within the same sentence
"For example / For instance" → Illustration of the previous point
```

---

### 🚀 TRICK 15 — Structural Map of Any Passage

Most RC passages follow one of these structures. Recognize the structure and you'll anticipate where the author is going.

```
Structure 1 (PROBLEM-SOLUTION):
  Para 1: Introduce the problem
  Para 2-3: Explain the problem's depth
  Para 4: Propose solution(s)
  Para 5: Conclusion

Structure 2 (ARGUMENT-COUNTER-REBUTTAL):
  Para 1: State main argument
  Para 2: Acknowledge opposing view
  Para 3: Refute opposing view
  Para 4: Reinforce main argument + conclusion

Structure 3 (COMPARE-CONTRAST):
  Para 1: Introduce two things being compared
  Para 2: Similarities
  Para 3: Differences
  Para 4: Conclusion on which is better / the relationship

Structure 4 (DESCRIPTIVE / INFORMATIONAL):
  Para 1: Overview
  Para 2-4: Different aspects / dimensions
  Para 5: Summary
```

## 8. TRANSITION WORDS & THEIR MEANING

Transition words are the skeleton of a passage. They tell you the relationship between ideas.

---

### Addition (More of the same direction)
> Moreover, Furthermore, Additionally, Also, In addition, Besides, Not only that

**Meaning:** The author is adding more support or information. The next idea aligns with the previous one.

---

### Contrast (Change of direction — CRITICAL signal!)
> However, But, Yet, Nevertheless, Nonetheless, On the other hand, Conversely, Although, Despite, In contrast, Whereas, While, Even so

**Meaning:** The author is about to CONTRADICT or qualify what was just said. The next idea is opposite or different. This is where the author's TRUE position often appears.

> ⚡ **Exam Tip:** Whatever comes AFTER a contrast word is usually more important and closer to the author's main point.

---

### Cause & Effect
> Therefore, Thus, Hence, Consequently, As a result, Because, Since, So, This leads to

**Meaning:** One thing causes or results from another. Look for these when answering "what caused X" or "what resulted from Y."

---

### Illustration / Example
> For example, For instance, To illustrate, Such as, Including, Namely

**Meaning:** The author is about to give a specific example to prove the previous general point.

---

### Emphasis
> Indeed, In fact, Certainly, Clearly, Obviously, Undoubtedly, Above all, Most importantly

**Meaning:** The author is reinforcing a point with extra weight. These signal the most important ideas.

---

### Concession (Acknowledging but maintaining position)
> Admittedly, Granted, Of course, It is true that, While it is correct that

**Meaning:** The author admits a point from the other side BUT will then reaffirm their own argument. The author's position follows the concession.

---

### Summary / Conclusion
> In conclusion, In summary, To sum up, Therefore, Ultimately, Finally, Overall

**Meaning:** The author is wrapping up. Main ideas are being restated.

## 9. COMMON MISTAKE ALERTS

---

### ⚠️ MISTAKE 1 — Using Outside Knowledge

```
Wrong: Choosing an answer because you know it's true from your studies.
Right: Choosing an answer that the passage directly states or clearly implies.
The passage is your ONLY source of truth.
```

---

### ⚠️ MISTAKE 2 — Choosing "Too Extreme" Inferences

```
Wrong: "This implies that the author believes technology ALWAYS harms society."
Right: "This implies that the author is concerned about certain effects of technology."
Extreme words (always, never, all, none) are almost always wrong in inference questions.
```

---

### ⚠️ MISTAKE 3 — Confusing Main Idea with a Supporting Detail

```
Wrong: Picking a point mentioned in ONE paragraph as the main idea.
Right: The main idea connects ALL paragraphs. Use the umbrella test.
```

---

### ⚠️ MISTAKE 4 — Picking the First "Relevant-Sounding" Option

```
Wrong: Stopping at the first option that sounds related to the passage.
Right: Read ALL four options before choosing. The "best" option is often not the first plausible one.
```

---

### ⚠️ MISTAKE 5 — Misreading Tone Due to Topic

```
Wrong: Assuming that because the topic is sad, the author's tone is melancholic.
Right: The tone comes from the LANGUAGE used, not the topic. An author can write about death in a clinical, analytical tone.
```

---

### ⚠️ MISTAKE 6 — Answering Vocabulary Questions from Dictionary Knowledge

```
Wrong: Picking the most common dictionary meaning of the word.
Right: Always read the word in its context. The passage determines the meaning.
```

---

### ⚠️ MISTAKE 7 — Ignoring "NOT" in Questions

```
Question: "Which of the following is NOT mentioned in the passage?"
Wrong: Rushing and picking an option that IS in the passage.
Right: Slow down, verify each option, pick the one that's absent.
```

---

### ⚠️ MISTAKE 8 — Spending Too Long on One Question

```
Wrong: Spending 4 minutes stuck on one hard question.
Right: Mark it, move on, return at the end. Never let one question eat your time for others.
```

---

### ⚠️ MISTAKE 9 — Reading Every Word at the Same Speed

```
Wrong: Reading the entire passage at the same slow pace.
Right: Read the first and last sentence of each paragraph carefully. Skim the middle. Slow down only when a question refers to a specific section.
```

---

### ⚠️ MISTAKE 10 — Ignoring Transition Words

```
Wrong: Missing a "however" and thinking two opposing ideas are in agreement.
Right: Transition words are your GPS. Pay close attention to contrast and conclusion words.
```

### PASSAGE 3 — Hard Level (Philosophy / Social Commentary)

## 🏆 TOP EXAM TIPS

1. **Read the questions BEFORE the passage** — You'll know exactly what to look for while reading.
2. **The word after "However" is gold** — The author's true position almost always follows a contrast word.
3. **Inference ≠ Imagination** — If you can't trace your answer back to the passage, it's wrong.
4. **Eliminate before you select** — Cut 2 obviously wrong options, then compare the remaining 2 carefully.
5. **Tone = Language, not Topic** — A sad topic can be described in an analytical, neutral tone. Read the words, not the subject.
6. **Never answer from memory alone** — Even if you know the answer, verify in the passage for Specific Detail questions.
7. **The Negation Test saves you on Assumption questions** — Negate each option and see which destroys the argument.
8. **"Best title" must cover everything** — If the title fits only one paragraph, eliminate it.
9. **Extreme options in inference questions are almost always wrong** — Words like "all," "never," "only," "completely" are red flags.
10. **Manage your time** — Do not spend more than 1.5 minutes on any single question. Mark it and return.

The more passages you read — across science, social science, philosophy, economics, literature — the faster you will identify passage structure, author intent, and the exact type of question being asked. Trust the systematic approach: Question type → Right strategy → Passage evidence → Best option. You've got this! 💪*
