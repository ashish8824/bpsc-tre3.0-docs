## 1. WHAT ARE IDIOMS & PHRASES?

---

### Definition

An **idiom** is a group of words whose meaning **cannot be understood** from the literal (dictionary) meanings of the individual words. The phrase means something completely different as a whole.

```
LITERAL MEANING vs IDIOMATIC MEANING:

"Kick the bucket"
  Literal:  To kick a bucket with your foot
  Idiomatic: To die

"Break the ice"
  Literal:  To physically break ice
  Idiomatic: To say or do something to ease tension and start a conversation

"Bite the bullet"
  Literal:  To bite on a bullet with your teeth
  Idiomatic: To endure a painful or difficult situation bravely
```

---

### The Golden Rule of Idioms

> **NEVER interpret an idiom word-by-word.**
> The meaning of an idiom is FIXED and FIGURATIVE — it has nothing to do with the literal meanings of its words.

---

### A Phrase

A **phrase** is a group of words that functions as a single unit (as a noun, verb, adjective, or adverb) but does NOT contain a complete subject + verb combination.

```
Types of phrases:
  Noun phrase:      "the tall man in the corner"
  Verb phrase:      "has been waiting"
  Prepositional:    "at the end of the day"
  Idiomatic phrase: "at the drop of a hat" (meaning: immediately)
```

## 3. TYPES OF QUESTIONS ASKED

---

### Type 1 — Find the Meaning

```
"What does the idiom 'BEAT AROUND THE BUSH' mean?"
(A) To garden enthusiastically
(B) To avoid the main topic
(C) To get lost in the forest
(D) To work very hard
Answer: (B) — Beat around the bush = to avoid coming to the point
```

---

### Type 2 — Find the Correct Usage in a Sentence

```
"Choose the sentence where the idiom is used CORRECTLY:"
(A) She was under the weather during the picnic, so she danced all night.
(B) He was under the weather and stayed home from work with a fever.
(C) Under the weather, the garden looked beautiful.
(D) They decided to go under the weather for a walk.
Answer: (B) — Under the weather = feeling slightly ill
```

---

### Type 3 — Fill in the Blank

```
"After years of effort, the project finally saw the _______ of day."
(A) light   (B) brightness   (C) sunshine   (D) glow
Answer: (A) — "See the light of day" = to come into existence / be made public
```

---

### Type 4 — Identify the Meaning from Context

```
"The new manager ruled with an IRON FIST, and no one dared question his decisions."
The phrase "IRON FIST" means:
(A) A metallic glove used in fights
(B) Strict and harsh control
(C) Working very hard
(D) Being unfriendly
Answer: (B) — Rule with an iron fist = to govern with strict, harsh authority
```

---

### Type 5 — Odd One Out (Idiom with Different Meaning)

```
"Three of the following idioms relate to 'being in trouble.' Find the odd one:"
(A) In hot water
(B) In a tight spot
(C) Up a creek without a paddle
(D) On cloud nine
Answer: (D) — On cloud nine = extremely happy; others = in trouble
```

---

### Type 6 — Match the Idiom with Its Meaning

```
Match:
1. Bite the dust       (a) To start a conversation
2. Break the ice       (b) To fail / die
3. Burn bridges        (c) To work late at night
4. Burn midnight oil   (d) To permanently damage a relationship

Answer: 1-b, 2-a, 3-d, 4-c
```

### 4.2 How Idioms Work — The Logic Behind Them

> Understanding the ORIGIN of an idiom helps you remember its meaning far better than rote memorization.

---

#### Historical Origins:

```
"BITE THE BULLET"
Origin: In early surgery before anaesthesia, soldiers were given a bullet
to bite on during painful operations to bear the pain.
Meaning: To endure a painful situation bravely.

"KICK THE BUCKET"
Origin: When a person was hanged, they stood on a bucket which was then
kicked away. OR from "bucket" = a yoke on which pigs were hung for slaughter.
Meaning: To die.

"TURN A BLIND EYE"
Origin: Admiral Nelson at the Battle of Copenhagen put his telescope to his
blind eye to avoid seeing the signal to retreat.
Meaning: To pretend not to notice something.

"COST AN ARM AND A LEG"
Origin: Before photography, portrait painters charged by how many body
parts they needed to paint — limbs were expensive to include.
Meaning: To be extremely expensive.

"RAINING CATS AND DOGS"
Origin: In 17th-century England, heavy storms would fill the streets,
causing dead animals (cats, dogs) to float through the gutters.
Meaning: Raining very heavily.

"BITE OFF MORE THAN YOU CAN CHEW"
Origin: From the practice of cutting off too large a piece of chewing tobacco.
Meaning: To take on more than you can handle.

"FEELING UNDER THE WEATHER"
Origin: Sailors who were seasick would go below deck — under the weather deck.
Meaning: Feeling slightly ill.
```

---

#### Logic-Based Origins (Easier to Remember):

```
"BURN BRIDGES"
Logic: If you burn a bridge, you can't cross back.
Meaning: To permanently damage a relationship or situation with no way back.

"BITE THE HAND THAT FEEDS YOU"
Logic: If you bite someone who feeds you, they'll stop feeding you.
Meaning: To harm or show ingratitude to someone who helps you.

"DON'T PUT ALL YOUR EGGS IN ONE BASKET"
Logic: If the basket falls, you lose all eggs — diversify!
Meaning: Don't risk everything on one outcome.

"A BIRD IN THE HAND IS WORTH TWO IN THE BUSH"
Logic: A bird you're holding is more valuable than two you might catch.
Meaning: It's better to be content with what you have than to risk it for more.
```

### 4.3 Phrasal Verbs as Idioms

**Phrasal verbs** are verb + preposition/adverb combinations whose meanings differ from their individual words. They function as idioms.

```

Give up             Give something up    To stop / surrender / quit
Run into            Run while entering   To encounter unexpectedly
Break down          Break downward       To stop working / to cry / to analyse
Put off             Put away             To postpone / to repel
Turn up             Turn in upward dir.  To arrive / to increase volume
Call off            Call offward         To cancel
Fall out            Fall outside         To quarrel / to result
Come across         Come across something  To find unexpectedly
Get away with       Get away             To escape without punishment
Look into           Look inside          To investigate
```

### 4.4 Fixed Expressions

Some idioms are completely **fixed** — you cannot change any word. Others allow minor variations.

```
COMPLETELY FIXED (never change any word):
"Kick the bucket"     → NEVER "Kick the barrel" or "Boot the bucket"
"Break the ice"       → NEVER "Crack the ice" or "Break the snow"
"Bite the dust"       → NEVER "Eat the dust" or "Bite the soil"

PARTIALLY FLEXIBLE (article or tense can change):
"Burn bridges" / "Burn one's bridges" — both correct
"On thin ice" / "Walking on thin ice" — both correct
"Hit the nail on the head" → can become "You hit the nail on the head"

VERB FORM CHANGES ARE ALLOWED:
"She kicked the bucket." / "He will kick the bucket." — tense changes OK
"The deal fell through." / "Deals often fall through." — tense changes OK
```

## 5. MASTER TRICKS & SHORTCUTS

---

### 🚀 TRICK 1 — The Positive/Negative Direction Test

```
Even if you don't know an idiom's exact meaning, determine:
Is this idiom used in a POSITIVE or NEGATIVE situation?

Look for clues in the sentence:
Positive clue words: success, happy, wonderful, great, achieve, rewarded
Negative clue words: trouble, difficult, fail, problem, worried, struggle

Then eliminate options in the wrong direction.

"After months of hard work, the project finally hit the jackpot."
→ "hit the jackpot" must be POSITIVE (hard work + "finally" signals success)
→ Eliminate: failed, struggled, collapsed
→ Choose: succeeded greatly, struck luck ✓
```

---

### 🚀 TRICK 2 — The Origin / Logic Method

```
When you see an unfamiliar idiom in the exam, think:
"What would logically happen if this were LITERAL?"

"Don't rock the boat."
→ Literal: If you rock a boat, it becomes unstable and people might fall out.
→ Logical meaning: Don't disturb the current stable situation.
→ Meaning: Don't cause trouble or upset the existing situation. ✓

"Strike while the iron is hot."
→ Literal: A blacksmith strikes iron while it's hot because it bends easily.
→ Logical meaning: Act at the right time when conditions are favourable.
→ Meaning: Take advantage of a favourable situation immediately. ✓
```

---

### 🚀 TRICK 3 — Key Word Association

```
Many idioms contain KEY WORDS that hint at their meaning.
Train yourself to associate KEY WORDS → THEMES:

"BURN" → destruction, damage, ending, permanence
  burn bridges, burn one's boats, burn the midnight oil, crash and burn

"WATER" → trouble, difficulty, survival
  in hot water, water under the bridge, test the waters, tread water

"COLD" → unfriendliness, unexpected setback, caution
  give someone the cold shoulder, get cold feet, in cold blood

"FIRE" → intensity, rapid movement, danger
  under fire, play with fire, catch fire, set the world on fire

"HAND" → control, generosity, effort
  have the upper hand, give a hand, wash your hands of, bite the hand that feeds
```

```
Animal idioms almost always relate to character traits associated with that animal:

CAT → independence, curiosity, danger
  cat got your tongue? / let the cat out of the bag / kill two birds...wait that's bird
  raining cats and dogs / no room to swing a cat / cat on a hot tin roof

DOG → loyalty, hard work, difficulty
  dog-eat-dog / let sleeping dogs lie / sick as a dog / dog's life / every dog has its day

FISH → out of place, plentiful, suspicious
  fish out of water / plenty of fish in the sea / smell fishy / bigger fish to fry

HORSE → power, stubbornness, effort
  straight from the horse's mouth / don't look a gift horse in the mouth /
  hold your horses / a dark horse / flog a dead horse

WOLF → deception, greed, danger
  cry wolf / wolf in sheep's clothing / keep the wolf from the door / throw to the wolves
```

### 🚀 TRICK 7 — The "Same Situation" Grouping

```
Group idioms that mean THE SAME THING together.
When you learn one, learn the whole group:

→ bite the dust / fall through / go up in smoke / come to nothing /
  blow up in one's face / come a cropper / come to grief / go belly up

→ in hot water / in a tight spot / in a pickle / in a fix / in a bind /
  between a rock and a hard place / up a creek (without a paddle) /
  in the doghouse / out on a limb

ALL MEAN "TO REVEAL A SECRET":
→ let the cat out of the bag / spill the beans / blow the whistle /
  let slip / give the game away / tip someone off

ALL MEAN "IMMEDIATELY/VERY QUICKLY":
→ at the drop of a hat / in a flash / in the blink of an eye /
  in no time / before you can say Jack Robinson / posthaste
```

### 🚀 TRICK 8 — The Elimination Method for MCQs

```
In a 4-option multiple choice question about idioms:

STEP 1: Determine if the idiom is POSITIVE or NEGATIVE from context.
STEP 2: Eliminate options in the WRONG direction (usually removes 2).
STEP 3: From remaining 2, use common sense or the origin logic.
STEP 4: Choose the option that's most commonly associated with the idiom.

Example: "He passed the exam WITH FLYING COLOURS."
Options: (A) barely  (B) with distinction  (C) accidentally  (D) with difficulty
→ "Flying colours" sounds POSITIVE (bright, victorious)
→ Eliminate (A) barely, (D) with difficulty
→ Between (B) distinction and (C) accidentally
→ Flying colours → victorious performance → (B) with distinction ✓
```

### 🚀 TRICK 10 — The "Fill the Blank" Strategy for Idioms

```
When a sentence has a blank that requires completing an idiom:

STEP 1: Identify the partial idiom provided.
STEP 2: Recall which words complete that idiom.
STEP 3: Look for the completing word in the options.

"She was working against the _______ to meet the deadline."
→ "Working against the ___" → "clock" (working against the clock = racing time)
→ Options: (A) time (B) clock (C) watch (D) hour → Answer: (B) clock ✓

"Don't count your chickens before they _______."
→ "Count chickens before they ___" → "hatch" (don't assume success early)
→ Options: (A) grow (B) hatch (C) run (D) eat → Answer: (B) hatch ✓
```

## 10. COMMON MISTAKE ALERTS

---

### ⚠️ MISTAKE 1 — Literal Interpretation

```
WRONG: "She let the cat out of the bag" → thinking about a real cat in a real bag.
RIGHT: "Let the cat out of the bag" = Accidentally revealed a secret.
→ NEVER interpret idioms literally. The meaning is ALWAYS figurative.
```

---

### ⚠️ MISTAKE 2 — Changing Fixed Words

```
WRONG: "He beat around the tree." (tree instead of bush)
RIGHT: "He beat around the bush."
→ Idioms have FIXED words. Changing even one word destroys the idiom.
Other examples of wrong substitutions:
✗ "Pull someone's arm" → ✓ "Pull someone's leg"
✗ "Spill the coffee" → ✓ "Spill the beans"
✗ "Bite the nail" → ✓ "Bite the bullet"
```

---

### ⚠️ MISTAKE 3 — Using Idioms in Wrong Context

```
WRONG: "The teacher was under the weather, so she explained the topic clearly."
       (Under the weather = ill → can't explain clearly)
RIGHT: "The teacher was under the weather, so she couldn't come to class."
→ Always check: does the idiom make LOGICAL SENSE in the sentence?
```

---

### ⚠️ MISTAKE 4 — Confusing Similar-Sounding Idioms

```
"Bite the dust" (fail/die) ≠ "Bite the bullet" (endure bravely)
"Spill the beans" (reveal secret) ≠ "Bean counter" (someone obsessed with money)
"Hit the nail on the head" (exactly right) ≠ "Hit below the belt" (act unfairly)
"Break the ice" (ease tension) ≠ "On thin ice" (in a risky situation)
"Pull someone's leg" (joke) ≠ "Pull strings" (use influence)
→ Focus on the KEY WORD that distinguishes each idiom.
```

---

### ⚠️ MISTAKE 5 — Confusing Idiom with Literal Phrase

```
"She was caught red-handed." (idiom: caught in the act)
vs
"Her hands were painted red." (literal: hands with red paint)

→ The presence of similar words doesn't make something an idiom.
   The idiom must have a fixed, established, non-literal meaning.
```

---

### ⚠️ MISTAKE 6 — Using Formal Idioms in Informal Contexts (and Vice Versa)

```
"He kicked the bucket" = too informal for formal writing
Better in formal context: "He passed away" or "He died"

"She was under the weather" = acceptable in semi-formal contexts
"I'm feeling a bit poorly" = British informal

→ Match the idiom's register with the context of the sentence.
```

---

### ⚠️ MISTAKE 7 — Forgetting Direction (Positive/Negative)

```
WRONG: "He passed the exam by the skin of his teeth, achieving an excellent result."
       (by the skin of your teeth = barely, narrowly — NOT excellently)
RIGHT: "He passed the exam by the skin of his teeth — he almost failed."

→ Always verify: is this idiom positive, negative, or neutral?
   Then check if the sentence's meaning matches that direction.
```

### SET A — MEANING OF IDIOMS (EASY)

**A1. (B)** Beat around the bush = to avoid coming to the main point; to be indirect or evasive.

**A2. (C)** Under the weather = feeling slightly ill or unwell. Origin: sailors going below deck when seasick.

**A3. (C)** Break the ice = to do or say something to ease tension and start a conversation in an awkward social situation.

**A4. (B)** Bite the bullet = to endure a painful or difficult situation with courage. Origin: soldiers biting bullets during surgery before anaesthesia.

**A5. (D)** Kick the bucket = to die. A colloquial euphemism.

**A6. (B)** Burn the midnight oil = to work or study very late into the night. Origin: before electricity, people used oil lamps late at night.

**A7. (B)** Cost an arm and a leg = to be extremely expensive. Origin: portrait painters charged per limb included.

**A8. (C)** Let the cat out of the bag = to accidentally reveal a secret or surprise.

**A9. (B)** Once in a blue moon = very rarely; a "blue moon" (second full moon in a month) is rare.

## 🏆 TOP 15 EXAM TIPS

1. **Never interpret literally** — The moment you see a well-known idiom, go straight to its figurative meaning. Don't let the literal words mislead you.

2. **Direction is your fastest tool** — Before reading all options, decide: is this idiom used in a positive or negative context? Eliminate wrong-direction options immediately.

3. **Learn idioms in same-meaning groups** — Knowing that "bite the dust," "fall through," "go up in smoke," and "throw in the towel" all relate to failure means learning 4 idioms with the effort of 1.

4. **Use origin/logic for unfamiliar idioms** — Think: "What would happen LITERALLY if this situation were real?" The logical outcome usually maps to the idiomatic meaning.

5. **Colour idioms are reliable** — Memorise the colour-to-theme associations. Red = danger/debt, Blue = sadness/rarity, Black = evil/finance, White = deception/uselessness, Green = envy/permission.

6. **Animal idioms reveal character** — Cat = mischief/secrets, Dog = difficulty/loyalty, Horse = directness/stubbornness, Wolf = deception, Fish = awkwardness. Use this to predict unfamiliar animal idioms.

7. **Phrasal verbs are idioms too** — "Fall through" doesn't mean falling physically through something — it means failing to happen. Apply the same non-literal approach to phrasal verbs.

8. **Fixed words = zero substitution** — "Beat around the BUSH" is never "beat around the TREE." One wrong word destroys the idiom and makes it meaningless in an exam.

9. **Context verification** — After choosing an answer, substitute it into the sentence and read it. Does the idiom make logical sense in that context? If not, reconsider.

10. **Similar-sounding idioms are traps** — "Bite the BULLET" (endure bravely) vs "Bite the DUST" (fail). Focus on the KEY distinguishing word.

11. **Tier 1 idioms first** — The 25 Tier 1 idioms in this guide appear in almost every competitive exam. Master these before moving to less common idioms.

12. **The positive/negative check for usage questions** — In "find the correct usage" questions, eliminate sentences where the idiom's positive/negative direction contradicts the sentence's overall meaning.

13. **Proverbs are standalone; idioms are not** — Idioms must be embedded in a sentence. "Bite the bullet" can't stand alone; "Actions speak louder than words" can.

14. **Phrasal verb separability** — Some phrasal verbs can be separated: "Call it off" = "Call off the match." Both are correct. Don't confuse separated phrasal verbs with random words.

15. **Build 10 idioms per day using theme clusters** — Study 1 theme (e.g., body parts) per day. By the end of 3 weeks, you'll have covered 200+ idioms systematically with connections between them.
