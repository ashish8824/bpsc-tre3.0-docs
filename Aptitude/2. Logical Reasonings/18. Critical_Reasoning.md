## 1. WHAT IS CRITICAL REASONING?

**Critical Reasoning (CR)** tests your ability to analyze, evaluate, and draw conclusions from arguments. Unlike reading comprehension (where you understand what's written), CR tests whether you can **think about how the argument is structured** — what assumptions it makes, what would strengthen or weaken it, and whether its reasoning is logically sound.

---

### Where CR Appears:

```
→ CAT / XAT / GMAT      → 10–15 questions (very high weight)
→ IBPS PO               → 5–10 questions per paper
→ SBI PO                → 5–10 questions per paper
→ SSC CGL Tier II       → 5–8 questions
→ UPSC CSAT             → 10–15 questions
→ Bank PO Exams         → 5–10 questions per section
→ RBI Grade B           → 10–12 questions
→ Insurance PO/Clerk    → 5–8 questions
→ MBA Entrance Exams    → High focus topic

TOTAL IMPACT: In bank/government exams, 5–15 marks directly from CR.
In MBA exams, CR can be 30–40% of verbal section marks.
```

---

### What CR Tests:

```
Critical Reasoning tests THREE core skills:

SKILL 1 — ANALYSIS:
   Can you break an argument into its parts?
   (Premise, Conclusion, Assumption, Gap)

SKILL 2 — EVALUATION:
   Can you assess whether an argument is strong or weak?
   Can you find its flaw or strengthen/weaken it?

SKILL 3 — SYNTHESIS:
   Can you draw valid inferences from given information?
   Can you complete a chain of reasoning?

YOU ARE NOT TESTED ON:
→ Your personal knowledge of the topic
→ Your opinion on the subject matter
→ Facts outside the passage
→ Common sense about the real world (unless it's a logical necessity)
```

---

### The Fundamental Difference: CR vs RC:

```
╔══════════════════════════════════╦══════════════════════════════════╗
║  READING COMPREHENSION (RC)      ║  CRITICAL REASONING (CR)         ║
╠══════════════════════════════════╬══════════════════════════════════╣
║ Long passage, 4–8 questions      ║ Short argument, 1 question       ║
║ What does the author SAY?        ║ How does the author REASON?       ║
║ Find information in the text     ║ Analyze the logic structure      ║
║ Author's view, tone, purpose     ║ Premise → Gap → Conclusion       ║
║ Outside knowledge irrelevant     ║ Logic, not topic knowledge       ║
╚══════════════════════════════════╩══════════════════════════════════╝

CR GOLDEN PRINCIPLE: The argument is the universe. 
Stay inside the argument. Don't bring in outside information.
```

## 2. ANATOMY OF AN ARGUMENT

Every CR question involves an **argument**. You must know its parts perfectly.

---

### The Three Parts of Every Argument:

```
╔═════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════╗
║                                                                     ║
║  PREMISE(S) ──────────► GAP (Assumption) ──────────► CONCLUSION    ║
║                                                                     ║
║  PREMISE = Evidence, facts, data given to support the conclusion   ║
║  ASSUMPTION = Unstated belief that connects premise to conclusion  ║
║  CONCLUSION = What the argument is trying to prove                 ║
║                                                                     ║
╚═════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════╝

ANALOGY:
Think of an argument as a BRIDGE:
→ Premise = The riverbank on one side (where you start)
→ Conclusion = The riverbank on the other side (where you want to go)
→ Assumption = The bridge planks (unstated supports holding it together)

If the bridge planks (assumptions) are weak → argument is weak.
If you ADD planks (strengthen) → argument gets stronger.
If you REMOVE planks (weaken) → argument collapses.
```

---

### Detailed Component Breakdown:

```
PREMISES:
→ Facts, data, statistics, observations, research findings
→ Things accepted as true for the argument's purpose
→ Usually introduced by: "because," "since," "given that," 
  "as," "for the reason that," "evidence shows," "studies indicate"
→ You CANNOT attack premises — they are given facts

CONCLUSION:
→ What the arguer is trying to prove or recommend
→ A claim or judgment drawn FROM the premises
→ Usually introduced by: "therefore," "thus," "hence," "so,"
  "consequently," "it follows that," "we can conclude," 
  "this shows," "clearly," "obviously," "this means"
→ Can be a PREDICTION, RECOMMENDATION, JUDGMENT, or CAUSAL CLAIM

ASSUMPTION:
→ An unstated premise that MUST be true for the argument to work
→ Bridges the gap between premises and conclusion
→ If the assumption is false, the conclusion does NOT follow
→ The assumption is NEVER stated in the argument (if it were stated, 
  it would be a premise, not an assumption)
→ Key insight: The assumption fills the LOGICAL GAP in the argument

BACKGROUND INFORMATION:
→ Context-setting information that doesn't directly support the conclusion
→ Often appears at the beginning of the passage
→ Provides necessary context but isn't a premise per se

COUNTER-PREMISE:
→ A fact that seems to go against the conclusion
→ Usually followed by "but," "however," "although," "despite"
→ The author acknowledges it and then argues against it
```

---

### Identifying the Conclusion — The Most Critical Skill:

```
THE MOST IMPORTANT SKILL IN CR:
Finding the CONCLUSION is the first step for EVERY question type.

METHOD 1: KEYWORD SPOTTING
Look for conclusion indicators:
→ "Therefore..." → "Thus..." → "Hence..." → "So..."
→ "This shows..." → "This suggests..." → "Clearly..."
→ "It follows that..." → "We can conclude..." → "Obviously..."
→ "As a result..." → "Consequently..." → "This means..."

METHOD 2: THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
Read each sentence and ask: "So what?"
The answer to "So what?" is usually the conclusion.
The sentences that answer "Why?" are usually premises.

METHOD 3: THE "BECAUSE" TEST
Put "because" between sentences. The sentence AFTER "because" = premise.
The sentence BEFORE "because" = conclusion.
Example: "Sales have increased [CONCLUSION] BECAUSE we launched a new product [PREMISE]."

METHOD 4: SUPPORT DIRECTION TEST
Ask: "Which sentence does the rest of the argument support?"
The sentence that all others are supporting = CONCLUSION.

METHOD 5: LAST SENTENCE RULE
In most exam passages, the LAST SENTENCE is the conclusion.
(Not always, but a strong starting assumption.)
```

## 3. HOW TO READ A CR PASSAGE

---

### The 3-Read Method:

```
READING #1 (5–7 seconds): SKIM for topic and structure.
   → What is this about? (Topic)
   → Is there a "but," "however," "therefore" signaling a shift?

READING #2 (20–30 seconds): IDENTIFY structure.
   → Underline/circle the conclusion (marked with keyword or last sentence).
   → Box the main premise(s).
   → Note the GAP between premise and conclusion.

READING #3 (5 seconds): READ THE QUESTION.
   → Only now look at the question type.
   → This tells you WHAT you're looking for in the answer choices.

TOTAL TIME: 35–45 seconds per question (target).
With practice: 25–35 seconds.
```

---

### The PICO Framework:

```
P = PREMISE(S) — What evidence is given?
I = INFERENCE GAP — What is assumed but not stated?
C = CONCLUSION — What is being claimed?
O = OBJECTIVE — What does the question ask me to do?

Apply PICO to every CR question before looking at answer choices.

EXAMPLE:
Passage: "Our city has seen a 30% increase in electric vehicle registrations 
this year. Therefore, air pollution in our city will decrease significantly."

P (Premise): 30% increase in EV registrations this year.
I (Inference gap): EVs cause less air pollution than conventional vehicles; 
                   the increased EVs will actually be used/driven; 
                   other pollution sources won't increase.
C (Conclusion): Air pollution in the city will decrease significantly.
O (Question objective): Depends on question asked (weaken/strengthen/assume/etc.)
```

---

### Practice: Identifying Premise and Conclusion:

```
EXAMPLE 1:
"[All employees who met their targets received bonuses] [PREMISE].
[Ravi received a bonus] [PREMISE].
[Therefore, Ravi met his targets] [CONCLUSION]."

Note: This argument has a FLAW — Ravi might have received a bonus 
for another reason. Not all bonus recipients necessarily met targets.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

EXAMPLE 2:
"[Since the new drug showed 90% effectiveness in trials] [PREMISE]
and [fewer side effects than existing treatments] [PREMISE],
[it should be approved for widespread use] [CONCLUSION]."

GAP/ASSUMPTION: Trial results reflect real-world performance;
the drug is safe enough for wide distribution; no other factors 
prevent approval.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

EXAMPLE 3:
"[Although our competitors reduced prices] [COUNTER-PREMISE],
[our sales increased by 20% this quarter] [PREMISE].
[Clearly, our quality improvements are paying off] [CONCLUSION]."

Note: The argument attributes the sales increase to quality improvement,
ASSUMING that the quality improvement (not other factors) caused the increase.
```

## 5. TYPES OF ARGUMENTS

---

### Deductive Arguments:

```
DEFINITION: If premises are true, conclusion MUST be true.
The conclusion is GUARANTEED by the premises.
No assumptions needed — it's logically airtight.

FORM: 
Premise 1: All A are B.
Premise 2: X is A.
Conclusion: Therefore, X is B. (VALID — must be true if premises are true)

EXAMPLE:
P1: All mammals are warm-blooded.
P2: Dogs are mammals.
C: Therefore, dogs are warm-blooded. ✓

KEY: In deductive arguments, you CANNOT weaken the conclusion
without attacking the premises. The logic is airtight.

EXAM RELEVANCE: Rare in CR. Most CR arguments are INDUCTIVE.
```

---

### Inductive Arguments (The CR Focus):

```
DEFINITION: Premises make the conclusion LIKELY or PROBABLE,
but do NOT guarantee it. There's always a gap.

The conclusion goes BEYOND what the premises strictly prove.
This gap = ASSUMPTION.

FORM:
Premise: In samples 1, 2, 3, the drug was effective.
Conclusion: The drug is effective in general.

GAP/ASSUMPTION: The samples are representative; the drug works
consistently; other factors don't affect outcomes.

EXAMPLE:
P: Sales increased after we redesigned the product.
C: The redesign caused the sales increase.

GAP: Assumes no other factor (season, promotion, competitor action) 
     caused the increase. Assumes correlation = causation.

KEY: Most CR arguments are inductive. Your job is to:
→ Find the GAP (assumption questions)
→ Add evidence to close the gap (strengthen questions)
→ Punch holes in the gap (weaken questions)
```

## 6. TYPE 1 — ASSUMPTION QUESTIONS

**The most fundamental CR question type. Master this first.**

---

### What is an Assumption?

```
An ASSUMPTION is an unstated premise that MUST be true 
for the argument's conclusion to hold.

KEY CHARACTERISTICS:
1. NOT stated in the argument (if it's stated, it's a premise).
2. NECESSARY for the argument — without it, the conclusion fails.
3. Usually fills a LOGICAL GAP between premise and conclusion.
4. Does NOT need to be the ONLY assumption — just A necessary one.

THE BRIDGE ANALOGY:
Premise ────[ASSUMPTION BRIDGE]────► Conclusion
If you remove the bridge → the argument collapses.
```

---

### How to Find the Assumption:

```
STEP 1: Find the CONCLUSION (first and always).
STEP 2: Find the main PREMISE(S).
STEP 3: Identify the GAP between premise and conclusion.
         Ask: "What must be true, but isn't stated, for the 
               conclusion to follow from the premise?"
STEP 4: The answer to Step 3 = The Assumption.
STEP 5: VERIFY using the NEGATION TEST.

THE NEGATION TEST (Most Reliable Method):
→ NEGATE the assumption (make it false).
→ If the negated assumption DESTROYS the argument's conclusion 
  → the assumption IS necessary → it's a valid assumption answer.
→ If the negated assumption doesn't really hurt the conclusion 
  → it's NOT the right assumption answer.
```

---

### Question Stem Identifiers:

```
"The argument above assumes that..."
"Which of the following is an assumption made in drawing the conclusion?"
"The conclusion drawn above depends on which of the following assumptions?"
"The argument depends on assuming which of the following?"
"Which of the following is an underlying assumption of the argument?"
"The argument above assumes, but does not state, that..."
```

---

### Worked Example — Assumption:

```
PASSAGE:
"The Riverside city has built a new metro rail system. 
Since metro systems reduce traffic congestion in every city 
where they are built, Riverside's traffic will improve."

FIND THE CONCLUSION: "Riverside's traffic will improve."
FIND THE PREMISE: "Metro systems reduce traffic congestion in every city."
FIND THE GAP: The premise talks about metro systems in general.
              The conclusion is specifically about Riverside.
              What bridges this? The assumption that RIVERSIDE IS A 
              CITY WHERE THE METRO SYSTEM WILL ALSO BE USED/OPERATE 
              EFFECTIVELY.

More specifically, the argument also assumes:
→ Riverside fits the pattern of "every city" in the premise.
→ No unique factors in Riverside prevent the metro from working.
→ The metro system is complete and operational.

ASSUMPTION ANSWER CHOICE WOULD BE:
"Riverside does not have unique factors that would prevent 
the metro from reducing traffic congestion there."

NEGATION TEST:
NEGATED: "Riverside DOES have unique factors that prevent the metro 
          from reducing traffic congestion."
EFFECT: If this is true, then the premise ("works everywhere") doesn't 
        apply to Riverside → Conclusion collapses. ✓
This confirms it IS a necessary assumption.

COMMON WRONG ANSWER TYPES:
→ Too broad: "Metro systems are good for cities." (Doesn't specifically 
  address the gap)
→ Already stated: "Metro systems reduce congestion." (This is the premise!)
→ Too specific: "The metro will be built on time." (Somewhat related but 
  not the core logical gap)
→ Irrelevant: "Riverside has a large population." (Doesn't bridge the gap)
```

---

### Assumption Question Traps:

```
TRAP 1 — RESTATING THE PREMISE:
The assumption will NOT simply restate what's already in the argument.
If an answer just paraphrases a premise → WRONG.

TRAP 2 — TOO STRONG:
Assumptions are usually the MINIMUM needed to bridge the gap.
"No traffic problems will ever occur in Riverside" is too strong.
"Riverside's traffic problems are solvable" is more likely correct.

TRAP 3 — IRRELEVANT STRENGTHENERS:
Some answers strengthen the argument but aren't NECESSARY assumptions.
A strengthener makes the conclusion MORE likely.
An assumption is something WITHOUT WHICH the conclusion FAILS.
(Every assumption strengthens, but not every strengthener is an assumption.)

TRAP 4 — OUTSIDE SCOPE:
The assumption stays within the scope of the argument.
Don't choose an answer that introduces completely new concepts 
not relevant to the specific logical gap.
```

## 7. TYPE 2 — STRENGTHEN QUESTIONS

---

### What Does "Strengthen" Mean?

```
A STRENGTHENING answer makes the conclusion MORE LIKELY to be true.
It doesn't PROVE the conclusion — it just makes it more probable.
It supports the argument by:
→ Providing additional evidence FOR the conclusion
→ Eliminating alternative explanations
→ Supporting an underlying assumption
→ Closing the gap between premise and conclusion

NOTE: A strengthener is broader than an assumption.
Every assumption strengthens, but not every strengthener is an assumption.
```

---

### Question Stem Identifiers:

```
"Which of the following, if true, most strengthens the argument?"
"Which of the following provides the most support for the argument?"
"Which of the following, if true, would most support the conclusion?"
"Which of the following, if true, would best support the claim that...?"
"The argument is most strengthened by which of the following?"
```

---

### How to Strengthen:

```
STRATEGY 1 — SUPPORT THE ASSUMPTION:
Find the assumption. An answer that CONFIRMS the assumption strengthens.

STRATEGY 2 — ELIMINATE ALTERNATIVE EXPLANATIONS:
If the argument says "X caused Y," an answer that rules out 
"Z caused Y" strengthens the causal claim.

STRATEGY 3 — PROVIDE ADDITIONAL EVIDENCE:
New data, examples, or facts that support the same conclusion.

STRATEGY 4 — CLOSE THE LOGICAL GAP:
An answer that explicitly bridges the premise-conclusion gap.

EXAMPLE:
Passage: "Students who eat breakfast score higher on tests. 
          Therefore, eating breakfast improves academic performance."

GAP/ASSUMPTION: Correlation = causation. Breakfast causes the higher scores
                (not that high-achievers happen to eat breakfast).

STRENGTHENER: "Students who were given free breakfasts for a month showed 
               improved test scores compared to before."
→ This eliminates the alternative (high-achievers eating breakfast) 
  by showing breakfast → improvement, not the other way around. ✓

WRONG STRENGTHENER: "Breakfast is nutritious." 
→ True, but doesn't specifically bridge the gap to ACADEMIC performance.
```

---

### Strengthen vs Assumption — The Key Difference:

```
ASSUMPTION: Without this, the argument FAILS.
            It's NECESSARY for the argument.
            (Use: Negation test — negation destroys argument)

STRENGTHEN: With this, the argument is MORE CONVINCING.
            It's not necessary — the argument could stand without it.
            It ADDS support beyond what's already given.

MEMORY: 
Assumption = FLOOR (remove it, everything falls)
Strengthen = WALL (add it, the building is sturdier, but floor is still the key)
```

## 8. TYPE 3 — WEAKEN QUESTIONS

---

### What Does "Weaken" Mean?

```
A WEAKENING answer makes the conclusion LESS LIKELY to be true.
It creates doubt about whether the premises support the conclusion.
It doesn't need to DISPROVE the conclusion — just undermine it.

METHODS TO WEAKEN:
→ Attack the assumption (show it's false or questionable)
→ Provide an alternative explanation for the evidence
→ Show the evidence doesn't support the conclusion
→ Show a flaw in the argument's logic
→ Show a counter-example
→ Introduce a new factor the argument ignores
```

---

### Question Stem Identifiers:

```
"Which of the following, if true, most seriously weakens the argument?"
"Which of the following, if true, most undermines the conclusion?"
"Which of the following casts the most doubt on the conclusion?"
"Which of the following, if true, is the strongest objection to...?"
"Which would most damage the argument above?"
```

---

### How to Weaken:

```
GOLDEN RULE: Attack the ASSUMPTION, not the premise.
Premises are given facts — you cannot attack them.
The assumption is the vulnerable point.

THE ALTERNATIVE EXPLANATION ATTACK (for causal arguments):
If the argument says "X caused Y," find an answer that says 
"Z caused Y" (not X). This eliminates the causal link.

THE COUNTER-EXAMPLE:
An instance where the conclusion is false despite the premises.

EXAMPLE:
Passage: "Violent crime in Cityville fell by 30% after a new police 
          surveillance system was installed. Therefore, the surveillance 
          system is responsible for the decrease in crime."

ASSUMPTION: No other factor caused the crime decrease.

WEAKENER: "During the same period, unemployment in Cityville fell by 
           40%, which has historically been the strongest predictor 
           of crime rates."
→ This provides an ALTERNATIVE EXPLANATION — the crime fell due to 
  lower unemployment, not the surveillance system. ✓

WRONG WEAKENER: "The surveillance system is expensive."
→ This doesn't undermine whether the system reduced crime. ✗

WRONG WEAKENER: "Crime increased in neighboring City B."
→ Irrelevant — we're talking about Cityville specifically. ✗
```

---

### Strengthen vs Weaken — Side by Side:

```
╔════════════════════════════════╦════════════════════════════════╗
║  STRENGTHEN                    ║  WEAKEN                        ║
╠════════════════════════════════╬════════════════════════════════╣
║ Makes conclusion MORE likely   ║ Makes conclusion LESS likely   ║
║ Supports the assumption        ║ Attacks the assumption         ║
║ Eliminates counter-explanations║ Provides counter-explanations  ║
║ Adds evidence FOR conclusion   ║ Adds evidence AGAINST          ║
║ Closes the logical gap         ║ Widens the logical gap         ║
╚════════════════════════════════╩════════════════════════════════╝
```

## 9. TYPE 4 — INFERENCE QUESTIONS

---

### What is an Inference?

```
An INFERENCE is a conclusion that MUST BE TRUE based on the given information.
Unlike assumptions (which go INTO the argument), 
inferences come OUT OF the argument.

THE CRITICAL DIFFERENCE:
→ ASSUMPTION: Fills the gap GOING IN (what the argument takes for granted)
→ INFERENCE: What you can conclude COMING OUT (what follows from the info)

VALID INFERENCE CRITERIA:
1. MUST follow from the information given (not just possibly).
2. Does NOT require information beyond what's given.
3. Is NOT already explicitly stated (that would be a restatement).
4. The STRONGEST valid inference is typically the answer.

"MUST BE TRUE" TEST:
Can you draw this conclusion based ONLY on what's given, with certainty?
If YES → valid inference.
If NO / MIGHT BE TRUE → not a valid inference for these questions.
```

---

### Question Stem Identifiers:

```
"Which of the following can be properly inferred from the passage?"
"The passage implies which of the following?"
"Which of the following follows logically from the above?"
"Which of the following must be true based on the passage?"
"Which of the following conclusions can be drawn from the above?"
"Based on the information above, which of the following is most likely true?"
(Note: "most likely" is slightly softer than "must be true")
```

---

### How to Find the Correct Inference:

```
STEP 1: Read the passage and understand ALL the information given.
STEP 2: Look for COMBINATIONS of facts that yield a new fact.
STEP 3: For each answer choice, ask: "Does the passage GUARANTEE this?"
STEP 4: Eliminate:
   → Answers requiring outside knowledge.
   → Answers that MIGHT be true but aren't guaranteed.
   → Answers that are too extreme or too absolute.
   → Answers that directly contradict the passage.
STEP 5: Choose the answer that is NECESSARILY TRUE from the passage alone.

COMMON TRAPS:
→ "Could be true" vs "MUST be true" — exams test MUST, not COULD.
→ Answers that add new information not in the passage.
→ Extreme language: "always," "never," "all," "none" — usually WRONG.
→ Reversals: If A → B is given, don't infer B → A.

EXAMPLE:
Passage: "All students who scored above 90% received scholarships. 
          Priya did not receive a scholarship."

Valid inference: "Priya did not score above 90%."
(Contrapositive: If NOT scholarship, THEN NOT above 90%)

Invalid inference: "Priya scored below 50%."
(The passage only tells us she's not ABOVE 90%; she could be at 89%)
```

---

## 10. TYPE 5 — MAIN POINT / CONCLUSION QUESTIONS

---

### What It Asks:

```
Find the PRIMARY claim or the OVERALL conclusion of the argument.
The main point is the central thing the author is trying to establish.

DIFFERS FROM:
→ Not a premise (which supports the main point).
→ Not a background fact.
→ Not a counter-premise that the author then rebuts.

THE MAIN POINT MUST:
→ Be supported by all other content in the passage.
→ Not be simply a restatement of a single sentence.
→ Capture the ESSENCE of the entire argument.
```

---

### Question Stem Identifiers:

```
"Which of the following best expresses the main point of the argument?"
"The main conclusion of the argument is that..."
"Which of the following best states the author's overall conclusion?"
"The argument above is primarily concerned with establishing that..."
"Which best summarizes the argument's conclusion?"
```

---

### How to Find the Main Point:

```
STEP 1: Find the conclusion keyword or apply the "So What?" test.
STEP 2: Check: Do all other parts of the passage SUPPORT this?
STEP 3: Is it a CLAIM (not a fact)? Main points are claims, not facts.
STEP 4: Is it the MOST COMPLETE statement of what the author argues?

TRAPS:
→ An intermediate conclusion (supports the main conclusion, but IS supported by premises AND supports another conclusion) — not the MAIN point.
→ A premise that LOOKS like a conclusion.
→ A counter-claim that the author then refutes.
→ An overly broad statement that the argument doesn't fully support.

EXAMPLE:
Passage: "The health benefits of meditation are well-documented. 
          Regular practitioners show lower stress levels and improved 
          focus. Given these benefits, and the fact that employee 
          productivity is closely tied to mental health, companies 
          should incorporate meditation programs into their wellness 
          initiatives."

MAIN POINT: "Companies should incorporate meditation programs into 
             their wellness initiatives."
→ All other sentences support this recommendation.
→ It's a claim (not a given fact).
→ It's the ultimate "So What?" conclusion.
```

---

## 11. TYPE 6 — FLAW / ERROR IN REASONING QUESTIONS

---

### What It Asks:

```
Identify the LOGICAL WEAKNESS or ERROR in the argument's reasoning.
The flaw is the mistake in logic — NOT a factual error.

KEY INSIGHT: The flaw is usually in the ASSUMPTION being made.
When an argument makes a questionable assumption, that assumption 
IS the flaw.

COMMON FLAW TYPES (see Section 16 for full list):
→ Correlation vs Causation error
→ Circular reasoning
→ Ad hominem (attacking the person)
→ False dilemma (only two options when more exist)
→ Hasty generalization (small sample → big claim)
→ Straw man (misrepresenting an opposing view)
→ Appeal to authority (claiming X is right because an expert says so)
→ Slippery slope (claiming A → B → C → disaster without evidence)
```

---

### Question Stem Identifiers:

```
"The argument is flawed because it..."
"Which of the following identifies a flaw in the reasoning above?"
"The reasoning above is vulnerable to which criticism?"
"The argument above can be criticized on which of the following grounds?"
"The argument commits which logical error?"
"Which of the following points to the most serious flaw in the argument?"
```

---

### Worked Example — Flaw:

```
PASSAGE:
"Students who listen to classical music while studying report higher 
exam scores. Therefore, listening to classical music causes higher 
exam scores."

THE FLAW: Correlation vs Causation error.
The argument assumes that the correlation (classical music + high scores) 
means classical music CAUSED the high scores.

ALTERNATIVE EXPLANATION: Maybe students who study classical music tend to 
be more disciplined or come from academic environments — the discipline, 
not the music, causes the scores.

ANSWER CHOICE FOR FLAW QUESTION:
"The argument mistakes a correlation between two factors for evidence 
that one factor causes the other."

ANOTHER POSSIBLE FLAW ANSWER:
"The argument assumes that a third factor doesn't explain 
both the classical music listening and the high scores."
```

---

## 12. TYPE 7 — EVALUATE THE ARGUMENT QUESTIONS

---

### What It Asks:

```
Find the information that would MOST HELP EVALUATE (determine 
the strength/validity of) the argument — either to confirm or deny it.

EVALUATE ≠ STRENGTHEN or WEAKEN specifically.
Evaluate = "What do you need to KNOW to determine if the argument is valid?"

THE KEY TECHNIQUE: The Variance Test.
The correct answer will MATTER to the argument in BOTH DIRECTIONS:
→ If the answer to the question is YES → argument is stronger.
→ If the answer to the question is NO → argument is weaker (or vice versa).

The wrong answers either:
→ Only strengthen (but can't weaken) — not evaluative, just supporting.
→ Only weaken (but can't strengthen) — not evaluative, just attacking.
→ Are irrelevant — don't affect the argument either way.
```

---

### Question Stem Identifiers:

```
"Which of the following would be most useful in evaluating the argument?"
"To evaluate the argument, it would be most helpful to determine..."
"Which of the following would it be most important to know in order to evaluate...?"
"Which piece of information would be most relevant to assessing...?"
```

---

### Worked Example — Evaluate:

```
PASSAGE:
"Last year, our company spent 20% more on employee training than the 
year before. During the same period, employee productivity increased 
by 15%. We should therefore increase training spending even further."

EVALUATION QUESTION:
"Which of the following would be most important to determine 
in order to evaluate the argument?"

CORRECT ANSWER: "Whether the productivity increase was caused by 
the training, or by other factors such as hiring better employees 
or improving equipment."

WHY CORRECT: 
→ IF training caused the productivity increase → argument is strengthened.
→ IF other factors caused the increase → argument is weakened.
The answer works in BOTH DIRECTIONS = perfect evaluative question. ✓

WRONG ANSWER: "Whether employee satisfaction also increased."
→ Employee satisfaction doesn't directly affect whether MORE training 
  will improve productivity. Too tangential. ✗
```

---

## 13. TYPE 8 — RESOLVE THE PARADOX QUESTIONS

---

### What It Asks:

```
Given two apparently CONTRADICTORY facts, find the answer choice 
that EXPLAINS BOTH — resolving the apparent contradiction.

STRUCTURE OF A PARADOX PASSAGE:
Fact 1: X happens (seems logical/expected)
BUT / HOWEVER / YET
Fact 2: Y happens (seems to contradict X)

YOUR JOB: Find the hidden explanation that makes BOTH facts true simultaneously.

CRITICAL RULE: The correct answer must explain BOTH facts.
→ If it only explains Fact 1 → WRONG.
→ If it only explains Fact 2 → WRONG.
→ If it explains both → RIGHT.
```

---

### Question Stem Identifiers:

```
"Which of the following, if true, best reconciles the apparent contradiction?"
"Which of the following would help explain the discrepancy?"
"Which of the following best resolves the paradox?"
"Which of the following, if true, explains the seemingly contradictory findings?"
"Which fact would best explain the apparent inconsistency?"
```

---

### Worked Example — Resolve the Paradox:

```
PASSAGE:
"Happytown increased its police force by 50% last year. 
Yet, crime rates in Happytown increased by 20% during the same period."

PARADOX: More police → yet MORE crime? Shouldn't more police = less crime?

RESOLVING ANSWER: "Happytown's population grew by 100% last year due 
                   to a major urban migration."

HOW IT RESOLVES BOTH:
→ More police: As population doubled, the city NEEDED more police 
  and hired more (explains the 50% increase).
→ More crime: Double the population = more crime overall, even though 
  crime RATE per person might have fallen (explains the 20% increase).

WRONG ANSWER: "Police in Happytown are highly trained."
→ Doesn't explain why crime went UP despite more police. ✗

ANOTHER WRONG ANSWER: "Crime increased in the neighboring city too."
→ Doesn't explain why Happytown specifically saw more crime. ✗

KEY TRAP: Don't choose an answer that EXPLAINS the paradox by 
          making one fact irrelevant. Both facts must remain true 
          and make sense WITH the answer.
```

---

## 14. TYPE 9 — BOLDFACE / ROLE QUESTIONS

---

### What It Asks:

```
The passage contains two BOLDED sections (or specially marked sections).
Identify the ROLE each bolded section plays in the argument.

ROLES A SENTENCE CAN PLAY:
→ Main conclusion (the author's primary claim)
→ Intermediate conclusion (a conclusion that supports the main one)
→ Premise (evidence supporting a conclusion)
→ Background (context, not directly supporting)
→ Counter-premise (opposing view that the author rebuts)
→ Counter-conclusion (opposing view's main claim)
→ Assumption (the author's unstated belief — rare as a bold)

COMMON ANSWER CHOICE LANGUAGE:
→ "The first is a claim that the argument seeks to establish;
    the second provides support for that claim."
→ "The first is evidence against the position the argument defends;
    the second is that position."
→ "The first is an intermediate conclusion; the second is the 
    main conclusion."
```

---

### Question Stem Identifiers:

```
"In the argument above, the two boldface portions play which of the following roles?"
"The role of the boldface statement in the argument is..."
"In the passage, the highlighted claim functions as..."
```

---

### Worked Example — Boldface:

```
PASSAGE:
"Some argue that remote work reduces productivity because employees 
face more distractions at home. **However, studies show that remote 
workers typically complete tasks 13% faster than office workers.** 
Furthermore, companies that adopted remote work policies reported lower 
absenteeism. **Therefore, remote work enhances, rather than reduces, 
overall workforce productivity.**"

IDENTIFY ROLES:
BOLD 1: "Studies show that remote workers complete tasks 13% faster."
→ This is EVIDENCE (a premise) AGAINST the opposing view (remote work reduces productivity).
→ More specifically: It's a premise supporting the author's conclusion.

BOLD 2: "Therefore, remote work enhances overall workforce productivity."
→ This is the MAIN CONCLUSION of the author's argument.
→ "Therefore" confirms this.

ANSWER: "The first is evidence that supports the argument's main conclusion; 
         the second is that main conclusion."
```

---

## 15. TYPE 10 — COMPLETE THE PASSAGE QUESTIONS

---

### What It Asks:

```
The passage ends with a blank (______) or "...".
Find the answer choice that LOGICALLY COMPLETES the passage.

THE PASSAGE STRUCTURE:
Usually: Premises/Evidence → Gap → Blank (= Conclusion or Assumption)

YOUR JOB: Identify what would LOGICALLY FOLLOW from the given information.

TYPES:
TYPE A: Complete the CONCLUSION (the passage gives premises, you give conclusion)
TYPE B: Complete the EXPLANATION (the passage gives facts + "because...", you complete)
TYPE C: Complete the INFERENCE (what follows from the logic chain)
```

---

### Question Stem Identifiers:

```
"Which of the following most logically completes the argument?"
"The passage most logically ends with which of the following?"
"Which of the following would most reasonably complete the argument below?"
```

---

### Worked Example — Complete the Passage:

```
PASSAGE:
"Every employee at TechCorp has access to the company's training portal. 
The training portal contains modules on data security. 
Moreover, employees who complete the data security modules have 
never had their accounts compromised. Maya's account was recently 
compromised. Therefore, ______."

LOGIC CHAIN:
→ All employees have access to training portal.
→ Portal has data security modules.
→ Completers NEVER compromised.
→ Maya WAS compromised.
→ Therefore (contrapositive): Maya did NOT complete the data security modules.

CORRECT COMPLETION: "Maya did not complete the data security modules."

WHY: Contrapositive of "completers never compromised" = "compromised → not a completer."
Since Maya was compromised → she didn't complete the modules.

WRONG COMPLETION: "Maya should be fired."
→ This goes beyond what the logic chain supports. ✗

WRONG COMPLETION: "The training portal is ineffective."
→ The portal IS effective (completers are safe). Maya just didn't use it. ✗
```

# PART C — LOGICAL FALLACIES

---

## 16. THE 20 MOST TESTED LOGICAL FALLACIES

Knowing these fallacies helps you quickly identify FLAWS in arguments.

---

### FALLACY 1 — Correlation vs Causation:

```
ERROR: Assuming that because A and B happen together, A caused B.
EXAMPLE: "Ice cream sales and drowning rates both increase in summer.
           Therefore, ice cream causes drowning."
REALITY: Both are caused by a THIRD FACTOR (hot weather).

SIGNAL IN QUESTIONS: "After X happened, Y happened, therefore X caused Y"
WEAKENER: Any alternative explanation for Y.
STRENGTHENER: Evidence that rules out other causes; experimental data.
```

---

### FALLACY 2 — Hasty Generalization:

```
ERROR: Drawing broad conclusions from a too-small or unrepresentative sample.
EXAMPLE: "I interviewed 5 students in my school and 4 prefer online learning.
           Therefore, most students nationwide prefer online learning."
WEAKNESS: Sample size (5), representativeness (one school only).

STRENGTHENER: A larger, more representative sample shows the same result.
WEAKENER: The sample is unrepresentative or too small.
```

---

### FALLACY 3 — False Dilemma (Either-Or):

```
ERROR: Presenting only two options when more exist.
EXAMPLE: "Either we cut the education budget or the country goes bankrupt."
REALITY: There may be many other solutions (tax reform, cuts elsewhere, etc.)

SIGNAL: "Either X or Y" (and no other options seem to exist)
WEAKENER: Showing a THIRD option exists.
```

---

### FALLACY 4 — Ad Hominem:

```
ERROR: Attacking the PERSON making the argument instead of the argument itself.
EXAMPLE: "Dr. Smith's climate research is wrong because he's funded by oil companies."
(Even if the funding is suspicious, it doesn't make the research wrong on its merits.)

SIGNAL: "You shouldn't believe X because X is a liar/hypocrite/etc."
NOTE: The source's credibility CAN be relevant, but it doesn't automatically 
      disprove the argument.
```

---

### FALLACY 5 — Circular Reasoning (Begging the Question):

```
ERROR: Using the conclusion as a premise (the argument assumes what it's trying to prove).
EXAMPLE: "The Bible is true because the Bible says it's true."
          "X is the best candidate because no one is better than X."

SIGNAL: The "reason" is just a restatement of the conclusion.
IDENTIFY: Premise and conclusion say essentially the SAME THING.
```

---

### FALLACY 6 — Slippery Slope:

```
ERROR: Assuming that one event will inevitably lead to extreme consequences 
       through a chain of events, without adequate evidence.
EXAMPLE: "If we allow students to retake one exam, soon they'll want to 
           retake all exams, and eventually grades will mean nothing."

SIGNAL: "If A, then B, then C, then catastrophe" (often with "eventually," "soon")
WEAKENER: Show the chain of events is unlikely or won't occur.
```

---

### FALLACY 7 — Appeal to Authority:

```
ERROR: Accepting a claim simply because an authority figure endorses it.
EXAMPLE: "Dr. Famous endorses this supplement, so it must be effective."

NOTE: Authority can strengthen but shouldn't be the ONLY support.
      The authority must be relevant (a doctor's opinion on medicine = 
      more relevant than a celebrity's).

SIGNAL: "Expert/celebrity/authority said so, therefore it's true."
WEAKENER: The authority is not relevant to the field; has a conflict of interest.
```

---

### FALLACY 8 — Straw Man:

```
ERROR: Misrepresenting an opponent's argument to make it easier to attack.
EXAMPLE: "My opponent says we should reduce military spending. 
           Clearly, she wants our country to be defenseless."
(The opponent didn't say "no military" — just reduced spending.)

SIGNAL: A description of someone else's view that seems extreme or distorted.
```

---

### FALLACY 9 — False Analogy:

```
ERROR: Comparing two things as if they're similar when they're significantly different.
EXAMPLE: "We put a man on the moon; surely we can cure cancer."
(Space travel and cancer biology are fundamentally different challenges.)

SIGNAL: "Just as X worked for A, it will work for B" (when A and B are very different)
WEAKENER: Showing that A and B differ in ways that matter.
```

---

### FALLACY 10 — Post Hoc Ergo Propter Hoc (After this, therefore because of this):

```
ERROR: Assuming that because A happened BEFORE B, A caused B.
(This is a specific form of correlation-causation error.)
EXAMPLE: "I wore my lucky socks and then won the match. 
           The socks caused me to win."

SIGNAL: Sequence of events used as causal evidence.
WEAKENER: The timing is coincidental; other factors caused B.
```

---

### FALLACY 11 — Equivocation:

```
ERROR: Using the same word in two different senses within the same argument.
EXAMPLE: "Exercise is recommended daily. 
           Writing is an exercise in creativity. 
           Therefore, writing should be done daily."
(The word "exercise" means different things in each premise.)

SIGNAL: A key term that seems to shift meaning between premises.
```

---

### FALLACY 12 — Appeal to Popularity (Ad Populum):

```
ERROR: Claiming something is right/true because many people believe it.
EXAMPLE: "Millions of people believe this diet works, so it must be effective."
REALITY: Popularity doesn't determine truth or effectiveness.

SIGNAL: "Most people," "everyone believes," "popular opinion says..."
```

---

### FALLACY 13 — Red Herring:

```
ERROR: Introducing irrelevant information to distract from the main issue.
EXAMPLE: "Why should we worry about climate change when people are 
           dying from poverty right now?"
(Poverty is a separate issue — one problem's existence doesn't negate another.)

SIGNAL: A fact or argument that seems related but diverts attention.
```

---

### FALLACY 14 — Unwarranted Assumption (Hidden Assumption):

```
ERROR: The argument relies on an assumption that isn't obvious or warranted.
EXAMPLE: "We should hire more people like our top performer, Arjun."
ASSUMPTION: Arjun's success is due to his specific, replicable characteristics 
            (not luck, circumstances, or unique skills).

NOTE: Most CR arguments have unwarranted assumptions — this is what 
      ASSUMPTION questions are testing.
```

---

### FALLACY 15 — Composition/Division:

```
COMPOSITION ERROR: What's true of the parts is true of the whole.
EXAMPLE: "Every part of this engine is lightweight, therefore the engine is lightweight."

DIVISION ERROR: What's true of the whole is true of the parts.
EXAMPLE: "This is an expensive car, therefore every part in it is expensive."

SIGNAL: Moving from individual components to the whole, or vice versa.
```

---

### FALLACY 16 — Appeal to Ignorance:

```
ERROR: Claiming something is true because it hasn't been proven false 
       (or vice versa).
EXAMPLE: "No one has proven that ghosts don't exist, therefore ghosts exist."

SIGNAL: "There's no evidence against X, therefore X is true."
```

---

### FALLACY 17 — Survivorship Bias:

```
ERROR: Drawing conclusions from only the visible successes, 
       ignoring the failures (which are less visible).
EXAMPLE: "Most successful entrepreneurs dropped out of college. 
           Therefore, dropping out of college leads to success."
(We don't see the many dropouts who failed.)

SIGNAL: Evidence only from success stories; ignores the full picture.
```

---

### FALLACY 18 — Base Rate Neglect:

```
ERROR: Ignoring the general frequency of an event when evaluating 
       a specific case.
EXAMPLE: "This test for disease X is 99% accurate. I tested positive.
           Therefore, I almost certainly have disease X."
(If disease X is very rare — say 0.1% of population — a positive test 
still means you're more likely to NOT have it, despite 99% accuracy.)

SIGNAL: Percentages used without considering the base rate.
```

---

### FALLACY 19 — False Cause (Cum Hoc):

```
ERROR: Claiming that two events occurring together proves causation.
       (Closely related to correlation vs causation)
EXAMPLE: "Countries with high TV ownership have high literacy rates.
           Therefore, TV watching increases literacy."
(Both are caused by economic development — a third factor.)
```

---

### FALLACY 20 — Argument from Consequences:

```
ERROR: Claiming something is true (or false) because of the consequences 
       of it being true (or false).
EXAMPLE: "This theory of evolution can't be right because if it were, 
           it would undermine religious belief."
(The consequences of a theory being true don't affect whether it IS true.)

SIGNAL: "X can't be true because if it were, Y would happen."
```

# PART D — MASTER TOOLS

---

## 17. MASTER TRICKS & SHORTCUTS

---

### 🚀 TRICK 1 — The CONCLUSION FIRST Rule

```
ALWAYS find the conclusion BEFORE reading the answer choices.

WHY: Every question type (weaken, strengthen, assume, infer, etc.) 
     is built around the conclusion. Without knowing it:
→ You can't find what strengthens or weakens (it).
→ You can't find what it assumes.
→ You can't evaluate it.

HOW: 
→ Scan for "therefore," "thus," "hence," "so," "clearly," "consequently."
→ If no keywords: Last sentence is usually the conclusion.
→ Apply "So What?" test.

TIME SAVED: 15–20 seconds per question (by not re-reading to find it later).
```

---

### 🚀 TRICK 2 — The NEGATION TEST for Assumptions

```
The fastest and most reliable way to verify an assumption answer.

STEP 1: Negate the answer choice. (Make it false/opposite.)
STEP 2: Ask: "If this negated version were true, would the argument 
         break down (conclusion no longer follows)?"
STEP 3: YES → This IS the assumption. ✓
         NO  → This is NOT the assumption. ✗

EXAMPLE:
Conclusion: "The new software will improve employee efficiency."
Premise: "The software automates repetitive tasks."
Candidate Assumption: "Employees spend significant time on repetitive tasks."

NEGATE: "Employees do NOT spend significant time on repetitive tasks."
EFFECT: If employees barely do repetitive tasks, automating them won't 
        improve efficiency. → The conclusion collapses! ✓
→ This IS the assumption.

NEGATE a WRONG ANSWER: "The software is user-friendly."
NEGATED: "The software is NOT user-friendly."
EFFECT: Even if hard to use, it could still improve efficiency 
        once learned. Argument weakened but not destroyed. ✗
→ NOT the core assumption.
```

---

### 🚀 TRICK 3 — The VARIANCE TEST for Evaluate Questions

```
For EVALUATE questions, the correct answer works in BOTH DIRECTIONS.

TEST: For each answer choice, ask:
"If the answer to this question is YES → does the argument strengthen?"
"If the answer to this question is NO  → does the argument weaken?"

If YES to both → This is the evaluative answer. ✓
If only one direction matters → It's a strengthener or weakener, not evaluator. ✗

EXAMPLE:
Evaluate argument: "Our new marketing campaign will increase sales."

Candidate evaluative question: "Are the new advertisements reaching the 
                                  target demographic?"
YES (ads reach target) → argument strengthens ✓
NO (ads don't reach target) → argument weakens ✓
→ This IS a good evaluative question. ✓
```

---

### 🚀 TRICK 4 — The BOTH FACTS Test for Paradox Questions

```
Every answer choice in a PARADOX question must explain BOTH 
contradictory facts. Use this as your filter.

FOR EACH ANSWER CHOICE:
"Does this explain Fact 1?" AND "Does this explain Fact 2?"
Only if BOTH = YES → potential correct answer.

EXAMPLE:
Fact 1: Coffee shop X charges higher prices than competitor Y.
Fact 2: Coffee shop X has more customers than competitor Y.

For answer: "X is located in a wealthier neighborhood with more foot traffic."
→ Explains higher prices (wealthy clientele) ✓
→ Explains more customers (more foot traffic) ✓
→ Resolves paradox. ✓

For wrong answer: "X has a better coffee brand."
→ Explains higher prices (better product) ✓
→ Does explain more customers to some extent ✓
→ Could work, but weaker than the location answer which explains BOTH 
  facts more specifically and directly. Compare to find the BEST answer.
```

---

### 🚀 TRICK 5 — The SCOPE TRAP Elimination

```
One of the most common wrong answer types in ALL CR questions is the 
SCOPE TRAP — an answer that's related to the topic but OUTSIDE 
the specific scope of the argument.

IDENTIFY SCOPE TRAPS:
→ The answer mentions something from the real world that the passage doesn't address.
→ The answer is TRUE but IRRELEVANT to the specific logical gap.
→ The answer addresses a DIFFERENT issue than the one the argument is about.

EXAMPLE:
Argument: "Company X's profits fell this quarter because they launched 
            a poorly reviewed product."

SCOPE TRAP ANSWER for weakening: "Companies that launch poorly reviewed 
                                   products often face customer backlash."
WHY WRONG: This actually seems to SUPPORT the argument, not weaken it.
           And it doesn't specifically address Company X's situation.

CORRECT WEAKENER: "Company X's profits fell even before the product launch 
                   due to supply chain disruptions."
WHY RIGHT: Provides alternative cause specific to Company X's situation.

RULE: Always ask "Does this answer specifically address the CONCLUSION 
      about THIS argument?" Generic truths don't usually work.
```

---

### 🚀 TRICK 6 — The OPPOSITE ANSWER Quick Elimination

```
In WEAKEN questions, eliminate any answer that STRENGTHENS.
In STRENGTHEN questions, eliminate any answer that WEAKENS.

This sounds obvious, but in pressure situations, students often pick 
an answer that SOUNDS related but actually goes in the WRONG DIRECTION.

HOW TO CHECK DIRECTION:
After identifying an answer as relevant, ask:
"Does this make the conclusion MORE likely or LESS likely?"
→ MORE likely = Strengthener
→ LESS likely = Weakener

If the question asks for WEAKENER and the answer makes the conclusion 
MORE likely → ELIMINATE immediately.

TIME SAVED: Instantly eliminates 1–2 wrong options without deep analysis.
```

---

### 🚀 TRICK 7 — The CAUSAL ARGUMENT Attack Plan

```
Causal arguments ("X caused Y") are the most common type in CR.
They ALWAYS have the same gap and can ALWAYS be attacked/supported 
in the same ways:

TO WEAKEN A CAUSAL ARGUMENT ("X caused Y"):
1. Alternative cause: "Z (not X) caused Y."
2. Reverse causation: "Y caused X (not the other way around)."
3. Coincidence: "X and Y occurred together by coincidence."
4. Pre-existing condition: "Y was already happening before X."

TO STRENGTHEN A CAUSAL ARGUMENT:
1. Rule out alternatives: "Z, W, V (other possible causes) did NOT change."
2. Show mechanism: "Here's HOW X causes Y (the biological/chemical/etc. process)."
3. Experimental evidence: "When X increased in controlled conditions, Y increased."
4. Dose-response: "When X increased MORE, Y increased MORE (proportionally)."

APPLY THIS PLAN automatically when you see a causal argument.
```

---

### 🚀 TRICK 8 — The EXTREME LANGUAGE Filter

```
For INFERENCE questions especially (and most CR answers):

EXTREME LANGUAGE = RED FLAG that an answer is WRONG.

RED FLAG WORDS:
→ "always," "never," "all," "none," "every," "only," "impossible"
→ "proves," "definitely," "certainly," "absolutely"
→ "no X," "all X," "the only"

These strong claims are rarely supported by the passage, which 
usually gives conditional or partial evidence.

SAFE LANGUAGE (more likely to be correct):
→ "some," "many," "often," "usually," "can," "may," "might"
→ "in most cases," "typically," "tends to"
→ "it is possible that," "under certain conditions"

EXCEPTION: When the passage gives UNIVERSAL statements 
("All A are B"), then "All A are B" in an answer IS valid.
But if the passage says "most" or "some," don't infer "all."
```

---

### 🚀 TRICK 9 — The THREE-STEP ELIMINATION Method

```
For ANY CR question, when unsure between two answers:

STEP 1: Ask "Is this relevant to the SPECIFIC logical gap?"
         (Eliminates irrelevant/scope-trap answers)

STEP 2: Ask "Does this go in the RIGHT DIRECTION?"
         (Eliminates wrong-direction answers in strengthen/weaken)

STEP 3: Apply the NEGATION TEST or VARIANCE TEST or BOTH FACTS TEST
         depending on question type.
         (Confirms the final answer)

IF STILL STUCK: Choose the answer that is MORE SPECIFIC (less vague)
                and more DIRECTLY addresses the core logical gap.
```

---

### 🚀 TRICK 10 — The ARGUMENT MAP Quick Draw

```
When the passage is complex (multiple premises, sub-conclusions), 
quickly MAP the argument:

FORMAT:
P1 + P2 → [GAP/ASSUMPTION] → Sub-conclusion → [GAP] → Main Conclusion

EXAMPLE:
"Workers who learn new skills earn more. [P1]
 Higher earnings reduce poverty. [P2]
 Our new training program teaches new skills. [P3]
 Therefore, our training program will reduce poverty." [C]

MAP: P1 + P3 → [Workers who complete the program will earn more] → 
     Sub-conclusion → + P2 → [These workers will have less poverty] → 
     Main conclusion: "Program reduces poverty."

GAPS IDENTIFIED:
→ Workers will actually complete and use the training (P3 → sub-C)
→ The earnings increase is enough to escape poverty (sub-C + P2 → C)
→ Program results mirror general skill-earning correlation (P1 + P3)

TIME TO DRAW: 15–20 seconds for complex passages.
```

## 19. STEP-BY-STEP ATTACK STRATEGY

---

### Universal 6-Step Process for Any CR Question:

```
STEP 1 (5 sec): IDENTIFY THE QUESTION TYPE.
   Read the question stem FIRST (before the passage in some strategies).
   This tells you WHAT you're looking for.
   → "Strengthens" = Look for what closes the gap
   → "Weakens" = Look for alternative explanations or gaps
   → "Assumes" = Look for the logical bridge
   → "Infers" = Look for what MUST follow
   → etc.

STEP 2 (15–20 sec): READ THE PASSAGE with purpose.
   Based on the question type, read differently:
   → For ASSUME: Actively look for the gap.
   → For WEAKEN: Look for the argument's most vulnerable link.
   → For INFER: Note all facts carefully (every word matters).
   → For MAIN POINT: Find the ultimate "so what."

STEP 3 (10 sec): IDENTIFY P + C + GAP.
   Premise: What's the evidence?
   Conclusion: What's being claimed?
   Gap: What connects them (unstated)?

STEP 4 (5 sec): PREDICT the answer type (before looking at choices).
   For weaken: "I'm looking for something that provides an alternative 
                explanation or attacks the assumption."
   For assume: "I'm looking for something that, if false, 
                destroys the argument."
   Pre-prediction prevents the answer choices from misleading you.

STEP 5 (20–30 sec): EVALUATE EACH ANSWER CHOICE.
   → Read each answer.
   → Quickly eliminate clearly irrelevant/wrong-direction ones.
   → For remaining 2–3, apply the specific test for the question type.

STEP 6 (5 sec): SELECT AND MOVE ON.
   Pick the best answer. Don't second-guess for more than 10 seconds.
   Mark and return if truly uncertain.

TOTAL TIME: 60–90 seconds per question.
```

## 20. COMMON MISTAKE ALERTS

---

### ⚠️ MISTAKE 1 — Attacking the Premise

```
WRONG: In a "weaken" question, choosing an answer that shows 
        a premise is false or questionable.
RIGHT: Premises are GIVEN as true. You cannot weaken an argument 
        by showing a premise is false — that's not how CR works.

The argument's weakness lies in the LOGIC (assumption/gap), 
not in whether the premises are true.

FIX: Ask "Does this answer attack the LOGIC connecting premises 
     to conclusion?" not "Does this show a premise is false?"
```

---

### ⚠️ MISTAKE 2 — Choosing "Could Be True" for Inference

```
WRONG: For inference questions, choosing an answer that MIGHT be true 
        based on the passage, or that seems reasonable.
RIGHT: The inference MUST follow from the given information with certainty.

TEST: Can you draw this conclusion even if you ONLY have the 
      information given? If any doubt exists → wrong answer.

EXAMPLE:
Passage: "Most marathon runners train at least 5 days per week."
WRONG inference: "All marathon runners train daily."
                 (Passage says "most" and "5 days" — not all, not daily)
RIGHT inference: "Some people train at least 5 days per week."
                 (Certainly follows: marathon runners are people; some of 
                  them definitely train 5+ days = "some people" do too)
```

---

### ⚠️ MISTAKE 3 — Missing the Real Conclusion

```
WRONG: Treating an intermediate conclusion as the MAIN conclusion.

EXAMPLE:
"Regular exercise reduces heart disease. [P]
 Therefore, it improves health. [Intermediate conclusion]
 Hence, health insurance companies should offer discounts 
 to people who exercise regularly." [MAIN CONCLUSION]

Many students pick "exercise improves health" as the main conclusion.
The MAIN conclusion is the recommendation about insurance discounts.

FIX: The MAIN conclusion is what ALL other content supports.
     Intermediate conclusions support the main one AND are supported 
     by earlier premises. They're in the "middle."
```

---

### ⚠️ MISTAKE 4 — Adding Outside Information

```
WRONG: In "strengthen" or "weaken" questions, using real-world knowledge 
        you have about the topic to evaluate answer choices.
RIGHT: Evaluate only based on whether the answer choice (IF TRUE) would 
        affect the argument as written.

EXAMPLE:
Argument about a new diet plan's effectiveness.
You know from biology that the diet can't work as described.
WRONG: Eliminate answer choices based on your biology knowledge.
RIGHT: Take each answer at face value as "if true" and ask how it 
        affects the argument.

FIX: CR tests your LOGIC skills, not your topical knowledge.
     Pretend you know NOTHING about the topic except what's in the passage.
```

---

### ⚠️ MISTAKE 5 — Confusing Assumption and Strengthen

```
WRONG: Choosing a "strengthener" for an assumption question.
RIGHT: Assumption = NECESSARY condition (without it, argument fails).
       Strengthener = HELPFUL addition (with it, argument is better).

EVERY assumption strengthens, but NOT every strengthener is an assumption.

EXAMPLE:
Argument: "Our app's new feature will increase user retention."
Assumption: "Users find the new feature valuable." 
            (Without this, the whole logic collapses)
Strengthener: "User research shows high interest in the new feature."
              (This supports the assumption but is more specific evidence)

FIX: Apply the NEGATION TEST — strengtheners won't DESTROY 
     the argument when negated; assumptions will.
```

---

### ⚠️ MISTAKE 6 — Wrong Direction in Strengthen/Weaken

```
WRONG: Choosing an answer that weakens when the question asks to strengthen 
        (or vice versa). This happens under time pressure.

EXAMPLE:
"Which strengthens: The new parking policy will reduce downtown congestion?"
WRONG ANSWER CHOSEN: "Many downtown workers prefer driving to public transit."
(This suggests the policy won't work well — it WEAKENS!)

FIX: After reading each answer, EXPLICITLY ask:
     "Does this make the conclusion MORE LIKELY (strengthen) or 
      LESS LIKELY (weaken)?"
     State this aloud or write S/W next to each answer.
```

---

### ⚠️ MISTAKE 7 — Choosing Interesting but Irrelevant Answers

```
WRONG: Picking an answer that's interesting, surprising, or 
        "sounds right" but doesn't actually address the logical gap.
RIGHT: The answer must specifically address the CONCLUSION of the 
        SPECIFIC argument, not just the general topic.

EXAMPLE:
Argument: "Product A's sales declined after the price increase. 
            Therefore, the price increase caused the decline."
Interesting but wrong strengthener: "Product A is of high quality."
RIGHT strengthener: "No other factors (new competitors, seasonal trends, 
                     marketing changes) affected Product A's sales 
                     during this period."

FIX: Always ask "Does this specifically address the CAUSAL/LOGICAL 
     link the argument is making?" Generic topic-related facts aren't enough.
```

## 🏆 TOP 10 EXAM TIPS FOR CRITICAL REASONING

1. **Find the conclusion FIRST — always, for every question** — Every CR question type (assumption, strengthen, weaken, infer, etc.) requires you to know the conclusion before you can do anything else. The 5 seconds you spend finding it save you 30+ seconds of confusion later.

2. **Treat premises as sacred truths** — You cannot and should not doubt the premises in a CR argument. They are given as facts. All weaknesses in an argument lie in the LOGICAL CONNECTION between premises and conclusion — the assumption/gap. Attack the logic, never the evidence.

3. **Master the Negation Test for Assumptions** — This is the single most powerful tool in CR. Negate every assumption answer choice. If the negated version destroys the argument, you've found your answer. Practice this until it's reflexive.

4. **For causal arguments (the most common type), always look for the Alternative Explanation** — If an argument says "A caused B," the weakener is almost always "Z caused B (not A)" or "B caused A (not the reverse)." When you see a causal conclusion, immediately start thinking: what else could explain this?

5. **Never bring in outside knowledge** — CR is not a test of what you know about medicine, economics, psychology, or anything else. It tests pure logical reasoning. An answer that's "true in real life" but doesn't address the specific logical gap is WRONG. Stay inside the argument's universe.

6. **For inference questions, ruthlessly apply "MUST be true" (not "might be true")** — Students lose the most points here by choosing answers that "seem reasonable" or "could be true." Only choose what the passage GUARANTEES with certainty. Watch for extreme language (always/never) as a red flag.

7. **For paradox questions, test EVERY answer against BOTH facts** — Many students find an answer that explains one surprising fact and stop there. The correct answer must explain BOTH contradictory facts simultaneously. If it only explains one, keep looking.

8. **Practice recognizing argument types quickly** — Causal, analogy, statistical generalization, recommendation/plan, prediction — once you recognize the type, you know its standard flaw and how to strengthen/weaken it. This pattern recognition is what separates fast solvers from slow ones.

9. **Comparison is key for selecting between two "good" answers** — CR rarely has one obviously correct and four obviously wrong answers. Usually, 2–3 answers are plausible. When you're down to the final two, ask: "Which one addresses the CORE logical gap more DIRECTLY?" The more specific and central answer wins.

10. **Build a fallacy library in your memory** — Know the top 10 fallacies cold (Correlation≠Causation, Hasty Generalization, False Dilemma, Ad Hominem, Circular Reasoning, Slippery Slope, Straw Man, False Analogy, Survivorship Bias, Appeal to Authority). For flaw questions, you'll be able to identify the fallacy in under 10 seconds and match it to the answer description.

---

*Critical Reasoning is ultimately about logical thinking, not content knowledge. The arguments are unfamiliar to keep the playing field level — your ability to STRUCTURE your thinking is what's being tested. With consistent practice of the 10 question types and the three power tests (Negation, Variance, Both-Facts), you will solve CR questions faster and more accurately than most test-takers. Practice actively — don't just read arguments; DIAGRAM them. 💪*
