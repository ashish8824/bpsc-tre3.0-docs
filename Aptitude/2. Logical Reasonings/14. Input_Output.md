## 1. WHAT IS INPUT–OUTPUT?

Input–Output is one of the most scoring topics in competitive exams. A **machine** or **process** takes an initial input (a row of words, numbers, or both) and rearranges or transforms them step by step following a specific hidden rule. Your job is to **find that rule** and then answer questions about specific steps.

---

### Why This Topic Matters:

```
→ IBPS PO / Clerk    → 5 questions per set (1–2 sets per paper)
→ SBI PO / Clerk     → 5 questions per set (very common)
→ RRB PO / Clerk     → 5 questions per set
→ Insurance Exams    → 5 questions per set
→ SSC (occasionally) → 3–5 questions

ONE SET = 5 questions based on ONE paragraph of information.
Master the pattern → get ALL 5 questions correct.
That's 5 FREE marks per set if you know the method!
```

---

### The Basic Concept:

```
INPUT:   A row of words/numbers given at the start.
MACHINE: Applies a rule at each STEP.
OUTPUT:  What the row looks like after EACH step.
GOAL:    Find the rule → apply it → answer questions.

VISUAL:

INPUT:   [raw, disorganized words/numbers]
           ↓  (Step 1: machine applies Rule)
Step I:  [partially arranged]
           ↓  (Step 2: machine applies Rule again)
Step II: [more arranged]
           ↓  (Step 3: machine applies Rule again)
Step III:[further arranged]
           ↓  ...continues...
Final:   [fully arranged / no more changes possible]

The machine STOPS when no further rearrangement is needed.
```

---

### What the Exam Asks:

```
After giving you the Input and several steps, questions ask:

TYPE A: "What is Step III?" (Find a specific step)
TYPE B: "Which step gives output: [specific arrangement]?"
TYPE C: "How many steps are required to complete the process?"
TYPE D: "What is the final output / last step?"
TYPE E: "In Step IV, what is the position of word/number X?"
TYPE F: "Which element is 3rd from the left in Step II?"
```

## 2. HOW TO READ AN INPUT–OUTPUT PROBLEM

---

### The Paragraph Structure:

```
Every Input–Output problem follows this paragraph structure:

"A word and number arrangement machine, when given an input line 
of words and numbers, rearranges them following a particular rule 
in each step. The following is an illustration of the input and 
the rearrangement:"

Input:     word1 word2 word3 num1 num2 num3 word4 num4
Step I:    [arrangement after step 1]
Step II:   [arrangement after step 2]
Step III:  [arrangement after step 3]
Step IV:   [arrangement after step 4 — often the last step]

"And Step IV is the last step of the arrangement of the above input."
"As per the rules followed in the above steps, find out in each of 
the following questions the appropriate step for the given input."
```

---

### Reading Strategy (Before Solving):

```
STEP 1: Read the INPUT carefully. List all elements.
STEP 2: Compare INPUT with STEP I — What changed? What moved?
STEP 3: Compare STEP I with STEP II — Same type of change?
STEP 4: Compare STEP II with STEP III — Confirm the pattern.
STEP 5: State the RULE clearly in your own words.
STEP 6: Apply the rule to answer questions.

CRITICAL: Always compare CONSECUTIVE steps, not input with step III directly.
```

---

## 3. CORE TERMINOLOGY

```
INPUT:       The original arrangement of words/numbers.
STEP I:      The arrangement after the 1st operation.
STEP II:     The arrangement after the 2nd operation.
FINAL STEP:  The last step — the arrangement that cannot be 
             rearranged further under the given rule.

ELEMENT:     Any single word or number in the arrangement.
POSITION:    Where an element sits (1st from left, 2nd from left, etc.)
FROM LEFT:   Counting position from the left end.
FROM RIGHT:  Counting position from the right end.

ARRANGED:    Already placed correctly — the machine won't move it again.
UNRANGED:    Not yet in its final position — machine will work on it.

ANCHOR:      An element that is already placed and STAYS in position.
             Once placed correctly, it acts as an anchor.
```

## 4. TYPE 1 — WORD ARRANGEMENT (ALPHABETICAL)

The simplest and most fundamental type. Words are arranged alphabetically, one per step.

---

### Sub-Type 1A — Alphabetical from Left (A to Z):

```
RULE: In each step, the word that comes FIRST alphabetically 
      (among unplaced words) is moved to the LEFTMOST available position.

EXAMPLE:

Input:   market flower basket earn danger
Step I:  basket flower market earn danger    ← 'basket' is first alphabetically
Step II: basket danger flower market earn   ← 'danger' is next, placed 2nd
Step III:basket danger earn flower market   ← 'earn' placed 3rd
Step IV: basket danger earn flower market   ← 'flower' and 'market' already in order
                                               (This is the final step)

HOW TO IDENTIFY THIS PATTERN:
→ Look at the LEFT side of each step — words are accumulating in A-Z order.
→ Words on the LEFT don't change positions once placed.
→ The RIGHT side still looks jumbled until those words get their turn.

COUNTING STEPS:
For N words: requires at most N-1 steps (the last word falls into place automatically).
```

---

### Sub-Type 1B — Alphabetical from Right (Z to A):

```
RULE: In each step, the word that comes LAST alphabetically 
      is moved to the RIGHTMOST available position.

EXAMPLE:

Input:   market flower basket earn danger
Step I:  flower basket earn danger market   ← 'market' goes rightmost (Z→A)
Step II: basket earn danger flower market   ← 'flower' goes to 2nd from right
Step III:basket earn danger flower market   ← 'earn' goes to 3rd from right
Step IV: basket danger earn flower market   ← wait, 'danger' before 'earn' alphabetically
                                               so 'earn' should be right of 'danger'

Let me redo:
Input:   market flower basket earn danger
Alphabetical order: basket, danger, earn, flower, market

Z→A (rightmost first): market → flower → earn → danger → basket

Step I:  flower basket earn danger market   ← 'market' to rightmost
Step II: basket earn danger flower market   ← 'flower' to 2nd from right
Step III:basket earn danger flower market   ← hmm, 'earn' to 3rd from right
Actually:
Step I:  flower basket earn danger | market      (market placed)
Step II: basket earn danger | flower market      (flower placed)
Step III:basket earn | danger flower market      (earn placed? No, danger next)
Step III:basket earn | danger flower market      
         Wait — Z to A means: market(last alpha) first, then flower, then earn, then danger, basket.
Step I:  flower basket earn danger market   
Step II: basket earn danger flower market   
Step III:basket earn danger flower market   ← no change needed (already sorted!)

Let me redo cleanly:
Input:   market(5th) flower(4th) basket(1st) earn(3rd) danger(2nd)

Z→A arrangement to the RIGHT:
market is highest → goes to position 5 (rightmost)
flower is next → goes to position 4
earn is next → goes to position 3
danger is next → goes to position 2
basket remains at position 1

Step I:  flower basket earn danger market   
         (market moved to end; others shift left)
Step II: basket earn danger flower market
         (flower moved to 4th position; others adjust)
Step III:basket danger earn flower market
         (earn moved to 3rd position; basket and danger remain)
Step IV: basket danger earn flower market   ← Final (already sorted Z→A from right)

IDENTIFYING SIGN: Words build up from the RIGHT in reverse-alphabetical order.
```

---

### Sub-Type 1C — Shortest Word First (Left):

```
RULE: In each step, the SHORTEST word (fewest letters) moves to the leftmost position.
      If two words have same length, use alphabetical order to break the tie.

EXAMPLE:

Input:   market flower basket earn danger
Lengths: 6      6      6      4    6

Step I:  earn market flower basket danger   ← 'earn' (4 letters) placed first
Remaining: market(6), flower(6), basket(6), danger(6) — all 6 letters
Alphabetical among them: basket, danger, flower, market

Step II: earn basket flower market danger   ← 'basket' (first alphabetically among 6-letter words)
Step III:earn basket flower market danger   
...continues alphabetically for same-length words.
```

---

### Sub-Type 1D — Longest Word First:

```
RULE: In each step, the LONGEST word moves to the leftmost position.
      Tie broken by reverse alphabetical order (or first occurrence).

Identify by: Longest words accumulate on the LEFT first.
```

## 5. TYPE 2 — NUMBER ARRANGEMENT (ASCENDING/DESCENDING)

---

### Sub-Type 2A — Ascending from Left (Smallest to Largest):

```
RULE: In each step, the SMALLEST remaining number 
      moves to the leftmost available position.

EXAMPLE:

Input:   85  16  32  51  24  48

Step I:  16  85  32  51  24  48   ← 16 (smallest) to position 1
Step II: 16  24  85  32  51  48   ← 24 (next smallest) to position 2
Step III:16  24  32  85  51  48   ← 32 to position 3
Step IV: 16  24  32  48  85  51   ← 48 to position 4
Step V:  16  24  32  48  51  85   ← 51 to position 5 (85 falls into place)

PATTERN IDENTIFICATION:
→ Numbers accumulate in ascending order from the LEFT.
→ Already-placed numbers on the left NEVER move.
→ The machine picks the smallest from the remaining (right side).

SHORTCUT: After K steps, the first K positions are filled with 
the K smallest numbers in ascending order.

For 6 numbers: maximum 5 steps needed (last number automatic).
```

---

### Sub-Type 2B — Descending from Left (Largest to Smallest):

```
RULE: In each step, the LARGEST remaining number 
      moves to the leftmost available position.

EXAMPLE:

Input:   85  16  32  51  24  48

Step I:  85  16  32  51  24  48   ← 85 already leftmost? No...
         Actually: 85 is already in position 1. But the rule picks LARGEST.
         
Let me use: Input: 16  85  32  51  24  48

Step I:  85  16  32  51  24  48   ← 85 (largest) to position 1
Step II: 85  51  16  32  24  48   ← 51 (next largest) to position 2
Step III:85  51  48  16  32  24   ← 48 to position 3
Step IV: 85  51  48  32  16  24   ← 32 to position 4
Step V:  85  51  48  32  24  16   ← 24 to position 5 (16 automatic)

IDENTIFICATION: Largest numbers pile up on the LEFT.
```

---

### Sub-Type 2C — Smallest from Right / Largest from Right:

```
SMALLEST TO RIGHT: Numbers arrange in DESCENDING order from left to right.
(Each step: smallest number goes to the RIGHTMOST available position)

EXAMPLE:

Input:   85  16  32  51  24  48

Step I:  85  32  51  24  48  16   ← 16 to rightmost
Step II: 85  32  51  48  24  16   ← 24 to 2nd from right  
         Wait: 85  32  51  48  24 | 16  → 24 is next smallest among {85,32,51,24,48}
Step II: 85  32  51  48  24  16   ← Hmm, 24 is already 2nd from right...

Let me redo:
Input:   85  16  32  51  24  48
Ascending order: 16, 24, 32, 48, 51, 85

If smallest goes RIGHT: position 6=16, 5=24, 4=32, 3=48, 2=51, 1=85
Step I:  85  32  51  24  48 | 16   ← 16 to pos 6
Step II: 85  32  51  48 | 24  16   ← 24 to pos 5
Step III:85  32 | 48  51  24  16   ← wait, remaining: {85,32,51,48}; smallest is 32
Step III:85  51  48 | 32  24  16   ← 32 to pos 4
Step IV: 85 | 51  48  32  24  16   ← wait: remaining {85,51,48}; smallest is 48
Step IV: 85  51 | 48  32  24  16   ← 48 already at pos 3!

Result builds descending from left when smallest goes to right.
```

## 6. TYPE 3 — WORD + NUMBER MIXED ARRANGEMENT

The MOST COMMON type in modern bank exams (IBPS PO, SBI PO).

---

### Sub-Type 3A — Words Left, Numbers Right (One Each Per Step):

```
RULE: In EACH step:
  → The word that comes FIRST alphabetically (among remaining words) 
    is placed at the LEFTMOST available position.
  → The LARGEST number (among remaining numbers) is placed at the 
    RIGHTMOST available position.
  → Both happen SIMULTANEOUSLY in the same step.

EXAMPLE:

Input:   hope 45 big 72 sky 18 mug 36

Words: hope, big, sky, mug
Numbers: 45, 72, 18, 36

Alphabetical order of words: big, hope, mug, sky
Descending order of numbers: 72, 45, 36, 18

Step I:  big hope 45 sky 18 mug 36 72
         ↑                          ↑
         'big' (1st alpha) to left  '72' (largest) to right

Step II: big hope 45 sky 18 mug 36 72
         → 'hope' (next alpha) to 2nd position
         → '36' (next largest) to 2nd from right? 
         Wait: remaining words: {hope, sky, mug}; remaining numbers: {45, 18, 36}
         Next word: hope; Next number: 45

Step II: big hope sky 18 mug 36 45 72
         ↑↑                    ↑↑
         'hope' to pos 2       '45' to 2nd from right

Step III: Remaining words: {sky, mug}; Remaining numbers: {18, 36}
          Next word: mug (m before s); Next number: 36

Step III: big hope mug sky 18 36 45 72
          ↑↑↑            ↑↑↑

Step IV:  Remaining words: {sky}; Remaining numbers: {18}
Step IV:  big hope mug sky 18 36 45 72
          All placed! (sky and 18 fall into their final positions)

FINAL: big hope mug sky 18 36 45 72

PATTERN: Left side fills with words A→Z; Right side fills with numbers high→low.
The middle progressively empties.
```

---

### Sub-Type 3B — Words Left, Numbers Right (Ascending Numbers):

```
SAME AS 3A BUT numbers go in ASCENDING order to the right.
Step I: Alphabetically first word → left; Smallest number → right.
Step II: Next alphabetical word → 2nd from left; Next smallest number → 2nd from right.
...and so on.

RESULT: Left = words in A-Z order; Right = numbers in ascending order (smallest rightmost).
```

---

### Sub-Type 3C — Numbers Left, Words Right:

```
RULE: Numbers go to the LEFT (ascending or descending); 
      Words go to the RIGHT (A-Z or Z-A).

EXAMPLE (Numbers ascending left, Words Z-A right):

Input:   hope 45 big 72 sky 18 mug 36

Step I:  18 hope 45 big 72 mug 36 sky
         ↑                          ↑
         18 (smallest) to left      'sky' (last alphabetically) to right

Step II: 18 36 hope 45 big mug 72 sky
         ↑↑                    ↑↑
         36 (next smallest)     'mug' (next last alpha)

Step III:18 36 45 hope big 72 mug sky
         ↑↑↑           ↑↑↑
         45             'hope' ? Wait: remaining words: {hope, big}
                         Last alpha among {hope, big} = hope (h > b).
                         Hmm, Z→A: among hope and big, 'hope' comes later in Z→A.

Step III:18 36 45 big 72 hope mug sky
         45 placed      'hope' to 3rd from right

Step IV: 18 36 45 72 big hope mug sky ← 72 placed; 'big' to 4th from right

FINAL: 18 36 45 72 big hope mug sky
```

---

### Sub-Type 3D — Alternate Positions (Complex Pattern):

```
RULE: Numbers go to ODD positions (1st, 3rd, 5th...); 
      Words go to EVEN positions (2nd, 4th, 6th...).
OR any systematic position-based arrangement.

This is less common but appears in advanced exams.
Look for: Numbers and words alternating in the output.
```

## 7. TYPE 4 — SHIFTING PATTERN (LEFT/RIGHT SHIFT)

---

### Sub-Type 4A — Rotating/Shifting Left:

```
RULE: Each step, the FIRST element moves to the LAST position.
      (Like a left rotation — everyone shifts left by one.)

EXAMPLE:

Input:   A  B  C  D  E  F

Step I:  B  C  D  E  F  A    ← A moved from front to back
Step II: C  D  E  F  A  B    ← B moved from front to back
Step III:D  E  F  A  B  C    ← C moved to back
Step IV: E  F  A  B  C  D
Step V:  F  A  B  C  D  E
Step VI: A  B  C  D  E  F    ← Back to original! (6 steps for 6 elements)

SHORTCUT: After K steps, the element originally at position (K+1) 
          is now at position 1.
          After N steps (N = total elements): back to original.
```

---

### Sub-Type 4B — Rotating Right:

```
RULE: Each step, the LAST element moves to the FIRST position.

Input:   A  B  C  D  E  F

Step I:  F  A  B  C  D  E    ← F moved from back to front
Step II: E  F  A  B  C  D
Step III:D  E  F  A  B  C
...
```

---

### Sub-Type 4C — First Element Goes Last AND Last Element Goes First (Simultaneously):

```
RULE: Each step:
  → First element moves to LAST position.
  → Last element moves to FIRST position.

Input:   A  B  C  D  E  F

Step I:  F  B  C  D  E  A    ← F from last to first; A from first to last
Step II: A  C  D  E  B  F    ← Wait, this gets complex.

Actually more common pattern:
Step I:  F  B  C  D  E  A
Step II: A  C  D  E  B  F    ← A (was last in Step I) goes to front; 
                                F (was first in Step I) goes to back
Step III:F  D  E  B  C  A
...

This creates a "zigzag" collapse pattern common in IBPS PO new pattern.
```

---

### Sub-Type 4D — Specific Position Shift:

```
RULE: In each step, a SPECIFIC type of element (e.g., the largest number, 
      or alphabetically first word) shifts to a new specific position.

This is essentially the same as Types 1–3 described as "shifting."
```

## 8. TYPE 5 — DOUBLE OPERATION (TWO THINGS PER STEP)

This is the most common exam pattern in IBPS PO, SBI PO (2018 onwards).

---

### The Core Pattern:

```
In EACH step, TWO operations happen simultaneously:

OPERATION 1: One element from the UNPLACED portion goes LEFT (to next available left position)
OPERATION 2: One element from the UNPLACED portion goes RIGHT (to next available right position)

The machine works from BOTH ENDS simultaneously, filling inward.

VISUAL:

Input:   [e1  e2  e3  e4  e5  e6  e7  e8]

Step I:  [A   e2  e3  e4  e5  e6  e7  Z  ]   ← A placed leftmost, Z placed rightmost
Step II: [A   B   e3  e4  e5  e6  Y   Z  ]   ← B placed 2nd left, Y placed 2nd right
Step III:[A   B   C   e4  e5  X   Y   Z  ]
Step IV: [A   B   C   D   W   X   Y   Z  ]   ← Final step
```

---

### Detailed Worked Example — Double Operation:

```
RULE: 
→ Words: Alphabetically FIRST word → LEFT side (position 1, then 2, then 3...)
→ Numbers: LARGEST number → RIGHT side (last position, then 2nd last...)

Input:   basket 48 mango 65 flower 29 tower 82

Words: basket, mango, flower, tower
Numbers: 48, 65, 29, 82
Alpha order: basket, flower, mango, tower
Descending numbers: 82, 65, 48, 29

STEP I:
→ LEFT: 'basket' (1st alphabetically) → position 1
→ RIGHT: 82 (largest) → position 8 (last)
Remaining middle: 48 mango 65 flower 29 tower

Step I:  basket  48  mango  65  flower  29  tower  82

STEP II:
→ LEFT: 'flower' (next alphabetically) → position 2
→ RIGHT: 65 (next largest) → position 7 (2nd from last)
Remaining middle: 48 mango 29 tower

Step II: basket  flower  48  mango  29  tower  65  82

STEP III:
→ LEFT: 'mango' (next alpha) → position 3
→ RIGHT: 48 (next largest) → position 6
Remaining middle: 29 tower

Step III: basket  flower  mango  29  tower  48  65  82

STEP IV:
→ LEFT: 'tower' (last word) → position 4
→ RIGHT: 29 (last number) → position 5
Remaining: nothing

Step IV: basket  flower  mango  tower  29  48  65  82

FINAL: basket  flower  mango  tower  29  48  65  82

This is the LAST STEP (Step IV) for 8 elements (4 pairs).
```

---

### Variation — Largest Number Left, Alphabetically Last Word Right:

```
RULE:
→ LEFT: LARGEST number → leftmost available
→ RIGHT: LAST alphabetically (Z→A) → rightmost available

Input:   basket 48 mango 65 flower 29 tower 82

Descending numbers: 82, 65, 48, 29
Z→A word order: tower, mango, flower, basket

Step I:  82  basket  48  mango  65  flower  29  tower
         ↑  (82 placed left)                        ↑ (tower placed right)

Wait — when placing left and right simultaneously, the middle compresses.
Left side builds: 82, 65, 48, 29 (largest to smallest)
Right side builds: tower, mango, flower, basket (Z to A)

Step I:  82  basket 48 mango 65 flower 29  tower
         ↑ 82 placed at pos 1              tower ↑ placed at pos 8

Step II: 82  65  basket 48 mango flower 29  tower
         Hmm — but where does 65 come from? Let me retrace.

Remaining after Step I: {basket, 48, mango, 65, flower, 29}

Step II: 82  65  basket 48 mango flower  29  tower
              ↑ 65 placed at pos 2   ↑ mango placed at pos 7

Remaining: {basket, 48, flower, 29}

Step III: 82  65  48  basket flower  29  mango  tower
               ↑ 48 at pos 3  ↑ flower at pos 6

Remaining: {basket, 29}

Step IV: 82  65  48  29  basket  flower  mango  tower
              ↑ 29 at pos 4  ↑ basket at pos 5

FINAL: 82  65  48  29  basket  flower  mango  tower
```

## 9. TYPE 6 — MATHEMATICAL OPERATIONS ON NUMBERS

---

### Sub-Type 6A — Numbers Change by a Fixed Rule:

```
RULE: Each step, every number is transformed by a mathematical operation.

Common operations:
→ Add a fixed number (e.g., +2 each step)
→ Multiply by a fixed number
→ Square the number
→ Reverse the digits
→ Sum of digits replaces the number

EXAMPLE (Add step number × 5):

Input:   12  35  48  21

Step I:  (12+5)  (35+5)  (48+5)  (21+5)
         17      40      53      26

Step II: (17+10) (40+10) (53+10) (26+10)
         27      50      63      36

Step III:(27+15) (50+15) (63+15) (36+15)
         42      65      78      51

The pattern: Step K adds K×5 to each number.
```

---

### Sub-Type 6B — Digit Reversal + Rearrangement:

```
RULE: Numbers are reversed in digits AND THEN rearranged (ascending/descending).

Input:   45  32  67  18  93
Reversed: 54  23  76  81  39

Then arrange ascending: 23  39  54  76  81

This 2-step operation happens in ONE step of the machine.
```

---

### Sub-Type 6C — Sum/Product Operations:

```
RULE: Consecutive elements are summed/multiplied to create new elements.

Input:   2  3  5  8  13  (Fibonacci!)
Step I:  5  8  13  21  34  (Each = previous two sum)

RULE TYPE: Sum of adjacent pairs:
Input:   1  2  3  4  5
Step I:  3  5  7  9        (1+2=3, 2+3=5, 3+4=7, 4+5=9)
Step II: 8  12  16         (3+5=8, 5+7=12, 7+9=16)
...
```

## 10. TYPE 7 — CODING-BASED INPUT–OUTPUT

---

### The Concept:

```
Instead of rearranging elements, the MACHINE CODES each element 
using a transformation rule. 

Common coding rules:
→ Replace each word with its letter count
→ Shift each letter by N positions in alphabet
→ Replace each word with a number based on alphabetical position
→ Reverse each word
→ Take first and last letters only
```

---

### Example — Letter Count Coding:

```
RULE: Each word is replaced by the number of letters it contains.
      Each number is replaced by the number × 2.

Input:   basket 48 mango 65 flower

Step I:  6  96  5  130  6
         (basket=6 letters; 48×2=96; mango=5; 65×2=130; flower=6)

Then Step II might sort these numbers: 5  6  6  96  130
Or apply another rule to these numbers.
```

---

### Example — Alphabetical Position Coding:

```
RULE: Replace each word with the position of its FIRST letter in alphabet.
      A=1, B=2, C=3...Z=26.

Input:   basket mango flower tower

Step I:  2  13  6  20
         (b=2; m=13; f=6; t=20)

Step II might sort: 2  6  13  20 (ascending)
Which represents:   basket flower mango tower
```

---

### Example — Shift Coding:

```
RULE: Each letter shifts by 2 positions forward. Numbers stay same.

Input:   bat 23 hot

Step I:  dcv 23 jqv
         (b→d, a→c, t→v; h→j, o→q, t→v)

Step II: dcv 25 jqv  (23 + 2 = 25? Or some other operation)

## 11. TYPE 8 — NEW PATTERN (SBI PO/IBPS PO RECENT)

### Pattern A — Words Rearranged by Length + Numbers by Even/Odd:

RULE:

→ Numbers: Odd numbers go left (ascending); Even numbers go right (descending).

Looking at parity of word lengths and numbers separately.
```

### Pattern B — Swap and Replace:

```
RULE: In each step:
→ The 1st and last elements SWAP.
→ The 2nd and 2nd-to-last SWAP.
→ Continue inward.

Input:   A  B  C  D  E  F

Step I:  F  E  D  C  B  A   (Complete reversal in one step — this is just reversal!)

OR:

RULE: In each step, only the FIRST and LAST elements swap.

Input:   A  B  C  D  E  F
Step I:  F  B  C  D  E  A
Step II: A  B  C  D  E  F   ← Back to original after 2 steps!
```

### Pattern C — New 2023 Bank Exam Pattern (Number Operations):

```
RULE (Common recent pattern):
Step I:  Each number is added to the step number (or multiplied, or squared).
         Then numbers rearranged in ascending/descending order.
Step II: Operation applied again to the new arrangement.

EXAMPLE:

Input:   45  32  67  18  93

Step I:  (Add 10 to each, then sort ascending):
         45+10=55, 32+10=42, 67+10=77, 18+10=28, 93+10=103
         Sorted: 28  42  55  77  103

Step II: (Add 10 again, then sort descending):
         28+10=38, 42+10=52, 55+10=65, 77+10=87, 103+10=113
         Sorted descending: 113  87  65  52  38

IDENTIFY BY: Numbers change their VALUES (not just positions) between steps.
```

Input:   A  B  C  1  2  3

Step I:  A  1  B  2  C  3    (word, number, word, number alternating)
Step II: 1  A  2  B  3  C    (number, word alternating — flipped)
Step III: A  1  B  2  C  3  (same as Step I — oscillates!)

IDENTIFYING: Words and numbers alternate in a fixed pattern.

## 12. FINDING THE PATTERN — UNIVERSAL METHOD

This is the MOST CRITICAL skill. Master this and you can crack ANY Input–Output puzzle.

---

### The 5-Step Pattern Recognition Framework:

```
FRAMEWORK:

STEP 1 — IDENTIFY ELEMENT TYPES
   → Are elements only words? Only numbers? Mixed?
   → If mixed: separate words from numbers in your mind.
   → Note any special properties: odd/even numbers, word lengths.

STEP 2 — COMPARE INPUT → STEP I
   Ask these questions:
   Q1: What MOVED from its original position?
   Q2: Where did it move TO? (Left end? Right end? Middle?)
   Q3: Did anything move from LEFT to RIGHT?
   Q4: Did anything move from RIGHT to LEFT?
   Q5: Did VALUES change, or only POSITIONS change?

STEP 3 — COMPARE STEP I → STEP II
   Ask same questions.
   Check: Is the SAME TYPE of operation happening?
   (If Step I placed "basket" leftmost and Step II placed "flower" 2nd from left → YES, same pattern.)

STEP 4 — CONFIRM WITH STEP II → STEP III
   Verify your identified rule works perfectly.
   If ANY element doesn't fit your rule → REVISE the rule.

STEP 5 — STATE THE RULE EXPLICITLY
   Write it clearly: "In each step, the alphabetically smallest remaining word 
   moves to the leftmost available position AND the largest remaining number 
   moves to the rightmost available position."

NEVER PROCEED TO QUESTIONS without completing all 5 steps.
```

---

### Pattern Recognition Quick Checks:

```
CHECK 1 — LEFT SIDE ACCUMULATION:
Are elements building up from the LEFT end?
→ YES: Something is being sent TO the left each step.
→ WHAT: Is it alphabetically first? Shortest? Smallest number?

CHECK 2 — RIGHT SIDE ACCUMULATION:
Are elements building up from the RIGHT end?
→ YES: Something is being sent TO the right each step.
→ WHAT: Is it largest number? Alphabetically last? Longest word?

CHECK 3 — BOTH SIDES SIMULTANEOUSLY:
Are BOTH ends growing at the same time?
→ YES: Double operation (Type 5). Two things placed each step.

CHECK 4 — VALUES CHANGING:
Are the numbers DIFFERENT in each step (not just repositioned)?
→ YES: Mathematical operation being applied (Type 6).
→ WHAT OPERATION: Add? Multiply? Reverse digits? Check difference between steps.

CHECK 5 — WORDS CHANGING:
Are the words DIFFERENT (coded differently) in each step?
→ YES: Coding operation (Type 7).
→ WHAT CODE: Check relationship between input words and output symbols.

CHECK 6 — ROTATION:
Does the arrangement look like a circular shift?
→ YES: Rotation pattern (Type 4).
→ Is first going to last, or last going to first?
```

---

### Counting Anchor Elements:

```
ANCHOR ELEMENT: An element that has reached its final position 
                and NO LONGER MOVES in subsequent steps.

HOW TO IDENTIFY ANCHORS:
→ Compare 3 consecutive steps. 
→ An element at the SAME POSITION in all 3 steps = ANCHOR.

COUNTING ANCHORS helps determine:
→ How many steps are already completed.
→ How many more steps are needed.

RULE: In a typical double-operation puzzle with N elements,
      after K steps: 2K elements are anchored (K on left, K on right).
      After N/2 steps: ALL elements are anchored (if N is even).
      Total steps needed = N/2 (for double operation, N even).
      Total steps needed = (N-1)/2 (for double operation, N odd — last element auto-places).
```

## 13. MASTER TRICKS & SHORTCUTS

---

### 🚀 TRICK 1 — The Anchor Counting Trick (Save 50% of time)

```
SITUATION: You need to find Step 4 but only Steps 1-2 are given.

SHORTCUT:
Count anchored elements. 
In double-operation (2 placed per step): K anchors = K/2 steps done.

If Step 1 has 2 anchors, Step 2 has 4, Step 3 has 6...
→ After K steps: 2K elements anchored.
→ For N elements: N/2 total steps (if N is even).

FAST CALCULATION:
If you need Step 4 but only have Step 2:
Step 2 → apply rule TWICE MORE → get Step 4.
Don't recount from Input. Start from the most recent known step.
```

---

### 🚀 TRICK 2 — The "Which Elements Remain" Trick

```
SITUATION: "In Step III, what is the 4th element from left?"

SHORTCUT (for double-operation with words-left, numbers-right):

After K steps:
→ The first K positions from left = first K words alphabetically.
→ The last K positions from right = first K numbers (descending).
→ The middle = remaining unplaced elements (in their relative order from Input).

So for Step III (K=3) with 8 elements:
Position 1-3 = 3 alphabetically earliest words
Position 6-8 = 3 largest numbers (descending)
Position 4-5 = remaining 2 elements (in original relative order)

This lets you answer "what's at position 4?" WITHOUT doing all steps:
→ The 4th element = first unplaced element from original input (after removing the 3 placed words).
```

---

### 🚀 TRICK 3 — The "Step from End" Trick

```
SITUATION: You need to find what Step the arrangement [X Y Z ...] represents,
           given that you only see the final step and one or two other steps.

SHORTCUT:
Count the anchors in the given arrangement.
→ Number of left-anchors = steps done for words.
→ Number of right-anchors = steps done for numbers.
→ For double-operation: anchors on left = anchors on right = number of steps.

EXAMPLE:
Given: basket  flower  mango  _  _  48  65  82
3 words anchored left, 3 numbers anchored right = Step 3.
```

---

### 🚀 TRICK 4 — The "Sorting Shortcut" for Position Questions

```
SITUATION: "In Step V of the given input, what is the position of 'mountain'?"

SHORTCUT (alphabetical arrangement, one word per step):

First, sort ALL words alphabetically:
apple, danger, forest, garden, hope, mountain, river, time

'mountain' is the 6th alphabetically → it's placed at position 6 in Step 6.
If asking about its position in Step 3: it hasn't been placed yet (Step 3 places word #3 = 'forest').
In Step 3: mountain is in the right portion, at some position.
To find its exact position in Step 3's middle section:
→ Remove the 3 placed words (apple, danger, forest from left).
→ Find mountain's position among remaining: garden, hope, mountain, river, time.
→ mountain = 3rd among remaining → it's at position 3 + 3 = 6th from left in Step 3.

GENERAL FORMULA (for single-operation alphabetical):
Position of word W in Step K:
→ If W is among the first K alphabetically: it's at its sorted position (1 to K).
→ If W is NOT in first K: its position in Step K = its original position, 
   adjusted by how many of the first K words were originally to its left.
```

---

### 🚀 TRICK 5 — The "Last Step" Identification Trick

```
SITUATION: "How many steps does the process require?"

SHORTCUT:
For SINGLE operation (one element per step):
→ N elements → N-1 steps (the last element is automatically placed).

For DOUBLE operation (two elements per step):
→ N elements → N/2 steps (if N is even)
→ N elements → (N-1)/2 steps for the pairs; last step may place 1 element if N is odd.
   More precisely: ceil(N/2) steps.

EXAMPLE: Input has 8 elements → Double operation → 4 steps.
EXAMPLE: Input has 9 elements → Double operation → 5 steps (last step places 1 element in center).
```

---

### 🚀 TRICK 6 — The "Middle Element" for Odd Count Inputs

```
SITUATION: Input has ODD number of elements in a double-operation puzzle.

The MIDDLE element (the one that's not paired) is placed in the LAST step:
→ It simply stays where it is (can't move left or right — both sides full).

EXAMPLE: Input has 7 elements.
Steps: 3 double-operation steps (placing 6 elements) + 1 middle element remaining.
The 4th step? Actually with 7 elements and double operation:
Step 1: 2 placed (1 left, 1 right). 5 remain.
Step 2: 2 more placed. 3 remain.
Step 3: 2 more placed. 1 remains — this one auto-places in center.
Total: 3 steps. The 4th "step" isn't needed.

Actually: 7 elements → 3 complete steps (6 placed) + the center element is in place.
Step III is the final step if the center element is already correct; otherwise:
Step IV (partial): the 1 remaining element goes to center.
→ Total steps = (N//2) + (1 if N is odd and center needs to be moved, else 0).

Most exam puzzles use even numbers to avoid ambiguity.
```

---

### 🚀 TRICK 7 — Cracking the Rule in 30 Seconds

```
LOOK AT ONLY TWO THINGS:

1. What's at the EXTREME LEFT of Step I that wasn't there in Input?
   → This tells you what goes LEFT each step.

2. What's at the EXTREME RIGHT of Step I that wasn't there in Input?
   → This tells you what goes RIGHT each step.

EXAMPLE:
Input: hope 45 big 72 sky 18 mug 36
Step I: big hope 45 sky 18 mug 36 72

Left end: 'big' appeared (wasn't leftmost in Input)
Right end: 72 appeared (wasn't rightmost in Input)

Rule decoded in 10 seconds:
→ Alphabetically first word → LEFT
→ Largest number → RIGHT

That's it! Apply this rule to every subsequent step.
```

---

### 🚀 TRICK 8 — When Words AND Numbers Both Go Same Direction

```
SITUATION: Both words and numbers are arranged in the SAME direction.

EXAMPLE (Both arranged ascending/alphabetical from left):
Input: 45  hope  32  big  67  mango
Step I: 32  45  hope  big  67  mango  ← 32 (smallest number) placed first
Step II: 32  45  67  hope  big  mango ← 45 stays, 67 jumps to position... 

WAIT — This doesn't sort cleanly unless the rule is:
"Numbers in ascending order fill the left, words fill the right."
OR
"All elements sorted together (numbers and words interleaved alphabetically/numerically)."

IDENTIFY BY: Looking at whether numbers and words are MIXED in the final arrangement,
or if they're SEPARATED (numbers on one side, words on the other).
MIXED final = numbers and words sorted together.
SEPARATED = numbers and words sorted independently to different sides.
```

---

### 🚀 TRICK 9 — The "Remaining Middle" Quick Method

```
WHEN TO USE: "What does Step N look like?" where N is given but you 
             need to compute several steps.

FASTEST METHOD:
1. Sort words (alphabetically) into a list: W1, W2, W3...
2. Sort numbers (as needed) into a list: N1, N2, N3...
3. For step K in double-operation:
   → Left K positions: W1, W2, ... Wk
   → Right K positions: N1, N2, ... Nk (from right: Nk ... N2, N1 at rightmost)
   → Middle: Remaining elements from INPUT in their ORIGINAL RELATIVE ORDER.

CRITICAL: Middle elements stay in their original relative order from the input.
They don't rearrange themselves — the machine only touches one from each end 
per step.

EXAMPLE:
Input: hope 45 big 72 sky 18 mug 36
Words sorted A-Z: big, hope, mug, sky
Numbers sorted desc: 82... wait, 82 isn't here. 72, 45, 36, 18.

Step II (K=2):
Left 2: big, hope
Right 2: 72 (rightmost), 45 (2nd from right)
Middle: remaining = {sky, 18, mug, 36} in original relative order

Original order of remaining: ...45 [big gone]...72 [sky, 18, mug, 36]...
Wait, let me retrace:
Input: hope  45  big  72  sky  18  mug  36
Placed Step I: big (left), 72 (right).
Remaining in middle: hope  45  sky  18  mug  36  (their relative order from input)

Step I: big  hope  45  sky  18  mug  36  72

Placed in Step II: hope (left), 45 (right).
Remaining: sky  18  mug  36 (relative order)

Step II: big  hope  sky  18  mug  36  45  72

Wait, that puts 45 at position 7 (2nd from right). And 72 is at position 8 (rightmost). ✓

Middle in Step II: sky  18  mug  36 (their relative order is preserved from Input!)
Check Input: hope  45  big  72  sky  18  mug  36
After removing hope, 45, big, 72: what remains? sky, 18, mug, 36.
In Input order: sky appears after 72, then 18, mug, 36. ✓

So Step II: big  hope  [sky  18  mug  36]  45  72  ✓
This matches our earlier calculation!

THE KEY INSIGHT: Middle elements maintain their INPUT RELATIVE ORDER.
```

## 15. STEP-BY-STEP ATTACK STRATEGY

---

### Universal 7-Step Approach for Any Input–Output Problem:

```
STEP 1 (10 sec): SCAN the input paragraph.
   → How many elements in the input?
   → Are they words, numbers, or mixed?
   → How many steps are shown?

STEP 2 (20 sec): SEPARATE elements by type.
   → List all words: [word1, word2, ...]
   → List all numbers: [num1, num2, ...]
   → Note any special properties.

STEP 3 (30 sec): SPOT what's new at each end.
   → Input→Step I: What appeared at the LEFT? What appeared at the RIGHT?
   → Step I→Step II: What new element is now at the LEFT? At the RIGHT?

STEP 4 (20 sec): DECODE the rule.
   → LEFT pattern: Alpha first? Largest? Shortest? Specific word type?
   → RIGHT pattern: Alpha last? Smallest? Longest? Specific number type?
   → Is it single or double operation?

STEP 5 (20 sec): VERIFY the rule.
   → Apply your rule to Step I → does it produce Step II exactly?
   → Apply to Step II → does it produce Step III exactly?
   → If YES: Rule confirmed. If NO: Revise.

STEP 6 (2-3 min): ANSWER the questions.
   → "What is Step N?" → Apply rule N times from Input.
   → "Which step is [arrangement]?" → Count anchors from both sides.
   → "How many steps?" → Use formula or count.
   → "Position of element X in Step N?" → Use anchor counting trick.

STEP 7 (30 sec): CROSS-CHECK one answer.
   → Verify at least one answer by tracing back.

TOTAL TIME: 5-7 minutes for a full 5-question set.
TARGET: Under 5 minutes with practice.
```

---

### Decision Tree for Identifying the Type:

```
START: Look at Input and Step I.

Did VALUES of any element CHANGE?
├── YES → Mathematical/Coding operation (Type 6 or 7)
│         → Identify the math rule (add/subtract/multiply/reverse/etc.)
└── NO  → Positional rearrangement (Types 1-5)
          ↓
          Are elements going to ONE end (left OR right)?
          ├── ONE END → Single operation (Types 1-3)
          │             → Which end? Left = ascending/alphabetical
          │                          Right = descending/reverse
          └── BOTH ENDS simultaneously → Double operation (Type 5)
                                          ↓
                                          What goes LEFT? What goes RIGHT?
                                          → Words LEFT + Numbers RIGHT (most common)
                                          → Numbers LEFT + Words RIGHT
                                          → Other combinations
```

## 16. COMMON MISTAKE ALERTS

---

### ⚠️ MISTAKE 1 — Mixing Up "From Left" and "From Right"

```
WRONG: "The 3rd element from right in Step II is 45."
RIGHT: Carefully count from the correct end.

Step II: big  flower  sky  18  mug  36  45  72
         1    2       3    4   5    6   7   8  ← positions from LEFT
         8    7       6    5   4    3   2   1  ← positions from RIGHT

3rd from right = position 6 from left = 36.

FIX: When the question says "from right," count backward from the last element.
     Always write out positions from BOTH ends when unsure.
```

---

### ⚠️ MISTAKE 2 — Thinking Anchored Elements Move

```
WRONG: "In Step III, 'basket' moved to position 2 because flower came before it."
RIGHT: Once 'basket' is placed in Step I at position 1, it STAYS at position 1 forever.
       It does NOT move to accommodate later placements.

FIX: Think of anchored elements as FIXED WALLS. New placements go NEXT TO them,
     never BEHIND them.
```

---

### ⚠️ MISTAKE 3 — Wrong Middle Order

```
WRONG: "Middle elements rearrange themselves as anchors are added."
RIGHT: Middle elements maintain their ORIGINAL RELATIVE ORDER from the Input.
       They only move to left or right when they are selected for placement.

FIX: After removing placed elements from the Input, 
     the remaining elements' ORDER does not change.
     Write: "Remaining middle: [list in Input order]" for each step.
```

---

### ⚠️ MISTAKE 4 — Confusing Which Number Goes Right

```
WRONG: "The largest number goes RIGHT, so 82 is rightmost. Next, 72 goes to 2nd from right."
ACTUAL SEQUENCE: Rightmost = 82. 2nd from right = 65. 3rd from right = 48. 4th from right = 29.
(Each step places the NEXT largest to the NEXT rightmost AVAILABLE position.)

FIX: Maintain a sorted list of numbers. Cross off each one as it gets placed.
     → Sorted desc: 82, 65, 48, 36, 29, 18
     → Step I: 82 placed at position 8.
     → Step II: 65 placed at position 7.
     → Step III: 48 placed at position 6.
     → Step IV: 36 placed at position 5.
```

---

### ⚠️ MISTAKE 5 — Miscounting Total Steps

```
WRONG: "8 elements with double operation → 8 steps needed."
RIGHT: 8 elements with double operation → 8/2 = 4 steps.

FIX: Use the formula. Single op: N-1 steps. Double op: N/2 steps (N even).
     Practice with small examples first.
```

---

### ⚠️ MISTAKE 6 — Applying Rule Incorrectly to New Input

```
SITUATION: The example input-output shows the rule. 
           Then a NEW input is given, and you must apply the SAME rule.

WRONG: "The new input has 'mango' which starts with M. M is the 13th letter,
        so it goes to position 13."
RIGHT: The rule is RELATIVE (e.g., alphabetically first among the given words),
       not absolute (not based on position in the alphabet).

FIX: Apply the rule to the NEW input's elements. 
     Re-sort the new input's words and numbers according to the rule.
     Don't assume any element's position based on the example.
```

---

### ⚠️ MISTAKE 7 — Missing Tie-Breaking Rules

```
SITUATION: Two words have the same first letter, or two numbers are equal.

WRONG: Ignoring the tie-break and picking randomly.
RIGHT: Look for the secondary rule in the examples.

Common tie-breaking rules:
→ For words: If same first letter, check second letter, then third...
             (Standard alphabetical order handles this automatically.)
→ For numbers: If equal, maintain original order (stable sort).
→ For word length: If same length, pick the one that appears FIRST in the original input.
                   OR pick alphabetically.

FIX: When in doubt, use pure alphabetical order for words 
     and first-occurrence for numbers.
```

## 🏆 TOP 10 EXAM TIPS

1. **Spend the first 60–90 seconds ONLY on identifying the rule** — don't attempt a single question until you've verified the rule with at least 2 consecutive step comparisons. This investment saves you from getting all 5 questions wrong.

2. **Pre-sort words and numbers before attempting any step** — write down: "Words A-Z: [list]" and "Numbers desc: [list]" at the top of your work area. This single step eliminates 90% of errors.

3. **Use the "2-second scan" on new inputs** — immediately identify: (a) number of elements, (b) number of words vs numbers, (c) expected number of steps. This sets your mental framework.

4. **For double-operation puzzles, use the direct formula for Step K** — left side = first K alphabetical words; right side = first K largest numbers (from rightmost); middle = remaining in original input order. Don't trace step-by-step if K is large.

5. **Circle or underline ANCHORED elements** in each step — anchored elements are your landmarks. They tell you exactly which step you're looking at and what remains to be placed.

6. **When "which step is [arrangement]?" is asked, count anchors** — count how many are locked on the left, count how many are locked on the right. If both counts are equal (which they always are in double-operation), that count = the step number.

7. **For value-changing puzzles, check differences between consecutive steps** — if numbers change, compute (Step I value) - (Input value) for each number. If the difference is constant, it's addition. If it's a ratio, it's multiplication. If digits are swapped, it's reversal.

8. **Never trust memory for the middle elements' order** — always trace back to the original input and list the remaining elements in their original sequence. Middle elements don't self-sort.

9. **Practice with 5-question sets, not individual questions** — Input–Output is a 5-question block topic. Your practice unit should always be a full set, because the time to identify the rule is amortized across all 5 questions.

10. **If the rule isn't clear after 90 seconds, move on** — return with fresh eyes. Never spend more than 2 minutes trying to identify the rule without progress. The remaining questions in the exam are worth more than a stubborn puzzle.
