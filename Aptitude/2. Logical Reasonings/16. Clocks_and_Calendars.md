## 1. CLOCK BASICS — THE FOUNDATION

Before solving any clock problem, these fundamentals must be crystal clear.

---

### The Clock Face:

```
A standard clock has:
→ 12 hours marked (1 through 12)
→ 60 minute divisions (small marks)
→ 2 main hands: Hour hand (short) and Minute hand (long)
→ Some clocks also have a Second hand

FULL ROTATION = 360°
One complete rotation of any hand = 360°
```

---

### Speed of Each Hand:

```
╔═══════════════╦══════════════════╦══════════════════╦══════════════╗
║  HAND         ║ Time for full    ║ Degrees per      ║ Degrees per  ║
║               ║ 360° rotation   ║ HOUR             ║ MINUTE       ║
╠═══════════════╬══════════════════╬══════════════════╬══════════════╣
║ Minute Hand   ║ 60 minutes       ║ 360°             ║ 6°           ║
║ Hour Hand     ║ 12 hours (720min)║ 30°              ║ 0.5°         ║
║ Second Hand   ║ 60 seconds       ║ 21600°           ║ 360°         ║
╚═══════════════╩══════════════════╩══════════════════╩══════════════╝

MEMORIZE:
Minute hand moves: 6° per minute
Hour hand moves:   0.5° per minute (= ½° per minute)
```

---

### Positions on the Clock Face:

```
Each hour number represents a position:
12 o'clock = 0° (top)
3 o'clock  = 90°
6 o'clock  = 180°
9 o'clock  = 270°

Each minute mark = 360°/60 = 6° apart
Each hour division = 360°/12 = 30° apart

So:
→ From 12 to 1 = 30°
→ From 12 to 2 = 60°
→ From 12 to 3 = 90°
→ From 12 to 4 = 120°
→ From 12 to 5 = 150°
→ From 12 to 6 = 180°
→ From 12 to 7 = 210°
→ From 12 to 8 = 240°
→ From 12 to 9 = 270°
→ From 12 to 10 = 300°
→ From 12 to 11 = 330°
→ From 12 to 12 = 360° = 0°
```

---

### Relative Speed of Minute Hand vs Hour Hand:

```
CRITICAL CONCEPT:
Minute hand speed    = 6° per minute
Hour hand speed      = 0.5° per minute
Relative speed       = 6 - 0.5 = 5.5° per minute

This means the minute hand GAINS 5.5° over the hour hand every minute.

In one hour (60 minutes):
Minute hand gains = 5.5 × 60 = 330° over the hour hand.

WHY 330° and not 360°?
Because the hour hand also moves 30° (one hour division) while 
the minute hand completes its full circle.
Net gain = 360° - 30° = 330°. ✓

ALTERNATIVE: Think of it as the minute hand LAPPING the hour hand.
The minute hand makes a complete lap over the hour hand once every
65 and 5/11 minutes (≈ 65.45 minutes).
```

## 2. ANGLES ON A CLOCK — CORE CONCEPT

---

### Position of Hour Hand at Any Time H:M:

```
At exactly H hours, the hour hand is at: H × 30°

But as minutes pass, the hour hand also moves:
Hour hand position = H × 30° + M × 0.5°
(where H = hours, M = minutes)

EXAMPLE: At 3:40
Hour hand = 3 × 30 + 40 × 0.5 = 90 + 20 = 110° from 12
```

---

### Position of Minute Hand at Any Time H:M:

```
Minute hand position = M × 6°
(always measured from 12)

EXAMPLE: At 3:40
Minute hand = 40 × 6 = 240° from 12
```

---

### Angle Between the Hands:

```
Angle = |Minute hand position - Hour hand position|
      = |M × 6 - (H × 30 + M × 0.5)|
      = |M × 6 - H × 30 - M × 0.5|
      = |M × 5.5 - H × 30|

If angle > 180°: Actual angle = 360° - calculated angle
(We always take the smaller angle, unless specified otherwise)
```

---

## 3. THE MASTER ANGLE FORMULA

```
╔══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════╗
║              THE MASTER FORMULA FOR CLOCK ANGLES                ║
║                                                                  ║
║    Angle = |11M/2 - 30H|                                        ║
║                                                                  ║
║    Where:  H = Hour (0 to 11)                                    ║
║            M = Minutes (0 to 59)                                 ║
║            | | = absolute value (always positive)                ║
║                                                                  ║
║    If result > 180°: Angle = 360° - result                       ║
╚══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════╝

DERIVATION (so you understand it, not just memorize):
Minute hand = M × 6° from 12
Hour hand   = H × 30° + M × 0.5° from 12
Difference  = |M×6 - H×30 - M×0.5|
            = |M×5.5 - 30H|
            = |11M/2 - 30H|     ← This is the Master Formula
```

---

### Applying the Master Formula — Step by Step:

```
EXAMPLE 1: Find the angle between hands at 3:20

H = 3, M = 20
Angle = |11×20/2 - 30×3|
      = |110 - 90|
      = |20|
      = 20°

ANSWER: 20°

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

EXAMPLE 2: Find the angle between hands at 6:30

H = 6, M = 30
Angle = |11×30/2 - 30×6|
      = |165 - 180|
      = |-15|
      = 15°

ANSWER: 15°

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

EXAMPLE 3: Find the angle at 8:45

H = 8, M = 45
Angle = |11×45/2 - 30×8|
      = |247.5 - 240|
      = |7.5|
      = 7.5°

ANSWER: 7.5°

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

EXAMPLE 4: Find the angle at 4:55

H = 4, M = 55
Angle = |11×55/2 - 30×4|
      = |302.5 - 120|
      = |182.5|
      = 182.5° > 180°
      
Since > 180°: Actual angle = 360° - 182.5° = 177.5°

ANSWER: 177.5°
```

---

### Quick Mental Check:

```
At the top of every hour (M=0): Hands are H×30° apart.
Example: At 4:00 → angle = 4×30 = 120°
         At 9:00 → angle = 9×30 = 270° → take 360-270 = 90°
         At 7:00 → angle = 7×30 = 210° → take 360-210 = 150°
```

These are the most commonly tested scenarios!

---

```
The minute hand is ON TOP of the hour hand.
Angle = 0°

Using formula: |11M/2 - 30H| = 0
→ 11M/2 = 30H
→ M = 60H/11

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

3         │  180/11 ≈ 16.36 min   │  3:16:22
4         │  240/11 ≈ 21.82 min   │  4:21:49
5         │  300/11 ≈ 27.27 min   │  5:27:16
```

```
→ 11M/2 - 30H = ±180
→ 11M/2 = 30H + 180 or 11M/2 = 30H - 180

FORMULA: M = (60H + 360)/11  OR  M = (60H - 360)/11

For the valid range (M between 0 and 60):

TABLE: Times when hands are opposite:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
12 & 1    │  12:32:44

6 & 7     │  6:32:44 (actually same as between 12&1)
...       │  (Repeats with offset of 6 hours)
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

KEY FACT: Hands are opposite exactly 22 times in 24 hours
          (11 times in 12 hours).

SHORTCUT: Times of opposite position = Times of coincidence + 30 minutes
(approximately, since the hour hand also moves in those 30 minutes,
the exact time is: coincidence time + 360/11 minutes = 32.73 minutes)

More precisely: opposite happens 360/11 minutes AFTER coincidence.
```

```
FORMULA 1: M = (60H + 180)/11
```

### Finding Time When Angle is a Specific Value:

```
EXAMPLE: At what time between 4 and 5 o'clock are the hands 
         at an angle of 60°?

Using |11M/2 - 30H| = 60 with H = 4:
Case 1: 11M/2 - 120 = 60 → 11M/2 = 180 → M = 360/11 = 32 8/11 minutes
Case 2: 11M/2 - 120 = -60 → 11M/2 = 60 → M = 120/11 = 10 10/11 minutes

Both are between 0 and 60, so BOTH are valid answers.

CONVERT TO SECONDS: 10/11 × 60 = 54.5 seconds ≈ 55 seconds

```

## 5. GAINING AND LOSING CLOCKS (FAULTY CLOCKS)

One of the most tricky yet common exam topics.

---

### Key Concepts:

```
A clock that runs FAST → GAINS time
→ Shows a later time than actual time
→ "The clock gains X minutes in Y hours"

A clock that runs SLOW → LOSES time
→ Shows an earlier time than actual time
→ "The clock loses X minutes in Y hours"

A CORRECT clock gains/loses 0 minutes.
```

---

### The Gain/Loss Formula:

```
If a clock gains X minutes in Y hours:

In 1 hour, it gains X/Y minutes.
In T hours, it gains T × (X/Y) minutes.

To find the ACTUAL TIME when you know the clock shows a certain time:
If clock is FAST (gains): Actual time = Clock time - (gain)
If clock is SLOW (loses): Actual time = Clock time + (loss)

TIME GAINED FORMULA:
Total gain in T hours = T × (gain per hour)

CORRECT TIME from fast clock: Correct time = Shown time - Gain
CORRECT TIME from slow clock: Correct time = Shown time + Loss
```

---

### The Critical Formula for Faulty Clocks:

```
FORMULA: For a clock gaining G minutes per hour:

If the clock shows time T (in minutes from a reference point):
Actual elapsed time = T × 60/(60 + G)   [if clock gains G min/hour]
Actual elapsed time = T × 60/(60 - L)   [if clock loses L min/hour]

EXAMPLE:
A clock gains 5 minutes every hour.
If the clock shows 6:00 PM, and it was set correctly at 12:00 PM, 
what is the actual time?

Clock shows 6 hours have passed (= 360 minutes on clock).
Actual time passed = 360 × 60/65 = 360 × 12/13 = 332.3 minutes
= 5 hours 32.3 minutes
Actual time = 12:00 PM + 5h 32.3min = 5:32 PM approximately.
```

---

### Worked Examples — Faulty Clocks:

```
EXAMPLE 1:
A clock loses 5 minutes every hour. If it shows 3:00 PM,
and was set correctly at 9:00 AM, what is the actual time?

STEP 1: Time shown by clock from 9 AM to 3 PM = 6 hours = 360 minutes.
STEP 2: Clock loses 5 min/hour. In every 60 min of actual time,
        the clock only shows 55 minutes.
STEP 3: In 360 clock-minutes, actual minutes = 360 × (60/55) = 360 × 12/11
        = 4320/11 = 392.7 minutes = 6 hours 32.7 minutes.
STEP 4: Actual time = 9:00 AM + 6h 32.7min = 3:32:42 PM.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

EXAMPLE 2:
Two clocks A and B are set correctly at 8:00 AM Monday.
Clock A gains 2 minutes/hour, Clock B loses 3 minutes/hour.
When will they show the same time again?

STEP 1: A gains 2 min/hour, B loses 3 min/hour.
        Relative rate = 2 + 3 = 5 min/hour (A gains over B).
STEP 2: For A to gain 12 hours (720 minutes) over B, which would make 
        them show the same time (since clock is 12-hour):
        Time = 720/5 = 144 hours = 6 days.
        
ANSWER: They show the same time after 144 hours = 6 days from Monday 8 AM
        = Sunday 8 AM.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

EXAMPLE 3:
A clock was set correctly at 12:00 noon. It gains 10 seconds per minute.
What time does it show when the actual time is 1:00 PM?

STEP 1: Actual time passed = 60 minutes.
STEP 2: Clock gains 10 seconds per actual minute.
        In 60 actual minutes, clock gains = 60 × 10 seconds = 600 seconds = 10 minutes.
STEP 3: Clock time = Actual time + Gain = 1:00 PM + 10 minutes = 1:10 PM.
```

---

### When Two Clocks Show Same Time:

```
FORMULA:
If Clock A gains 'a' minutes per hour and Clock B gains 'b' minutes per hour
(one gaining, one losing):

Time for them to show the same time =
    720 minutes / (a + b) hours   [if one gains, one loses]
OR
    720 minutes / (a - b) hours   [if both gain/lose but at different rates]

Note: 720 = 12 hours × 60 minutes (since clock repeats every 12 hours)

IMPORTANT: "Show the same time" = differ by 12 hours (clock face is same)
```

## 6. MIRROR AND WATER REFLECTION OF CLOCKS

Commonly asked in SSC, IBPS, and similar exams.

---

### Mirror Reflection:

```
When you see a clock in a MIRROR:
→ The image is LEFT-RIGHT REVERSED.
→ What appears to be on the right is actually on the left.

TRICK: To find mirror time, subtract the shown time from 11:60.
If the reflected time has no seconds: subtract from 12:00.

FORMULA: Mirror time = 11:60 - Shown time
         OR
         Mirror time = 12:00 - Shown time (when minutes = 0)

WHY 11:60?
Because 11:60 = 12:00 (60 min = next hour).
The reflection of a clock showing H:M is (11:60 - H:M).

EXAMPLE 1: Clock shows 3:30 in mirror. Actual time?
Actual time = 11:60 - 3:30 = 8:30

EXAMPLE 2: Clock shows 7:15 in mirror. Actual time?
Actual time = 11:60 - 7:15 = 4:45

EXAMPLE 3: Clock shows 4:00 in mirror. Actual time?
Actual time = 12:00 - 4:00 = 8:00 (use 12:00 when minutes = 0)

EXAMPLE 4: Clock shows 10:50 in mirror. Actual time?
Actual time = 11:60 - 10:50 = 1:10

VERIFICATION TRICK:
Actual time + Mirror time = 12:00 (always!)
3:30 + 8:30 = 12:00 ✓
7:15 + 4:45 = 12:00 ✓
```

---

### Water Reflection:

```
When you see a clock reflected in WATER:
→ The image is TOP-BOTTOM REVERSED (upside down).
→ Left and right remain the same.

FORMULA: Water time = 17:60 - Shown time
         OR
         Water time = 18:00 - Shown time (when minutes = 0)

WHY 17:60?
For an analog clock face, the water reflection produces:
Water reflection time = 17:60 - Shown time

EXAMPLE 1: Clock shows 8:00. Water reflection shows?
Water time = 18:00 - 8:00 = 10:00

EXAMPLE 2: Clock shows 3:30. Water reflection shows?
Water time = 17:60 - 3:30 = 14:30 → but clock is 12-hour, so 2:30

EXAMPLE 3: Clock shows 6:15. Water reflection shows?
Water time = 17:60 - 6:15 = 11:45

NOTE: Water reflection is less commonly tested than mirror reflection.
Some exams skip this entirely. Focus more on mirror reflection.
```

---

### Mirror Reflection — Visual Understanding:

```
ACTUAL CLOCK (facing you):
         12
      11    1
    10        2
   9            3
    8          4
      7       5
          6

MIRROR IMAGE (left-right flipped):
         12
      1     11
    2          10
   3              9
    4           8
      5        7
          6

So if the mirror shows what looks like 3 o'clock → actual time is 9 o'clock.
If mirror shows 8:20 → actual = 11:60 - 8:20 = 3:40.
```

## 7. CLOCK ANGLE — ADVANCED PROBLEMS

---

### Finding When Angle is Given — Between Two Specific Hours:

```
PROBLEM TYPE: "At what time between X and (X+1) o'clock will the 
               angle between hands be Y degrees?"

FORMULA: M = (60H ± 2Y) / 11
Where Y = given angle, H = starting hour, M = minutes after H o'clock.

The ± gives TWO possible answers. Check which falls between 0 and 60.

EXAMPLE: Between 5 and 6, when do hands make a 40° angle?

H = 5, Y = 40
M = (60×5 ± 2×40)/11 = (300 ± 80)/11

Case 1: M = (300 + 80)/11 = 380/11 = 34 6/11 minutes
Case 2: M = (300 - 80)/11 = 220/11 = 20 minutes

Both between 0-60? Yes!
Answers: 5h 20min and 5h 34 6/11 min

VERIFICATION for 5:20:
|11×20/2 - 30×5| = |110 - 150| = |-40| = 40° ✓
```

---

### Angle at H Hours M Minutes — Direct Problems:

```
STEP 1: Calculate hour hand position = H×30 + M×0.5
STEP 2: Calculate minute hand position = M×6
STEP 3: Difference = |Step2 - Step1|
STEP 4: If > 180, subtract from 360

SHORTCUT TABLE for whole hours:
Time    │ Angle (reflex if >180 noted)
────────┼───────────────────────────────
12:00   │ 0°
1:00    │ 30°
2:00    │ 60°
3:00    │ 90°
4:00    │ 120°
5:00    │ 150°
6:00    │ 180°
7:00    │ 150° (= 360-210)
8:00    │ 120° (= 360-240)
9:00    │ 90°  (= 360-270)
10:00   │ 60°  (= 360-300)
11:00   │ 30°  (= 360-330)
12:00   │ 0°
```

---

### Hands Between a Specific Angle — Count Problems:

```
Q: How many times between 12 PM and 12 AM do the hands:
   (a) coincide?
   (b) form 90°?
   (c) form 180°?

ANSWER:
(a) Coincide: 11 times (in 12 hours)
(b) Form 90°: 22 times (in 12 hours)
(c) Form 180°: 11 times (in 12 hours)
(d) Form 60°: 22 times (in 12 hours) — any non-special angle: 22 times

GENERAL RULE:
For 0° → 11 times per 12 hours
For 180° → 11 times per 12 hours
For any other angle (0° < θ < 180°) → 22 times per 12 hours
```

## 8. CALENDAR BASICS — DAYS, WEEKS, MONTHS, YEARS

### Fundamental Calendar Facts:

```
1 week    = 7 days

DAYS IN EACH MONTH (rhyme to remember):
"Thirty days has September, April, June, and November.
All the rest have thirty-one, except February alone,
which has twenty-eight (or twenty-nine in a leap year)."

Jan=31, Feb=28/29, Mar=31, Apr=30, May=31, Jun=30,
Jul=31, Aug=31, Sep=30, Oct=31, Nov=30, Dec=31

MEMORIZATION TRICK (Knuckle Method):
Close your fist. Start from the index knuckle = January.
Knuckle = 31 days, valley between = 30 days (or 28/29 for Feb).
January (knuckle-31), Feb (valley-28/29), March (knuckle-31), 
April (valley-30), May (knuckle-31), June (valley-30),
July (knuckle-31) — restart with same knuckle — 
August (knuckle-31), September (valley-30), October (knuckle-31),
November (valley-30), December (knuckle-31).
```

```
Sunday    = 0 (or 7 in some systems)
Monday    = 1

IN MODULAR ARITHMETIC (used for calendar calculations):

```

## 9. ODD DAYS — THE MASTER CONCEPT

**This is the FOUNDATION of all calendar date calculations.**

---

### What Are Odd Days?

```
"Odd days" = the REMAINDER when dividing days by 7.

365 ÷ 7 = 52 weeks + 1 day → 1 odd day (ordinary year)
366 ÷ 7 = 52 weeks + 2 days → 2 odd days (leap year)

ODD DAYS FOR EACH YEAR TYPE:
Ordinary year (365 days): 365 = 52×7 + 1 → 1 odd day
Leap year (366 days):     366 = 52×7 + 2 → 2 odd days

ODD DAYS FOR EACH MONTH (non-leap year):
January   = 31 days = 4 weeks + 3 days → 3 odd days
February  = 28 days = 4 weeks exactly  → 0 odd days
March     = 31 days → 3 odd days
April     = 30 days = 4 weeks + 2 days → 2 odd days
May       = 31 days → 3 odd days
June      = 30 days → 2 odd days
July      = 31 days → 3 odd days
August    = 31 days → 3 odd days
September = 30 days → 2 odd days
October   = 31 days → 3 odd days
November  = 30 days → 2 odd days
```

## 11. LEAP YEAR RULES

**Critical — must know perfectly!**

---

### The Complete Leap Year Rules:

```
╔══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════╗
║                   LEAP YEAR RULES                                ║
╠══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════╣
║ RULE 1: A year divisible by 4 is a LEAP YEAR.                   ║
║         Examples: 2004, 2008, 2012, 2016, 2020 are leap years.  ║
╠══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════╣
║ RULE 2: BUT if the year is a CENTURY YEAR (divisible by 100),   ║
║         it is NOT a leap year UNLESS...                          ║
║         Examples: 1700, 1800, 1900 are NOT leap years.          ║
╠══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════╣
║ RULE 3: ...the century year is ALSO divisible by 400,           ║
║         in which case it IS a leap year.                         ║
║         Examples: 1600, 2000, 2400 ARE leap years.              ║
╚══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════╝

PRIORITY ORDER (check in this sequence):
1. Is year divisible by 400? → YES = Leap year. STOP.
2. Is year divisible by 100? → YES = NOT a leap year. STOP.
3. Is year divisible by 4?   → YES = Leap year. STOP.
4. None of the above         → NOT a leap year.

EXAMPLES:
2000: ÷400? Yes → LEAP YEAR ✓
1900: ÷400? No. ÷100? Yes → NOT LEAP YEAR ✓
1904: ÷400? No. ÷100? No. ÷4? Yes → LEAP YEAR ✓
2023: ÷400? No. ÷100? No. ÷4? No → NOT LEAP YEAR ✓
2024: ÷400? No. ÷100? No. ÷4? Yes → LEAP YEAR ✓
```

---

### Why Century Years Need Special Treatment:

```
The Gregorian calendar approximates the solar year (365.2425 days):
→ Every 4 years: add 1 day → average = 365.25 days/year
→ Every 100 years: skip the added day → 365.24 days/year
→ Every 400 years: add it back → 365.2425 days/year (very accurate!)

This is why:
→ Regular rule: every 4 years = leap year
→ Exception: century years are NOT leap years (skip the day)
→ Exception to exception: every 400 years IS a leap year (add it back)
```

---

### Number of Leap Years Between Two Dates:

```
FORMULA: Leap years from year A to year B (inclusive):
= floor(B/4) - floor((A-1)/4) 
  - floor(B/100) + floor((A-1)/100)
  + floor(B/400) - floor((A-1)/400)

SIMPLER APPROACH for exam:
Between years A and B:
Count multiples of 4: floor(B/4) - floor(A/4)  (approximately)
Subtract century years that are not div by 400.
Add century years that ARE div by 400.

QUICK COUNTING (for a range within a century, no century years involved):
Number of leap years from Y1 to Y2 = floor(Y2/4) - floor((Y1-1)/4)

EXAMPLE: How many leap years from 2001 to 2020?
= floor(2020/4) - floor(2000/4) = 505 - 500 = 5
Leap years: 2004, 2008, 2012, 2016, 2020. ✓
```

## 13. CALENDAR REPETITION

---

### When Does a Calendar Repeat?

```
A calendar for year X repeats in year Y if:
1. X and Y have the same day for January 1 (same starting day)
2. AND both are either both leap years or both ordinary years.

RULES FOR CALENDAR REPETITION:
If year X is an ORDINARY year:
→ Next repetition is generally after 6, 11, or 28 years
   (specifically: 6 years if after a leap year, 11 years otherwise, 
    then 28-year cycle)

If year X is a LEAP year:
→ Next repetition is generally after 28 years

THE 28-YEAR CYCLE:
Any calendar repeats EXACTLY after 28 years
(because 28 years = 7 day cycles × 4 year leap cycle = LCM pattern)

EXCEPTION: Does not hold across century years (like 1900, 2100)
that break the leap year pattern.
```

---

### Calendar Repeat Quick Reference:

```
For ORDINARY years (not leap):
Year X repeats in X+6 (if X+5 is a leap year)
Year X repeats in X+11 (if X+6 is after the leap cycle)
Year X always repeats in X+28.

For LEAP years:
Year X (leap) repeats in X+28 always.

EXAMPLE:
2019 (ordinary) → next repetition: check 2019+6=2025.
Between 2019 and 2025: 2020 is a leap year. 
Odd days from 2019 to 2025: 2019(1)+2020(2)+2021(1)+2022(1)+2023(1)+2024(2) 
= 8 days... Let me verify: 
From Jan 1, 2019 to Jan 1, 2025 = 6 years = 4 ordinary + 2 leap = 4+4 = 8 odd days.
8 mod 7 = 1. Since Jan 1, 2019 = Tuesday, Jan 1, 2025 = Tuesday + 1 = Wednesday. 
Not the same! So 2019 doesn't repeat in 2025.

Try 2019+11 = 2030:
Years 2019-2029: leap years = 2020, 2024 = 2 leap years. Ordinary = 9.
Odd days = 9×1 + 2×2 = 13. 13 mod 7 = 6.
Jan 1, 2019 (Tue) + 6 = Mon? 
If 2019 Jan1 = Tue(2), 2030 Jan1 = (2+6) mod 7 = 8 mod 7 = 1 = Mon.
2019 Jan1 was Tue, 2030 Jan1 would be Mon. Different. 

Try 2019+6 = 2025 (already tried: didn't match).
Try specific: 2019 January 1 = Tuesday. Find year after 2019 with Jan 1 = Tuesday AND ordinary year.
2019: Tue (ordinary)
2020: Thu (leap +2)
2021: Fri (+1)

```

```
28 years = 4 × 7 (four complete 7-year patterns of days)
         = 7 × 4 (seven complete 4-year leap cycles)
```

#### TRICK K1 — Day After N Days (Quick Mod)

```
N days after a known day:
New day = (Known day + N) mod 7

N days BEFORE a known day:
New day = (Known day - N) mod 7 → add 7 if negative.

SHORTCUT: N mod 7 first, then add to current day.

EXAMPLE: 250 days after Monday:
250 mod 7 = 250 - 245 = 5.
Monday + 5 = Saturday.

EXAMPLE: 365 days after Thursday:
365 mod 7 = 1 (ordinary year = 1 odd day).
Thursday + 1 = Friday.

EXAMPLE: 366 days after Thursday (leap year):
366 mod 7 = 2.
Thursday + 2 = Saturday.
```

#### TRICK K5 — Same Day Last Year / Next Year

```
If this year is ordinary:
Same date next year = current day + 1 (one odd day shift).
Same date last year = current day - 1.

If this year is a leap year:
For dates BEFORE Feb 29: same date next year = current day + 1.
For dates AFTER Feb 29: same date next year = current day + 2.
(Because the extra leap day falls before March 1.)

EXAMPLE: My birthday Aug 15 falls on Monday 2024 (2024 is leap).
What day is it in 2025?
2025 is ordinary. Aug 15 is after Feb 29, 2024.
2025 day = Monday + 2 (from leap year) = Wednesday.
```

#### TRICK K6 — Calendar Repeat Shortcut

```
For ordinary years: repeats after 6 or 11 years (11 most common).
For leap years: repeats after 28 years.
Any year: GUARANTEED repeat after 28 years (within same century type).

QUICK CHECK for repetition:
Count odd days between the two years.
If odd days = 0 (mod 7) AND both are same type (leap/ordinary) → same calendar.
```

#### TRICK K7 — Days Between Two Dates

```
SHORTCUT for common pairs:
Jan 1 to Dec 31 = 364 days (ordinary) or 365 days (leap).
Jan 1 to Jun 30 = 31+28+31+30+31+30 = 181 days (ordinary).
Same month, different dates: simple subtraction.

COUNTING METHOD:
1. Count remaining days in start month.
2. Add full months between.
3. Add days in end month.

EXAMPLE: March 10 to July 25:
Remaining in March: 31-10 = 21 days
April: 30
May: 31
June: 30
Days in July: 25
Total = 21+30+31+30+25 = 137 days.
```

## 17. COMMON MISTAKE ALERTS

---

### ⚠️ CLOCK MISTAKE 1 — Thinking Hands Coincide 12 Times Per Hour

```
WRONG: "The minute and hour hands coincide 12 times in 12 hours."
RIGHT: They coincide 11 times in 12 hours.

WHY: At 11 o'clock, the next coincidence would be at 12:00 (not "11 o'clock coincidence" 
     separately). Both the "12:00" coincidence and the "start at 12:00" are the same event.
     So: 12:00, 1:05+, 2:10+, 3:16+, 4:21+, 5:27+, 6:32+, 7:38+, 8:43+, 9:49+, 10:54+ 
     = 11 coincidences in 12 hours.
```

---

### ⚠️ CLOCK MISTAKE 2 — Wrong Mirror Formula

```
WRONG: "Mirror time = 12:00 - shown time" always.
RIGHT: Use 11:60 - shown time (unless M=0, then use 12:00).

EXAMPLE: Shown = 7:30
Wrong: 12:00 - 7:30 = 4:30.
Right: 11:60 - 7:30 = 4:30. (Same here!)

Shown = 7:20:
Wrong: 12:00 - 7:20 = 4:40.
Right: 11:60 - 7:20 = 4:40. (Same again!)

Actually 11:60 - 7:20 = (11-7):(60-20) = 4:40. ✓
And 12:00 - 7:20: you need to borrow: 11:60 - 7:20 = 4:40. Same result!

The formula 12:00 - H:M works for ALL cases if you handle the borrowing:
12:00 - 7:30: borrow 1 hour → 11:60 - 7:30 = 4:30. ✓

So BOTH formulas work if applied correctly. The 11:60 form is just pre-borrowed.
The KEY is: Actual time + Mirror time = 12:00 always.
```

---

### ⚠️ CLOCK MISTAKE 3 — Wrong Application of Faulty Clock Formula

```
WRONG: "Clock gained 10 min in 5 hours → actual time = shown time - 10 min"
RIGHT: The gain is NOT a flat 10 min for all durations!

The clock gains 10 min per 5 hours = 2 min per hour.
If the clock shows 8 hours have passed (from a set point):
Actual time passed = 8 × 60/(60+2) = 480/62 ≈ 7.74 hours.

The subtraction approach only works when:
"The clock shows exactly T o'clock" and you know it was set at another time.
```

---

### ⚠️ CALENDAR MISTAKE 1 — Applying Divisibility-by-4 to All Century Years

```
WRONG: "1900 is divisible by 4? No (1900/4=475, yes it IS!). So 1900 is a leap year?"
Actually: 1900 IS divisible by 4 (1900/4 = 475). 
BUT it's ALSO divisible by 100 (1900/100 = 19). 
AND it's NOT divisible by 400 (1900/400 = 4.75).
THEREFORE: 1900 is NOT a leap year.

RIGHT: Always check the priority: 400 → 100 → 4.

Common trap: 2100 — many students say leap year (÷4). But ÷100, not ÷400 → NOT leap.
```

---

### ⚠️ CALENDAR MISTAKE 2 — Wrong Odd Day Calculation for Centuries

```
WRONG: "100 years = 100/4 = 25 leap years → 25×2 + 75×1 = 125 odd days."
RIGHT: In 100 years (ending at 2100, for example), ONLY 24 leap years exist
       (not 25) because the 100th year itself (the century year) is NOT a leap year
       unless divisible by 400.

100 years: 24 leap + 76 ordinary = 24×2 + 76×1 = 48+76 = 124 days → 5 odd days.
```

---

### ⚠️ CALENDAR MISTAKE 3 — Forgetting Leap Year When Counting Odd Days

```
WRONG: Counting 2000 as ordinary year.
RIGHT: 2000 IS a leap year (÷400 ✓). Always give it 2 odd days.

When counting odd days for a range that includes 2000: 
→ Give 2000 two odd days (not one).
```

---

### ⚠️ CALENDAR MISTAKE 4 — Confusing "What day was it X days ago" Direction

```
WRONG: "50 days before Monday" = Monday + 50 mod 7.
RIGHT: "50 days before Monday" = Monday - 50 mod 7.

50 mod 7 = 1. Monday - 1 = Sunday.

MEMORY AID:
"After" → add days.
"Before/ago" → subtract days.
When subtracting, if result is negative, add 7.
```

### SET 1 — Clock Angles Basic (Q1–Q10)

**A1. (B) 90°**
At 3:00: H=3, M=0. Angle = |11×0/2 - 30×3| = |0 - 90| = 90°.

**A2. (B) 15°**
H=6, M=30. Angle = |11×30/2 - 30×6| = |165 - 180| = 15°.

**A3. (A) 110°**
H=12 (or 0), M=20. Angle = |11×20/2 - 30×0| = |110 - 0| = 110°.

**A4. (B) 90°**
At 9:00: H=9, M=0. Angle = |0 - 30×9| = |−270| = 270°. Since 270 > 180: 360 - 270 = 90°.

**A5. (A) 100°**
H=4, M=40. Angle = |11×40/2 - 30×4| = |220 - 120| = 100°.

## 🏆 TOP 10 EXAM TIPS

1. **Master the angle formula |11M/2 - 30H| first** — this single formula solves 80% of clock angle questions. Practice applying it mentally in under 10 seconds.

2. **Remember: hands coincide 11 times per 12 hours, NOT 12** — this is the most common wrong answer in multiple-choice options. The trap option "24 times" (in 24 hours) is always wrong.

3. **Mirror clock: memorize "Actual + Mirror = 12:00"** — instead of computing 11:60 - shown, simply ask "what time adds to the shown time to give 12:00?" This is faster and error-free.

4. **For faulty clocks: establish the rate clearly first** — write "gains X min per Y hours" as a fraction. Then either use the quick subtraction/addition method (approximate) or the exact formula for precise answers.

5. **Leap year: check in priority order (400 → 100 → 4)** — never just check "÷4" without checking century years. The ÷100 and ÷400 rules catch you in 1900, 2100, 2200, 2300 type questions.

6. **Memorize century odd days (5-3-1-0 pattern)** — this pattern (100 yrs=5, 200=3, 300=1, 400=0) is tested directly in some exams and is essential for computing any historical date's day of week.

7. **For "N days from day X" problems: always reduce N mod 7 first** — calculating 500 days from Monday becomes trivially "500 mod 7 = 3 days from Monday = Thursday." Never add days without reducing mod 7 first.

8. **Know that the angle formula gives the SMALLER angle** — if the result exceeds 180°, subtract from 360°. Exams often ask for the "reflex angle" (the larger one) — don't confuse them.

9. **Calendar repetition: 28-year cycle is the safest answer** — when asked "next year with same calendar," verify using odd days, but know that 28 years is the guaranteed repeat within the same century type.

10. **Practice the month odd days table until automatic** — Jan=3, Feb=0, Mar=3, Apr=2, May=3, Jun=2, Jul=3, Aug=3, Sep=2, Oct=3, Nov=2, Dec=3. This unlocks any "find the day" problem quickly.
