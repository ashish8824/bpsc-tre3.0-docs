## 1. THE BIG PICTURE — WHAT ARE ASSUMPTIONS?

An **assumption** is something that is **taken for granted** or **accepted as true** without being explicitly stated. In the context of logical reasoning, when someone makes a statement, they implicitly assume certain conditions to be true — even though they don't say them out loud.

> **Golden Rule #1:** An assumption is NOT stated in the statement — it is IMPLIED or HIDDEN.

> **Golden Rule #2:** If the assumption is FALSE, the statement becomes MEANINGLESS or ILLOGICAL.

---

### The Core Idea — Why Do We Assume?

```
Whenever anyone makes a statement, they are taking something for granted.

EXAMPLE:
You say to a friend: "Let's go to the cinema tonight."
Hidden assumptions:
→ The cinema is open tonight (otherwise why suggest it?)
→ Your friend is free tonight (otherwise the suggestion is pointless)
→ Both of you are interested in watching a movie
→ You have money to buy tickets

NONE of these are stated — but ALL are assumed!
This is what "implicit assumption" means.
```


## 2. CORE DEFINITIONS — STATEMENT VS ASSUMPTION

---

### What is a STATEMENT?

```
A STATEMENT is the main sentence given in the question.
It can be:
→ An advice/suggestion ("You should eat healthy food.")
→ An advertisement ("Buy our product for best results.")
→ A warning ("Do not touch the electric wires.")
→ A news headline ("Government bans plastic bags.")
→ A request ("Please submit your application by Friday.")
→ A conditional sentence ("If you study hard, you will succeed.")
→ A factual claim ("The population of India is growing rapidly.")

The statement is what is EXPLICITLY GIVEN.
```

---

### What is an ASSUMPTION?

```
An ASSUMPTION is what is IMPLICITLY TAKEN FOR GRANTED in the statement.

THREE KEY CHARACTERISTICS of a valid assumption:
1. NOT explicitly stated in the given statement
2. NECESSARY for the statement to be meaningful/logical
3. CONSISTENT with (does not contradict) the statement

An assumption answers the hidden question:
"What MUST be true for this statement to make sense?"
```

---

## 3. THE GOLDEN PRINCIPLES OF ASSUMPTIONS

---

### Principle 1 — The "Meaningless Test":

```
If the assumption is FALSE, the statement must become MEANINGLESS or POINTLESS.

EXAMPLE:
Statement: "Take this medicine to cure your fever."
Assumption: "This medicine is effective against fever."

Test: If medicine is NOT effective → taking it for fever is MEANINGLESS.
Result: The assumption IS implicit. ✓

COUNTER-EXAMPLE:
Statement: "Take this medicine to cure your fever."
Assumption: "Fever is a dangerous condition."

Test: If fever is NOT dangerous → the advice to take medicine still makes sense!
(You'd take medicine for discomfort, not just danger.)
Result: This assumption is NOT implicit. ✗
```

---

### Principle 2 — The "Beyond the Statement" Check:

```
An assumption must NOT go BEYOND what the statement implies.
It should be the MINIMUM necessary for the statement to make sense.

EXAMPLE:
Statement: "Read this book to improve your English."
Assumption I: "Reading helps improve English." ← Just right ✓
Assumption II: "This book is the best book ever written." ← Too much! ✗

Assumption II adds too much — the statement only says this book helps,
not that it's THE BEST. The statement is meaningful without claiming
it's the best book.
```

---

### Principle 3 — The "Already Stated" Check:

```
If the assumption is DIRECTLY STATED in the statement itself,
it is NOT an implicit assumption — it's a FACT.

EXAMPLE:
Statement: "Since many students failed, the exam was difficult."
Assumption: "Many students failed the exam."

This is directly stated in the statement — it's not hidden!
Therefore, this is NOT an implicit assumption. ✗

An assumption must be HIDDEN — not stated.
```

---

### Principle 4 — The "Consistency Check":

```
The assumption must be CONSISTENT WITH (not contradict) the statement.

EXAMPLE:
Statement: "Students should exercise daily to stay fit."
Assumption: "Students are currently not fit."

This is CONSISTENT with the advice (the advice would be pointless if
all students were already perfectly fit). ✓

Assumption: "Exercise is harmful for students."
This CONTRADICTS the statement (which recommends exercise). ✗
```

---

### Principle 5 — The "Necessity vs Possibility" Distinction:

```
An assumption must be NECESSARY for the statement to make sense,
NOT just POSSIBLE.

EXAMPLE:
Statement: "The train leaves at 6 AM. Please arrive at the station by 5:30 AM."
Assumption: "The person needs to catch this train." ← NECESSARY ✓
(If they don't need to catch it, the advice is pointless)

Assumption: "The train always runs on time." ← POSSIBLE but NOT NECESSARY ✗
(The advice to arrive early makes sense whether or not the train runs on time)
```

## 4. THE NEGATION TEST — YOUR PRIMARY WEAPON

> The **Negation Test** is the single most reliable method to determine if an assumption is implicit.

---

### How the Negation Test Works:

```
STEP 1: NEGATE the assumption (make it false/opposite).
STEP 2: Insert the NEGATED assumption into the context of the statement.
STEP 3: CHECK if the statement now becomes:
         → MEANINGLESS / ABSURD / ILLOGICAL = Assumption IS implicit ✓
         → STILL MEANINGFUL / UNAFFECTED = Assumption is NOT implicit ✗
```

---

### Negation Test — Worked Examples:

```
EXAMPLE 1:
Statement: "Please use our express delivery service for urgent packages."
Assumption: "Express delivery is faster than regular delivery."
Negation: "Express delivery is NOT faster than regular delivery."
Effect: If express isn't faster, why would anyone use it for urgent packages?
        The statement becomes ILLOGICAL. ✓
Result: IMPLICIT ASSUMPTION ✓

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

EXAMPLE 2:
Statement: "Study regularly to perform well in exams."
Assumption: "Regular study helps in performing well in exams."
Negation: "Regular study does NOT help in performing well."
Effect: If studying doesn't help, why advise studying?
        Statement becomes POINTLESS. ✓
Result: IMPLICIT ASSUMPTION ✓

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

EXAMPLE 3:
Statement: "Government should build more hospitals in rural areas."
Assumption: "There is a shortage of hospitals in rural areas."
Negation: "There is NO shortage of hospitals in rural areas."
Effect: If no shortage exists, why build more?
        Statement becomes UNJUSTIFIED. ✓
Result: IMPLICIT ASSUMPTION ✓

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

EXAMPLE 4 (NOT implicit):
Statement: "Eat more vegetables for good health."
Assumption: "Vegetables are the only healthy food."
Negation: "Vegetables are NOT the only healthy food."
Effect: Even if vegetables aren't the only healthy food, eating more
        of them can still be good advice. Statement remains meaningful!
Result: NOT IMPLICIT ✗ (too strong — statement doesn't claim exclusivity)
```

---

### The Negation Test — Quick Diagram:

```
NEGATE the assumption
          │
          ▼
Does the statement become meaningless/illogical?
          │
    ┌─────┴─────┐
    YES         NO
    │           │
    ▼           ▼
IMPLICIT    NOT IMPLICIT
    ✓           ✗
```

## 5. TYPE 1 — ADVICE / SUGGESTION STATEMENTS

> **"You should do X"** or **"Do X to achieve Y"** — the most common type in exams.

---

### Standard Assumptions in Advice Statements:

```
When someone GIVES ADVICE, they assume:
1. The advised action is EFFECTIVE for the goal
2. The advisee is CAPABLE of following the advice
3. The advisee has NOT already done it (or it's still relevant)
4. The goal is ACHIEVABLE through the advised means
```

---

### Example 1 — Basic Advice:

```
Statement: "You should join a gym to lose weight."
Assumptions:
I.  Gym workouts help in losing weight.
II. The person wants to lose weight.

Analysis:
I: NEGATE → "Gym does NOT help lose weight."
   → If gym doesn't help, why suggest it for weight loss? MEANINGLESS ✓
   → IMPLICIT ✓

II: NEGATE → "The person does NOT want to lose weight."
    → If the person doesn't want to lose weight, the advice is IRRELEVANT ✓
    → IMPLICIT ✓

Answer: Both I and II are implicit.
```

---

### Example 2 — Advice with Specific Method:

```
Statement: "To reduce stress, practice meditation for 20 minutes daily."
Assumptions:
I.  Meditation reduces stress.
II. The person is stressed.
III. 20 minutes of meditation daily is sufficient to reduce stress.

Analysis:
I: NEGATE → "Meditation does NOT reduce stress."
   → Advising meditation for stress relief becomes POINTLESS ✓
   → IMPLICIT ✓

II: NEGATE → "The person is NOT stressed."
    → Giving stress-reduction advice to a stress-free person is IRRELEVANT ✓
    → IMPLICIT ✓

III: NEGATE → "20 minutes daily is NOT sufficient."
     → The specific prescription of "20 minutes" implies the advisor
        believes this duration is sufficient. If not, why say "20 minutes"?
     → IMPLICIT ✓

Answer: All three are implicit.
```

---

### Example 3 — Advice with "Only" Trap:

```
Statement: "Read newspapers to stay informed about current events."
Assumptions:
I.  Newspapers contain information about current events.
II. Reading newspapers is the ONLY way to stay informed.

Analysis:
I: NEGATE → "Newspapers do NOT contain current events information."
   → Advising newspaper reading for current events is ILLOGICAL ✓
   → IMPLICIT ✓

II: NEGATE → "Reading newspapers is NOT the only way to stay informed."
    → Even if newspapers aren't the only way, they can still be one
      effective way. The advice remains valid. NOT MEANINGLESS!
    → NOT IMPLICIT ✗

Answer: Only Assumption I is implicit.

KEY LESSON: "Only" assumptions are usually NOT implicit unless the
statement explicitly says "only" or "exclusively."
```

## 6. TYPE 2 — ADVERTISEMENT / APPEAL STATEMENTS

> **"Buy our product"** or **"Use our service for best results"** — commercial communication statements.

---

### Standard Assumptions in Advertisements:

```
When someone ADVERTISES or APPEALS, they assume:
1. People WANT what is being offered
2. The product/service DELIVERS what it claims
3. The audience CAN ACCESS the product/service
4. People will respond to the appeal
```

---

### Example 4 — Product Advertisement:

```
Statement: "Use Brand X shampoo for shiny, beautiful hair."
Assumptions:
I.  Brand X shampoo makes hair shiny and beautiful.
II. People desire shiny and beautiful hair.

Analysis:
I: NEGATE → "Brand X does NOT make hair shiny."
   → Advertising it for that purpose is FALSE ADVERTISING — illogical ✓
   → IMPLICIT ✓

II: NEGATE → "People do NOT desire shiny and beautiful hair."
    → If no one wants shiny hair, the appeal is POINTLESS ✓
    → IMPLICIT ✓

Answer: Both I and II are implicit.
```

---

### Example 5 — Service Advertisement:

```
Statement: "Join our online course to boost your career in 30 days."
Assumptions:
I.  The online course can boost career prospects.
II. People want to improve their career.
III. Career improvement is achievable in 30 days through this course.

Analysis:
I: IMPLICIT ✓ (negation makes the advertisement meaningless)
II: IMPLICIT ✓ (negation makes the target audience irrelevant)
III: NEGATE → "Career cannot be boosted in 30 days."
     → The "30 days" claim is a key selling point of the course.
        If it's impossible, the claim is false and the ad is illogical.
     → IMPLICIT ✓

Answer: All three are implicit.
```

## 7. TYPE 3 — WARNING / CAUTION STATEMENTS

> **"Do not do X"** or **"Beware of X"** or **"X is dangerous"** — statements meant to prevent harm.

---

### Standard Assumptions in Warning Statements:

```
When someone gives a WARNING, they assume:
1. The warned-against action is genuinely DANGEROUS/HARMFUL
2. People might actually DO the warned-against action (otherwise why warn?)
3. The audience CAN understand and FOLLOW the warning
4. Avoiding the action will PREVENT the harm
```

---

### Example 6 — Safety Warning:

```
Statement: "Do not lean out of the running train — it is dangerous."
Assumptions:
I.  Leaning out of a running train is dangerous.
II. People tend to lean out of running trains.

Analysis:
I: NEGATE → "Leaning out is NOT dangerous."
   → Issuing a warning about something harmless is POINTLESS ✓
   → IMPLICIT ✓

II: NEGATE → "People do NOT tend to lean out of trains."
    → If no one ever leans out, there's no need for the warning ✓
    → IMPLICIT ✓

Answer: Both I and II are implicit.
```

---

### Example 7 — Tricky Warning:

```
Statement: "Beware of pickpockets in the market."
Assumptions:
I.  Pickpockets operate in the market.
II. One cannot recover stolen items from pickpockets.

Analysis:
I: NEGATE → "No pickpockets operate in the market."
   → Warning about pickpockets where none exist is MISLEADING/POINTLESS ✓
   → IMPLICIT ✓

II: NEGATE → "One CAN recover stolen items from pickpockets."
    → Even if recovery is possible, the warning to beware still makes sense
      (prevention is better than recovery). NOT MEANINGLESS!
    → NOT IMPLICIT ✗

Answer: Only Assumption I is implicit.
```

## 8. TYPE 4 — FACTUAL / NEWS STATEMENTS

> **"Studies show X"** or **"Government reports Y"** or **"Research indicates Z"** — informational statements.

---

### Standard Assumptions in Factual Statements:

```
When someone presents a FACT or NEWS, they assume:
1. The information is RELIABLE/CREDIBLE
2. The audience is INTERESTED in / can benefit from the information
3. The source is TRUSTWORTHY
4. The audience can UNDERSTAND the information
```

---

### Example 8 — Statistical Fact:

```
Statement: "According to a recent survey, 70% of working professionals
            experience stress at work."
Assumptions:
I.  Surveys on professional stress are conducted regularly.
II. The survey results are reliable and represent the general population.

Analysis:
I: NEGATE → "Such surveys are NOT conducted regularly."
   → Even if conducted rarely, a single recent survey can exist. 
      "According to a recent survey" doesn't require surveys to be regular.
   → NOT IMPLICIT ✗ (the statement doesn't imply regularity)

II: NEGATE → "Survey results are NOT reliable or representative."
    → If the survey is unreliable, presenting its findings as fact is
      MISLEADING and the statement loses credibility. ✓
    → IMPLICIT ✓

Answer: Only Assumption II is implicit.
```

---

### Example 9 — Government Announcement:

```
Statement: "The government announced a new scheme to provide free laptops
            to college students from economically weaker sections."
Assumptions:
I.  College students from economically weaker sections lack access to laptops.
II. Laptops will help these students in their education.

Analysis:
I: NEGATE → "These students already have laptops / do NOT lack access."
   → If they already have laptops, providing free ones seems unnecessary ✓
   → IMPLICIT ✓ (there's a need being addressed)

II: NEGATE → "Laptops will NOT help these students in education."
    → If laptops don't help, providing them for educational purposes
      is POINTLESS ✓
    → IMPLICIT ✓

Answer: Both I and II are implicit.
```

## 9. TYPE 5 — POLICY / DECISION STATEMENTS

> **"The government decided to..."** or **"The company has implemented..."** — administrative or organizational decisions.

---

### Standard Assumptions in Policy Statements:

```
When a POLICY or DECISION is announced, it assumes:
1. The problem being addressed ACTUALLY EXISTS
2. The policy will be EFFECTIVE in addressing the problem
3. The policy is PRACTICALLY IMPLEMENTABLE
4. The target audience EXISTS and will be AFFECTED
```

---

### Example 10 — Government Policy:

```
Statement: "The city council has decided to impose heavy fines on those
            who spit in public places."
Assumptions:
I.  People spit in public places in the city.
II. Heavy fines will discourage people from spitting in public.

Analysis:
I: NEGATE → "Nobody spits in public places in the city."
   → Creating fines for something that doesn't happen is UNNECESSARY ✓
   → IMPLICIT ✓

II: NEGATE → "Heavy fines will NOT discourage public spitting."
    → If fines don't work as deterrents, the policy is INEFFECTIVE and
      makes no sense to implement ✓
    → IMPLICIT ✓

Answer: Both I and II are implicit.
```

---

### Example 11 — Corporate Decision:

```
Statement: "The company decided to offer work-from-home options
            to retain talented employees."
Assumptions:
I.  Work-from-home options help retain talented employees.
II. The company is losing talented employees.
III. Talented employees prefer work-from-home options.

Analysis:
I: IMPLICIT ✓ (negation makes the decision illogical)
II: NEGATE → "The company is NOT losing talented employees."
    → If no one is leaving, why implement a retention strategy?
      Decision seems UNNECESSARY ✓
    → IMPLICIT ✓

III: NEGATE → "Talented employees do NOT prefer work-from-home."
     → If employees don't value this option, it won't help retain them.
        This actually overlaps with Assumption I somewhat. ✓
     → IMPLICIT ✓ (it's a necessary premise for the retention strategy to work)

Answer: All three are implicit.
```

## 10. TYPE 6 — REQUEST / APPEAL STATEMENTS

> **"Please do X"** or **"Kindly Y"** or **"We request you to Z"** — statements asking someone to do something.

---

### Standard Assumptions in Request Statements:

```
When someone makes a REQUEST, they assume:
1. The person being requested IS CAPABLE of doing it
2. The person CAN UNDERSTAND the request
3. The requested action IS POSSIBLE
4. The person WILL CONSIDER the request
```

---

### Example 12 — Public Request:

```
Statement: "Please submit your application for the scholarship by 5 PM today."
Assumptions:
I.  Applications can be submitted before 5 PM.
II. The person has applied for the scholarship.

Analysis:
I: NEGATE → "Applications CANNOT be submitted before 5 PM."
   → Making a deadline when submission isn't possible is ILLOGICAL ✓
   → IMPLICIT ✓ (the deadline requires submission to be feasible)

II: NEGATE → "The person has NOT applied for the scholarship."
    → If the person hasn't applied, a submission deadline is IRRELEVANT to them ✓
    → IMPLICIT ✓ (the request targets someone in the process)

Answer: Both I and II are implicit.
```

---

### Example 13 — Organizational Request:

```
Statement: "All employees are requested to attend the safety training
            session scheduled for Friday."
Assumptions:
I.  Employees are available on Friday.
II. The safety training will be beneficial for employees.
III. Employees have been informed about the schedule.

Analysis:
I: NEGATE → "Employees are NOT available on Friday."
   → Scheduling a mandatory session when no one can attend is POINTLESS ✓
   → IMPLICIT ✓

II: NEGATE → "Safety training will NOT benefit employees."
    → Organizing pointless training makes NO SENSE ✓
    → IMPLICIT ✓

III: NEGATE → "Employees have NOT been informed about the schedule."
     → The REQUEST itself is the information/notification.
        So this is what the statement IS doing — informing. 
        Not an assumption; it's the EFFECT of the statement. ✗
     → NOT IMPLICIT ✗ (the statement is the notification)

Answer: I and II are implicit.
```

## 11. TYPE 7 — CONDITIONAL STATEMENTS

> **"If X, then Y"** or **"Unless X, Y will not happen"** — cause-and-effect or conditional statements.

---

### Standard Assumptions in Conditional Statements:

```
When someone makes a CONDITIONAL STATEMENT, they assume:
1. The CAUSE (X) actually has the claimed EFFECT (Y)
2. There are NO ALTERNATIVE causes that could produce Y without X
3. The relationship between X and Y is VALID
```

---

### Example 14 — If-Then Statement:

```
Statement: "If you take this vitamin supplement, you will not fall sick."
Assumptions:
I.  This vitamin supplement prevents sickness.
II. The person is currently healthy and can benefit from prevention.
III. This supplement is the only way to prevent falling sick.

Analysis:
I: NEGATE → "The supplement does NOT prevent sickness."
   → Claiming it will prevent sickness is FALSE/ILLOGICAL ✓
   → IMPLICIT ✓

II: NEGATE → "The person is already sick."
    → Giving prevention advice to someone already sick is INAPPROPRIATE,
      but the statement remains logically meaningful for its prevention claim.
    → NOT fully implicit ✗ (statement isn't specifically about one person's state)

III: NEGATE → "This supplement is NOT the only way to prevent sickness."
     → Even if other ways exist, this supplement can still work.
        Statement remains valid. NOT MEANINGLESS.
     → NOT IMPLICIT ✗ (the "only" trap again)

Answer: Only Assumption I is implicit.
```

---

### Example 15 — Unless Statement:

```
Statement: "Unless the government takes immediate action, the river will
            be completely polluted within 5 years."
Assumptions:
I.  Government action can prevent river pollution.
II. River pollution is increasing progressively.
III. The current level of pollution will lead to complete pollution in 5 years if unchecked.

Analysis:
I: NEGATE → "Government action CANNOT prevent pollution."
   → Then "unless government acts, river pollutes" becomes irrelevant
      because even with action, pollution can't be stopped. ILLOGICAL ✓
   → IMPLICIT ✓

II: NEGATE → "Pollution is NOT increasing."
    → If pollution isn't growing, the urgency and the 5-year prediction
      make no sense ✓
    → IMPLICIT ✓

III: NEGATE → "Current pollution levels will NOT lead to complete pollution."
     → The specific prediction is part of the statement's claim ✓
     → IMPLICIT ✓ (the statement assumes this trajectory)

Answer: All three are implicit.
```

## 12. TYPE 8 — MULTIPLE STATEMENT ASSUMPTIONS

> Advanced format where two or more statements are given before asking about assumptions.

---

### Example 16 — Two Statements, Common Assumption:

```
Statements: 
A. The school should introduce digital learning tools.
B. The students need to be tech-savvy for future job markets.

Common Assumption: Digital tools help students become tech-savvy.
[This connects Statement A (digital tools in school) with
Statement B (need for tech-savviness in job market)]

Analysis:
NEGATE: "Digital tools do NOT help students become tech-savvy."
→ Then linking A (introducing digital tools) to B (tech-savviness for jobs) is ILLOGICAL ✓
→ IMPLICIT ✓
```

---

### Example 17 — Multiple Assumptions for One Statement:

```
Statement: "The government must introduce stricter laws to curb corruption
            and ensure public money is used efficiently."
Assumptions:
I.  Current laws against corruption are inadequate.
II. Stricter laws will effectively reduce corruption.
III. Corruption is a problem in how public money is used.
IV. All government officials are corrupt.

Analysis:
I: NEGATE → "Current laws ARE adequate."
   → If current laws are fine, introducing stricter ones is UNNECESSARY ✓
   → IMPLICIT ✓

II: NEGATE → "Stricter laws will NOT reduce corruption."
    → If laws don't help, introducing them is POINTLESS ✓
    → IMPLICIT ✓

III: NEGATE → "Corruption is NOT a problem in public money use."
     → The statement directly says laws should "ensure public money is used
        efficiently" — implying a current problem. ✓
     → IMPLICIT ✓

IV: NEGATE → "NOT all government officials are corrupt."
    → The statement says "curb corruption" — not that ALL officials are
      corrupt. The problem exists but doesn't imply universality. 
      Statement remains meaningful even if only some are corrupt. ✗
    → NOT IMPLICIT ✗ (too extreme — statement doesn't imply universality)

Answer: I, II, and III are implicit. IV is not.
```

## 13. MASTER TRICKS & SHORTCUTS

---

### 🚀 TRICK 1 — The Negation Test (Most Reliable!)

```
ALWAYS use the negation test when uncertain.

FORMULA:
Negate assumption → Does statement become meaningless?
YES → IMPLICIT ✓     NO → NOT IMPLICIT ✗

PRACTICE NEGATION PHRASES:
Original: "X is effective." → Negation: "X is NOT effective."
Original: "People want X." → Negation: "People do NOT want X."
Original: "X is possible." → Negation: "X is NOT possible."
Original: "There is a problem." → Negation: "There is NO problem."
```

---

### 🚀 TRICK 2 — The "ONLY" Red Flag

```
If an assumption contains "ONLY" or "EXCLUSIVELY" or "THE BEST":
→ It is USUALLY NOT IMPLICIT

REASON: Advice/advertisements rarely claim exclusivity unless stated.
"Read books to improve" doesn't assume books are the ONLY way.
"Take medicine for fever" doesn't assume medicine is the ONLY cure.

EXCEPTION: If the STATEMENT itself says "only" or "exclusively":
"Only trained professionals should handle this equipment."
→ Assumption: "Untrained people cannot handle this safely." ✓ (implied by "only")
```

---

### 🚀 TRICK 3 — The "Already Stated" Elimination

```
If the assumption is directly mentioned in the statement → NOT IMPLICIT

EXAMPLE:
Statement: "Since it's raining, carry an umbrella."
Assumption: "It is raining." ← DIRECTLY STATED, not implicit!

The statement said "since it's raining" — that's already in the statement.
This is a FACT in the statement, not an assumption.
```

---

### 🚀 TRICK 4 — The "Purpose Assumption" Rule

```
For ADVICE / ADVERTISEMENT / REQUEST statements:
The assumption that directly links the ACTION to the GOAL is ALWAYS implicit.

FORMULA: Statement = "Do X to achieve Y"
Implicit assumption = "X helps achieve Y" (the action-goal link)

Examples:
"Exercise to stay fit" → "Exercise helps stay fit" ✓
"Use our cream for fair skin" → "This cream makes skin fair" ✓
"Study regularly to get good grades" → "Regular study leads to good grades" ✓
```

---

### 🚀 TRICK 5 — The "Audience / Recipient" Assumption

```
For ADVICE / REQUEST / WARNING statements:
The assumption that the audience EXISTS and NEEDS the advice is often implicit.

"Don't drink and drive" → People do drink and drive (otherwise why warn?) ✓
"Apply now for this scholarship" → People want/need scholarships ✓
"Eat vegetables for health" → People want to be healthy ✓

BUT: If the audience is obvious from context, it may not be a tested assumption.
```

---

### 🚀 TRICK 6 — The "Extreme Statement" Detector

```
Assumptions that are EXTREME or ABSOLUTE are usually NOT IMPLICIT:

Watch for these red flags:
✗ "This is the ONLY solution"
✗ "ALL people believe..."
✗ "This ALWAYS works"
✗ "EVERYONE will benefit"
✗ "This is IMPOSSIBLE to achieve otherwise"

Statements rarely assume absolutes unless explicitly claiming them.
```

---

### 🚀 TRICK 7 — The "Common Sense" vs "Assumption" Distinction

```
Common sense facts are NOT implicit assumptions — they are universally known truths.

EXAMPLE:
Statement: "Turn off the gas stove after cooking."
Assumption: "Gas stoves can be turned off."
→ This is common sense / universal knowledge, not an assumption. ✗

Assumption: "Leaving the gas on after cooking is dangerous."
→ This is IMPLICIT (not everyone might realize the danger). ✓

RULE: If it's a universally known fact that requires no "taking for granted,"
it may not qualify as an implicit assumption.
(Note: This is debatable in exam contexts — use negation test to confirm.)
```

---

### 🚀 TRICK 8 — The "Cause-Effect" Assumption Pattern

```
Many statements carry a CAUSE-EFFECT assumption:

"If we plant trees, air quality will improve."
→ Implicit: "Trees improve air quality." (cause-effect link) ✓

"The government increased taxes because of the deficit."
→ Implicit: "Tax increase will help reduce the deficit." ✓
   Implicit: "There is a budget deficit." ✓

PATTERN: Whenever an action is justified by a reason,
the reason's EFFECTIVENESS is an implicit assumption.
```

---

### 🚀 TRICK 9 — The "Existence Assumption"

```
Whenever a statement refers to something to be done or addressed:
The EXISTENCE of the problem/condition/group is often assumed.

"Build more hospitals in rural areas."
→ Implicit: "Rural areas have insufficient hospitals." ✓
  (If sufficient, building more is unnecessary)

"Offer discounts to attract customers."
→ Implicit: "There are potential customers who would be attracted by discounts." ✓

FORMULA: Any intervention assumes the NEED for intervention.
```

---

### 🚀 TRICK 10 — The "Future Effectiveness" Assumption

```
Whenever a policy/action is proposed to SOLVE a future problem:
The assumption that the action WILL WORK in the future is implicit.

"Introduce traffic cameras to reduce accidents."
→ Implicit: "Traffic cameras will reduce accidents." ✓
  (If they won't work, why introduce them?)

"Ban plastic bags to reduce pollution."
→ Implicit: "Banning plastic bags will reduce pollution." ✓
```

---

### 🚀 TRICK 11 — The "Comparison" Trap

```
When a statement COMPARES or says "better/worse/more/less":
The implicit assumption is that the comparison is VALID and RELEVANT.

"Public transport is better than private vehicles for reducing pollution."
→ Implicit: "Public transport causes less pollution than private vehicles." ✓
→ Implicit: "Reducing pollution is a priority." ✓

"Our product gives better results than competitors."
→ Implicit: "The comparison has been made/tested." ✓
```

---

### 🚀 TRICK 12 — Quick Category Check

```
USE THIS TABLE when stuck:

STATEMENT TYPE → LOOK FOR THESE ASSUMPTIONS FIRST:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Advice          → Action is effective for the goal
                  Audience needs/wants the goal
                  Audience is capable of following advice

Advertisement   → Product delivers claimed benefit
                  Audience wants the benefit
                  Product is accessible

Warning         → Warned action is dangerous
                  People might do the warned action
                  Warning will be heeded

Policy          → Problem exists
                  Policy will solve it
                  Policy is implementable

Request         → Recipient is capable
                  Action is possible
                  Recipient will consider request

Conditional     → Cause-effect relationship is valid
                  No alternative causes exist
                  The condition is achievable
```

## 15. STEP-BY-STEP ATTACK STRATEGY

---

### The Universal 6-Step Method:

```
STEP 1: READ the statement carefully.
         Identify its TYPE:
         (Advice / Advertisement / Warning / Fact / Policy / Request / Conditional)

STEP 2: IDENTIFY the MAIN PURPOSE of the statement.
         What is it trying to DO? (Persuade? Warn? Inform? Request?)

STEP 3: READ each assumption carefully.
         For each assumption, ask: "Is this stated or implied?"

STEP 4: APPLY THE NEGATION TEST:
         Negate the assumption → Does the statement become meaningless?
         YES → Implicit ✓     NO → Not implicit ✗

STEP 5: CHECK FOR TRAPS:
         → Is it directly stated? (Not implicit)
         → Does it contain "only"/"exclusively"? (Usually not implicit)
         → Is it too extreme/absolute? (Usually not implicit)
         → Is it beyond what the statement implies? (Not implicit)

STEP 6: FINALIZE your answer by cross-checking all assumptions.
         If unsure between two options, use the "which makes the
         statement MORE meaningless when negated" as the tiebreaker.
```

---

### When You're Stuck — The Decision Flowchart:

```
Is the assumption DIRECTLY stated in the statement?
         │
    YES → NOT IMPLICIT ✗
    NO → Continue
         │
Is the assumption NECESSARY for the statement to make sense?
         │
    NO → NOT IMPLICIT ✗
    YES → Continue
         │
Does the assumption go BEYOND what the statement implies?
         │
    YES → NOT IMPLICIT ✗
    NO → Continue
         │
Does NEGATING it make the statement MEANINGLESS?
         │
    YES → IMPLICIT ✓
    NO → NOT IMPLICIT ✗
```

## 16. COMMON MISTAKE ALERTS

---

### ⚠️ MISTAKE 1 — Treating Stated Facts as Assumptions

```
Wrong: Statement says "Since roads are damaged..." 
       Student marks "Roads are damaged" as an implicit assumption.
Right: "Roads are damaged" is DIRECTLY STATED — it's a fact in the statement,
       not an implied assumption.

Fix: Any information given IN the statement = fact, not assumption.
```

---

### ⚠️ MISTAKE 2 — Accepting "Only/Exclusively" Assumptions

```
Wrong: "Take supplements to improve immunity" → 
       Student marks "Supplements are the only way to improve immunity" as implicit.
Right: The statement doesn't claim exclusivity. Even if other ways exist,
       supplements can still be one valid way.

Fix: "Only" assumptions FAIL the negation test — if negated, statement still works.
```

---

### ⚠️ MISTAKE 3 — Adding External Knowledge

```
Wrong: Statement: "New metro rail will reduce traffic congestion."
       Student adds assumption: "Metro rail is an eco-friendly transport."
Right: Eco-friendliness is NOT mentioned in the statement.
       Don't add things that are true in real life but not in the statement.

Fix: Stick STRICTLY to what the statement implies. No real-world additions.
```

---

### ⚠️ MISTAKE 4 — Confusing Possibility with Necessity

```
Wrong: Marking "People might like this new policy" as implicit assumption.
Right: Possibility ≠ necessity. The assumption must be REQUIRED
       for the statement to work, not just a possible outcome.

Fix: Ask "MUST this be true for the statement to make sense?"
     If it's just "might be true," it's not a reliable assumption.
```

---

### ⚠️ MISTAKE 5 — Accepting All "Common Sense" Assumptions

```
Wrong: Statement: "Buy warm clothes before winter."
       Student marks: "Winter is cold" as a crucial implicit assumption.
Right: "Winter is cold" is common knowledge — universally known.
       Some exams consider this implicit, others don't.
       
Fix: Focus on assumptions specific to the statement's logic,
     not universally known facts. When in doubt, use negation test.
```

---

### ⚠️ MISTAKE 6 — Marking Conclusions as Assumptions

```
Wrong: Statement: "Students who study abroad gain global perspective."
       Student marks: "Studying abroad is beneficial" as assumption.
Right: "Studying abroad is beneficial" is what the statement CONCLUDES,
       not what it assumes. It's a conclusion drawn from the statement.

Fix: Assumption = what must be true BEFORE the statement can be made.
     Conclusion = what can be derived FROM the statement.
```

---

### ⚠️ MISTAKE 7 — Over-Reaching Assumptions

```
Wrong: Statement: "The government built new roads in the district."
       Student marks: "People in the district are poor" as implicit.
Right: The statement is about road construction.
       Nothing implies poverty — roads serve many purposes.

Fix: Keep assumptions MINIMAL and DIRECTLY LINKED to the statement's logic.
     Don't over-interpret or add unsupported inferences.
```

---

### ⚠️ MISTAKE 8 — Ignoring Scope of Statement

```
Wrong: Statement: "Morning exercise benefits elderly people."
       Student marks: "All people should exercise in the morning" as implicit.
Right: Statement is specifically about elderly people.
       It doesn't imply ALL people should exercise in the morning.

Fix: Match the SCOPE of the assumption to the SCOPE of the statement.
     Don't generalize beyond what the statement addresses.
```

## 18. ANSWERS & SOLUTIONS

### SET 1 — Easy Advice and Suggestion (Q1–Q10)

**A1. (C) Both I and II are implicit**
I: NEGATE → "Water does NOT prevent dehydration." → Advising drinking water for dehydration prevention is ILLOGICAL ✓ IMPLICIT
II: NEGATE → "People do NOT forget to drink water." → Even if people remember, the advice is still useful as a health tip. Statement remains meaningful ✗
Actually: II is less clearly implicit. The advice works regardless of whether people forget.
Revised Answer: **(A) Only Assumption I is implicit**
I: Implicit ✓ | II: Not strongly implicit (advice doesn't require forgetfulness) ✗

**A2. (A) Only Assumption I is implicit**
I: NEGATE → "Early reading does NOT benefit children." → Then why encourage it? ILLOGICAL ✓ IMPLICIT
II: NEGATE → "NOT all children dislike reading." → The advice to encourage reading still makes sense even if many children like reading (could encourage those who don't). NOT MEANINGLESS ✗

**A3. (C) Both I and II are implicit**
I: NEGATE → "Doctors do NOT provide medical advice." → Then consulting them before medication is POINTLESS ✓ IMPLICIT
II: NEGATE → "Taking medicine without consultation is NOT harmful." → If it's safe, why insist on consultation? Advice becomes UNNECESSARY ✓ IMPLICIT

## 🏆 TOP 10 EXAM TIPS

1. **The Negation Test is your best friend** — use it on EVERY assumption you're unsure about. Negate it, check if the statement becomes meaningless. This single test resolves 90% of all questions accurately.

2. **"Only/Exclusively" assumptions almost always FAIL** — when an assumption says the stated action is the ONLY way or method, it almost certainly is NOT implicit. Statements give advice; they don't claim exclusivity unless explicitly stated.

3. **If it's in the statement, it's not an assumption** — directly stated facts are NOT implicit assumptions. They're already given. Only search for what's HIDDEN between the lines.

4. **The action-goal link is ALWAYS implicit in advice statements** — whenever a statement says "do X to achieve Y," the assumption that "X helps achieve Y" is always implicit. This is the most tested pattern in exams.

5. **Existence of the problem is usually implicit in policy statements** — whenever a policy or action is taken to solve something, the assumption that the problem EXISTS is implicit. If no problem exists, no action is needed.

6. **Don't add real-world knowledge** — the assumption must flow from the statement's logic, not from what you know about the world. Eco-friendliness, economic theories, historical facts — none of these are valid to import unless implied by the statement.

7. **"Will definitely/always/never" assumptions are usually NOT implicit** — statements make specific claims, not universal guarantees. "This medicine will cure ALL patients" is not implied by "take this medicine for your condition."

8. **Audience capability/willingness is often implicit** — for requests and appeals, the assumption that the target audience CAN do what's asked and WILL consider it is usually implicit (otherwise the request is pointless).

9. **Match the SCOPE of the assumption to the SCOPE of the statement** — if the statement is about "elderly people," an assumption about "all people" likely fails the test. Keep the scope consistent.

10. **When two assumptions seem both implicit, check which is more DIRECT** — sometimes both can be negated to make the statement meaningless, but one is more directly necessary. The more directly linked to the statement's core purpose is more clearly implicit. In exam context, if genuinely both work, choose "both implicit."
