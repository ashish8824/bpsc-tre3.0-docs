## 1. CORE CONCEPTS & THE BIG IDEA

---

### 🔑 The One Big Idea:

> **Pipes & Cisterns is exactly Time & Work in disguise.**
>
> - Filling a tank = completing a job
> - Inlet pipe = a worker building
> - Outlet pipe / Leak = a worker destroying
> - Tank capacity = total work to be done

---

### The Mental Model:

```
TANK (capacity = 1 unit or LCM units)
          │
    ┌─────┴─────┐
    │           │
INLET PIPES   OUTLET PIPES / LEAKS
(+ve rate)      (−ve rate)

Net Rate = Sum of Inlet rates − Sum of Outlet rates

If Net Rate > 0 → Tank is FILLING
If Net Rate < 0 → Tank is EMPTYING
If Net Rate = 0 → Tank level stays CONSTANT
```

---

### Why This Works:

- Pipe A fills tank in **n hours** → fills **1/n** of tank per hour
- Pipe B empties tank in **m hours** → empties **1/m** of tank per hour
- Both open → Net rate = **1/n − 1/m** per hour

If 1/n > 1/m → Tank fills. Time = **1 / (1/n − 1/m)**
If 1/n < 1/m → Tank empties. Time = **1 / (1/m − 1/n)**

---

### Sign Convention — Never Forget:

| Pipe Type | Effect | Rate Sign |
|-----------|--------|-----------|
| Inlet / Fill pipe | Fills tank | **POSITIVE (+)** |
| Outlet / Drain pipe | Empties tank | **NEGATIVE (−)** |
| Leak | Empties tank | **NEGATIVE (−)** |

## 2. FUNDAMENTAL TERMINOLOGY

---

### 💧 Cistern / Tank
The container that stores water. Total capacity = **1 unit** (or LCM in LCM method).

---

### 🔵 Inlet Pipe
A pipe that **supplies water INTO** the tank. It has a **positive** contribution.

> "Pipe A can fill the tank in 6 hours" → A's rate = **+1/6 per hour**

---

### 🔴 Outlet Pipe
A pipe that **removes water FROM** the tank. It has a **negative** contribution.

> "Pipe B can empty the tank in 8 hours" → B's rate = **−1/8 per hour**

---

### 💦 Leak
A crack or hole in the tank that allows water to escape. Acts exactly like an outlet pipe.

> "There is a leak that can empty the tank in 10 hours" → Leak rate = **−1/10 per hour**

---

### ⏱️ Time to Fill / Empty
How long it takes for the tank to go from empty → full (fill) or full → empty (drain).

---

### 📊 Efficiency / Rate
The fraction of tank filled or emptied per unit time.

---

## 3. FUNDAMENTAL FORMULAS

---

### Formula 1: Single Inlet Pipe

$$\text{Time to fill} = n \text{ hours (given directly)}$$
$$\text{Rate} = \frac{1}{n} \text{ per hour}$$

---

### Formula 2: Two Inlet Pipes Together

$$\boxed{T = \frac{A \times B}{A + B}}$$

Where A and B are the hours each pipe takes alone.

---

### Formula 3: One Inlet + One Outlet

$$\text{Net Rate} = \frac{1}{A} - \frac{1}{B}$$

$$\text{Time to fill} = \frac{AB}{B - A} \quad \text{(only when B > A, i.e., fill faster than drain)}$$

---

### Formula 4: Multiple Pipes

$$\text{Net Rate} = \sum(\text{Inlet rates}) - \sum(\text{Outlet rates})$$

$$\text{Time} = \frac{1}{\text{Net Rate}} = \frac{\text{Total Capacity}}{\text{Net Rate}}$$

---

### Formula 5: Finding Leak Time

If tank fills in **T₁ hours** (without leak) and in **T₂ hours** (with leak open):

$$\frac{1}{\text{Leak}} = \frac{1}{T_1} - \frac{1}{T_2}$$

$$\text{Leak empties in} = \frac{T_1 \times T_2}{T_2 - T_1}$$

---

### Formula 6: Pipe Opened After Partial Fill

$$\text{Work remaining} = 1 - \text{work already done}$$
$$\text{Time remaining} = \frac{\text{Work remaining}}{\text{Net rate with new pipe(s)}}$$

## 4. THE LCM METHOD FOR PIPES

---

> 🔥 **Use this for ALL multi-pipe problems. It eliminates fractions!**

---

### Steps:

**Step 1:** Find LCM of all the given times (hours).

**Step 2:** Set LCM = **Total Capacity of Tank** (in units).

**Step 3:** Each pipe's rate (units/hour):
- Inlet pipe rate = Total Capacity / Hours to fill (POSITIVE)
- Outlet pipe rate = Total Capacity / Hours to empty (NEGATIVE)

**Step 4:** Net rate = algebraic sum of all pipe rates.

**Step 5:** Time = Total Capacity / Net rate.

---

### Example of LCM Method:

> Pipe A fills in 6 hrs, B fills in 4 hrs, C empties in 12 hrs. All open. Time to fill?

**Step 1:** LCM(6, 4, 12) = **12 units**

**Step 2:** Rates:
- A = 12/6 = **+2 units/hr**
- B = 12/4 = **+3 units/hr**
- C = 12/12 = **−1 unit/hr**

**Step 3:** Net = 2 + 3 − 1 = **4 units/hr**

**Step 4:** Time = 12/4 = **3 hours** ✓

---

### Why LCM is Faster:

| Fraction Method | LCM Method |
|----------------|------------|
| 1/6 + 1/4 − 1/12 | 2 + 3 − 1 = 4 |
| = 2/12 + 3/12 − 1/12 | Net = 4 units/hr |
| = 4/12 = 1/3 | Time = 12/4 = 3 hrs |
| Time = 3 hrs | Same answer, no fractions! |

## 5. BASIC TO ADVANCED EXAMPLES

---

### ─── BASIC LEVEL ───

#### Example 1: Two Inlet Pipes Together

> Pipe A fills a tank in 10 hours and Pipe B in 15 hours. Both pipes are opened together. How long to fill the tank?

**Formula Method:**
$$T = \frac{10 \times 15}{10 + 15} = \frac{150}{25} = 6 \text{ hours}$$

**LCM Method:**
LCM(10, 15) = 30 units.
A = 30/10 = 3 units/hr. B = 30/15 = 2 units/hr.
Net = 5 units/hr. Time = 30/5 = **6 hours** ✓

---

#### Example 2: One Inlet + One Outlet

> Pipe A fills in 8 hours. Pipe B empties in 12 hours. Both open simultaneously with empty tank. Time to fill?

**LCM Method:**
LCM(8, 12) = 24 units.
A = 24/8 = +3 units/hr. B = 24/12 = −2 units/hr.
Net = 3 − 2 = 1 unit/hr.
Time = 24/1 = **24 hours**

**Verify (Formula):**
$$T = \frac{8 \times 12}{12 - 8} = \frac{96}{4} = 24 \text{ hours}$$ ✓

---

#### Example 3: Three Pipes — Mix of Inlet and Outlet

> Pipes A, B fill in 6 hrs and 8 hrs. C empties in 12 hrs. All open together. Time to fill?

**LCM Method:**
LCM(6, 8, 12) = 24 units.
A = +4, B = +3, C = −2.
Net = 4 + 3 − 2 = **5 units/hr**.
Time = 24/5 = **4.8 hours = 4 hrs 48 min**

---

#### Example 4: Find Individual Rate from Combined

> Two pipes together fill a tank in 6 hours. Pipe A alone takes 10 hours. How long does Pipe B alone take?

$$\frac{1}{B} = \frac{1}{6} - \frac{1}{10} = \frac{5 - 3}{30} = \frac{2}{30} = \frac{1}{15}$$

**Pipe B takes 15 hours alone.**

**LCM Method:**
LCM(6, 10) = 30. Together rate = 5. A's rate = 3. B's rate = 5 − 3 = 2.
B's time = 30/2 = **15 hours** ✓

---

#### Example 5: All Outlets Open — Tank Empties

> A full tank has 3 outlet pipes: A empties in 4 hrs, B in 6 hrs, C in 12 hrs. All open. How long to empty?

LCM(4, 6, 12) = 12 units.
A = 3, B = 2, C = 1 (all outlets).
Net drain = 3 + 2 + 1 = 6 units/hr.
Time = 12/6 = **2 hours**

---

#### Example 6: Time to Empty with Outlet Rates

> A full tank has Pipe A (inlet, fills in 8 hrs) and Pipe B (outlet, empties in 5 hrs). Both open. How long to empty full tank?

Here outlet is faster than inlet → tank **empties**.
Net drain rate = 1/5 − 1/8 = 8/40 − 5/40 = 3/40 per hour.
Time to empty = 1/(3/40) = **40/3 ≈ 13.33 hours**

**LCM Method:**
LCM(5, 8) = 40 units.
A = +5 (fill), B = −8 (drain). Net = 5 − 8 = −3 (draining!).
Time to empty full tank = 40/3 = **13.33 hours** ✓

---

### ─── INTERMEDIATE LEVEL ───

#### Example 7: One Pipe Opened Late

> Pipe A fills in 10 hrs. Pipe B fills in 15 hrs. A starts alone. After 3 hrs, B also opens. Total time to fill?

**LCM Method:**
LCM(10, 15) = 30 units.
A = 3, B = 2.

Work by A in 3 hrs = 3 × 3 = 9 units.
Remaining = 30 − 9 = 21 units.
Both together = 3 + 2 = 5 units/hr.
Time for remaining = 21/5 = 4.2 hrs.

**Total = 3 + 4.2 = 7.2 hours = 7 hrs 12 min**

---

#### Example 8: One Pipe Closed Early

> Pipes A and B fill tank in 8 hrs and 12 hrs. Both start together. After 4 hrs, B is closed. How much more time for A alone to complete?

**LCM Method:**
LCM(8, 12) = 24 units.
A = 3, B = 2.

In 4 hrs (both): 4 × 5 = 20 units.
Remaining = 4 units.
A alone: 4/3 hrs.

**Total = 4 + 4/3 = 5⅓ hours = 5 hrs 20 min**

---

#### Example 9: When is Tank Half Full?

> Pipe A fills in 12 hrs, Pipe B in 16 hrs. Both open. When is the tank half full?

LCM(12, 16) = 48 units.
A = 4, B = 3. Together = 7 units/hr.
Half tank = 24 units.
Time = 24/7 = **3 3/7 hours ≈ 3 hrs 26 min**

---

#### Example 10: Pipe Opened and Closed Alternately

> Pipe A fills in 6 hrs, Pipe B empties in 10 hrs. Both are alternately opened every hour (A first). How long to fill empty tank?

**LCM Method:**
LCM(6, 10) = 30 units.
A = 5 units/hr, B = 3 units/hr (drain).

In 2-hr cycle: A fills 5, then B drains 3. Net = **+2 units per 2-hr cycle**.

Cycles needed = 30/2 = 15 cycles = **30 hours**.

But check if exactly 30 units at end of 15th cycle:
After 14 cycles = 28 units. Start of cycle 15: A fills 5 → 33 units?
Wait — tank can't exceed 30. After 14 cycles: 28 units.
Hour 29: A fills 5 → 33 would exceed capacity. Only 2 units needed.
Time for 2 units by A = 2/5 hr.

**Total = 28 hrs + 2/5 hr = 28 hrs 24 min**

---

#### Example 11: Finding Total Capacity

> Two pipes fill a tank in 20 and 30 minutes. An outlet empties 3 gallons/minute. When all open, tank fills in 1 hour. Find capacity.

Let capacity = C gallons.
Fill rates: C/20 + C/30 − 3 = C/60.

$$\frac{C}{20} + \frac{C}{30} - 3 = \frac{C}{60}$$

Multiply by 60:
$$3C + 2C - 180 = C$$
$$5C - 180 = C$$
$$4C = 180$$
$$C = 45 \text{ gallons}$$

---

#### Example 12: Pipes Opened One After Another

> Pipe A fills in 12 hrs. Pipe B fills in 18 hrs. A is opened, then after some time B is also opened. Tank fills in total 8 hrs. How long was A open before B joined?

Let A work alone for **t** hours, then both work for **(8 − t)** hours.

LCM(12, 18) = 36. A = 3, B = 2.

Work: 3t + 5(8−t) = 36
3t + 40 − 5t = 36
−2t = −4
t = 2 hrs.

**A was opened 2 hours before B.**

---

#### Example 13: Pipe Shut to Save Time

> Three pipes A, B, C fill in 10, 12, 15 hrs. All start together. C is closed 2 hrs before tank is full. Total time?

LCM(10, 12, 15) = 60. A=6, B=5, C=4.

Let total time = T hrs. C works for (T−2) hrs.
Work by A + B + C = 60:
6T + 5T + 4(T−2) = 60
6T + 5T + 4T − 8 = 60
15T = 68
T = 68/15 = **4 hrs 32 min**

---

#### Example 14: Pipe Opened After Partial Fill

> Pipe A fills tank in 20 hrs. When tank is 1/4 full, Pipe B (which fills in 15 hrs) is also opened. How long from the start until tank is full?

Time for A to fill 1/4 tank = (1/4) × 20 = **5 hrs**.

Remaining = 3/4 tank.

LCM(20, 15) = 60. A = 3, B = 4. Together = 7/hr.
3/4 of 60 = 45 units. Time = 45/7 = 6 3/7 hrs.

**Total = 5 + 6 3/7 = 11 3/7 ≈ 11.43 hours**

### ─── ADVANCED LEVEL ───

#### Example 15: Complex Multi-Pipe Scenario

> Tank has 3 inlet pipes (A, B, C) filling in 6, 8, 12 hrs and 2 outlet pipes (D, E) emptying in 10, 15 hrs. All open. Time to fill?

LCM(6, 8, 12, 10, 15) = 120 units.
A = +20, B = +15, C = +10, D = −12, E = −8.
Net = 20 + 15 + 10 − 12 − 8 = **25 units/hr**.
Time = 120/25 = **4.8 hours = 4 hrs 48 min**

---

#### Example 16: When Should a Pipe be Shut?

> Pipes A and B together fill in 6 hrs. Pipe C drains in 8 hrs. All open. When should C be shut so tank fills in exactly 9 hrs?

LCM(6, 8) = 24. Together (A+B) = 4/hr. C = 3/hr drain.
Net when all open = 4 − 3 = 1 unit/hr.
Net when only A+B = 4 units/hr.

Let C be open for **t** hrs, then closed for **(9−t)** hrs.

Work: 1×t + 4×(9−t) = 24
t + 36 − 4t = 24
−3t = −12
t = 4 hrs.

**Close C after 4 hours.**

---

#### Example 17: Tank Fills Due to Leakage Difference

> An inlet pipe fills in 6 hrs. Due to a leak at the bottom, it takes 8 hrs to fill. If the tank is full and inlet is closed, how long does leak take to empty?

$$\frac{1}{\text{Leak}} = \frac{1}{6} - \frac{1}{8} = \frac{4-3}{24} = \frac{1}{24}$$

**Leak alone empties in 24 hours.**

---

#### Example 18: Two Leaks Problem

> A full tank has two leaks. First leak empties in 30 hrs, second in 45 hrs. An inlet pipe fills in 20 hrs. How long to empty full tank with all open?

LCM(30, 45, 20) = 180.
Inlet = +9, Leak1 = −6, Leak2 = −4.
Net = 9 − 6 − 4 = **−1 unit/hr** (draining!).
Time to empty = 180/1 = **180 hours**

---

#### Example 19: Pipe Fills, Then Empties — How Long Total?

> Pipe A fills tank in 5 hrs. After tank is full, Pipe B empties it in 9 hrs. How many hrs for one complete fill-drain cycle?

Fill time = 5 hrs. Empty time = 9 hrs. Total cycle = 5 + 9 = **14 hours**

---

#### Example 20: Find How Long Drain Pipe was Open

> Pipe A fills tank in 12 hrs. Tank was filled in 16 hrs because drain Pipe B was open for some time. Pipe B drains in 24 hrs. How long was B open?

LCM(12, 24) = 24. A = 2, B = 1 (drain).

Let B open for **t** hrs.
A works all 16 hrs. B works t hrs (draining).

Work: 2×16 − 1×t = 24
32 − t = 24
t = 8 hrs.

**Pipe B was open for 8 hours.**

---

#### Example 21: Pipes Opened on Alternate Hours

> Pipe A fills in 8 hrs. Pipe B fills in 12 hrs. They are opened alternatively each hour, A starting first. When does tank get full?

LCM(8, 12) = 24 units.
A = 3 units/hr. B = 2 units/hr.

2-hr cycle = 3 + 2 = 5 units.

In 4 complete cycles (8 hrs): 4 × 5 = 20 units.
Remaining = 4 units.

Hour 9: A fills 3 units → total = 23. Remaining = 1.
Hour 10: B fills 2/hr. Time for 1 unit = 1/2 hr.

**Total = 9 + 1/2 = 9.5 hours = 9 hrs 30 min**

---

#### Example 22: Three Pipes — Find Capacity

> Pipes A, B fill at 10 L/min and 6 L/min. Pipe C drains at 4 L/min. Tank fills in 40 min with all open. Find capacity.

Net rate = 10 + 6 − 4 = 12 L/min.
Capacity = 12 × 40 = **480 litres**

---

#### Example 23: Worker + Pipe Analogy

> Tap A fills a drum in 4 min, Tap B in 6 min, Tap C in 8 min. All three start simultaneously. Tap A is closed after 2 min. How long does it take in total to fill the drum?

LCM(4, 6, 8) = 24 units.
A = 6, B = 4, C = 3 units/min.

First 2 min (all 3): 2 × (6+4+3) = 2 × 13 = 26 units.
Remaining = 24 − 26 = −2 units? Over-filled!

Wait — 26 > 24, so tank fills before 2 min.
Net rate = 13. Time = 24/13 = **1 min 50.8 sec ≈ 1.85 min**

---

#### Example 24: Ratio of Pipes' Rates

> Pipes A and B fill a tank in the ratio 2:3 (in terms of rate). Together they fill in 10 hours. Find individual times.

Let A's rate = 2k, B's rate = 3k.
Together = 5k = 1/10 → k = 1/50.

A's rate = 2/50 = 1/25 → **A alone = 25 hrs**
B's rate = 3/50 → **B alone = 50/3 = 16⅔ hrs**

## 6. LEAK PROBLEMS

---

### 🔑 Core Concept:

A **leak** is an unintended outlet. It empties the tank. It always has a **negative** rate.

---

### The Standard Leak Formula:

If tank fills in **A** hours (no leak) and in **B** hours (with leak):

$$\boxed{\text{Leak empties in} = \frac{A \times B}{B - A}}$$

> Note: B > A always (leak makes filling slower).

---

### The Reverse — Leak Empties, Pipe Fills:

If tank is **full** and:
- Inlet fills in A hrs
- Leak empties in L hrs
- Together (inlet + leak) tank empties in E hrs

$$\frac{1}{\text{L}} = \frac{1}{\text{A}} + \frac{1}{\text{E}}$$

Wait — re-read:
$$\frac{1}{A} - \frac{1}{L} = -\frac{1}{E}$$
$$\frac{1}{L} = \frac{1}{A} + \frac{1}{E}$$

---

#### Example 25: Standard Leak Problem

> A pipe fills a tank in 5 hrs. A leak at the bottom empties it in 7 hrs. How long to fill the tank?

Net rate = 1/5 − 1/7 = 7/35 − 5/35 = 2/35.
Time = 35/2 = **17.5 hours**

---

#### Example 26: Find Leak from Two Fill Times

> Without leak: fills in 10 hrs. With leak: fills in 14 hrs. How long for leak to empty full tank?

$$\text{Leak} = \frac{10 \times 14}{14 - 10} = \frac{140}{4} = 35 \text{ hours}$$

---

#### Example 27: Leak Discovered Mid-Fill

> Pipe fills in 12 hrs. After 3 hrs, a leak appears. Tank takes 4 more hrs to fill. Find leak rate.

In 3 hrs, pipe fills 3/12 = 1/4.
Remaining = 3/4.
Time with leak = 4 hrs. Combined rate = (3/4)/4 = 3/16 per hr.
Pipe rate = 1/12 per hr.
Leak rate = 1/12 − 3/16 = 4/48 − 9/48 = −5/48 (draining).

Wait — if combined rate = 3/16 and pipe = 1/12:
Leak drain rate = pipe rate − combined rate = 1/12 − 3/16 = 4/48 − 9/48 = −5/48.

This is negative meaning drain. So:
$$\text{Leak empties in} = \frac{48}{5} = 9.6 \text{ hrs}$$

---

#### Example 28: Multiple Leaks

> A full tank has two leaks emptying it in 15 hrs and 20 hrs. An inlet fills in 10 hrs. All open. How long to empty?

LCM(15, 20, 10) = 60.
Inlet = +6, Leak1 = −4, Leak2 = −3.
Net = 6 − 4 − 3 = **−1** (draining).
Time to empty = 60/1 = **60 hours**

## 7. PIPE OPENS/CLOSES AT DIFFERENT TIMES

---

### Strategy:

1. Divide the timeline into **phases**.
2. Calculate work done in each phase.
3. Add up until total = 1 (or LCM units).

---

#### Example 29: B Joins, Then C Joins

> A fills in 6 hrs. B fills in 9 hrs. C empties in 12 hrs.
> A opens at t=0. B joins at t=2. C also opens at t=4. When is tank full?

LCM(6, 9, 12) = 36.
A = 6, B = 4, C = 3 (drain).

Phase 1 (0 to 2 hrs): Only A.
Work = 6 × 2 = 12 units. Remaining = 24.

Phase 2 (2 to 4 hrs): A + B.
Rate = 6 + 4 = 10/hr. Work = 10 × 2 = 20. Remaining = 4.

Phase 3 (after 4 hrs): A + B + C.
Rate = 6 + 4 − 3 = 7/hr. Time = 4/7 hr.

**Total = 2 + 2 + 4/7 = 4 4/7 hours**

---

#### Example 30: Pipe Closed at Specific Hour

> Pipes A (fill, 8 hrs), B (fill, 12 hrs), C (drain, 6 hrs). A and B open at t=0. C opens at t=3. How long to fill?

LCM(8, 12, 6) = 24.
A = 3, B = 2, C = 4 (drain).

Phase 1 (0 to 3 hrs): A + B.
Work = 5 × 3 = 15. Remaining = 9.

Phase 2 (after 3): A + B − C = 3 + 2 − 4 = 1/hr.
Time = 9/1 = 9 hrs.

**Total = 3 + 9 = 12 hours**

## 8. TANK PARTIALLY FILLED PROBLEMS

---

### Key Scenarios:

| Scenario | Approach |
|---------|---------|
| Tank is 1/3 full | Remaining = 2/3 |
| Tank is x% full | Remaining = (100−x)% |
| x units already filled | Remaining = Total − x |

---

#### Example 31: Tank Already Has Water

> A tank is 2/5 full. Pipe A fills it in 10 hrs, Pipe B drains in 15 hrs. Both open. How long to fill completely?

Remaining = 3/5 of tank.
Net rate = 1/10 − 1/15 = 3/30 − 2/30 = 1/30.
Time = (3/5)/(1/30) = (3/5) × 30 = **18 hours**

---

#### Example 32: Tank Has Water, Find When Empty

> A tank is 3/4 full. An outlet empties in 8 hrs. An inlet fills in 12 hrs. Both open. When does it become full?

Net rate = 1/12 − 1/8 = 2/24 − 3/24 = −1/24 (draining!).
Remaining to fill = 1/4.

But tank is draining! So it will never fill. Instead:
Time to empty 3/4 = (3/4)/(1/24) = (3/4) × 24 = **18 hours to empty completely**

#### Example 33: Two Outlets, No Inlet

> A full tank has Outlet A (empties in 4 hrs) and Outlet B (empties in 6 hrs). Both open. How long to empty?

$$T = \frac{4 \times 6}{4 + 6} = \frac{24}{10} = 2.4 \text{ hours} = 2 \text{ hrs 24 min}$$

#### Example 34: Inlet + Two Outlets

> Inlet fills in 10 hrs. Outlet X empties in 15 hrs. Outlet Y empties in 20 hrs. All open. Time to fill empty tank?

LCM(10, 15, 20) = 60.
Inlet = +6, X = −4, Y = −3.
Net = 6 − 4 − 3 = −1 (draining!).

**With these pipes, tank can never be filled — it drains.**

If asked how long to empty FULL tank:
Time = 60/1 = **60 hours**

#### Example 35: Three Outlets, Find Time

> Full tank. Outlets A, B, C empty in 3, 4, 6 hrs. How long to empty all?

LCM(3, 4, 6) = 12.
A = 4, B = 3, C = 2. All drain. Net = 9 units/hr.
Time = 12/9 = **1 hr 20 min**

## 10. FINDING TIME WHEN PIPE IS CLOSED

---

### Strategy:

Let the time the pipe is **closed** (or **open**) be **t**.
Set up equation using total work = Total capacity.

---

#### Example 36: Find Duration Pipe Was Shut

> Pipes A, B, C fill in 6, 9, 12 hrs. All start together but B is closed for some time. Tank fills in 5 hrs. How long was B closed?

LCM(6, 9, 12) = 36. A=6, B=4, C=3.

A and C work all 5 hrs: (6+3) × 5 = 45 units (but tank = 36, excess is 9 units).

Hmm — if B also worked some time, total could be more. Re-approach:

Let B work for **x** hrs (closed for 5−x hrs).
6×5 + 4×x + 3×5 = 36
30 + 4x + 15 = 36
4x = 36 − 45 = −9 → x = negative? Impossible.

Re-check: A=6, C=3. In 5 hrs just A+C = 45 units > 36. Tank overfills without B. So even without B, tank fills before 5 hrs.

**Corrected question:** Tank fills in 3 hrs with all. Find how long B is closed if tank fills in 4 hrs.

6×4 + 4×x + 3×4 = 36
24 + 4x + 12 = 36
4x = 0 → x = 0 (B not closed at all for these numbers).

---

#### Example 37: Pipe Opened Late — Find Exact Time

> Pipe A fills tank in 15 hrs. Pipe B fills in 10 hrs. A starts alone. B is opened after some time and together they finish in total 9 hrs. How late was B opened?

LCM(15, 10) = 30. A = 2, B = 3.

A works all 9 hrs: 2 × 9 = 18 units.
B works for **t** hrs: 3 × t units.
Total = 18 + 3t = 30.
3t = 12 → t = 4 hrs.

B worked for 4 hrs → B was opened after (9 − 4) = **5 hours**

## 11. QUICK TRICKS & SHORTCUTS

---

### 🚀 TRICK 1: Two-Pipe Formula

$$T = \frac{A \times B}{A + B} \quad \text{(both inlets)}$$

$$T = \frac{A \times B}{B - A} \quad \text{(one inlet A, one outlet B, and B > A)}$$

---

### 🚀 TRICK 2: LCM — Always Use for 3+ Pipes

Set Total = LCM. Find each rate. Add with signs. Divide.

---

### 🚀 TRICK 3: Leak Formula

$$\text{Leak time} = \frac{\text{Fill time (no leak)} \times \text{Fill time (with leak)}}{\text{Fill time (with leak)} - \text{Fill time (no leak)}}$$

$$= \frac{A \times B}{B - A}$$

---

### 🚀 TRICK 4: If Tank Drains, Net Rate is Negative

When outlet > inlet → multiply net rate by −1 for time to empty.
Time = Total capacity / |Net rate|.

---

### 🚀 TRICK 5: Alternating Pipes — 2-Cycle Method

Find work per 2-hr cycle.
Full cycles = Total / Cycle work.
Handle remaining carefully (check which pipe runs last).

---

### 🚀 TRICK 6: Pipe Opened After t Hours

Work by lone pipe in t hrs = rate × t.
Remaining = Total − work done.
Then solve for time with combined rate.

---

### 🚀 TRICK 7: Finding Capacity in Litres

If problem gives rates in L/min:
Capacity = Net rate × Time to fill.

---

### 🚀 TRICK 8: Ratio of Pipe Rates → Individual Times

If Rate_A : Rate_B = m : n, and together time = T:
Total rate = (m+n) parts = 1/T.
A alone = T(m+n)/m. B alone = T(m+n)/n.

---

### 🚀 TRICK 9: Pipe Closed Before Completion

If pipe X is closed **k hrs before** completion:
Time X works = (Total time − k).
Set up: Work by all × full time − Work X missed = Total capacity.

---

### 🚀 TRICK 10: Three Pipes — Two Open Alternately, Third Always

Always-on pipe works full time. Others work alternately.
Add: (Always-on rate × T) + (Alternating net) = Capacity.

---

### 🚀 TRICK 11: If Fill Rate = Drain Rate → Tank Stays Constant

1/A = 1/B → Net = 0 → Level never changes!
(Not a fill time problem — check question carefully.)

---

### 🚀 TRICK 12: Quick Drain Time from Full Tank

If only outlet pipes:
Time = LCM / (sum of all outlet rates in LCM units).

---

### 🚀 TRICK 13: Inlet Pipe + Multiple Leaks

Total drain rate = sum of all leak rates.
Net = Inlet rate − Total drain rate.
If positive → filling. If negative → draining.

---

### 🚀 TRICK 14: Convert to Minutes or Hours Consistently

Always use the SAME unit throughout. Never mix hours and minutes in one calculation.

---

### 🚀 TRICK 15: Use Fraction Form for Non-LCM Friendly Numbers

If numbers don't give clean LCM (e.g., 7, 11, 13):
Just use the fraction method directly:
Net = 1/A + 1/B − 1/C. Time = 1/Net.

## 12. COMMON MISTAKE ALERTS

---

### ⚠️ MISTAKE 1: Adding Times Instead of Rates

**Wrong:** A fills in 6 hrs, B in 4 hrs. Together = 6+4 = 10 hrs.
**Right:** Together = (6×4)/(6+4) = 24/10 = **2.4 hrs**

Always add **RATES** (1/time), never times directly!

---

### ⚠️ MISTAKE 2: Forgetting the Sign for Outlet/Leak

**Wrong:** Net rate = 1/A + 1/B (treating outlet as inlet)
**Right:** Net rate = 1/A − 1/B (outlet is negative!)

---

### ⚠️ MISTAKE 3: Leak Formula Denominator

**Wrong:** Leak time = A×B / (A+B)
**Right:** Leak time = A×B / (B−A) ← denominator is DIFFERENCE, not sum

Because: 1/leak = 1/A − 1/B = (B−A)/(AB) → Leak = AB/(B−A).

---

### ⚠️ MISTAKE 4: Assuming Tank Always Fills

If outlet is faster than inlet, the tank NEVER fills — it drains.
Always check: **Net rate > 0** (fills) or **Net rate < 0** (drains).

---

### ⚠️ MISTAKE 5: Not Checking Overflow in Alternating Problems

In alternating pipe problems, the tank may fill during the FILLING pipe's turn — don't go beyond capacity.

---

### ⚠️ MISTAKE 6: Confusing "Tank is Full in T hours" with "Together Rate"

"Fills in T hours" → Rate = 1/T per hour.
"Both pipes together fill in T hours" → Combined rate = 1/T per hour.

---

### ⚠️ MISTAKE 7: Misreading "How long to EMPTY" vs "How long to FILL"

Read carefully:
- Empty tank + inlet pipes → asks for FILL time
- Full tank + outlet pipes → asks for EMPTY time
- Mix of inlet + outlet → check which dominates!

---

### ⚠️ MISTAKE 8: Partial Tank — Forgetting Correct Fraction

"Tank is 1/3 full" → **Remaining = 2/3**, NOT 1/3!
"Tank is 3/4 empty" → **Tank is 1/4 full** → Remaining to fill = 3/4.

---

### ⚠️ MISTAKE 9: LCM with Rates, Not Times

In LCM method, always use:
Rate = LCM / **Time** (not LCM × Time).

---

### ⚠️ MISTAKE 10: Same Pipe, Different Contexts

In a question where pipe A "fills" and pipe B "empties", make sure you correctly assign + and − before computing. Don't flip midway.

Net rate              : 1/A + 1/B − 1/C ...

LCM METHOD (FASTEST)
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
Step 1: Total = LCM of all times
Step 2: Rate = Total / Time (+ for fill, − for drain)
Step 3: Net rate = sum of all signed rates
Step 4: Time = Total / Net rate

LEAK FORMULA
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
Remaining = 1 − fraction already filled
Time = Remaining / Net rate

ALTERNATE PIPES
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
Work per cycle = sum of rates in that cycle
Full cycles = Total work / Cycle work
Check remaining after last complete cycle carefully!

NET RATE CHECK
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
Net > 0  →  Tank FILLING  →  Time = Capacity / Net rate

Net = 0  →  Level CONSTANT → Tank never fills or empties

## 🏆 TOP EXAM TIPS

1. **Always check signs first** — inlet = positive, outlet = negative. Never mix these up.
2. **Use LCM method for 3+ pipes** — it's faster and eliminates all fractions.
3. **Net rate < 0?** → Tank is draining, not filling. Reread the question!
4. **Leak formula denominator = DIFFERENCE** not sum: Leak = AB/(B−A).
5. **Partial tank problems** → Remaining = 1 − already filled. Apply net rate to remaining.
6. **Alternate pipe problems** → Work per 2-cycle, find full cycles, handle remainder manually.
7. **Pipe opens/closes at different times** → Split into phases, calculate each phase separately.
8. **Check if tank fills before time is up** in complex problems — don't overshoot capacity.
9. **Capacity in litres** → Capacity = rate (L/hr) × time (hrs).
10. **If asked "when should pipe be closed"** → set up equation with unknown time variable t.
