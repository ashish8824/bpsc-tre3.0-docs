### ­čöĹ What is Probability?

> **Probability** measures how **likely** an event is to occur.
> It is always a number between **0 and 1** (inclusive).

```
0 ÔöÇÔöÇÔöÇÔöÇÔöÇÔöÇÔöÇÔöÇÔöÇÔöÇÔöÇÔöÇÔöÇÔöÇÔöÇÔöÇÔöÇÔöÇÔöÇÔöÇÔöÇÔöÇÔöÇÔöÇÔöÇÔöÇÔöÇÔöÇÔöÇÔöÇÔöÇÔöÇÔöÇÔöÇÔöÇÔöÇÔöÇÔöÇ 1

IMPOSSIBLE        EQUALLY            CERTAIN
(never happens)   LIKELY             (always happens)
                  (50-50)

P = 0          P = 0.5            P = 1
```

---

### The Core Formula:

$$\boxed{P(\text{Event}) = \frac{\text{Number of Favourable Outcomes}}{\text{Total Number of Possible Outcomes}}}$$

---

### The Golden Rules:

| Rule | Formula |
|------|---------|
| Probability range | $0 \leq P(E) \leq 1$ |
| Certain event | $P(S) = 1$ |
| Impossible event | $P(\emptyset) = 0$ |
| Complement rule | $P(E) + P(\bar{E}) = 1$ |
| Sum of all outcomes | $\sum P = 1$ |

## 2. CORE DEFINITIONS & TERMINOLOGY

---

### ­čôŽ Sample Space (S)

The **set of all possible outcomes** of an experiment.

| Experiment | Sample Space |
|-----------|-------------|
| Toss 1 coin | {H, T} Ôćĺ n(S) = 2 |
| Toss 2 coins | {HH, HT, TH, TT} Ôćĺ n(S) = 4 |
| Roll 1 die | {1,2,3,4,5,6} Ôćĺ n(S) = 6 |
| Roll 2 dice | 36 ordered pairs Ôćĺ n(S) = 36 |
| Draw a card | 52 cards Ôćĺ n(S) = 52 |

---

### ­čÄ» Event (E)

A **subset of the sample space** ÔÇö any collection of outcomes.

Example: "Getting an even number on a die" = {2, 4, 6} Ôćĺ 3 outcomes.

---

### Ôťů Favourable Outcomes

The outcomes that **satisfy the condition** of the event.

---

### ­čöä Complementary Event (─ĺ or A')

The event of **NOT A** happening.

$$P(\bar{E}) = 1 - P(E)$$

$$P(E) + P(\bar{E}) = 1 \quad \text{(always)}$$

---

### ­čÄ▓ Trial

Each **performance of an experiment** (each coin toss, die roll, etc.).

---

### ­čôŐ Equally Likely Outcomes

Outcomes where each has the **same probability** of occurring.

> A fair coin Ôćĺ P(H) = P(T) = 1/2 (equally likely)

---

### ­čöŚ Types of Probability:

| Type | Description |
|------|------------|
| **Classical** | Equal likelihood assumed. P = favourable/total |
| **Empirical** | Based on observed data/experiments |
| **Subjective** | Based on personal judgement or belief |

## 3. FUNDAMENTAL FORMULAS

---

### Formula 1: Basic Probability

$$P(E) = \frac{n(E)}{n(S)}$$

---

### Formula 2: Complement

$$P(\bar{E}) = 1 - P(E)$$

---

### Formula 3: Addition Rule (General)

$$P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A \cap B)$$

---

### Formula 4: Addition Rule (Mutually Exclusive)

$$P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B) \quad \text{[when } P(A \cap B) = 0\text{]}$$

---

### Formula 5: Multiplication Rule (General)

$$P(A \cap B) = P(A) \times P(B|A)$$

---

### Formula 6: Multiplication Rule (Independent Events)

$$P(A \cap B) = P(A) \times P(B)$$

---

### Formula 7: Conditional Probability

$$P(A|B) = \frac{P(A \cap B)}{P(B)}$$

---

### Formula 8: Bayes' Theorem

$$P(A|B) = \frac{P(B|A) \times P(A)}{P(B)}$$

---

### Formula 9: Total Probability

$$P(B) = P(B|A_1)P(A_1) + P(B|A_2)P(A_2) + \ldots$$

## 4. TYPES OF EVENTS

---

### Type 1: Mutually Exclusive Events

> Events that **cannot occur simultaneously**.
> If one happens, the other CANNOT happen at the same time.

$$A \cap B = \emptyset \quad \Rightarrow \quad P(A \cap B) = 0$$

**Examples:**
- Getting Head and Tail on the SAME coin toss
- Rolling a 2 and a 5 on the SAME die roll
- Being born on Monday and Tuesday (same day)

---

### Type 2: Independent Events

> Events where the occurrence of one does **NOT affect** the probability of the other.

$$P(A|B) = P(A) \quad \Rightarrow \quad P(A \cap B) = P(A) \times P(B)$$

**Examples:**
- Tossing two different coins
- Rolling two different dice
- Drawing with replacement

---

### Type 3: Dependent Events

> Events where the occurrence of one **DOES affect** the probability of the other.

$$P(A \cap B) = P(A) \times P(B|A)$$

**Examples:**
- Drawing cards without replacement (second draw depends on first)
- Selecting balls without replacement

---

### Type 4: Exhaustive Events

> Events that **cover ALL possible outcomes** of the sample space.

$$P(A_1) + P(A_2) + \ldots + P(A_n) = 1$$

---

### Type 5: Complementary Events

> Exactly two events where one is the "NOT" of the other.
> Always mutually exclusive AND exhaustive.

$$P(E) + P(\bar{E}) = 1$$

---

### Comparison Table:

| Feature | Mutually Exclusive | Independent |
|---------|-------------------|------------|
| Can both occur? | NO | YES (can both occur) |
| Formula | P(AÔłęB) = 0 | P(AÔłęB) = P(A)├ŚP(B) |
| Effect on each other | Incompatible | No effect |
| Example | H and T (same toss) | H on coin 1 and H on coin 2 |

> ÔÜá´ŞĆ **Critical:** Mutually exclusive Ôëá Independent! They are different concepts.

## 5. ADDITION RULE OF PROBABILITY

---

### The General Addition Rule:

$$\boxed{P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A \cap B)}$$

> "A or B" = A happens + B happens Ôłĺ (counted twice: both happening)

---

### For Three Events:

$$P(A \cup B \cup C) = P(A) + P(B) + P(C) - P(A \cap B) - P(B \cap C) - P(A \cap C) + P(A \cap B \cap C)$$

### Examples:

#### Example 1: Basic Addition Rule

> P(A) = 0.4, P(B) = 0.3, P(AÔłęB) = 0.1. Find P(AÔł¬B).

$$P(A \cup B) = 0.4 + 0.3 - 0.1 = 0.6$$

---

#### Example 2: Mutually Exclusive

> A die is rolled. P(even) = 1/2. P(factor of 6) = P({1,2,3,6}) = 4/6.
> Are these mutually exclusive? No (2,6 are both even and factors of 6).

$$P(\text{even} \cap \text{factor of 6}) = P(\{2,6\}) = 2/6 = 1/3$$

$$P(\text{even} \cup \text{factor of 6}) = \frac{1}{2} + \frac{4}{6} - \frac{1}{3} = \frac{3}{6} + \frac{4}{6} - \frac{2}{6} = \frac{5}{6}$$

---

#### Example 3: Finding P(AÔłęB) from Other Values

> P(A) = 0.5, P(B) = 0.4, P(AÔł¬B) = 0.7. Find P(AÔłęB).

$$P(A \cap B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A \cup B) = 0.5 + 0.4 - 0.7 = 0.2$$

---

#### Example 4: Three Events ÔÇö All Conditions Given

> P(A)=0.3, P(B)=0.4, P(C)=0.2, P(AÔłęB)=0.1, P(BÔłęC)=0.05, P(AÔłęC)=0.08, P(AÔłęBÔłęC)=0.02

$$P(A \cup B \cup C) = 0.3+0.4+0.2-0.1-0.05-0.08+0.02 = 0.69$$

## 6. MULTIPLICATION RULE OF PROBABILITY

---

### For Independent Events:

$$\boxed{P(A \cap B) = P(A) \times P(B)}$$

---

### For Dependent Events:

$$\boxed{P(A \cap B) = P(A) \times P(B|A)}$$

---

#### Example 5: Two Coins Tossed

> Find P(both heads).

Events are independent. P(H) = 1/2 each.

$$P(HH) = \frac{1}{2} \times \frac{1}{2} = \frac{1}{4}$$

---

#### Example 6: Drawing Without Replacement (Dependent)

> A bag has 5 red and 3 blue balls. Two drawn without replacement. P(both red)?

P(1st red) = 5/8
P(2nd red | 1st was red) = 4/7 (now 4 red, 7 total)

$$P(\text{both red}) = \frac{5}{8} \times \frac{4}{7} = \frac{20}{56} = \frac{5}{14}$$

---

#### Example 7: Drawing With Replacement (Independent)

> Same bag. Two drawn WITH replacement. P(both red)?

P(1st red) = 5/8. P(2nd red) = 5/8 (same, since replaced).

$$P(\text{both red}) = \frac{5}{8} \times \frac{5}{8} = \frac{25}{64}$$

---

#### Example 8: Multiple Independent Events

> A machine works with probability 0.9. Three machines. P(all work)?

$$P = 0.9 \times 0.9 \times 0.9 = 0.729$$

## 7. CONDITIONAL PROBABILITY

---

### Definition:

> **P(A|B)** = Probability of A **given that B has already occurred**.

$$\boxed{P(A|B) = \frac{P(A \cap B)}{P(B)}}$$

> Think of it as: B is the new "restricted" sample space.

---

### The Updated Sample Space Concept:

When B occurs, we update sample space to only B.
Then we check: how much of A overlaps with this new B space?

### Examples:

#### Example 9: Basic Conditional Probability

> A die is rolled. P(number > 4 | number is even)?

Let A = {number > 4} = {5,6}, B = {even} = {2,4,6}.

$$P(A \cap B) = P(\{6\}) = \frac{1}{6}$$

$$P(A|B) = \frac{1/6}{1/2} = \frac{1}{3}$$

> So given the number is even, probability it's > 4 is 1/3 (only 6 satisfies both).

---

#### Example 10: From a Table (Conditional Probability)

> A class has 60 students. 35 like Math, 25 like Science, 10 like both. Given a student likes Math, P(also likes Science)?

$$P(M) = 35/60, \quad P(M \cap S) = 10/60$$

$$P(S|M) = \frac{10/60}{35/60} = \frac{10}{35} = \frac{2}{7}$$

---

#### Example 11: Testing Independence Using Conditional Probability

> P(A) = 0.3, P(B) = 0.4, P(AÔłęB) = 0.12.

$$P(A|B) = \frac{0.12}{0.4} = 0.3 = P(A)$$

Since P(A|B) = P(A), the events are **independent**.

## 8. BAYES' THEOREM

---

### What It Does:

> **Bayes' Theorem** helps us **update** our probability belief when we receive **new information**.

---

### The Formula:

$$\boxed{P(A_i|B) = \frac{P(B|A_i) \cdot P(A_i)}{\sum_{j} P(B|A_j) \cdot P(A_j)}}$$

---

### Understanding the Terms:

| Term | Name | Meaning |
|------|------|---------|
| P(AßÁó) | **Prior probability** | Probability before new evidence |
| P(B\|AßÁó) | **Likelihood** | How likely is B if AßÁó is true |
| P(AßÁó\|B) | **Posterior probability** | Updated probability after seeing B |
| P(B) | **Marginal probability** | Overall probability of evidence B |

---

### Examples:

#### Example 12: Defective Items Problem

> Factory 1 makes 60% of items, Factory 2 makes 40%.
> Defect rate: Factory 1 = 2%, Factory 2 = 3%.
> An item is defective. P(it came from Factory 1)?

Let FÔéü = from Factory 1, FÔéé = from Factory 2, D = defective.

P(FÔéü) = 0.6, P(FÔéé) = 0.4
P(D|FÔéü) = 0.02, P(D|FÔéé) = 0.03

$$P(D) = P(D|F_1)P(F_1) + P(D|F_2)P(F_2)$$
$$= 0.02 \times 0.6 + 0.03 \times 0.4 = 0.012 + 0.012 = 0.024$$

$$P(F_1|D) = \frac{P(D|F_1) \cdot P(F_1)}{P(D)} = \frac{0.012}{0.024} = \frac{1}{2} = 0.5$$

---

#### Example 13: Medical Test Problem

> Disease affects 1% of people. Test is 95% accurate (95% detect disease if present, 5% false positive).
> Person tests positive. P(actually has disease)?

Let D = has disease, T = tests positive.
P(D) = 0.01, P(D') = 0.99
P(T|D) = 0.95, P(T|D') = 0.05

$$P(T) = 0.95 \times 0.01 + 0.05 \times 0.99 = 0.0095 + 0.0495 = 0.059$$

$$P(D|T) = \frac{0.95 \times 0.01}{0.059} = \frac{0.0095}{0.059} \approx 0.161$$

> Even with a positive test, probability of disease is only ~16.1%! (Because disease is rare.)

## 10. BINOMIAL DISTRIBUTION IN PROBABILITY

---

### When to Use Binomial:

A binomial experiment has:
1. Fixed number of trials: **n**
2. Each trial has only **2 outcomes**: success (p) or failure (q = 1Ôłĺp)
3. Trials are **independent**
4. Probability of success **p is same** for every trial

---

### The Formula:

$$\boxed{P(X = k) = \binom{n}{k} p^k (1-p)^{n-k} = ^nC_k \cdot p^k \cdot q^{n-k}}$$

Where:
- n = number of trials
- k = number of successes desired
- p = probability of success on one trial
- q = 1 Ôłĺ p = probability of failure

---

### Mean and Variance of Binomial:

$$\text{Mean} = \mu = np$$

$$\text{Variance} = \sigma^2 = npq = np(1-p)$$

$$\text{Standard Deviation} = \sigma = \sqrt{npq}$$

---

### Examples:

#### Example 18: Coin Tossed Multiple Times

> A fair coin is tossed 5 times. P(exactly 3 heads)?

n=5, k=3, p=1/2, q=1/2.

$$P(X=3) = ^5C_3 \times \left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^3 \times \left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^2 = 10 \times \frac{1}{32} = \frac{10}{32} = \frac{5}{16}$$

---

#### Example 19: Defective Items

> P(defective item) = 0.1. 4 items selected. P(at most 1 defective)?

n=4, p=0.1, q=0.9.

P(X=0) = $^4C_0 \times 0.1^0 \times 0.9^4 = 0.6561$
P(X=1) = $^4C_1 \times 0.1^1 \times 0.9^3 = 4 \times 0.1 \times 0.729 = 0.2916$

P(at most 1) = 0.6561 + 0.2916 = **0.8477**

---

#### Example 20: Exam Pass Problem

> 70% pass an exam. 6 students appear. P(at least 4 pass)?

n=6, p=0.7, q=0.3.

P(XÔëą4) = P(4) + P(5) + P(6)

P(X=4) = $^6C_4 \times 0.7^4 \times 0.3^2 = 15 \times 0.2401 \times 0.09 = 0.3241$
P(X=5) = $^6C_5 \times 0.7^5 \times 0.3^1 = 6 \times 0.16807 \times 0.3 = 0.3025$
P(X=6) = $^6C_6 \times 0.7^6 \times 0.3^0 = 1 \times 0.1176 = 0.1176$

P(XÔëą4) = 0.3241 + 0.3025 + 0.1176 = **0.7442**

## 11. GEOMETRIC & OTHER DISTRIBUTIONS (BRIEF)

---

### Geometric Distribution:

> X = number of trials until **first success**.

$$P(X = k) = (1-p)^{k-1} \cdot p$$

**Mean** = 1/p. **Example:** Keep tossing until first Head ÔÇö how many tosses?

---

### Hypergeometric Distribution:

> Drawing **without replacement** from population with K successes in N items, selecting n.

$$P(X=k) = \frac{^KC_k \times ^{N-K}C_{n-k}}{^NC_n}$$

**Used when:** Population is finite and sampling without replacement.

## 12. ODDS ÔÇö IN FAVOUR AND AGAINST

---

### Definition:

**Odds in Favour** of an event = (Favourable outcomes) : (Unfavourable outcomes)

**Odds Against** an event = (Unfavourable outcomes) : (Favourable outcomes)

---

### Formulas:

$$\text{Odds in Favour} = \frac{P(E)}{P(\bar{E})} = \frac{P(E)}{1 - P(E)}$$

$$\text{Odds Against} = \frac{P(\bar{E})}{P(E)} = \frac{1 - P(E)}{P(E)}$$

---

### Converting Odds to Probability:

If odds in favour = m:n, then:

$$P(E) = \frac{m}{m+n}$$

---

### Examples:

#### Example 21: Odds from Probability

> P(winning) = 2/5. Find odds in favour and odds against.

Odds in favour = 2 : (5Ôłĺ2) = **2:3**
Odds against = 3 : 2 = **3:2**

---

#### Example 22: Probability from Odds

> Odds in favour of an event = 4:7. Find probability.

$$P(E) = \frac{4}{4+7} = \frac{4}{11}$$

---

#### Example 23: Card Problem with Odds

> A card is drawn. Find odds against it being a spade.

P(spade) = 13/52 = 1/4. P(not spade) = 3/4.
Odds against = (3/4):(1/4) = **3:1**

## 13. EXPECTED VALUE

---

### Definition:

> **Expected Value (E[X])** = The long-run average outcome if experiment repeated many times.

$$E[X] = \sum x_i \cdot P(x_i)$$

---

### Examples:

#### Example 24: Dice Expected Value

> Find expected value of a single die roll.

$$E[X] = 1 \times \frac{1}{6} + 2 \times \frac{1}{6} + 3 \times \frac{1}{6} + 4 \times \frac{1}{6} + 5 \times \frac{1}{6} + 6 \times \frac{1}{6}$$

$$= \frac{1+2+3+4+5+6}{6} = \frac{21}{6} = 3.5$$

---

#### Example 25: Game/Lottery Expected Value

> A game: Win Ôé╣100 with P=0.3, lose Ôé╣50 with P=0.7. Expected outcome?

$$E = 100 \times 0.3 + (-50) \times 0.7 = 30 - 35 = -Ôé╣5$$

> Expected to lose Ôé╣5 per game ÔÇö not worth playing!

---

#### Example 26: Insurance Example

> A person pays Ôé╣1000 as premium. Claim of Ôé╣50,000 with P=0.02, no claim with P=0.98.

From insurer's view:
Expected payout = 50000 ├Ś 0.02 + 0 ├Ś 0.98 = Ôé╣1000.
Premium = Ôé╣1000. Expected profit = Ôé╣1000 Ôłĺ Ôé╣1000 = Ôé╣0. (Break-even point)

## 14. QUICK TRICKS & SHORTCUTS

---

### ­čÜÇ TRICK 1: Complement for "At Least One"

$$P(\text{at least one}) = 1 - P(\text{none})$$

This is almost always faster than direct calculation.

**Example:** P(at least one head in 4 tosses) = 1 Ôłĺ P(no heads) = 1 Ôłĺ (1/2)Ôü┤ = 15/16

---

### ­čÜÇ TRICK 2: P(AÔłęB) from Addition Rule

$$P(A \cap B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A \cup B)$$

Rearrange the addition formula when P(AÔłęB) is unknown.

---

### ­čÜÇ TRICK 3: Independent Events Check

Events A and B are independent if and only if:
$$P(A \cap B) = P(A) \times P(B)$$

Quick check: multiply the individual probabilities and compare with P(AÔłęB).

---

### ­čÜÇ TRICK 4: Odds to Probability (Instant Conversion)

Odds in favour = m:n Ôćĺ P = m/(m+n)
Odds against = m:n Ôćĺ P = n/(m+n)

---

### ­čÜÇ TRICK 5: Total Probability Law (Partition Trick)

Whenever you see "given two types of sources (Box A and Box B)":
$$P(\text{event}) = P(\text{event}|A) \times P(A) + P(\text{event}|B) \times P(B)$$

---

### ­čÜÇ TRICK 6: At Least One of Two Events

$$P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A)P(B) \quad \text{[if independent]}$$

$$= 1 - P(\bar{A}) \times P(\bar{B}) \quad \text{[complement approach, faster]}$$

---

### ­čÜÇ TRICK 7: Mutually Exclusive ÔÇö Just Add

If events are mutually exclusive: P(A OR B) = P(A) + P(B). No subtraction needed.

---

### ­čÜÇ TRICK 8: Symmetric Distributions

For 2 dice: P(sum = k) = P(sum = 14Ôłĺk).
P(sum=5) = P(sum=9) = 4/36.
P(sum=4) = P(sum=10) = 3/36.

Use this symmetry to avoid re-counting.

---

### ­čÜÇ TRICK 9: Quick Card Probabilities

- Any specific card = 1/52
- Any suit = 1/4
- Any rank (e.g., all Kings) = 4/52 = 1/13
- Any color = 1/2
- Face card = 12/52 = 3/13
- Number card = 40/52 = 10/13

---

### ­čÜÇ TRICK 10: Binomial ÔÇö At Least 1

$$P(X \geq 1) = 1 - P(X=0) = 1 - q^n$$

Much faster than summing P(1) + P(2) + ... + P(n).

---

### ­čÜÇ TRICK 11: Expected Value of Binomial

Mean = np (no need to sum all x┬ĚP(x)).
Standard Deviation = ÔłÜ(npq).

---

### ­čÜÇ TRICK 12: Conditional Probability Shortcut

$$P(A|B) = \frac{n(A \cap B)}{n(B)} \quad \text{[when using counts]}$$

When you have actual counts, just use counts directly ÔÇö skip dividing by n(S).

---

### ­čÜÇ TRICK 13: P(AÔłęB) = P(A) for Subset Relationship

If A ÔŐé B (A is a subset of B):
- P(AÔłęB) = P(A)
- P(AÔł¬B) = P(B)
- P(B|A) = 1
- P(A|B) = P(A)/P(B)

---

### ­čÜÇ TRICK 14: Doublet Probability for n Dice

P(all n dice show same number) = 6/6Ôü┐ = 1/6Ôü┐Ôü╗┬╣

For 2 dice: 6/36 = 1/6.
For 3 dice: 6/216 = 1/36.

### ­čÜÇ TRICK 15: Quick "Neither" Probability

P(neither A nor B) = P(─Ç Ôłę B╠ä) = 1 Ôłĺ P(AÔł¬B) = 1 Ôłĺ P(A) Ôłĺ P(B) + P(AÔłęB)

## 15. COMMON MISTAKE ALERTS

---

### ÔÜá´ŞĆ MISTAKE 1: Confusing Mutually Exclusive with Independent

**Mutually Exclusive:** Cannot happen at the same time (P(AÔłęB) = 0).
**Independent:** One doesn't affect the other (P(AÔłęB) = P(A)P(B)).

If P(A) > 0 and P(B) > 0, mutually exclusive events CANNOT be independent.
(Because if A occurs, B cannot Ôćĺ B is affected by A Ôćĺ dependent!)

---

### ÔÜá´ŞĆ MISTAKE 2: Using Addition Rule Without Subtracting Intersection

**Wrong:** P(A or B) = P(A) + P(B) always
**Right:** P(AÔł¬B) = P(A) + P(B) Ôłĺ P(AÔłęB) [subtract the overlap!]

Only when mutually exclusive: P(AÔłęB) = 0, then P(AÔł¬B) = P(A) + P(B).

---

### ÔÜá´ŞĆ MISTAKE 3: Applying Independent Formula for Dependent Events

**Wrong:** P(2 red cards without replacement) = (26/52) ├Ś (26/52)
**Right:** P = (26/52) ├Ś (25/51) [after first red, only 25 red left from 51]

Whenever "without replacement" Ôćĺ dependent events Ôćĺ use conditional multiplication.

---

### ÔÜá´ŞĆ MISTAKE 4: Probability Greater Than 1

If your calculated probability > 1 or < 0, you've made an error.
Probability is ALWAYS between 0 and 1 (inclusive).

---

### ÔÜá´ŞĆ MISTAKE 5: Forgetting to Subtract Overlapping Events in "OR" Problems

"P(King OR Spade)" contains King of Spades in BOTH groups.
**Wrong:** P = 4/52 + 13/52 = 17/52
**Right:** P = 4/52 + 13/52 Ôłĺ 1/52 = 16/52 = 4/13

---

### ÔÜá´ŞĆ MISTAKE 6: Using Wrong Sample Space

When condition is given (conditional probability), the sample space CHANGES.
"Given the card is red" Ôćĺ new sample space is 26 cards, not 52.

---

### ÔÜá´ŞĆ MISTAKE 7: Complement Includes ALL Remaining Cases

P(at least 2 heads) complement = P(0 heads) + P(1 head).
**Wrong:** Complement = P(0 heads) only.
"At least 2" means 2 or more. Complement = "less than 2" = {0, 1}.

---

### ÔÜá´ŞĆ MISTAKE 8: Confusing P(A|B) with P(B|A)

**P(A|B) Ôëá P(B|A)** in general!

P(disease | positive test) Ôëá P(positive test | disease).
The famous medical test problem shows these can be very different.

---

### ÔÜá´ŞĆ MISTAKE 9: With vs Without Replacement

Always identify whether sampling is with or without replacement.
- **With replacement** Ôćĺ Independent events Ôćĺ Multiply same probabilities
- **Without replacement** Ôćĺ Dependent events Ôćĺ Update denominator

---

### ÔÜá´ŞĆ MISTAKE 10: Order in Coin/Card Problems

"First head, then tail" = P(HT) = 1/4 (specific order).
"One head and one tail" (any order) = P(HT) + P(TH) = 2/4 = 1/2.

Be clear whether order is specified.

## ­čĆć TOP EXAM TIPS

1. **Always check: Does order matter? With/without replacement?** These two questions shape the entire approach.
2. **Complement ("at least one") is almost always the fastest path** ÔÇö use it without hesitation.
3. **Mutually exclusive Ôëá Independent** ÔÇö the #1 conceptual confusion. Memorise the difference.
4. **For "without replacement" problems** ÔÇö update both numerator AND denominator after each draw.
5. **Conditional probability** ÔÇö the denominator becomes the condition's event count.
6. **Bayes' Theorem problems** ÔÇö always use Total Probability Law first to find P(evidence).
7. **Card problems** ÔÇö memorise: 52 total, 4 suits, 13 each, 12 face cards, 4 aces.
8. **Two dice** ÔÇö sum of 7 has 6 ways (most likely). Build the sum table once and memorise.
9. **Binomial** ÔÇö use when you have n independent trials with same p. Mean = np is always asked.
10. **Final check** ÔÇö all probabilities must be between 0 and 1. If not, there's an error.
