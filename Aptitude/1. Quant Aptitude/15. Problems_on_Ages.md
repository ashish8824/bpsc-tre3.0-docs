## 1. CORE CONCEPT & THE BIG IDEA

---

### 🔑 The One Big Idea:

> **Age problems are just algebra problems in disguise.**
>
> The trick is to identify the **reference point (present)** and express all ages — past and future — relative to the present.

---

### The Timeline Concept:

```
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
       PAST          PRESENT         FUTURE
        ←               │               →
   (x - n) years    Age = x       (x + n) years

   n years AGO      RIGHT NOW     n years HENCE/LATER
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
```

---

### The Golden Rule — How Age Changes:

| Situation | Age Expression |
|-----------|---------------|
| Present age of A | x (or any variable) |
| Age of A, **n years ago** | x − n |
| Age of A, **n years later/hence** | x + n |
| Age of A, **n years before birth** | Negative (makes no sense — avoid) |

---

### Key Insight — Difference of Ages NEVER Changes:

> If A is 10 years older than B **today**, then A was 10 years older than B **20 years ago**, and A will be 10 years older than B **30 years from now**.
>
> **Age difference between two people is always constant.**

```
If A − B = 10 years (today)
Then (A−n) − (B−n) = A − B = 10 years (n years ago) ✓
And (A+n) − (B+n) = A − B = 10 years (n years later) ✓
```

---

### What "Ratio Changes" Mean:

> Even though the **difference** of ages stays the same, the **ratio** of ages keeps changing.

Example: A = 40, B = 20 today. Ratio = 2:1.
10 years later: A = 50, B = 30. Ratio = 5:3. Different!
10 years ago: A = 30, B = 10. Ratio = 3:1. Different!

This is why ratio-based age problems always ask "WHEN is the ratio…?" — it changes over time.

## 2. FUNDAMENTAL RULES OF AGE PROBLEMS

---

### Rule 1: Always Define the Present Age

Let present age of person = x (or A, B, etc.)
**Never** define age as past or future age — always define the **present** first.

---

### Rule 2: The Three Translations

| English Statement | Mathematical Expression |
|------------------|------------------------|
| "A is 5 years older than B" | A = B + 5 |
| "A is twice as old as B" | A = 2B |
| "5 years ago, A was…" | A − 5 = … |
| "After 10 years, A will be…" | A + 10 = … |
| "A was twice B's age 5 years ago" | A − 5 = 2(B − 5) |
| "After 5 years, A will be twice B" | A + 5 = 2(B + 5) |
| "Sum of ages = 50" | A + B = 50 |
| "Product of ages = 200" | A × B = 200 |

---

### Rule 3: The Time Shift Rule

When shifting time by n years (past or future):
- **Every person's age shifts by the same n**
- Sum of n persons' ages shifts by **n × (number of persons)**
- Average of n persons shifts by **exactly n**

---

### Rule 4: Ratio in Past/Future

If present ratio A:B = m:n, let A = mk and B = nk.
Then use the given condition (past/future) to find k.

---

### Rule 5: Age Can Never Be Negative

After solving, always verify:
- No age is negative at any point being discussed
- "n years ago" means n < current age of each person mentioned

---

## 3. TYPES OF AGE PROBLEMS

---

### Type 1: Present Age from Sum/Difference

> "Sum of ages of A and B is 50. A is 10 years older than B."

→ Two equations, two unknowns. Straightforward.

---

### Type 2: Past and Future Conditions

> "5 years ago, A was 3 times B. After 5 years, A will be twice B."

→ Express past/future ages using present age variable.

---

### Type 3: Ratio-Based Problems

> "Present ratio of A to B is 3:2. After 10 years, ratio becomes 4:3."

→ Let A = 3k, B = 2k. Apply future condition.

---

### Type 4: Average Age Problems

> "Average age of a group is 30. When a new member joins, average becomes 32."

→ Use: Total age = Average × Count.

---

### Type 5: Father-Son / Family Problems

> "Father is 4 times as old as his son. After 20 years, he'll be twice as old."

→ Common parent-child pattern.

---

### Type 6: Multiple Person Problems

> "A is older than B by 3 years. B is older than C by 5 years. Sum = 45."

→ Express everything in terms of one variable.

---

### Type 7: Product / Square Conditions

> "The product of ages of A and B is 240. A's age is the square of B's age."

→ Algebraic/quadratic approach.

---

## 4. SETTING UP EQUATIONS — THE METHOD

---

### Step-by-Step Approach:

```
STEP 1: Read the problem carefully.
          Identify HOW MANY people and HOW MANY conditions.

STEP 2: Assign PRESENT age as a variable.
          Let present age of A = x, B = y (or x+n if relationship given).

STEP 3: Translate EVERY sentence into an equation.
          "5 years ago" → subtract 5 from each age.
          "After 10 years" → add 10 to each age.

STEP 4: Solve the system of equations.

STEP 5: VERIFY — check each condition with your answer.
```

---

### The One-Variable Trick:

Whenever possible, express all ages in terms of **ONE variable**.

Example: "A is 5 years older than B" → Let B = x, then A = x + 5.
Now only one unknown to solve.

## 5. BASIC EXAMPLES

---

### ─── BASIC LEVEL ───

#### Example 1: Sum and Difference of Ages

> The sum of ages of A and B is 40. A is 10 years older than B. Find their ages.

**Let B = x. Then A = x + 10.**

Sum: (x + 10) + x = 40
2x = 30 → **x = 15**

B = **15 years**, A = **25 years**

**Verify:** Sum = 40 ✓, Difference = 10 ✓

---

#### Example 2: Simple Past Condition

> 5 years ago, A was 20 years old. How old is A now?

A (5 years ago) = 20
A (present) = 20 + 5 = **25 years**

---

#### Example 3: Simple Future Condition

> After 8 years, B will be 35 years old. How old is B now?

B + 8 = 35
B = **27 years**

---

#### Example 4: Age in Terms of Another

> A's age is three times B's age. Sum of their ages is 48.

Let B = x → A = 3x.
x + 3x = 48 → 4x = 48 → x = 12.

B = **12 years**, A = **36 years**

---

#### Example 5: Past Ratio

> 10 years ago, the ratio of ages of A and B was 3:2. Find their present ages if A is now 35.

A now = 35 → A then = 25.
25/B_then = 3/2 → B_then = 50/3 ≈ 16.67 years.
B now = 16.67 + 10 ≈ **26.67 years**

---

#### Example 6: Father and Son — Classic

> A father is 30 years older than his son. 5 years hence, the father will be 3 times as old as the son. Find their present ages.

Let son's age = x. Father's age = x + 30.

After 5 years:
x + 30 + 5 = 3(x + 5)
x + 35 = 3x + 15
20 = 2x → x = 10.

Son = **10 years**, Father = **40 years**

**Verify:** After 5 years → Son = 15, Father = 45 = 3 × 15 ✓

---

#### Example 7: Two Conditions — Past and Future

> A is 5 years older than B. 5 years ago, A was twice B's age. Find their ages.

Let B = x → A = x + 5.

5 years ago: A − 5 = 2(B − 5)
(x + 5 − 5) = 2(x − 5)
x = 2x − 10
x = **10**

B = **10 years**, A = **15 years**

**Verify:** 5 years ago → A = 10, B = 5 → A = 2B ✓

---

#### Example 8: After n Years Condition

> Sachin is 4 years younger than Rahul. After 10 years, Rahul will be twice as old as Sachin. Find their ages.

Let Sachin = x → Rahul = x + 4.

After 10 years: (x + 4 + 10) = 2(x + 10)
x + 14 = 2x + 20
−x = 6 → x = −6?

Negative age — let's recheck. This means Sachin's present age < 0 which is impossible. Recheck problem.

"Rahul twice Sachin" after 10 years: Rahul + 10 = 2(Sachin + 10).
Let Sachin = s, Rahul = s + 4.
(s+4+10) = 2(s+10)
s+14 = 2s+20
-s = 6 → s = -6

Still negative. The problem data is inconsistent. **Correct version:**
"Sachin is 6 years younger than Rahul. After 6 years, Rahul will be twice Sachin."

Let Sachin = x, Rahul = x+6.
x+6+6 = 2(x+6) → x+12 = 2x+12 → x = 0. Still edge case.

Better version: Sachin is 8 years younger. After 4 years, Rahul = twice Sachin.
Let Sachin = x, Rahul = x+8.
x+8+4 = 2(x+4) → x+12 = 2x+8 → x = 4.

Sachin = **4**, Rahul = **12**. Verify: After 4 yrs → 8 and 16. 16 = 2×8 ✓

## 6. INTERMEDIATE EXAMPLES

---

### ─── INTERMEDIATE LEVEL ───

#### Example 9: Two Ratio Conditions

> The ratio of ages of P and Q is 3:5. After 6 years, the ratio becomes 2:3. Find present ages.

Let P = 3k, Q = 5k.

After 6 years: (3k+6)/(5k+6) = 2/3
3(3k+6) = 2(5k+6)
9k+18 = 10k+12
k = **6**

P = 3×6 = **18 years**, Q = 5×6 = **30 years**

**Verify:** After 6 years → 24:36 = 2:3 ✓

---

#### Example 10: Past and Future Ratio

> 6 years ago, the ratio of ages of A and B was 1:3. After 8 years, it will be 1:2. Find present ages.

Let present ages = A and B.

6 years ago: (A−6)/(B−6) = 1/3 → 3(A−6) = B−6 → 3A−18 = B−6 → **B = 3A−12** ...(i)

After 8 years: (A+8)/(B+8) = 1/2 → 2(A+8) = B+8 → 2A+16 = B+8 → **B = 2A+8** ...(ii)

From (i) and (ii):
3A−12 = 2A+8 → **A = 20**
B = 2×20+8 = **48**

**Verify:**
6 years ago: 14/42 = 1/3 ✓
After 8 years: 28/56 = 1/2 ✓

#### Example 11: Three People Ages

> Sum of ages of A, B, C is 90. A is 5 years older than B. B is 10 years older than C. Find all ages.

Let C = x. B = x+10. A = x+10+5 = x+15.
x + (x+10) + (x+15) = 90
3x + 25 = 90
3x = 65 → x = 65/3 ≈ 21.67

Hmm — let's use a cleaner version:
Sum = 96: 3x = 71 (still not clean).
Sum = 75: 3x = 50 (not clean).

Let's use: A is 5 older than B, B is 5 older than C, sum = 75.
C = x, B = x+5, A = x+10.
3x+15 = 75 → x = 20. C = **20**, B = **25**, A = **30**.

#### Example 12: Father-Son with Two Conditions

> Father's age is 4 times son's age. After 16 years, father will be only twice son's age. Find their ages.

Let son = x → father = 4x.

4x + 16 = 2(x + 16)
4x + 16 = 2x + 32
2x = 16 → x = **8**

Son = **8 years**, Father = **32 years**

**Verify:** After 16 → son=24, father=48=2×24 ✓

#### Example 13: Age Difference with Ratio

> The ratio of ages of A to B is 4:3. The difference of their ages is 8 years. Find their ages.

A = 4k, B = 3k.
4k − 3k = 8 → k = 8.

A = **32 years**, B = **24 years**

#### Example 14: Present and Past Conditions

> The sum of present ages of a father and son is 56. 4 years ago, the father was 3 times as old as the son. Find their present ages.

Let son = x → father = 56 − x.

4 years ago: (56−x−4) = 3(x−4)
52−x = 3x−12
64 = 4x → x = **16**

Son = **16**, Father = **40**

**Verify:** 4 years ago → son=12, father=36=3×12 ✓

#### Example 15: Mother-Daughter Problem

> A mother is 3 times as old as her daughter. After 12 years, she will be twice her daughter's age. Find their present ages.

Let daughter = x → mother = 3x.

3x+12 = 2(x+12)
3x+12 = 2x+24
x = **12**

Daughter = **12 years**, Mother = **36 years**

**Verify:** After 12 years → daughter=24, mother=48=2×24 ✓

#### Example 16: A's Age When B Was Born

> A is 25 years old. B is 10 years old. How old was A when B was born?

B was born 10 years ago.
A's age when B was born = 25 − 10 = **15 years**

#### Example 17: Age When Marriage Happened

> A man married at 28. At that time his wife was 4 years younger. After 6 years of marriage, their total age is 60. How old were each when married?

Let time of marriage = reference point.
Man's age at marriage = 28. Wife's age = 24.
After 6 years: Man = 34, Wife = 30.
Total = 34 + 30 = 64 ≠ 60. (Inconsistent data — let's solve the reverse.)

"After 6 years of marriage their total age is 60":
28 + 6 + (28−4) + 6 = 34 + 30 = 64.

Correct data: Total age after 6 years = 64.

**Alternative correct problem:**
Man married at 28. Wife was 4 younger. After 4 years of marriage, total age = 60.
28+4 + 24+4 = 32+28 = **60** ✓

Man at marriage = **28**, Wife at marriage = **24**.

#### Example 18: Age Ratio — Three People

> Ratio of ages A:B:C = 3:5:7. Sum of ages = 60. Find each age and their ages 10 years ago.

A = 3k, B = 5k, C = 7k.
15k = 60 → k = 4.

A = **12**, B = **20**, C = **28**

10 years ago: A = 2, B = 10, C = 18. Ratio = 2:10:18 = **1:5:9**

### ─── ADVANCED LEVEL ───

#### Example 19: Three Conditions — Simultaneous Equations

> Present ages of A, B, C are in ratio 4:7:9. 8 years ago, the sum of their ages was 56. Find their present ages.

A = 4k, B = 7k, C = 9k.

8 years ago: (4k−8) + (7k−8) + (9k−8) = 56
20k − 24 = 56
20k = 80 → k = **4**

A = **16**, B = **28**, C = **36**

**Verify:** 8 years ago → 8+20+28 = 56 ✓

---

#### Example 20: Complex Ratio — Two Time Points

> 5 years ago, A:B = 2:3. 10 years from now, A:B = 3:4. Find present ages.

Let present ages = A and B.
A−5 = 2k, B−5 = 3k → A = 2k+5, B = 3k+5.

After 10 years: (A+10)/(B+10) = 3/4.
4(A+10) = 3(B+10)
4A+40 = 3B+30
4(2k+5)+40 = 3(3k+5)+30
8k+20+40 = 9k+15+30
8k+60 = 9k+45
k = **15**

A = 2×15+5 = **35**, B = 3×15+5 = **50**

**Verify:**
5 years ago: 30:45 = 2:3 ✓
After 10 years: 45:60 = 3:4 ✓

#### Example 21: Group Average Age Change

> The average age of 5 members of a family is 28. If the grandfather is excluded, the average drops by 8 years. Find the grandfather's age.

Total age with grandfather = 5 × 28 = 140.
Total age without grandfather = 4 × 20 = 80.
Grandfather's age = 140 − 80 = **60 years**

Decrease = 20.

## 8. RATIO-BASED AGE PROBLEMS

---

### The Standard Method:

When ratio is given as **m:n**, always let:
- Smaller age = nk (or just multiply by k)
- Larger age = mk

Then apply the condition to find k.

---

#### Example 31: Ratio Changes After Time

> Ratio of ages of X and Y is 5:3. After 6 years, the ratio becomes 7:5. Find ages.

X = 5k, Y = 3k.
(5k+6)/(3k+6) = 7/5
5(5k+6) = 7(3k+6)
25k+30 = 21k+42
4k = 12 → k = 3.

X = **15**, Y = **9**

**Verify:** After 6 → 21:15 = 7:5 ✓

---

#### Example 32: Ratio in Past

> 8 years ago, ratio of ages of A to B was 5:7. Present ratio is 3:4. Find ages.

Let present A = 3k, B = 4k.
(3k−8)/(4k−8) = 5/7
7(3k−8) = 5(4k−8)
21k−56 = 20k−40
k = **16**

A = **48**, B = **64**

**Verify:** 8 years ago: 40:56 = 5:7 ✓

---

#### Example 33: Three-Person Ratio

> Ratio of ages of A:B:C = 2:3:5. After 7 years, ratio becomes 3:4:6. Find present ages.

Let A=2k, B=3k, C=5k.
(2k+7)/(3k+7) = 3/4 → 4(2k+7) = 3(3k+7) → 8k+28 = 9k+21 → k = **7**

A=**14**, B=**21**, C=**35**

**Verify:** After 7 → 21:28:42 = 3:4:6 ✓

#### Example 34: Find When Ratio Will Be Specific

> A = 30, B = 18. After how many years will ratio A:B = 5:4?

(30+n)/(18+n) = 5/4
4(30+n) = 5(18+n)
120+4n = 90+5n
n = **30 years**

**Verify:** After 30 → 60:48 = 5:4 ✓

## 9. AVERAGE AGE PROBLEMS

---

### Key Formula:

$$\text{Total Age} = \text{Average Age} \times \text{Number of Persons}$$

$$\text{New Average} = \frac{\text{Old Total} + \text{New Person's Age}}{\text{New Count}}$$

---

### The Change in Average Formula:

If average changes by Δ when:
- A new person replaces old person:
$$\text{New person's age} = \text{Old person's age} \pm (\Delta \times n)$$

(+ if average increases, − if decreases)

---

#### Example 35: New Member Joins

> Average age of a group of 12 = 32 years. New person joins. Average = 33. Find new person's age.

Old total = 12 × 32 = 384.
New total = 13 × 33 = 429.
New person = 429 − 384 = **45 years**

---

#### Example 36: Member Replaced

> Average age of 6 persons = 35. One person (age 48) is replaced by another. New average = 34. Find age of new person.

Old total = 6 × 35 = 210.
New total = 6 × 34 = 204.
Decrease = 6.
New person = 48 − 6 = **42 years**

**Shortcut:** New person age = Old person age − (Decrease × n) = 48 − (1 × 6) = **42** ✓

---

#### Example 37: Average of Family

> Average age of a family of 5 = 24. Average of 4 members excluding the youngest = 26. Find the youngest's age.

Total = 5 × 24 = 120.
Total of 4 = 4 × 26 = 104.
Youngest = 120 − 104 = **16 years**

---

#### Example 38: Average Changes Over Time

> Average age of a class of 40 students is 14 years. After 1 year, what will be the average?

Each student gets 1 year older → Total increases by 40.
New total = 40 × 14 + 40 = 40 × 15.
**New average = 15 years**

> **Trick:** Average age increases by exactly n years after n years (for same group).

---

#### Example 39: Mixture — Two Groups

> Group A: 10 members, average age 28. Group B: 15 members, average age 32. Find combined average.

Total A = 280. Total B = 480. Combined = 760.
Combined count = 25.
Average = 760/25 = **30.4 years**

## 10. AGE PROBLEMS WITH SPECIAL CONDITIONS

---

#### Example 40: Age is Perfect Square Now and Future

> A's age is a perfect square. After 5 years, it'll be another perfect square. Find age.

Perfect squares: 1, 4, 9, 16, 25, 36, 49...
Pairs differing by 5: 4 and 9 (diff=5), 20 and 25 (diff=5).
Wait: 20 is not perfect square. Let's check: 4→9 ✓ (diff=5).

**Current age = 4, after 5 years = 9** (both perfect squares) ✓

Also: Any larger pair differing by 5? 25+5=30 (not square). 36+5=41 (not). So **A = 4 years** is the main answer. Or **16+5=21** (not square) ...
Only solution for small ages: **A = 4 years**.

---

#### Example 41: Cyclic Age Problem

> The ages of three persons are in AP. Sum = 45. The difference between the largest and smallest is 10. Find ages.

Let ages = a−d, a, a+d.
Sum: 3a = 45 → a = 15.
Difference: (a+d)−(a−d) = 2d = 10 → d = 5.

Ages: **10, 15, 20**

---

#### Example 42: Age with Fraction Condition

> 1/6 of A's age 3 years ago + 1/7 of A's age 7 years hence = 1/3 of A's current age + 1. Find A's age.

Let A's present age = x.
(x−3)/6 + (x+7)/7 = x/3 + 1

Multiply through by 42:
7(x−3) + 6(x+7) = 14x + 42
7x−21 + 6x+42 = 14x+42
13x+21 = 14x+42
x = **−21**

This is negative — the data creates an inconsistency. For exam purposes, such questions always produce positive results. Let me use a valid version:

(x+3)/6 + (x+7)/7 = x/3 + 7 (multiplied by 42):
7(x+3)+6(x+7) = 14x+294
7x+21+6x+42=14x+294
13x+63=14x+294
x=**−231** (still invalid)

Correct exam-style fraction problem:
"1/3 of A's age 6 years ago = 1/4 of A's age 10 years later":
(x−6)/3 = (x+10)/4
4(x−6) = 3(x+10)
4x−24 = 3x+30
**x = 54**

## 11. QUICK TRICKS & SHORTCUTS

---

### 🚀 TRICK 1: One-Variable Always Works

Express all ages in terms of ONE unknown. Reduce to one equation. 
If A is 5 older than B → Let B = x, A = x+5. ONE variable, not two.

---

### 🚀 TRICK 2: Age Difference is Constant

A − B = constant (never changes with time).
Use this to quickly verify answers or set up simple equations.

---

### 🚀 TRICK 3: Ratio → Multiply by k

Ratio A:B = m:n → Let A = mk, B = nk.
Apply condition → find k → get actual ages.

---

### 🚀 TRICK 4: Average Age Shifts by n Every n Years

Same group → average increases by exactly 1 year each year.
After t years, average = original average + t.

---

### 🚀 TRICK 5: Replacement Effect on Average

If old member (age O) is replaced by new member (age N):
$$\text{Change in average} = \frac{N - O}{n} \quad \text{where n = group size}$$

If N > O → average increases. If N < O → average decreases.

---

### 🚀 TRICK 6: New Member Joining Effect

New member's age = New average + (New average − Old average) × Old count

$$A_{new} = \bar{x}_{new} + (\bar{x}_{new} - \bar{x}_{old}) \times n_{old}$$

Or simply: New age = New total − Old total.

---

### 🚀 TRICK 7: When Will Ratio Become p:q?

Present ages A and B. Ratio will be p:q after n years when:
$$(A+n)/(B+n) = p/q$$
$$qA+qn = pB+pn$$
$$n(q-p) = pB-qA$$
$$n = \frac{pB-qA}{q-p}$$

If n is negative → it was in the past, not future.

---

### 🚀 TRICK 8: Sum Changes with Number of People

For k people, after t years: Sum of ages increases by **k × t**.

---

### 🚀 TRICK 9: Father-Son Standard Relationship

Common patterns:
- Father = x times son now, (x−1) times later → classic pattern
- Use: Father − Son = constant = Father(future) − Son(future)
- Set up: F = xS and F + t = y(S + t)

---

### 🚀 TRICK 10: Age When B Was Born = A's current age − B's current age

This is the most direct shortcut:
"How old was A when B was born?" = A_now − B_now.

---

### 🚀 TRICK 11: Number of Years for Ratio to Occur (Using AP Trick)

If ratio of two ages changes:
Present ratio = m:n → Future ratio = p:q.
The relationship: (mk+t)/(nk+t) = p/q gives t directly.

---

### 🚀 TRICK 12: Three Persons Sum Given — Pair-Wise Sums Known

If A+B = x, B+C = y, A+C = z:
$$A+B+C = \frac{x+y+z}{2}$$
$$A = (A+B+C) - y, \quad B = (A+B+C) - z, \quad C = (A+B+C) - x$$

---

### 🚀 TRICK 13: Quick Verification Method

After finding ages, ALWAYS verify by substituting back into the original problem statement. This catches errors in under 30 seconds.

---

### 🚀 TRICK 14: Negative Result Means Past

If you solve for "after n years" and get n = negative value, the event already happened |n| years ago.

---

### 🚀 TRICK 15: Present Age = Sum/2 (When Equal Ages Assumed)

If "A + B = 60 and A = B", then each = 30. Basic but many miss this as a quick check.

## 12. COMMON MISTAKE ALERTS

---

### ⚠️ MISTAKE 1: Defining Past/Future Age as Variable

**Wrong:** Let A's age 5 years ago = x.
**Right:** Let A's **present** age = x. Then 5 years ago = x−5.

Always define PRESENT age as variable. Past and future are derived.

---

### ⚠️ MISTAKE 2: Forgetting to Shift ALL Ages

"5 years ago, A was twice B":
**Wrong:** A − 5 = 2B (forgetting to also subtract from B)
**Right:** A − 5 = 2(B − 5)

Both people's ages must be shifted by the same amount.

---

### ⚠️ MISTAKE 3: Adding n to Only One Person in Ratio

"After 10 years, ratio A:B = 3:2":
**Wrong:** (A+10)/B = 3/2
**Right:** (A+10)/(B+10) = 3/2

Both ages go forward by the same amount of time.

---

### ⚠️ MISTAKE 4: Ratio Reversal

"Ratio A:B = 3:5 → A = 5k, B = 3k" — WRONG!
**Right:** A:B = 3:5 → **A = 3k, B = 5k** (match in order!)

---

### ⚠️ MISTAKE 5: Ignoring Negative Age Results

If solution gives age = −5 years:
- Either the data is inconsistent
- Or you've made an equation error
- Always check: Is this realistic?

---

### ⚠️ MISTAKE 6: Average Confusion — New Member vs Replacement

**New member joins:** New total = Old total + New age → Count increases by 1.
**Member replaced:** Count stays SAME. New total = Old total − Old age + New age.

These are different! Don't confuse the two formulas.

---

### ⚠️ MISTAKE 7: "Older Than" vs "Times Older"

"A is 5 years **older than** B" → A = B + 5
"A is 5 **times** as old as B" → A = 5B (NOT A = 5 + B)

These are completely different statements!

---

### ⚠️ MISTAKE 8: "n Years Ago" vs "n Years Hence" Mix-Up

"n years ago" → **subtract** n from present age.
"n years hence/later" → **add** n to present age.

Re-read problem slowly to identify which time direction is involved.

---

### ⚠️ MISTAKE 9: Product of Ages — Using Sum Formula

If A × B = 120 is given, do NOT use A + B = 120.
Product and sum are completely different. Set up correctly.

---

### ⚠️ MISTAKE 10: Forgetting to Add Time to Age When Joining Late

"Person joined a class 2 years ago when average was 15. Current average?"
Every student (including the one who joined) has aged 2 years. Add 2 to everything.

### Types vs Approach — Quick Guide:

| Problem Type | Key Approach |
|-------------|-------------|
| Sum + Difference | One variable. A=x+d, B=x |
| Ratio + Future/Past | A=mk, B=nk. Find k |
| Two ratios (different times) | Two equations, two unknowns |
| Average + new member | New age = New total − Old total |
| Average + replacement | New age = Old age ± Δ×n |
| Father-Son (2 conditions) | F = kS now, F+t = m(S+t) later |
| Three persons | Express all in one variable C = x, B = x+a, A = x+b |

## 🏆 TOP EXAM TIPS

1. **Always define PRESENT age as the variable** — never past or future.
2. **Shift ALL ages together** — when "5 years ago", subtract 5 from EVERY person mentioned.
3. **Ratio = mk, nk pattern** — use it every time a ratio is given. Find k.
4. **Age difference never changes** — use this for quick verification.
5. **Verify every answer** — substitute back into original conditions. Takes 20 seconds and saves marks.
6. **"Older than" ≠ "times older"** — "5 years older" = add 5. "5 times older" = multiply by 5.
7. **Average problems** — always calculate Total = Average × Count first, then proceed.
8. **Replacement vs New joining** — count stays same in replacement, increases by 1 when new person joins.
9. **Negative result in time** → event was in the past (change "after" to "before" for interpretation).
10. **For complex problems with 3 people** — express B and A in terms of C, reduce to one equation.
