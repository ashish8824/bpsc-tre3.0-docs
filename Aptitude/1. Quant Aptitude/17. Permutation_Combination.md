## 1. THE BIG PICTURE — P&C IN ONE PAGE

---

### 🔑 The Core Question:

> Before solving ANY problem, ask yourself:
>
> **"Does ORDER matter?"**
>
> - YES → **PERMUTATION** (arrange, sequence, rank, line up)
> - NO → **COMBINATION** (select, choose, group, team, committee)

---

### The Entire Topic in One Visual:

```
                    COUNTING PROBLEMS
                          │
          ┌───────────────┴───────────────┐
          │                               │
    ORDER MATTERS                  ORDER DOESN'T MATTER
    (Arrangement)                    (Selection)
          │                               │
   PERMUTATION (P)               COMBINATION (C)
          │                               │
     nPr = n!/(n-r)!              nCr = n!/[r!(n-r)!]
          │                               │
   "How many ways to             "How many ways to
    arrange r items               choose r items
    from n items"                 from n items"
```

---

### The Two Master Formulas:

$$\boxed{^nP_r = \frac{n!}{(n-r)!}}$$

$$\boxed{^nC_r = \frac{n!}{r!(n-r)!}}$$

### The Link Between Them:

$$^nP_r = ^nC_r \times r!$$

> **In words:** Permutation = Combination × (arrangements of selected items)

## 2. FACTORIAL — THE FOUNDATION

---

### Definition:

$$n! = n \times (n-1) \times (n-2) \times \ldots \times 2 \times 1$$

### Special Values — MEMORISE THESE:

| n | n! |
|---|-----|
| 0 | **1** (by definition — crucial!) |
| 1 | 1 |
| 2 | 2 |
| 3 | 6 |
| 4 | 24 |
| 5 | 120 |
| 6 | 720 |
| 7 | 5040 |
| 8 | 40320 |
| 9 | 362880 |
| 10 | 3628800 |

### Key Properties:

$$n! = n \times (n-1)!$$

$$\frac{n!}{(n-1)!} = n$$

$$\frac{n!}{r!} = n \times (n-1) \times \ldots \times (r+1) \quad \text{(product of consecutive integers)}$$

---

### Examples:

$$7! = 7 \times 6! = 7 \times 720 = 5040$$

$$\frac{10!}{7!} = 10 \times 9 \times 8 = 720$$

$$\frac{8!}{5! \times 3!} = \frac{8 \times 7 \times 6}{3 \times 2 \times 1} = \frac{336}{6} = 56$$

## 3. FUNDAMENTAL COUNTING PRINCIPLE

---

### The Two Rules That Govern All Counting:

---

### Rule 1: Multiplication Principle (AND Rule)

> If task A can be done in **m** ways AND task B can be done in **n** ways,
> then BOTH tasks together can be done in **m × n** ways.

**Key word: AND → MULTIPLY**

---

### Rule 2: Addition Principle (OR Rule)

> If task A can be done in **m** ways OR task B can be done in **n** ways (not both simultaneously),
> then one of the tasks can be done in **m + n** ways.

**Key word: OR → ADD**

---

### Examples:

#### Example 1: Multiplication Principle

> A person has 4 shirts and 3 pants. How many outfit combinations?

4 shirts AND 3 pants → 4 × 3 = **12 combinations**

---

#### Example 2: Addition Principle

> A student can go from A to B by 3 routes or by 2 other routes. Total routes?

3 OR 2 → 3 + 2 = **5 routes**

---

#### Example 3: Combined

> A password needs 1 letter (from A-Z) followed by 1 digit (0-9). How many passwords?

26 letters AND 10 digits = 26 × 10 = **260 passwords**

---

#### Example 4: Filling Positions Step by Step

> How many 3-digit numbers can be formed using digits 1-9 with no repetition?

- Hundreds place: 9 choices
- Tens place: 8 choices (one used)
- Units place: 7 choices (two used)

Total = 9 × 8 × 7 = **504**

## 4. PERMUTATION — ORDERED ARRANGEMENTS

---

### Definition:

A **Permutation** is an arrangement of objects in a specific **order**.

> The number of ways to **arrange r objects** chosen from **n distinct objects**.

$$^nP_r = \frac{n!}{(n-r)!} = n \times (n-1) \times (n-2) \times \ldots \times (n-r+1)$$

**Always r terms in the product!**

---

### When All n Objects Are Arranged:

$$^nP_n = n! \quad \text{(all n objects arranged)}$$

---

### Worked Examples:

#### Example 5: Basic Permutation

> In how many ways can 5 students be arranged in a row?

$^5P_5 = 5! = 120$

---

#### Example 6: Selecting and Arranging

> How many 3-letter words can be formed from 7 distinct letters?

$^7P_3 = 7 \times 6 \times 5 = 210$

---

#### Example 7: Understanding the Formula

> Find $^8P_3$

$^8P_3 = \frac{8!}{(8-3)!} = \frac{8!}{5!} = 8 \times 7 \times 6 = 336$

---

#### Example 8: Rank Problems

> How many 4-digit numbers can be formed using digits 0-9 without repetition?

- Thousands: 9 choices (0 cannot be first)
- Hundreds: 9 choices (remaining 9 digits)
- Tens: 8 choices
- Units: 7 choices

Total = 9 × 9 × 8 × 7 = **4536**

---

## 5. COMBINATION — UNORDERED SELECTION

---

### Definition:

A **Combination** is a selection of objects where **order does NOT matter**.

> The number of ways to **choose r objects** from **n distinct objects**.

$$^nC_r = \frac{n!}{r!(n-r)!} = \frac{^nP_r}{r!}$$

---

### Key Properties:

$$^nC_r = ^nC_{n-r}$$

$$^nC_0 = ^nC_n = 1$$

$$^nC_1 = n$$

$$^nC_r + ^nC_{r-1} = ^{n+1}C_r \quad \text{(Pascal's Identity)}$$

---

### Worked Examples:

#### Example 9: Basic Combination

> In how many ways can 3 students be selected from a group of 8?

$^8C_3 = \frac{8!}{3! \times 5!} = \frac{8 \times 7 \times 6}{3 \times 2 \times 1} = \frac{336}{6} = 56$

---

#### Example 10: Committee Selection

> A committee of 4 is to be formed from 10 people. How many ways?

$^{10}C_4 = \frac{10!}{4! \times 6!} = \frac{10 \times 9 \times 8 \times 7}{4 \times 3 \times 2 \times 1} = \frac{5040}{24} = 210$

---

#### Example 11: Using the Symmetry Property

> Find $^{20}C_{17}$

$^{20}C_{17} = ^{20}C_{20-17} = ^{20}C_3 = \frac{20 \times 19 \times 18}{3 \times 2 \times 1} = 1140$

> **Shortcut:** When r is large, use $^nC_{n-r}$ — same value, less calculation!

---

#### Example 12: Handshakes / Matches

> In a tournament, each team plays every other team once. 10 teams are there. Total matches?

Each pair of teams = 1 match. Order doesn't matter (Team A vs B = B vs A).

$^{10}C_2 = \frac{10 \times 9}{2} = 45$ **matches**

---

## 6. PERMUTATION VS COMBINATION — HOW TO DECIDE

---

### The Decision Table:

| Keyword in Problem | Type | Formula |
|-------------------|------|---------|
| Arrange, Order, Rank, Line up, Seat | PERMUTATION | nPr |
| Select, Choose, Committee, Team, Group | COMBINATION | nCr |
| How many ways to seat n in n seats | PERMUTATION | n! |
| How many ways to pick a team | COMBINATION | nCr |
| Form a word | PERMUTATION | nPr |
| Form a group | COMBINATION | nCr |

---

### The Crucial Differentiator:

| Scenario | Is ABC same as BAC? | Type |
|----------|-------------------|------|
| President, VP, Secretary | NO — different roles | PERMUTATION |
| Committee of 3 | YES — same group | COMBINATION |
| Sitting arrangement | NO — different seats | PERMUTATION |
| Sports team selection | YES — same team | COMBINATION |

---

### Example 13: Quick Decision

> How many ways can 3 people be chosen as President, Vice-President, and Secretary from 10 people?

Roles are different → Order matters → **PERMUTATION**
$^{10}P_3 = 10 \times 9 \times 8 = 720$

---

### Example 14: Quick Decision 2

> How many ways can 3 people be chosen for a committee from 10 people?

Committee → Order doesn't matter → **COMBINATION**
$^{10}C_3 = 120$

## 7. SPECIAL PERMUTATION CASES

---

### Case 1: Permutation with Repetition Allowed

> Arranging n objects in r positions with repetition allowed:

$$\text{Total} = n^r$$

**Example:** 4-digit PIN from 0-9 (with repetition): $10^4 = 10,000$

---

### Case 2: Permutations of Objects NOT All Distinct (Repeated Items)

> Arranging n objects where:
> - p objects are of type 1 (identical)
> - q objects are of type 2 (identical)
> - r objects are of type 3 (identical)

$$\text{Total} = \frac{n!}{p! \times q! \times r!}$$

#### Example 15: Word with Repeated Letters

> How many distinct ways can the letters of "MISSISSIPPI" be arranged?

M=1, I=4, S=4, P=2. Total = 11 letters.

$$= \frac{11!}{1! \times 4! \times 4! \times 2!} = \frac{39916800}{1 \times 24 \times 24 \times 2} = \frac{39916800}{1152} = 34650$$

---

#### Example 16: Simpler Word

> Arrangements of "BANANA":

B=1, A=3, N=2. Total = 6.

$$= \frac{6!}{1! \times 3! \times 2!} = \frac{720}{12} = 60$$

---

### Case 3: Rank of a Word in Dictionary

> To find the rank of a word like "GAMES" in dictionary order:

**Method:**
1. Arrange all letters alphabetically.
2. Count words starting with each letter before the target letter.
3. Repeat for each position.

#### Example 17: Rank of "EXAM"

Letters in "EXAM": A, E, M, X (alphabetical order).

Words before "EXAM":
- Starting with A: 3! = 6 words
- Starting with E then A: 2! = 2 words (EAM_, EAX_)
- Starting with EM: 2! = 2 words
- Starting with EX then A: 1! = 1 word (EXAM = our word, so count till here)

Rank = 6 + 2 + 2 + 1 = **Position 11**? Let's recount properly.

"EXAM" = E, X, A, M.

Words before E: Starting with A = 3! = 6.
E is fixed. Remaining: X, A, M → alphabetical = A, M, X.
Words before EX: Starting with EA = 2! = 2 words. Starting with EM = 2! = 2 words.
EX is fixed. Remaining: A, M → alphabetical = A, M.
Words before EXA: Starting with EXM = 1! = 1 word.
EXA is fixed. Remaining: M.
EXAM = 1 arrangement.

Rank = 6 + 2 + 2 + 1 + 1 = **12th**

## 8. SPECIAL COMBINATION CASES

---

### Case 1: At Least / At Most Conditions

> "At least 1" = Total − None
> "At most r" = (0 items) + (1 item) + … + (r items)

#### Example 18: At Least One

> From 5 items, select at least 1. How many ways?

Total subsets = $2^5 = 32$.
Empty set = 1.
At least 1 = 32 − 1 = **31**

---

#### Example 19: At Least 2 Women in Committee

> Committee of 4 from 6 men and 4 women. At least 2 women?

Cases:
- Exactly 2W, 2M: $^4C_2 \times ^6C_2 = 6 \times 15 = 90$
- Exactly 3W, 1M: $^4C_3 \times ^6C_1 = 4 \times 6 = 24$
- Exactly 4W, 0M: $^4C_4 \times ^6C_0 = 1 \times 1 = 1$

Total = 90 + 24 + 1 = **115**

---

### Case 2: Dividing into Groups

> Dividing n objects into groups of r, s, t (where r+s+t=n):

**Named groups (different groups):**
$$\frac{n!}{r! \times s! \times t!}$$

**Unnamed groups (identical groups of size r):**
$$\frac{n!}{r! \times r! \times \ldots \times k!} \div k! \quad \text{where k = number of equal groups}$$

---

#### Example 20: Dividing 9 into Groups

> 9 people divided into groups of 2, 3, 4:

$$\frac{9!}{2! \times 3! \times 4!} = \frac{362880}{2 \times 6 \times 24} = \frac{362880}{288} = 1260$$

---

### Case 3: Selecting from Multiple Groups

#### Example 21:

> 3 boys from 5 boys AND 2 girls from 4 girls:

$$^5C_3 \times ^4C_2 = 10 \times 6 = 60$$

---

### Case 4: Sum of All Combinations

$$\sum_{r=0}^{n} ^nC_r = 2^n$$

$$^nC_1 + ^nC_2 + \ldots + ^nC_n = 2^n - 1$$

---

## 9. CIRCULAR PERMUTATIONS

---

### Definition:

Arranging n objects in a **circle** (not a line).

---

### The Key Insight:

In a line: ABCD ≠ BCDA (they're different — position matters).
In a circle: ABCD = BCDA = CDAB = DABC (all same — just rotated).

**So we fix one person and arrange the rest:**

$$\text{Circular Permutations of n objects} = (n-1)!$$

---

### Clockwise vs Anticlockwise:

- **Necklace / Garland** (can be flipped): $\frac{(n-1)!}{2}$
- **Seating around a table** (cannot flip): $(n-1)!$

---

#### Example 22: Circular Seating

> In how many ways can 6 people sit around a circular table?

$(6-1)! = 5! = 120$

---

#### Example 23: Necklace

> In how many ways can 8 different beads form a necklace?

$\frac{(8-1)!}{2} = \frac{7!}{2} = \frac{5040}{2} = 2520$

---

#### Example 24: Circular with Restriction

> 5 men and 5 women sit alternately around a circular table. How many ways?

Fix 1 man → arrange remaining 4 men: $4!$ ways.
Now 5 women fill the 5 gaps between men: $5!$ ways.

Total = $4! \times 5! = 24 \times 120 = 2880$

---

## 10. PERMUTATIONS WITH RESTRICTIONS

---

### Type 1: Specific Items Must Be Together

**Treat the group as ONE unit, then multiply by arrangements within group.**

Total = $(n - k + 1)! \times k!$

Where n = total items, k = items that must stay together.

#### Example 25: Two Friends Must Sit Together

> 7 people in a row. A and B must sit together.

Treat AB as 1 unit → 6 units total → $6!$ arrangements.
A and B can switch: $2!$ arrangements within the unit.

Total = $6! \times 2! = 720 \times 2 = 1440$

---

### Type 2: Specific Items Must NOT Be Together

**Total arrangements − Arrangements where they ARE together**

#### Example 26: Two Specific Letters Not Adjacent

> Letters A, B, C, D, E in a row. A and B must not be adjacent.

Total = $5! = 120$
A and B together = $4! \times 2! = 48$

Not adjacent = $120 - 48 = 72$

---

### Type 3: Relative Order Fixed

> If A must always come before B (among all arrangements):

$$\text{Total} = \frac{n!}{2}$$

Because exactly half the arrangements have A before B.

---

### Type 4: Certain Positions Fixed

#### Example 27: Vowels at Even Positions

> SUNDAY: Arrange so that vowels (U, A) occupy even positions.

SUNDAY has 6 letters. Even positions: 2, 4, 6.
Choose 2 even positions for U, A: $^3P_2 = 6$ ways.
Arrange remaining 4 consonants: $4! = 24$ ways.

Total = $6 \times 24 = 144$

---

### Type 5: Specific Positions for Specific Items

#### Example 28: Numbers with 0 Restriction

> 5-digit numbers using 0,1,2,3,4 without repetition. How many even numbers?

Case 1: Last digit = 0 → First 4 positions: $4! = 24$ ways.
Case 2: Last digit = 2 or 4:
- Last digit: 2 choices (2 or 4)
- First digit: 3 choices (can't be 0 or last digit)
- Remaining 3 positions: $3!$ = 6 ways
- = $2 \times 3 \times 6 = 36$

Total = $24 + 36 = 60$

---

### Type 6: At Least One from Each Category

#### Example 29:

> 6 boys and 4 girls. Select 4 people. At least 1 girl must be there.

Total = $^{10}C_4 = 210$
No girls (all boys) = $^6C_4 = 15$

At least 1 girl = $210 - 15 = 195$

---

## 11. COMBINATIONS WITH RESTRICTIONS

---

### Type 1: Certain People MUST Be Included

> n people, r must be selected. k specific people must always be in.

**Fix the k people → Choose (r−k) from remaining (n−k):**

$$^{n-k}C_{r-k}$$

#### Example 30:

> Committee of 5 from 12. Two specific people (A and B) must be included.

Fix A and B → Choose 3 from remaining 10:
$^{10}C_3 = 120$

---

### Type 2: Certain People MUST NOT Be Included

> k specific people must be excluded → Choose r from remaining (n−k):

$$^{n-k}C_r$$

#### Example 31:

> Committee of 5 from 12. Three specific people must not be included.

$^{12-3}C_5 = ^9C_5 = 126$

---

### Type 3: Exactly One of Two Specific People

> Exactly one of A and B must be in:

$$(^{n-2}C_{r-1}) \times 2$$

Or: Select A but not B + Select B but not A.

#### Example 32:

> Team of 4 from 10 people. Exactly one of A and B must be in team.

= (A in, B out) + (B in, A out)
= $^8C_3 + ^8C_3 = 56 + 56 = 112$

---

### Type 4: Neither or Both Specific People

> Either both A and B are in, or neither is:

(Both in) + (Neither in) = $^{n-2}C_{r-2} + ^{n-2}C_r$

---

## 12. DISTRIBUTION PROBLEMS

---

### Type 1: n Distinct Items to r Distinct Groups

$$\text{Each item can go to any group} = r^n$$

---

### Type 2: n Distinct Items to r Identical Groups

→ Stirling numbers (advanced — rarely in standard exams)

---

### Type 3: n Identical Items to r Distinct Groups

> Stars and Bars method:

$$\text{Ways} = ^{n+r-1}C_{r-1}$$

#### Example 33:

> Distribute 10 identical chocolates among 4 students (0 or more each):

$^{10+4-1}C_{4-1} = ^{13}C_3 = 286$

---

### Type 4: n Identical Items to r Distinct Groups (Each Gets At Least 1)

$$\text{Ways} = ^{n-1}C_{r-1}$$

#### Example 34:

> Distribute 10 chocolates among 4 students (each gets at least 1):

$^{10-1}C_{4-1} = ^9C_3 = 84$

---

## 13. THE COMPLEMENT METHOD

---

### When to Use It:

> Use complement when the condition says **"at least one"** or when direct counting is complex.

$$\text{Required} = \text{Total} - \text{(What we DON'T want)}$$

---

#### Example 35: At Least One Head

> 5 coins tossed. Probability of at least 1 head?

Total outcomes = $2^5 = 32$
No heads (all tails) = 1

At least 1 head = $32 - 1 = 31$ outcomes

---

#### Example 36: Numbers with No Restriction Digit

> How many 4-digit numbers have at least one digit = 5?

Total 4-digit numbers = 9000 (from 1000 to 9999).
No 5 at all: 
- Thousands: 8 choices (1-9 except 5)
- Others: 9 choices each (0-9 except 5)
= $8 \times 9 \times 9 \times 9 = 5832$

At least one 5 = $9000 - 5832 = 3168$

---

#### Example 37: Committee with Restriction

> Committee of 5 from 6 men and 5 women. Not all can be men.

Total = $^{11}C_5 = 462$
All men = $^6C_5 = 6$

At least 1 woman = $462 - 6 = 456$

---

## 14. QUICK TRICKS & SHORTCUTS

---

### 🚀 TRICK 1: The Decision Rule

**Order matters → Permutation (P)**
**Order doesn't matter → Combination (C)**

OR keyword → ADD
AND keyword → MULTIPLY

---

### 🚀 TRICK 2: Simplify nCr When r is Large

$^nC_r = ^nC_{n-r}$

When r > n/2, switch to $^nC_{n-r}$ for faster computation.

$^{20}C_{17} = ^{20}C_3$ (much easier!)

---

### 🚀 TRICK 3: nCr Formula — Numerator Has r Terms

$$^nC_r = \frac{n(n-1)(n-2)\ldots (n-r+1)}{r!}$$

Numerator: r consecutive integers starting from n going down.
Denominator: r! = r factorial.

---

### 🚀 TRICK 4: Together Items → Bundle Trick

Treat items that MUST be together as ONE single unit.
Arrangements = (remaining + 1 bundle)! × (arrangements within bundle)!

---

### 🚀 TRICK 5: Alternating Arrangement

Men and women alternately in a line:
- If n men and n women: $n! \times n!$ (two cases: MWMW... or WMWM...)
- Actually: $2 \times n! \times n!$ for linear, $n! \times n!$ for circular (fix one).

Wait — for linear:
- MWMW... pattern: $n! \times n!$ ways
- WMWM... pattern: $n! \times n!$ ways
- **Total: $2 \times n! \times n!$**

For circular (n men n women alternate):
- Fix one man: $(n-1)! \times n!$

---

### 🚀 TRICK 6: Circular = Linear/n

Circular arrangements of n objects = Linear arrangements of n objects / n = $(n-1)!$

---

### 🚀 TRICK 7: Complement for "At Least One"

"At least 1" = Total − None
"At least 2" = Total − None − Exactly 1

---

### 🚀 TRICK 8: Total Subsets of n Items

$2^n$ (including empty set)
$2^n - 1$ (excluding empty set)

---

### 🚀 TRICK 9: Handshakes / Diagonals

Handshakes between n people = $^nC_2 = \frac{n(n-1)}{2}$

Diagonals in an n-sided polygon = $^nC_2 - n = \frac{n(n-1)}{2} - n = \frac{n(n-3)}{2}$

---

### 🚀 TRICK 10: Identical Items — Stars & Bars

n identical items, r distinct boxes, 0 or more each:
$$^{n+r-1}C_{r-1}$$

n identical items, r distinct boxes, at least 1 each:
$$^{n-1}C_{r-1}$$

---

### 🚀 TRICK 11: Word Arrangement with Repetition

Arrangements of word = $\frac{(\text{total letters})!}{\text{product of (count of each repeated letter)!}}$

---

### 🚀 TRICK 12: Rank of a Word

For each position, count how many letters are alphabetically smaller than the current letter.
Multiply by (remaining positions)! and sum up. Add 1 for the word itself.

---

### 🚀 TRICK 13: No Two Items Adjacent (Gap Method)

To arrange k items among n items such that no two of the k items are adjacent:

1. First arrange the n items: $n!$ ways.
2. k items must go in the n+1 gaps created: $^{n+1}P_k$ ways.

---

### 🚀 TRICK 14: Pascal's Identity

$^nC_r + ^nC_{r-1} = ^{n+1}C_r$

Used in quick calculation: $^7C_3 = ^6C_2 + ^6C_3 = 15 + 20 = 35$ ✓

---

### 🚀 TRICK 15: Quick Value of Common nCr

| Expression | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| $^nC_0$ or $^nC_n$ | 1 |
| $^nC_1$ or $^nC_{n-1}$ | n |
| $^nC_2$ | n(n-1)/2 |
| $^nC_3$ | n(n-1)(n-2)/6 |

---

## 15. COMMON MISTAKE ALERTS

---

### ⚠️ MISTAKE 1: 0! ≠ 0

**Wrong:** 0! = 0
**Right:** 0! = **1** (by definition — this is crucial for formulas to work)

---

### ⚠️ MISTAKE 2: Confusing Permutation and Combination

"Select a team of 5" → Combination (order doesn't matter)
"Assign 5 roles to 5 people" → Permutation (order matters)

Always check: Is ABC same as BAC in this context?

---

### ⚠️ MISTAKE 3: Circular Permutation — Using n! Instead of (n-1)!

**Wrong:** 6 people around a table = $6!$
**Right:** $6!$ for linear, $(6-1)! = 5!$ for circular

---

### ⚠️ MISTAKE 4: Together Problem — Forgetting Internal Arrangements

"A and B must be together":
**Wrong:** Just compute $(n-1)!$
**Right:** $(n-1)! \times 2!$ (the 2! accounts for AB and BA inside the bundle)

---

### ⚠️ MISTAKE 5: Identical Objects — Dividing by Wrong Factorial

"LEVEL" has L=1, E=2, V=1.
**Wrong:** Divide by $2!$ only (just for E)
**Right:** $\frac{5!}{1! \times 2! \times 1! \times 1!}$ — ALL letters' factorials in denominator

---

### ⚠️ MISTAKE 6: Not Treating 0 as Special in Number Problems

In multi-digit numbers, **0 cannot be the leading digit**.
Always handle the first digit separately when 0 is in the set.

---

### ⚠️ MISTAKE 7: nPr vs nCr Swap in Exam Pressure

Before writing nPr or nCr, ask the order question:
"Can I swap two selected items and get a different outcome?"
YES → P. NO → C.

---

### ⚠️ MISTAKE 8: Over-counting in Identical Group Division

When 12 people split into 3 groups of 4 each (identical groups):

$$\frac{^{12}C_4 \times ^8C_4 \times ^4C_4}{3!}$$

Divide by 3! because the 3 groups are identical (unordered).

### ⚠️ MISTAKE 9: Adding When Should Multiply (and Vice Versa)

AND → Multiply. OR → Add.

"Red OR Blue ball" → Add.
"Red ball AND a blue ball" → Multiply.

### ⚠️ MISTAKE 10: Forgetting Complementary Case

"At least 2" requires: total − (0 case) − (1 case).
Don't forget to subtract ALL unwanted cases.

## 🏆 TOP EXAM TIPS

1. **ALWAYS ask "Does order matter?"** before writing any formula. This one question determines whether you use P or C.
2. **0! = 1** — never forget this. The entire formula breaks if you treat it as 0.
3. **nCr = nC(n-r)** — use this to switch to the smaller value when r > n/2.
4. **"Together" problems** — bundle the together items, treat as one, multiply by internal arrangements.
5. **"Not together"** — use complement: Total − Together.
6. **"At least one"** — almost always use complement: Total − None.
7. **Circular ≠ Linear** — divide by n for circular, or equivalently fix one person and arrange rest in (n-1)!.
8. **Leading digit problem** — when 0 is in digit set, always handle first digit separately (it cannot be 0).
9. **Repeated items in word arrangement** — always divide by factorial of each letter's frequency.
10. **Check AND vs OR** — AND means multiply the counts, OR means add them. This single distinction resolves many confusion points.
