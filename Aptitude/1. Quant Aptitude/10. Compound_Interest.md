## 1. CORE DEFINITIONS & TERMINOLOGY

---

### 💰 Principal (P)
The **original sum** invested or borrowed.

---

### 📈 Compound Interest (CI)
Interest calculated on **Principal + previously accumulated interest**.

> Unlike Simple Interest (calculated only on original P), CI grows on itself — this is called **"interest on interest"**.

---

### 🏦 Amount (A)
Total money at the end of the period.
$$A = P + CI$$

---

### 📊 Rate of Interest (R)
Percentage interest charged/earned **per annum** (unless stated otherwise).

---

### 🕐 Time (n)
Number of **compounding periods** (years, unless stated otherwise).

---

### 🔄 Compounding Period
How frequently interest is added to principal:
- **Annually** — once a year
- **Semi-annually** — twice a year
- **Quarterly** — 4 times a year
- **Monthly** — 12 times a year

---

### 🔑 The Core Idea — WHY CI > SI

```
Year 1: Interest = R% of P              → Same as SI
Year 2: Interest = R% of (P + Year1 CI) → MORE than SI
Year 3: Interest = R% of (P + CI so far) → Even MORE

→ CI snowballs. SI stays flat.
```

---

## 2. FUNDAMENTAL FORMULAS

---

### The Master Formula:

$$\boxed{A = P \times \left(1 + \frac{R}{100}\right)^n}$$

$$\boxed{CI = A - P = P \left[\left(1 + \frac{R}{100}\right)^n - 1\right]}$$

Where:
- **P** = Principal
- **R** = Rate % per annum
- **n** = Time in years
- **A** = Amount (Principal + CI)

---

### Derived Formulas:

$$P = \frac{A}{\left(1 + \frac{R}{100}\right)^n}$$

$$R = \left[\left(\frac{A}{P}\right)^{1/n} - 1\right] \times 100$$

$$n = \frac{\log(A/P)}{\log\left(1 + \frac{R}{100}\right)}$$

---

### The Multiplying Factor (MF) — Speed Tool:

| Rate R% | MF = (1 + R/100) |
|---------|-----------------|
| 5% | 1.05 |
| 10% | 1.10 |
| 20% | 1.20 |
| 25% | 1.25 |
| 4% | 1.04 |
| 8% | 1.08 |

$$A = P \times (MF)^n$$

---

## 3. COMPOUNDING FREQUENCIES

---

### When compounding is NOT annual — adjust R and n:

$$\boxed{A = P \times \left(1 + \frac{R}{100 \times k}\right)^{n \times k}}$$

Where **k** = number of times compounded per year.

---

### Quick Reference Table:

| Compounded | k | Rate per period | Periods |
|------------|---|----------------|---------|
| Annually | 1 | R/1 | n×1 |
| Semi-annually | 2 | R/2 | n×2 |
| Quarterly | 4 | R/4 | n×4 |
| Monthly | 12 | R/12 | n×12 |

---

#### Example: Semi-annual vs Annual

> P = ₹10,000, R = 10% p.a., n = 2 years.
> Compare Annual and Semi-annual compounding.

**Annual:**
$$A = 10000 \times (1.10)^2 = 10000 \times 1.21 = ₹12,100$$

**Semi-annual (k=2):**
$$A = 10000 \times \left(1 + \frac{10}{200}\right)^{2 \times 2} = 10000 \times (1.05)^4$$
$$= 10000 \times 1.21550625 = ₹12,155.06$$

> **Semi-annual gives more** because interest compounds more frequently!

## 4. BASIC TO ADVANCED EXAMPLES

---

### ─── BASIC LEVEL ───

#### Example 1: Find CI and Amount (2 years)

> P = ₹8000, R = 10% p.a., n = 2 years. Find CI and Amount.

**Method 1 — Formula:**
$$A = 8000 \times (1.10)^2 = 8000 \times 1.21 = ₹9680$$
$$CI = 9680 - 8000 = ₹1680$$

**Method 2 — Year by Year (builds intuition):**

| Year | Opening Balance | Interest (10%) | Closing Balance |
|------|----------------|---------------|----------------|
| 1 | 8000 | 800 | 8800 |
| 2 | 8800 | 880 | 9680 |

**Total CI = 800 + 880 = ₹1680** ✓

> Notice: Year 2 interest (₹880) > Year 1 interest (₹800) — this is the "interest on interest" effect!

---

#### Example 2: Find CI for 3 Years

> P = ₹5000, R = 8% p.a., n = 3 years.

$$A = 5000 \times (1.08)^3 = 5000 \times 1.259712 = ₹6298.56$$
$$CI = 6298.56 - 5000 = ₹1298.56$$

---

#### Example 3: Find Principal

> Amount after 2 years at 5% CI = ₹11025. Find P.

$$11025 = P \times (1.05)^2 = P \times 1.1025$$
$$P = \frac{11025}{1.1025} = ₹10,000$$

---

#### Example 4: Find Rate

> ₹1000 becomes ₹1210 in 2 years at CI. Find R.

$$1210 = 1000 \times \left(1 + \frac{R}{100}\right)^2$$
$$\left(1 + \frac{R}{100}\right)^2 = 1.21$$
$$1 + \frac{R}{100} = 1.1$$
$$R = 10\%$$

---

#### Example 5: Find Time

> At 10% CI, ₹5000 becomes ₹6655. Find time (n).

$$6655 = 5000 \times (1.10)^n$$
$$(1.10)^n = \frac{6655}{5000} = 1.331$$

Check: $(1.10)^3 = 1.331$ ✓

**n = 3 years**

---

### ─── INTERMEDIATE LEVEL ───

#### Example 6: Half-yearly Compounding

> P = ₹16,000, R = 10% p.a., compounded half-yearly for 1 year. Find CI.

$$A = 16000 \times \left(1 + \frac{10}{200}\right)^{1 \times 2} = 16000 \times (1.05)^2$$
$$= 16000 \times 1.1025 = ₹17,640$$
$$CI = 17640 - 16000 = ₹1640$$

---

#### Example 7: Quarterly Compounding

> P = ₹10,000, R = 8% p.a., compounded quarterly for 1 year.

$$A = 10000 \times \left(1 + \frac{8}{400}\right)^{4} = 10000 \times (1.02)^4$$
$$= 10000 \times 1.08243 = ₹10,824.30$$
$$CI = ₹824.30$$

---

#### Example 8: Different Rates for Different Years

> P = ₹12,000. Rate = 5% in Year 1, 8% in Year 2, 10% in Year 3. Find Amount.

$$A = 12000 \times \left(1 + \frac{5}{100}\right) \times \left(1 + \frac{8}{100}\right) \times \left(1 + \frac{10}{100}\right)$$
$$= 12000 \times 1.05 \times 1.08 \times 1.10$$
$$= 12000 \times 1.2474 = ₹14,968.80$$

---

#### Example 9: CI When Rate is Given as Fraction

> P = ₹6250, R = 8% p.a., n = 2 years.

$$A = 6250 \times \left(\frac{108}{100}\right)^2 = 6250 \times \frac{11664}{10000} = \frac{6250 \times 11664}{10000}$$
$$= \frac{72900000}{10000} = ₹7290$$

---

#### Example 10: CI for 1.5 Years — Semi-annual

> P = ₹20,000, R = 20% p.a., compounded semi-annually for 1.5 years.

$$A = 20000 \times \left(1 + \frac{20}{200}\right)^{3} = 20000 \times (1.10)^3$$
$$= 20000 \times 1.331 = ₹26,620$$
$$CI = ₹6,620$$

> **Tip:** 1.5 years × 2 (semi-annual) = 3 periods, rate per period = 20/2 = 10%

---

#### Example 11: Find Sum When CI and SI Differ

> The CI on a sum for 2 years at 4% is ₹2 more than SI. Find the sum.

**Using formula:**
$$CI - SI = P \times \left(\frac{R}{100}\right)^2$$
$$2 = P \times \left(\frac{4}{100}\right)^2 = P \times 0.0016$$
$$P = \frac{2}{0.0016} = ₹1250$$

---

#### Example 12: CI with Annual Amounts Given

> A sum at CI amounts to ₹26,460 in 2 years and ₹27,783 in 3 years. Find P and R.

**Step 1:** Find Rate
$$\frac{A_3}{A_2} = 1 + \frac{R}{100}$$
$$\frac{27783}{26460} = 1 + \frac{R}{100} = 1.05$$
$$R = 5\%$$

**Step 2:** Find P
$$26460 = P \times (1.05)^2 = P \times 1.1025$$
$$P = \frac{26460}{1.1025} = ₹24,000$$

> **KEY INSIGHT:** In CI, the RATIO of consecutive amounts equals the multiplying factor (1 + R/100).

---

#### Example 13: When Does a Sum Double at CI?

> At what rate will ₹1000 double in 2 years at CI?

$$2000 = 1000 \times \left(1 + \frac{R}{100}\right)^2$$
$$\left(1 + \frac{R}{100}\right)^2 = 2$$
$$1 + \frac{R}{100} = \sqrt{2} \approx 1.4142$$
$$R \approx 41.42\%$$

---

### ─── ADVANCED LEVEL ───

#### Example 14: Rule of 72 (Doubling Time Trick)

> At 8% CI, approximately in how many years will a sum double?

$$\text{Years to double} \approx \frac{72}{R} = \frac{72}{8} = 9 \text{ years}$$

> **Rule of 72:** Divide 72 by rate → approximate years to double. (Works best for 6–12%)

---

#### Example 15: CI — Instalment Problem

> What annual payment will discharge a debt of ₹7620 in 3 years at 10% CI?

Let instalment = x.

The present value of all instalments = ₹7620:

$$\frac{x}{1.10} + \frac{x}{(1.10)^2} + \frac{x}{(1.10)^3} = 7620$$

$$x \left[\frac{1}{1.10} + \frac{1}{1.21} + \frac{1}{1.331}\right] = 7620$$

$$x \left[0.9091 + 0.8264 + 0.7513\right] = 7620$$

$$x \times 2.4868 = 7620$$

$$x = \frac{7620}{2.4868} \approx ₹3063.27$$

---

#### Example 16: Population Growth (CI Model)

> A town's population is 64,000. It grows at 5% p.a. for 2 years, then decreases at 2% for next 2 years. Find final population.

$$\text{After growth} = 64000 \times (1.05)^2 = 64000 \times 1.1025 = 70,560$$

$$\text{After decrease} = 70560 \times (0.98)^2 = 70560 \times 0.9604 = 67,778.5 \approx 67,779$$

---

#### Example 17: Depreciation

> A machine costs ₹1,00,000. It depreciates at 10% p.a. Find value after 3 years.

$$\text{Value} = 100000 \times \left(1 - \frac{10}{100}\right)^3 = 100000 \times (0.90)^3$$
$$= 100000 \times 0.729 = ₹72,900$$

---

#### Example 18: Effective Annual Rate (EAR)

> What is the effective annual rate if nominal rate = 12% compounded quarterly?

$$EAR = \left(1 + \frac{R}{100k}\right)^k - 1 = \left(1 + \frac{12}{400}\right)^4 - 1$$
$$= (1.03)^4 - 1 = 1.12551 - 1 = 12.551\%$$

---

#### Example 19: CI on CI (Two-Stage)

> ₹10,000 is invested at 10% CI for 3 years. The amount is reinvested at 8% CI for 2 more years. Find total amount.

**Stage 1:**
$$A_1 = 10000 \times (1.10)^3 = 10000 \times 1.331 = ₹13,310$$

**Stage 2:**
$$A_2 = 13310 \times (1.08)^2 = 13310 \times 1.1664 = ₹15,525.38$$

---

#### Example 20: Find P When CI for Two Consecutive Years Are Given

> CI for 3rd year = ₹5324.80. CI for 4th year = ₹5591.04. Find P and R.

**Step 1:** Ratio of CI in consecutive years = (1 + R/100)

$$\frac{5591.04}{5324.80} = 1 + \frac{R}{100} = 1.05 \Rightarrow R = 5\%$$

**Step 2:** CI for 3rd year = A after year 3 − A after year 2

Let's denote:
$$CI_n = P \times \left(\frac{R}{100}\right) \times \left(1 + \frac{R}{100}\right)^{n-1}$$

$$5324.80 = P \times 0.05 \times (1.05)^2 = P \times 0.05 \times 1.1025 = P \times 0.055125$$

$$P = \frac{5324.80}{0.055125} = ₹96,590.91 \approx ₹96,000$$

> Verify: If P = 96000, CI for year 3 = 96000 × 0.05 × (1.05)² = 96000 × 0.055125 = 5292 ≈ close.

Exact: P = ₹80,000 (try): 80000 × 0.055125 = 4410. Not matching.
Let P = ₹96,000: 96000 × 0.055125 = 5292. Not 5324.8.

Back-solve: P = 5324.80/0.055125 = **₹96,590.91** ← This is the exact answer.

## 5. CI AND SI RELATIONSHIP

---

### For Same P, R, T — CI is ALWAYS greater than SI (for T > 1 year).

---

### Difference Formulas:

**For 2 Years:**
$$CI - SI = P \times \left(\frac{R}{100}\right)^2$$

**For 3 Years:**
$$CI - SI = P \times \left(\frac{R}{100}\right)^2 \times \left(3 + \frac{R}{100}\right)$$

**For n years:**
$$CI - SI = \text{SI} \times \left[\left(1 + \frac{R}{100}\right)^{n-1} - 1\right] \div n \times ... $$

> For exams: **Just memorise the 2-year and 3-year formulas.**

---

#### Example 21: Classic CI−SI Problem

> The difference between CI and SI on ₹5000 for 2 years at 4% is:

$$CI - SI = 5000 \times \left(\frac{4}{100}\right)^2 = 5000 \times 0.0016 = ₹8$$

**Verify:**
- SI = 5000 × 4 × 2/100 = ₹400
- CI: A = 5000 × (1.04)² = 5000 × 1.0816 = 5408. CI = ₹408.
- Difference = 408 − 400 = **₹8** ✓

---

#### Example 22: 3-Year CI−SI Difference

> P = ₹10,000, R = 10%. Find CI − SI for 3 years.

$$CI - SI = 10000 \times \left(\frac{10}{100}\right)^2 \times \left(3 + \frac{10}{100}\right)$$
$$= 10000 \times 0.01 \times 3.1 = ₹310$$

**Verify:**
- SI = 10000 × 10 × 3/100 = ₹3000
- CI = 10000 × (1.10)³ − 10000 = 13310 − 10000 = ₹3310
- Difference = 3310 − 3000 = **₹310** ✓

---

#### Year-by-Year Breakdown of CI vs SI:

> P = ₹1000, R = 10%, for 4 years

| Year | SI Interest | CI Interest | Difference |
|------|------------|------------|------------|
| 1 | 100 | 100 | 0 |
| 2 | 100 | 110 | 10 |
| 3 | 100 | 121 | 21 |
| 4 | 100 | 133.1 | 33.1 |
| **Total** | **400** | **464.1** | **64.1** |

> Year 1 is always the same. From year 2 onwards, CI > SI.

## 6. POPULATION & DEPRECIATION PROBLEMS

---

### Population Growth Formula:

$$\text{Population after n years} = P_0 \times \left(1 + \frac{R}{100}\right)^n$$

### Population Decrease Formula:

$$\text{Population after n years} = P_0 \times \left(1 - \frac{R}{100}\right)^n$$

### Depreciation Formula (Asset value decreases):

$$\text{Value after n years} = \text{Original Value} \times \left(1 - \frac{R}{100}\right)^n$$

---

#### Example 23: Mixed Growth and Decline

> Town population = 10,000. Grows 20% in year 1, declines 20% in year 2. Final population?

$$= 10000 \times 1.20 \times 0.80 = 10000 \times 0.96 = 9600$$

> ⚠️ **Not back to 10,000!** A 20% gain followed by 20% loss always gives a NET LOSS of (20²/100)% = **4%**

---

#### Example 24: Car Depreciation

> A car worth ₹5,00,000 depreciates at 15% annually. Find value after 2 years.

$$= 500000 \times (0.85)^2 = 500000 \times 0.7225 = ₹3,61,250$$

---

#### Example 25: Find Initial Population

> After 2 years of growth at 5% p.a., a town's population is 44,100. Find the initial population.

$$44100 = P_0 \times (1.05)^2 = P_0 \times 1.1025$$
$$P_0 = \frac{44100}{1.1025} = 40,000$$

## 7. GROWTH & DECAY MODELS

---

### General Model:

$$\text{Final} = \text{Initial} \times \prod_{i} \left(1 \pm \frac{R_i}{100}\right)$$

- Use **+** for growth
- Use **−** for decay/depreciation

---

#### Example 26: Export Growth Problem

> India's exports = ₹200 crore. Grows 10% in 2014, 20% in 2015, decreases 5% in 2016. Find 2016 exports.

$$= 200 \times 1.10 \times 1.20 \times 0.95 = 200 \times 1.254 = ₹250.8 \text{ crore}$$

## 8. CI WITH DIFFERENT RATES EACH YEAR

---

### Formula:

$$A = P \times \left(1 + \frac{R_1}{100}\right) \times \left(1 + \frac{R_2}{100}\right) \times \left(1 + \frac{R_3}{100}\right) \times \ldots$$

---

#### Example 27:

> ₹15,000 is invested. Rate = 4% in Year 1, 8% in Year 2, 12% in Year 3.

$$A = 15000 \times 1.04 \times 1.08 \times 1.12$$
$$= 15000 \times 1.257 = ₹18,858.24$$

$$CI = 18858.24 - 15000 = ₹3858.24$$

---

#### Example 28: Two Years, Find P if CI Given

> CI for 2 years at 10% in year 1 and 12% in year 2 on some principal is ₹2312. Find P.

$$A = P \times 1.10 \times 1.12 = P \times 1.232$$
$$CI = A - P = 0.232P = 2312$$
$$P = \frac{2312}{0.232} = ₹9966 \approx ₹10,000$$

## 9. QUICK TRICKS & SHORTCUTS

---

### 🚀 TRICK 1: The Multiplying Factor Method

Instead of computing (1 + R/100)ⁿ each time, use:

| R% | (1+R/100) | Square | Cube |
|----|----------|--------|------|
| 5% | 1.05 | 1.1025 | 1.157625 |
| 10% | 1.10 | 1.21 | 1.331 |
| 20% | 1.20 | 1.44 | 1.728 |
| 25% | 1.25 | 1.5625 | 1.953125 |
| 4% | 1.04 | 1.0816 | 1.124864 |

> **Memorise the squares and cubes of common multiplying factors!**

---

### 🚀 TRICK 2: CI for 2 Years — Quick Formula

$$CI_{2\text{ yrs}} = \frac{R}{100} \times P \times \left(2 + \frac{R}{100}\right)$$

Example: P = ₹1000, R = 10%:
$$CI = 0.10 \times 1000 \times 2.10 = ₹210$$

Verify: 1000 × (1.1)² − 1000 = 1210 − 1000 = ₹210 ✓

---

### 🚀 TRICK 3: Rule of 72

$$\text{Years to Double} \approx \frac{72}{R}$$

| Rate | Years to Double |
|------|----------------|
| 6% | 12 years |
| 8% | 9 years |
| 9% | 8 years |
| 12% | 6 years |
| 18% | 4 years |
| 24% | 3 years |
| 36% | 2 years |

---

### 🚀 TRICK 4: Rule of 114

$$\text{Years to Triple} \approx \frac{114}{R}$$

---

### 🚀 TRICK 5: CI − SI Shortcut (2 years)

$$CI - SI = P \times \left(\frac{R}{100}\right)^2 = \frac{SI}{2} \times \frac{R}{100} = \frac{(SI)^2}{4P}$$

---

### 🚀 TRICK 6: When Two Consecutive Year CIs are Given

$$R = \frac{CI_{n+1} - CI_n}{CI_n} \times 100$$

Example: CI in year 2 = ₹110, CI in year 3 = ₹121
$$R = \frac{121 - 110}{110} \times 100 = 10\%$$

---

### 🚀 TRICK 7: Ratio of Consecutive Amounts = (1 + R/100)

$$\frac{A_2}{A_1} = \frac{A_3}{A_2} = \frac{A_n}{A_{n-1}} = 1 + \frac{R}{100}$$

This ratio is CONSTANT throughout.

---

### 🚀 TRICK 8: Half-yearly at R% = Annual at (R/2)% for 2n periods

$$\text{Half-yearly: } A = P \times \left(1 + \frac{R}{200}\right)^{2n}$$

---

### 🚀 TRICK 9: Effective Interest Rate Comparison

Annual 10% vs Semi-annual 10%:
- Annual: 10% effective
- Semi-annual: (1.05)² − 1 = 10.25% effective

**Semi-annual always gives higher effective rate!**

---

### 🚀 TRICK 10: Growth-Decay Net Effect

If something grows by x% then declines by x%:
$$\text{Net change} = -\frac{x^2}{100}\% \text{ (always a NET LOSS)}$$

Example: +20% then −20% → net = −4%

---

### 🚀 TRICK 11: Quick Check — CI > SI Always for n > 1

For n = 1: CI = SI (exactly)
For n = 2: CI > SI by P(R/100)²
For n = 3: CI > SI by P(R/100)²(3 + R/100)

---

### 🚀 TRICK 12: Fraction-Based Calculation

For R = 20% → MF = 6/5

$$A = P \times \left(\frac{6}{5}\right)^n$$

Useful fractions:

| R% | MF Fraction |
|----|------------|
| 10% | 11/10 |
| 20% | 6/5 |
| 25% | 5/4 |
| 33.33% | 4/3 |
| 50% | 3/2 |
| 5% | 21/20 |
| 4% | 26/25 |

Example: P = ₹1000, R = 20%, n = 3
$$A = 1000 \times \left(\frac{6}{5}\right)^3 = 1000 \times \frac{216}{125} = ₹1728$$

---

### 🚀 TRICK 13: Instalment Formula

For a loan of P to be repaid in n equal annual instalments at R% CI:

$$\text{Instalment} = \frac{P \times \left(\frac{R}{100}\right) \times \left(1 + \frac{R}{100}\right)^n}{\left(1 + \frac{R}{100}\right)^n - 1}$$

## 10. COMMON MISTAKE ALERTS

---

### ⚠️ MISTAKE 1: Using Simple Interest Formula for CI

**Wrong:** CI = P × R × T / 100
**Right:** CI = P[(1 + R/100)ⁿ − 1]

---

### ⚠️ MISTAKE 2: Not Adjusting Rate for Non-Annual Compounding

**Wrong (quarterly):** A = P × (1 + R/100)⁴
**Right:** A = P × (1 + R/400)⁴

---

### ⚠️ MISTAKE 3: Applying CI−SI Formula Incorrectly

**Wrong (3 years):** CI − SI = P × (R/100)²
**Right (3 years):** CI − SI = P × (R/100)² × (3 + R/100)

The formula CI − SI = P(R/100)² is ONLY for **2 years**.

---

### ⚠️ MISTAKE 4: Growth + Decline = Zero Change (WRONG!)

**Wrong:** 20% up then 20% down = same value
**Right:** 20% up then 20% down = **4% net loss**

Net = (1 + x/100)(1 − x/100) = 1 − x²/10000

---

### ⚠️ MISTAKE 5: Ratio Trap — Amounts vs Interest

In CI problems, the ratio of **consecutive amounts** is (1 + R/100).
But the ratio of **consecutive year's interest** is also (1 + R/100).

These are different quantities — don't mix them up.

---

### ⚠️ MISTAKE 6: Rounding Too Early

CI calculations involve many decimal multiplications. Round only at the **final step** to avoid accumulation errors.

---

### ⚠️ MISTAKE 7: Using n = Time in Months/Days Directly

Just like SI, **n must be in years** (or periods matching the rate). Always convert.

---

### ⚠️ MISTAKE 8: CI for Depreciation — Sign Error

For depreciation: use (1 − R/100), **NOT** (1 + R/100).

**Wrong:** Value = P × (1 + R/100)ⁿ
**Right:** Value = P × (1 − R/100)ⁿ

## 🏆 TOP EXAM TIPS

1. **Year-by-year table** never fails — when confused, build the table.
2. **Ratio of consecutive amounts = (1 + R/100)** — use this to find R instantly.
3. **For n doubles in T years → n×T years for 2ⁿ times** (4T for 4×, 3T for 8×).
4. **Half-yearly/quarterly** — halve/quarter the rate, double/quadruple the time.
5. **CI − SI formulas** — memorise both 2-year and 3-year versions exactly.
6. **Growth then Decay ≠ Back to start** — always compute, never assume.
7. **Use fraction MF** (e.g., 6/5 for 20%) to avoid decimals in long calculations.
8. **Different rates each year** — just multiply (1 + Rᵢ/100) for each year.
9. **Instalment problems** — use Present Value approach: sum of PVs = loan.
10. **Rule of 72** — memorise it; saves time for "how many years to double" questions.
