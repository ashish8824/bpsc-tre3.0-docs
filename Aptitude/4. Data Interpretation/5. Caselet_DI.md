## 1. WHAT IS CASELET DI?

**Caselet DI** (also called **Paragraph-Based DI**) is a question format where all data is given in the form of **a paragraph of text** instead of a chart, graph, or table.

- No visual aids — data is embedded in one or more paragraphs
- You must **read, extract, and organize** the data yourself before solving
- Each caselet paragraph is followed by **5–10 questions**
- The data is intentionally scattered across sentences to test your ability to synthesize

```
Example of a Caselet Paragraph:
"A school has 800 students. 60% of the students are boys. 40% of the boys
and 25% of the girls participate in sports. Of the boys who participate in
sports, 50% play cricket. Of the girls who participate in sports, 60% play
basketball. The remaining students who participate in sports play other games."

From this paragraph, 5–8 questions can be asked, none of which are
answered by a single sentence — all require extracting and combining data.
```

---

### Where It Appears

```
Caselet DI appears in:
→ IBPS PO Mains / SBI PO Mains (2–3 sets of 5 questions each)
→ CAT (most complex variant — 4–6 questions per caselet)
→ SSC CGL Tier-II

In banking exams, caselet DI typically carries 10–15 marks per paper.
Mastering it can be the difference between clearing and not clearing.
```

## 2. WHY CASELET DI IS THE HARDEST DI FORMAT

```
REASON 1 — No Visual Structure:
Bar graphs, pie charts, and tables organize data visually.
Your eyes locate the right row and column, or the right bar height.
In a caselet, data is hidden inside sentences and must be extracted manually.

REASON 2 — Data is Scattered and Indirect:
Information about the same entity is spread across multiple sentences.
"A has 40% of total. B has 30 more than A. C has twice B."
No single sentence gives you everything. You must chain sentences together.

REASON 3 — Derived Quantities Pile Up:
Each piece of data is used to generate the next.
Error in Step 1 → wrong answers in Steps 2, 3, 4, 5.
Chain errors are catastrophic in caselets.

REASON 4 — Language Traps:
Subtle differences in phrasing:
"40% of boys" vs "40% are boys"
"25 more than X" vs "25% more than X"
"twice as many as Y" vs "twice the % of Y"
One misread → complete failure on that caselet.

REASON 5 — No Built-In Verification:
In a table, row totals let you check your math.
In a caselet, there's no automatic check. You must build your own.
```

## 3. TYPES OF CASELET DI QUESTIONS

---

### Type 1 — Direct Value Extraction

```
"How many boys are there in the school?"
→ One step: Total × boy% = answer.
→ Easiest type. Requires only one sentence from the paragraph.
```

---

### Type 2 — Two-Step Derived Value

```
"How many boys play cricket?"
→ Step 1: Find total boys.
→ Step 2: Find sports-playing boys.
→ Step 3: Find cricket-playing boys among sports boys.
→ Requires 3 sentences chained together.
```

---

### Type 3 — Comparison / Ratio

```
"The number of girls who play basketball is how many times the number of boys who play cricket?"
→ Compute both values independently.
→ Divide to find the ratio.
→ Requires data from multiple parts of the paragraph.
```

---

### Type 4 — Percentage / Share

```
"What % of total students play cricket?"
→ Find cricket players (numerator).
→ Use total students as denominator.
→ Calculate %.
```

---

### Type 5 — Conditional / Nested

```
"Of the boys who do NOT play sports, 30% study in Grade 10.
 How many Grade 10 non-sports boys are there?"
→ Find non-sports boys.
→ Apply 30% condition.
→ Requires tracking "complement" groups.
```

---

### Type 6 — Unknown Variable / Reverse

```
"If the total number of girls who play sports is 90, what is the total number of students?"
→ Work backward from a given derived value to find a fundamental value.
→ Uses algebra / reverse percentage.
```

---

### Type 7 — Change / Update Condition

```
"If 20 more boys join the sports program, what % of all boys now play sports?"
→ Update the relevant value.
→ Recompute the %.
```

## 5. CONCEPT 1 — READING A CASELET PARAGRAPH

> Reading a caselet is fundamentally different from reading any other DI format. You are not looking at numbers on axes — you are extracting a hidden dataset from dense prose. Every sentence may (or may not) contain a data point. Your job is to find all data points, organize them, and only then answer questions.

---

### 5.1 The SCAN Method — How to Read Any Caselet

**Apply this four-step method to every caselet, in order, before answering any question.**

---

#### STEP S — Survey (30 seconds)

```
Read the entire paragraph ONCE without stopping.
Do NOT try to extract data on first read.
Goal: understand the general topic and structure.

Ask yourself:
→ What is this caselet about? (School? Company? City? Survey?)
→ How many groups are mentioned? (Males, females, departments, products?)
→ What is the "universe"? (Total students? Total employees? Total population?)
→ Are there sub-groups within groups? (Boys who play sports, girls who don't, etc.)

This survey tells you WHAT to look for before you look for it.
```

---

#### STEP C — Categorize (1 minute)

```
On second read, CATEGORIZE all entities and groups mentioned.

Draw a simple hierarchy or tree diagram:
                    [TOTAL: 800 students]
                    /                 \
              [Boys: ?]           [Girls: ?]
             /       \            /        \
       [Sports]  [No sports]  [Sports]  [No sports]
        /    \                 /    \
   [Cricket] [Other]    [Basketball] [Other]

This tree shows ALL groups and their relationships.
Any question will ask about one or more of these boxes.

Building this tree prevents:
→ Forgetting a group
→ Double-counting
→ Using the wrong denominator
```

---

#### STEP A — Assign Values (2 minutes)

```
On third read (sentence by sentence), assign values to the tree nodes.

Strategy:
→ Start from the TOP of the tree (the total / universe).
→ Work DOWNWARD, computing each sub-group from its parent.
→ Use "remaining = total − given" to fill complement groups.

Example:
"800 students. 60% boys."
→ Boys = 60% × 800 = 480. Girls = 800 − 480 = 320.

"40% of boys participate in sports."
→ Sports boys = 40% × 480 = 192. Non-sports boys = 480 − 192 = 288.

"25% of girls participate in sports."
→ Sports girls = 25% × 320 = 80. Non-sports girls = 320 − 80 = 240.

"50% of sports boys play cricket."
→ Cricket boys = 50% × 192 = 96. Non-cricket sports boys = 96.

"60% of sports girls play basketball."
→ Basketball girls = 60% × 80 = 48. Other sports girls = 32.

Now EVERY node of the tree has a value.
```

---

#### STEP N — Note-Make (30 seconds)

```
Write a clean, organized summary of ALL computed values.

Format example:
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
Total students = 800
Boys = 480 | Girls = 320
Sports boys = 192 | Non-sports boys = 288
Sports girls = 80 | Non-sports girls = 240
Cricket boys = 96 | Other sports boys = 96
Basketball girls = 48 | Other sports girls = 32
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

This note is your personal data table.
Every question becomes a lookup + formula on this table.
No re-reading of the paragraph needed after this point.

TIME FOR SCAN: 4–5 minutes total.
BENEFIT: Answering each of 5–8 questions takes < 30 seconds each.
```

### 5.3 Types of Data Given in Caselets

**You must recognize all of these and convert them to usable numbers:**

---

#### DATA TYPE 1 — Absolute Value (Direct)

```
"A company has 1200 employees."
"There are 500 male workers."
Extract: Total = 1200. Males = 500.
Females = 1200 − 500 = 700 (by subtraction).
```

---

#### DATA TYPE 2 — Percentage of Total

```
"60% of students are boys."
"25% of all employees work in IT."
Convert: Boys = 60% × Total. IT employees = 25% × Total.
```

---

#### DATA TYPE 3 — Percentage of Sub-group

```
"40% of the boys participate in sports."
"Of the IT employees, 30% are female."
Convert: Sports boys = 40% × (Boys). Female IT = 30% × (IT employees).
TRAP: This % is of the SUB-GROUP, not the total.
```

---

#### DATA TYPE 4 — Ratio

```
"The ratio of male to female is 3:2."
"Boys and girls are in the ratio 5:3."
Convert: Males = [3/(3+2)] × Total = (3/5) × Total.
         Females = (2/5) × Total.
```

---

#### DATA TYPE 5 — Fractional

```
"One-third of the employees are managers."
"Two-fifths of the students passed."
Convert: Managers = (1/3) × Total. Passed = (2/5) × Total.
```

---

#### DATA TYPE 6 — Comparative (Additive)

```
"B has 50 more than A."
"The number of girls is 120 more than the number of boys."
Use algebra: If Boys = x, Girls = x + 120. x + (x+120) = Total → solve for x.
```

---

#### DATA TYPE 7 — Comparative (Multiplicative)

```
"C has twice as many employees as D."
"The number of Science students is 3 times the number of Arts students."
Algebra: Science = 3 × Arts. Science + Arts = [known total] → solve.
```

---

#### DATA TYPE 8 — Growth / Change

```
"Sales increased by 20% compared to last year."
"Population grew by 15% annually over 3 years."
Convert: New = Old × (1 + 20/100) = Old × 1.20.
Compound: New = Old × 1.15³ (3 years compound growth).
```

---

#### DATA TYPE 9 — Conditional / Nested

```
"Of those who passed, 40% scored distinction."
"Among the urban males, 60% are employed."
These require MULTI-LEVEL extraction:
First extract the intermediate group (passed, urban males).
Then apply the given % to that intermediate group.
```

---

#### DATA TYPE 10 — Complement (Remaining)

```
"The remaining students chose Science."
"Others who didn't play cricket played football."
ALWAYS compute: Remaining = Total − (All explicitly given sub-groups).
Never skip computing complements — they are frequently asked about.
```

---

### 5.4 Identifying Hidden / Derived Values

```
Many values in a caselet are NOT directly stated — they are derived.
Derived values are computed from given data. They are JUST as important
as stated values because questions often ask about them.

TYPES OF DERIVED VALUES:

1. COMPLEMENT: "Not in a group" = Total of that group − Given sub-group.
   Boys not playing sports = Total boys − Boys playing sports.

2. CROSS-GROUP: Combining parts from different branches.
   Total sports players = Sports boys + Sports girls.

3. REMAINDER WITHIN A SUB-GROUP:
   Boys playing cricket = given.
   Boys playing other sports = Sports boys − Cricket boys.

4. CONDITIONAL COMPLEMENT:
   If 40% of sports boys play cricket, then 60% play other sports.
   Other sports boys = 60% × Sports boys.

RULE: For every group mentioned, ALWAYS compute its complement.
Even if not directly asked, you may need it indirectly for another question.
```

## 6. CONCEPT 2 — RATIO AND PROPORTION CASELETS

---

### How Ratio Caselets Work

```
Ratio caselets give data as a:b format.
From the ratio and a total (or one actual value), find individual values.

FORMULA: If A:B = m:n and Total = T:
  A = [m/(m+n)] × T
  B = [n/(m+n)] × T

FORMULA: If A:B = m:n and A = given value X:
  B = (n/m) × X
  Total = X + B = X × (m+n)/m

FORMULA: If A:B:C = p:q:r and Total = T:
  A = [p/(p+q+r)] × T
  B = [q/(p+q+r)] × T
  C = [r/(p+q+r)] × T
```

---

### Complete Example — Ratio Caselet

```
CASELET:
"A company has employees in three departments: HR, IT, and Finance.
The ratio of employees in HR:IT:Finance is 2:5:3. The total number of employees
is 1000. In the IT department, 40% are female. In the HR department, the ratio
of male to female is 3:1. In Finance, females are 200 more than males."

SCAN → CATEGORIZE → ASSIGN:

Total = 1000.
HR:IT:Finance = 2:5:3 → Sum of parts = 10.
HR  = (2/10) × 1000 = 200.
IT  = (5/10) × 1000 = 500.
Finance = (3/10) × 1000 = 300.

IT department:
Female IT = 40% × 500 = 200. Male IT = 500 − 200 = 300.

HR department: Male:Female = 3:1.
Male HR = (3/4) × 200 = 150. Female HR = (1/4) × 200 = 50.

Finance department: Females = Males + 200.
Let Males = x. Females = x + 200. x + (x+200) = 300 → 2x = 100 → x = 50.
Male Finance = 50. Female Finance = 150.

COMPLETE TABLE:
           | Male | Female | Total |
HR         |  150 |    50  |   200 |
IT         |  300 |   200  |   500 |
Finance    |   50 |   150  |   300 |
Total      |  500 |   400  |  1000 |

CHECK: Total males + females = 500 + 400 = 900 ≠ 1000. ERROR!
Wait: 150+300+50=500 (males) ✓. 50+200+150=400 (females) ✓. 500+400=900 ≠ 1000.
This is a contradiction in the data! Let me re-examine.

Actually: Total = 500+400 = 900 but Total should be 1000.
This means I made an error. Let me recheck Finance:
x + (x+200) = 300 → 2x = 100 → x = 50. Male=50, Female=150. Total=200 ≠ 300.
Error: 50+150=200 ≠ 300. The equation should give: x + (x+200) = 300 → x=50, female=250.
Female=250, Male=50. Total=300 ✓.
Updated: Male Finance=50, Female Finance=250.
Males: 150+300+50 = 500. Females: 50+200+250 = 500. Total = 1000. ✓ Consistent!

FINAL TABLE:
           | Male | Female | Total |
HR         |  150 |    50  |   200 |
IT         |  300 |   200  |   500 |
Finance    |   50 |   250  |   300 |
Total      |  500 |   500  |  1000 |

Now answer any question from this table in seconds.
```

---

### Quick Answer Examples from the Above

```
Q: "How many more males than females are in IT?"
A: 300 − 200 = 100 more males.

Q: "Female Finance employees are what % of total female employees?"
A: (250/500) × 100 = 50%.

Q: "What is the ratio of HR employees to Finance employees?"
A: 200:300 = 2:3.

Q: "Male IT employees are what % more than Male HR employees?"
A: [(300−150)/150] × 100 = 100%.
```

## 7. CONCEPT 3 — PERCENTAGE-BASED CASELETS

---

### How Percentage Caselets Work

```
These caselets give data as percentages of various totals.
The challenge is tracking WHICH total each percentage refers to.

THE GOLDEN RULE:
Always ask: "X% of WHAT?"
The answer is your denominator.

Common confusion: "40% of students are boys" vs "40% of boys play cricket."
First: boys = 40% of TOTAL students.
Second: cricket = 40% of BOYS (not total students).
```

---

### Part-of-Part Calculations

```
This is the most common calculation in percentage caselets.

"60% of students are boys. 40% of boys play sports. 50% of sports boys play cricket."

Cricket boys = 50% of (40% of (60% of Total))
             = 0.50 × 0.40 × 0.60 × Total
             = 0.12 × Total
             = 12% of Total

GENERAL RULE:
Part-of-part = Multiply all the fractions / percentages in the chain.
A% of B% of C% of Total = (A/100) × (B/100) × (C/100) × Total × 10000
= (A × B × C) / 10000 % of Total

Example: 50% of 40% of 60% of 1000.
= 0.50 × 0.40 × 0.60 × 1000
= 0.12 × 1000
= 120.

Cross-check: 60% of 1000 = 600. 40% of 600 = 240. 50% of 240 = 120. ✓
```

---

### Complete Example — Percentage Caselet

```
CASELET:
"In a survey of 2000 people, 55% are males. 70% of males and 80% of females
have a college degree. Of those with a college degree, 60% are employed.
Of those without a college degree, 30% are employed."

SCAN → DATA EXTRACTION:

Total = 2000.
Males = 55% × 2000 = 1100. Females = 2000 − 1100 = 900.

Males with degree = 70% × 1100 = 770. Males without degree = 1100−770 = 330.
Females with degree = 80% × 900 = 720. Females without degree = 900−720 = 180.

Total with degree = 770+720 = 1490. Total without degree = 330+180 = 510.
Check: 1490+510 = 2000. ✓

Employed with degree = 60% × 1490 = 894.
Employed without degree = 30% × 510 = 153.
Total employed = 894+153 = 1047. Unemployed = 2000−1047 = 953.

FULL DATA TABLE:
                | With Degree | Without Degree | Total |
Males           |     770     |      330       | 1100  |
Females         |     720     |      180       |  900  |
Total           |    1490     |      510       | 2000  |

Employed/Unemployed:
Employed with degree = 894.
Employed without degree = 153.
Total employed = 1047. Unemployed = 953.

Now answer any question from this data in under 30 seconds.

Q: "What % of females are employed?"
Male employed with degree: need to split 894 into males and females.
Employed male with degree = 60% × 770 = 462.
Employed female with degree = 60% × 720 = 432.
Employed male without degree = 30% × 330 = 99.
Employed female without degree = 30% × 180 = 54.
Total employed males = 462+99 = 561.
Total employed females = 432+54 = 486.
% of females employed = (486/900) × 100 = 54%.
```

## 8. CONCEPT 4 — POPULATION / SURVEY CASELETS

---

### Structure of Population / Survey Caselets

```
Population caselets involve a total population or survey respondents
split by multiple attributes: gender, age group, location, education, income, etc.

Key characteristics:
→ Multiple splitting dimensions (e.g., gender AND education AND location).
→ Data given for some intersections, not all — you derive the rest.
→ Questions often ask about specific intersections or cross-group totals.

TOOL: The TWO-WAY TABLE (Matrix)
When two splitting dimensions exist, draw a 2×2 or 3×3 matrix.
Fill in all given values. Compute missing cells using row/column totals.
```

---

### Complete Example — Survey Caselet

```
CASELET:
"A survey of 1500 people was conducted. 800 of them are from urban areas
and the rest from rural areas. Of the urban respondents, 60% are male.
Of the rural respondents, 45% are male. 40% of urban males and 50% of
rural males are employed. 35% of urban females and 30% of rural females
are employed."

DATA EXTRACTION:

Total = 1500. Urban = 800. Rural = 1500−800 = 700.

Urban males = 60% × 800 = 480. Urban females = 800−480 = 320.
Rural males = 45% × 700 = 315. Rural females = 700−315 = 385.

TWO-WAY TABLE:
                | Urban | Rural | Total |
Male            |  480  |  315  |  795  |
Female          |  320  |  385  |  705  |
Total           |  800  |  700  | 1500  |
Check: 480+320=800 ✓, 315+385=700 ✓, 795+705=1500 ✓.

Employment:
Urban male employed = 40% × 480 = 192. Urban male unemployed = 288.
Rural male employed = 50% × 315 = 157.5 ≈ 158 (if not exact).
Urban female employed = 35% × 320 = 112. Urban female unemployed = 208.
Rural female employed = 30% × 385 = 115.5 ≈ 116.

EMPLOYMENT TABLE:
                        | Employed | Unemployed | Total |
Urban Male              |    192   |    288     |  480  |
Urban Female            |    112   |    208     |  320  |
Rural Male              |  157.5   |   157.5    |  315  |
Rural Female            |  115.5   |   269.5    |  385  |
Total Employed = 192+112+157.5+115.5 = 577.

Q: "Total employed = ?"  → 577.
Q: "Urban employed as % of total urban?"
Urban employed = 192+112 = 304. % = (304/800)×100 = 38%.
```

## 9. CONCEPT 5 — INCOME / EXPENDITURE / PROFIT CASELETS

---

### Structure of Income/Expenditure Caselets

```
These caselets involve financial data:
→ Income / Revenue / Sales
→ Expenditure / Cost / Expenses
→ Savings / Profit / Loss = Income − Expenditure
→ % profit margin, savings rate, expenditure ratio

Key relationships:
Profit = Revenue − Cost.
Savings = Income − Expenditure.
% Margin = (Profit/Revenue) × 100.
Savings rate = (Savings/Income) × 100.
Expenditure rate = (Expenditure/Income) × 100.
Note: Savings rate + Expenditure rate = 100%.
```

---

### Complete Example — Income/Expenditure Caselet

```
CASELET:
"A family has 4 members: father, mother, son, and daughter. The father's monthly
income is ₹60,000. The mother earns 75% of the father's income. The son's income
is one-third of the mother's. The daughter does not earn. The total family
expenditure is ₹80,000 per month. 30% of the expenditure is on food, 25% on rent,
15% on education, and the rest on miscellaneous. The family saves 20% of the
total family income."

DATA EXTRACTION:

Father income = 60,000.
Mother = 75% × 60,000 = 45,000.
Son = (1/3) × 45,000 = 15,000.
Daughter = 0.
Total income = 60,000+45,000+15,000+0 = 1,20,000.

Savings = 20% × 1,20,000 = 24,000.
Given: Expenditure = 80,000.
Check: Income = Expenditure + Savings → 1,20,000 = 80,000+40,000 = 1,20,000.
But savings = 20% × 1,20,000 = 24,000 ≠ 40,000. Contradiction!

Re-read: "Expenditure = ₹80,000" AND "saves 20% of income = 24,000."
Then actual income used = Expenditure + Savings = 80,000+24,000 = 1,04,000 ≠ 1,20,000.
This suggests the caselet has inconsistency OR "total expenditure" is given as
a separate fact AND the savings = 20% refers only to one interpretation.

In exam caselets, use ONLY the data that is self-consistent.
If contradiction exists, prioritize the more specific/numerical data.
Here: use total income = 1,20,000 and savings = 20% × 1,20,000 = 24,000.
Then expenditure (computed) = 1,20,000−24,000 = 96,000 (override the given 80,000
OR use given 80,000 and compute actual savings as 40,000).

CLEAN VERSION (without contradiction):
Let's use: Expenditure = 80,000. Savings = Total income − 80,000.

Food = 30% × 80,000 = 24,000.
Rent = 25% × 80,000 = 20,000.
Education = 15% × 80,000 = 12,000.
Miscellaneous = (100−30−25−15)% × 80,000 = 30% × 80,000 = 24,000.
Savings = 1,20,000 − 80,000 = 40,000. Savings rate = (40,000/1,20,000)×100 = 33.33%.

Q: "What is the food expenditure?" → 24,000.
Q: "What % of the father's income is spent on rent?" → (20,000/60,000)×100 = 33.33%.
Q: "By how much does mother's income exceed son's income?" → 45,000−15,000 = 30,000.
```

## 10. CONCEPT 6 — WORK / PRODUCTION / OUTPUT CASELETS

---

### Structure of Work / Production Caselets

```
These caselets involve:
→ Production / output by multiple units (factories, workers, machines)
→ Work completion rates
→ Efficiency / defect rates
→ Capacity utilization

Key formulas:
Total output = Sum of individual outputs.
Defective % → Non-defective = (100 − defective%) × Total output.
Capacity utilization = (Actual output / Maximum capacity) × 100.
```

---

### Complete Example — Production Caselet

```
CASELET:
"A factory produces goods in three shifts. In Shift A, 600 units are produced.
Shift B produces 40% more than Shift A. Shift C produces 25% less than Shift B.
5% of units from Shift A are defective, 8% from Shift B, and 10% from Shift C.
The defective units are discarded. Non-defective units are packed and sold."

DATA EXTRACTION:

Shift A = 600. Shift B = 600 × 1.40 = 840. Shift C = 840 × 0.75 = 630.
Total production = 600+840+630 = 2070.

Defective:
Shift A defective = 5% × 600 = 30. Non-defective A = 570.
Shift B defective = 8% × 840 = 67.2. Non-defective B = 772.8.
Shift C defective = 10% × 630 = 63. Non-defective C = 567.
Total defective = 30+67.2+63 = 160.2. Non-defective = 2070−160.2 = 1909.8.

SUMMARY TABLE:
| Shift | Production | Defective | Non-Defective |
|-------|-----------|-----------|---------------|
| A     |    600    |    30     |      570      |
| B     |    840    |   67.2    |     772.8     |
| C     |    630    |    63     |      567      |
| Total |   2070    |  160.2    |    1909.8     |

Q: "What % of total production is non-defective?" → (1909.8/2070)×100 = 92.26%.
Q: "Shift C produces how much % less than Shift B?" → [(840−630)/840]×100 = 25% ✓.
Q: "Total defective units = ?" → 160.2 ≈ 160.
```

## 11. CONCEPT 7 — MULTI-GROUP COMPARISON CASELETS

---

### Structure

```
Multi-group caselets involve 3 or more entities (companies, schools, teams)
with data about each, and questions comparing across entities.

Strategy:
→ Create a comparison table with entities as rows and attributes as columns.
→ Fill in given values. Compute derived values (profit, average, total).
→ Answer comparison questions from the table.
```

---

### Complete Example — Multi-Group Caselet

```
CASELET:
"Five teams A, B, C, D, and E participated in a tournament. Team A scored 120 points.
Team B scored 20% more than Team A. Team C scored 15 points less than Team B.
Team D scored the average of A, B, and C. Team E scored twice the difference between
B and C. The total prize money is ₹5,00,000 distributed in the ratio of their scores."

DATA EXTRACTION:

A = 120.
B = 120 × 1.20 = 144.
C = 144 − 15 = 129.
D = Average of A,B,C = (120+144+129)/3 = 393/3 = 131.
Difference B−C = 144−129 = 15. E = 2×15 = 30.

Total scores = 120+144+129+131+30 = 554.

Prize money distribution (ratio of scores):
A = (120/554) × 5,00,000 = 1,08,303.25 ≈ ₹1,08,303.
B = (144/554) × 5,00,000 = ₹1,29,964.
C = (129/554) × 5,00,000 = ₹1,16,426.
D = (131/554) × 5,00,000 = ₹1,18,232.
E = (30/554) × 5,00,000 = ₹27,076.

Q: "Which team gets the most prize money?" → B (highest score = highest prize).
Q: "Team E's prize is what % of Team A's prize?" → (30/120)×100 = 25%.
(Same total → ratio of prizes = ratio of scores.)
Q: "What is the prize money of teams A and C combined?"
= [(120+129)/554] × 5,00,000 = (249/554) × 500,000 = ₹2,24,729.
```

## 12. CONCEPT 8 — TIME-SERIES / GROWTH CASELETS

---

### Structure

```
Time-series caselets give data for multiple years with % changes or
absolute changes between years.

Key formulas:
Year N value = Year (N-1) value × (1 + growth rate/100).
Or: Year N = Year (N-1) + absolute change.

Backward calculation (finding past values):
Old = New ÷ (1 + growth rate/100).

Average growth rate (simple): Mean of individual year growth rates.
CAGR: [(Final/Initial)^(1/n) − 1] × 100 (compound annual growth rate).
```

---

### Complete Example — Growth Caselet

```
CASELET:
"The population of a city was 5 lakh in 2018. It grew by 10% in 2019,
declined by 5% in 2020, grew by 20% in 2021, and grew by 8% in 2022.
The ratio of male to female population is constant at 3:2 throughout all years."

DATA EXTRACTION:

2018 = 5,00,000.
2019 = 5,00,000 × 1.10 = 5,50,000.
2020 = 5,50,000 × 0.95 = 5,22,500.
2021 = 5,22,500 × 1.20 = 6,27,000.
2022 = 6,27,000 × 1.08 = 6,77,160.

Male:Female = 3:2 (constant). Males = 3/5 × each year's total.

YEAR TABLE:
| Year | Population | Males    | Females  |
|------|-----------|----------|----------|
| 2018 | 5,00,000  | 3,00,000 | 2,00,000 |
| 2019 | 5,50,000  | 3,30,000 | 2,20,000 |
| 2020 | 5,22,500  | 3,13,500 | 2,09,000 |
| 2021 | 6,27,000  | 3,76,200 | 2,50,800 |
| 2022 | 6,77,160  | 4,06,296 | 2,70,864 |

Q: "By what % did population change from 2018 to 2022?"
% change = [(6,77,160 − 5,00,000) / 5,00,000] × 100
= (1,77,160/5,00,000)×100 = 35.43%.

Q: "In which year was population decline observed?" → 2020 (5.22L < 5.50L).
Q: "What is the total male population across all 5 years?"
= 3,00,000+3,30,000+3,13,500+3,76,200+4,06,296 = 17,25,996.
```

## 13. CONCEPT 9 — NESTED CONDITION CASELETS

---

### What Are Nested Conditions?

```
Nested conditions = Conditions within conditions = Multi-level filtering.

"Of all students (Level 0), 60% passed (Level 1).
Of those who passed, 40% scored distinction (Level 2).
Of distinction scorers, 25% got scholarships (Level 3)."

Computing scholarship winners = 25% of (40% of (60% of Total))
= 0.25 × 0.40 × 0.60 × Total
= 0.06 × Total
= 6% of Total.

Each "of those" creates a new level. The base changes at each level.
```

---

### The Tree Diagram for Nested Caselets

```
Draw a binary/multi-branch tree:

                        [1000 students]
                       /               \
             [600 passed]           [400 failed]
            /           \
  [240 distinction]  [360 non-distinction]
  /             \
[60 scholar.]  [180 no scholar.]

Every node has a value. Every question asks about one or two nodes.

Computing any node:
→ Start from the ROOT (total).
→ Apply each branching % in order going down the tree.
→ The VALUE at any node = (branch %) × (parent node value).
```

---

### Complete Example — Nested Caselet

```
CASELET:
"A company employs 2000 people. 60% work in the technical division and
the rest in non-technical. Of the technical employees, 35% are senior staff
and the rest are junior. Of the senior technical staff, 80% hold a master's degree.
Of the junior technical staff, 50% hold a master's degree. Of the non-technical
employees, 20% are senior. Senior non-technical staff all hold degrees but
only 40% of junior non-technical staff hold degrees."

SCAN → BUILD TREE:

Total = 2000.
Technical = 60% × 2000 = 1200. Non-Technical = 800.

Technical senior = 35% × 1200 = 420. Technical junior = 1200−420 = 780.
Non-Technical senior = 20% × 800 = 160. Non-Technical junior = 800−160 = 640.

Degrees in Technical:
Senior with degree = 80% × 420 = 336. Senior without = 84.
Junior with degree = 50% × 780 = 390. Junior without = 390.

Degrees in Non-Technical:
Senior with degree = 100% × 160 = 160. Senior without = 0.
Junior with degree = 40% × 640 = 256. Junior without = 384.

MASTER TABLE:
                       | Senior | Junior | Total  |
Technical with degree  |   336  |   390  |   726  |
Technical no degree    |    84  |   390  |   474  |
Non-Tech with degree   |   160  |   256  |   416  |
Non-Tech no degree     |     0  |   384  |   384  |
TOTAL with degree = 726+416 = 1142. Without = 474+384 = 858.
Grand total = 1142+858 = 2000. ✓

QUESTIONS:
Q: "What % of all employees hold a master's degree?" → (1142/2000)×100 = 57.1%.
Q: "Technical junior employees without degree are what % of all without degree?"
→ (390/858)×100 = 45.45%.
Q: "What is the ratio of senior to junior employees overall?"
Senior total = 420+160 = 580. Junior total = 780+640 = 1420.
Ratio = 580:1420 = 29:71.
```

## 14. MASTER TRICKS & SHORTCUTS

---

### 🚀 TRICK 1 — The SCAN Method (Non-Negotiable First Step)

```
S — Survey: Read entire paragraph once without extracting data.
C — Categorize: Identify all groups and subgroups. Draw hierarchy.
A — Assign: Compute values for every node in the hierarchy.
N — Note: Write clean data table before answering Question 1.

WHY IT WORKS:
Answering questions from memory while re-reading = dangerous.
Answering questions from a pre-built personal table = safe and fast.

TIME: 4–5 minutes to SCAN.
BENEFIT: Each question takes < 30 seconds after SCAN is done.
```

---

### 🚀 TRICK 2 — Always Compute Complements Immediately

```
Whenever you compute a group's value, IMMEDIATELY compute its complement.

If Sports boys = 192, IMMEDIATELY: Non-sports boys = 480 − 192 = 288.
If Degree holders = 1490, IMMEDIATELY: Non-degree = 510.

WHY: Questions almost always ask about "those who did NOT" or "the remaining."
Computing complements on first pass = no re-computation later.

Rule of thumb: For every branching (A → A₁ and A₂), compute BOTH A₁ and A₂.
Never compute only A₁ and leave A₂ blank.
```

---

### 🚀 TRICK 3 — The "Of WHAT?" Trap Buster

```
Before applying any %, always ask: "This is X% of WHAT?"

"40% of boys play sports" → 40% of [boys], NOT 40% of [total].
"30% of sports players are girls" → 30% of [sports players], NOT [girls].
"60% distinction scorers got scholarships" → 60% of [distinction scorers].

The denominator (the "of what") changes at every nested level.
ALWAYS identify the correct denominator before computing.

Wrong: Cricket boys = 50% of 800 (total). [Used wrong base]
Right: Cricket boys = 50% of 192 (sports boys). [Correct base]
```

---

### 🚀 TRICK 4 — Part-of-Part Shortcut (Multiply Fractions)

```
For nested percentage calculations:

X% of Y% of Z% of Total = (X×Y×Z) / 10000 % of Total × 100
= X×Y×Z / 1,000,000 × Total (when all are in %)

Easier: Convert each % to decimal and multiply.
50% of 40% of 60% of 1000
= 0.50 × 0.40 × 0.60 × 1000
= 0.12 × 1000 = 120.

Or chain it: 60% of 1000 = 600 → 40% of 600 = 240 → 50% of 240 = 120.
Both give 120. Use whichever feels faster.

The decimal multiplication (0.50 × 0.40 × 0.60) is faster for 3+ levels.
```

---

### 🚀 TRICK 5 — Verify Total at Each Level

```
After computing all sub-groups at a level, verify they sum to the parent group.

Level 1: Boys + Girls = Total ✓
Level 2: Sports boys + Non-sports boys = Total boys ✓
         Sports girls + Non-sports girls = Total girls ✓
Level 3: Cricket boys + Other sports boys = Sports boys ✓

If any level fails verification → you made an arithmetic error. Fix before proceeding.

Time: 5 seconds per level.
Benefit: Prevents systematic chain errors across all questions.
```

---

### 🚀 TRICK 6 — Ratio Distribution Formula

```
When given A:B:C = p:q:r and Total = T:

A = p/(p+q+r) × T
B = q/(p+q+r) × T
C = r/(p+q+r) × T

FAST COMPUTATION:
Compute the denominator (p+q+r) ONCE.
Then compute each numerator quickly.

Example: A:B:C = 3:5:2, Total = 4000.
Denominator = 10.
A = 3/10 × 4000 = 1200.
B = 5/10 × 4000 = 2000.
C = 2/10 × 4000 = 800.
Check: 1200+2000+800 = 4000 ✓.
```

---

### 🚀 TRICK 7 — Reverse Percentage (Find Total from Part + %)

```
"X% of some total = given value. Find the total."

Formula: Total = Given value × (100/X)
         OR: Total = Given value / (X/100)

Example: "40% of total employees = 600. Find total."
Total = 600 × (100/40) = 600 × 2.5 = 1500.

This appears when a caselet gives you a derived value and a % but not the base.
Recognize the pattern: you have the result and the rate → find the base.
```

---

### 🚀 TRICK 8 — Algebra for "More Than / Less Than" Data

```
"A has 50 more than B" → A = B + 50.
"Girls are 120 more than boys" → G = B + 120.
Given total → B + (B+120) = Total → 2B + 120 = Total → B = (Total−120)/2.

"C is twice B" → C = 2B.
Given B+C = 600 → B + 2B = 600 → B = 200, C = 400.

For three entities with one constraint:
"A:B = 3:2 and B:C = 4:5" → A:B:C = 12:8:10 = 6:4:5.
(Find common ratio by making B the same in both ratios.)
```

---

### 🚀 TRICK 9 — The Allotment Method for Fast Ratio Splits

```
For distributing a total in ratio p:q:r:
Use "allotment units."
Total units = p+q+r.
Each unit = Total / (p+q+r).
A's share = p × (each unit value).

Example: ₹12,000 in ratio 3:4:5.
Total units = 12. Each unit = 1000.
A = 3×1000 = 3000. B = 4×1000 = 4000. C = 5×1000 = 5000.
Check: 3000+4000+5000 = 12000. ✓

This is faster than computing 3/12 × 12000 for each separately.
```

---

### 🚀 TRICK 10 — Missing Category by Subtraction

```
If N categories are given and their total is known:
Missing category = Total − (Sum of all given categories).

Example:
Groups: A=30%, B=25%, C=20%, D=?, E=10%. Total = 100%.
D = 100 − 30 − 25 − 20 − 10 = 15%.

In terms of values: if Total=2000:
A=600, B=500, C=400, E=200.
D = 2000 − (600+500+400+200) = 2000−1700 = 300.

ALWAYS compute the missing category before reading questions.
It is almost certainly asked about in at least one question.
```

---

### 🚀 TRICK 11 — Two-Way Table for Multi-Attribute Data

```
When a caselet involves 2 attributes (e.g., Gender + Location, or Grade + Stream):

Draw a 2×2 or 3×2 table IMMEDIATELY.

Row = Attribute 1 (Male/Female).
Column = Attribute 2 (Urban/Rural).
Cells = Intersection values (Urban Males, Urban Females, Rural Males, Rural Females).
Row totals = totals for Attribute 1.
Column totals = totals for Attribute 2.
Grand total = overall total.

Fill what's given → compute what's missing using row/column totals.

Any 2-attribute question is just: "read from the correct cell."
```

---

### 🚀 TRICK 12 — Constant Ratio Simplification

```
"The ratio of male to female is 3:2 in ALL years."
This means: for any year, Males = 3/5 × Total and Females = 2/5 × Total.

You don't need to compute separately for each year.
Just multiply each year's total by 3/5 or 2/5.

Questions like "how many more males than females in Year X?"
Answer: always (3/5 − 2/5) × Year X total = (1/5) × Year X total.

One formula, works for all years where ratio is constant.
```

---

### 🚀 TRICK 13 — The "% of Total" vs "% of Sub-Group" Fast Check

```
KEY SIGNAL WORDS:

"X% of [group name]" → X% is of that specific group (sub-group %).
"X% are [attribute]" (without specifying group) → X% of TOTAL.

Examples:
"40% of boys play sports." → 40% of [boys] — not total.
"40% play sports." → 40% of [total] — ambiguous but implies total.
"40% of students who play sports are boys." → 40% of [sports players].

When in doubt: identify the grammatical subject of "of" → that's the denominator.
```

---

### 🚀 TRICK 14 — Approximation for Non-Integer Results

```
In exam caselets, most answers are clean integers.
If you get 157.5, 288.6, or 67.2 → either:
(a) The question expects you to round to nearest integer.
(b) You made an arithmetic error somewhere.
(c) The question specifically says "approximately."

Protocol:
→ First: recheck your calculation (most likely error in Step 1 or 2).
→ If still non-integer: round to nearest whole number.
→ Match to the nearest MCQ option.

Never report non-integers as answers in banking exams without checking.
```

---

### 🚀 TRICK 15 — The Cross-Check Equation

```
The most powerful verification in caselet DI:

Sum of all level-2 values = Level-1 parent value.
Sum of all level-1 values = Total (universe).

Also: Sum of all "employed" + "unemployed" = Total.
Sum of all "passed" + "failed" = Total students.
Sum of all "with degree" + "without degree" = Total.

BUILD THIS CHECK INTO YOUR DATA TABLE:
After filling every row, compute the row total and verify against the known total.
After filling every column, verify the column total.

The moment a check fails → there's an error in your extraction.
Fix it before answering any question.
```

## 16. COMMON MISTAKE ALERTS

---

### ⚠️ MISTAKE 1 — Using Wrong Base for a Percentage

```
"40% of boys play sports."
Wrong: Sports players = 40% of TOTAL students.
Right: Sports players = 40% of BOYS (not total).

Prevention: Always ask "40% of WHAT?" before multiplying.
The "of WHAT" comes from the sentence structure — find the noun that follows "of."
```

---

### ⚠️ MISTAKE 2 — Not Computing Complements

```
"40% of boys play sports. 50% of sports boys play cricket."
Wrong: Computing cricket boys = 50% × [all boys].
Right: Sports boys = 40% × Boys. Cricket boys = 50% × Sports boys.
Non-sports boys = Boys − Sports boys. [Often needed for later questions.]

Prevention: Immediately compute the complement whenever you compute a sub-group.
```

---

### ⚠️ MISTAKE 3 — Chain Error from Wrong Step 1

```
If Total = 1000, Boys = 60% of 1000 = 600.
If you mistakenly write Boys = 650, then EVERY derived value is wrong.

Prevention:
→ Compute slowly for Step 1 (the "universe" or "first split").
→ Cross-check: Boys + Girls should = Total.
→ An early verification prevents chain contamination.
```

---

### ⚠️ MISTAKE 4 — Confusing "More Than" with "More Than X%"

```
"B has 50 more than A" → B = A + 50 (additive, absolute difference).
"B has 50% more than A" → B = A × 1.50 (multiplicative, relative).

These look similar but are completely different calculations.
"25 more" vs "25% more" — one word changes everything.

Prevention: Read the sentence with extreme care.
Does "more" have a "%" before it? If YES → multiplicative. If NO → additive.
```

---

### ⚠️ MISTAKE 5 — Wrong Denominator for % Share Questions

```
"Female Arts students are what % of total female students?"
Wrong: (Female Arts) / (Total students) × 100.
Right: (Female Arts) / (Total FEMALE students) × 100.

Prevention: The denominator is always specified in the question.
Underline the "of [what]" part of every % share question.
```

---

### ⚠️ MISTAKE 6 — Double Counting in Cross-Group Totals

```
"How many people have EITHER a degree OR a job?"
This requires careful Venn diagram logic.

If Degree holders = 1490 and Employed = 1047 in a group of 2000:
"Degree OR employed" ≠ 1490 + 1047 (that double-counts those with BOTH).

"Degree OR employed" = Degree + Employed − (Degree AND employed).

Caselet DI questions usually avoid this complexity — but beware if the question
says "at least one" or "either...or."
```

---

### ⚠️ MISTAKE 7 — Applying Growth to Wrong Base Year

```
"Population grew by 10% in 2019, 15% in 2020."
"Population in 2018 = 5,00,000."

Wrong: 2020 = 5,00,000 × 1.10 × 1.15 = ??? Wait, this IS right.

The trap: "Population grew from 2020 onwards by 15%. What is 2021?"
Base for 2021 = 2020 value (not 2018 or 2019).

Prevention: Always confirm: which year is the base for a given growth rate?
```

---

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
Absolute       → Use directly.
% of total     → Value = % × Total ÷ 100.
% of sub-group → Value = % × Sub-group total ÷ 100. [Not global total!]
Ratio a:b      → A = a/(a+b) × Total. B = b/(a+b) × Total.
"B more than A"→ B = A + constant (additive).
"50% more"     → B = A × 1.50 (multiplicative — note the % sign!).
"Twice as many"→ B = 2 × A.
Fraction (1/3) → Value = (1/3) × Total.
Remaining      → Remaining = Total − Sum of given sub-groups.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
THE "OF WHAT?" GOLDEN RULE
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
Before applying any %: ask "This is X% of WHAT?"
The answer = your denominator.

"40% of boys" → denominator = boys (NOT total).
"30% of sports players" → denominator = sports players.
"% of total" → denominator = total (only when "total" is specified).

## 🏆 TOP 15 EXAM TIPS

1. **SCAN before Question 1 — always, without exception** — reading a caselet and immediately trying to answer questions is the single biggest mistake. Invest 4–5 minutes to extract all data into a personal table. After that, every question takes under 30 seconds. Skipping SCAN costs more time than it saves.

2. **Build the group hierarchy BEFORE assigning values** — draw the tree of all groups and sub-groups first. Then fill in numbers. This ensures you don't miss any sub-group and don't apply a percentage to the wrong parent.

3. **Always ask "X% of WHAT?" before computing** — the most common error in caselet DI is applying a percentage to the wrong denominator. Every percentage has a specific base. Identify it from the sentence before multiplying.

4. **Compute EVERY complement immediately** — the moment you compute Sports boys = 192, immediately write Non-sports boys = 288. Never leave a complement blank. Questions about "those who did NOT" are as common as questions about "those who did."

5. **Verify every level of your tree** — after computing sub-groups, check that they sum to the parent. If Boys = 480 and Girls = 320, verify 480+320 = 800. This catches errors before they cascade into 5 wrong answers.

6. **"B more than A" vs "B% more than A" — read with extreme care** — "50 more" is additive. "50% more" is multiplicative. This is the most common language trap in caselet DI. Train your eyes to always check for a "%" sign before the word "more."

7. **Use algebra for "comparative" data** — when told "Girls = Boys + 120" and total = 1500, set up: B + (B+120) = 1500 → B = 690. Don't guess or estimate. Simple algebra solves it exactly.

8. **Part-of-part = multiply decimals** — "50% of 40% of 60% of 1000" = 0.50 × 0.40 × 0.60 × 1000 = 120. One multiplication, no intermediate steps. Faster than chaining three separate % calculations.

9. **For ratio splits: compute the "unit value" first** — ratio 3:5:2, total = 2000. Unit = 2000/10 = 200. Then A = 3×200=600, B = 5×200=1000, C = 2×200=400. Verify: 600+1000+400=2000. Clean and fast.

10. **Reverse percentage: Old = New ÷ (1 + r/100)** — when a caselet says "this year's value is 20% more than last year's" and gives the current value, never subtract 20% from the current value. Divide by 1.20 to get the old value.

11. **Draw a 2-way table for multi-attribute caselets** — whenever data involves two splitting dimensions (Gender × Location, or Stream × Grade), immediately draw a 2×2 or 3×2 matrix. Fill given values, compute missing cells from row/column totals.

12. **For nested caselets: draw the tree** — each "of those who [condition]" creates a new branch in the tree. Label every node. Compute every branch value. Every question asks about one node or a combination of nodes.

13. **Non-integer results signal an error** — if you compute 157.5 students, recheck your calculation. Banking exam caselets are designed with clean integer answers. A decimal in the computation suggests a misread percentage or wrong base.

14. **Answer questions sequentially for derived values** — Q1 may give you "total boys = 780." Q2 may need "sports boys = 35% of 780 = 273." Q3 may need "cricket boys = 40% of 273 = 109." If you skip Q1, Q2 is uncomputable. Follow the dependency chain.

15. **Use the data table you built — don't re-read the paragraph** — every question should be answerable from your SCAN note. If you find yourself re-reading the paragraph for every question, your SCAN was incomplete. Improve the SCAN, not the question-answering.

*Caselet DI separates candidates who "just know formulas" from those who can think systematically under pressure. The formulas are the same as all other DI formats — ratios, percentages, averages, profit. The unique skill is DATA EXTRACTION from dense text. Master the SCAN method, build the habit of computing complements, and draw the group hierarchy before touching any calculation. Once your personal data table is ready, a caselet that looks like a wall of words becomes a simple table-lookup exercise. You've got this! 💪*
